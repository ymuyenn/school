# UNIT 1: BIOLOGICAL BASES OF BEHAVIOR (Complete Detail)

## 1.1 Interaction of Heredity and Environment

**Heredity (Nature):** Genetic/predisposed characteristics influencing physical, behavioral, and mental traits.

**Environment (Nurture):** External factors (family interactions, education, culture, trauma).

**Evolutionary Perspective:** Natural selection favors behaviors that increase survival and reproductive success.
- **Warning:** Eugenics = discriminatory application (outside scope of what you need to defend, just know it's bad).

**Research Methods for Nature/Nurture:**
- **Twin studies:** Compare identical (monozygotic) vs fraternal (dizygotic) twins. Higher concordance rate in identical twins = genetic influence.
- **Adoption studies:** Compare adopted children to biological vs adoptive parents. Similarities to biological parents = nature. Similarities to adoptive parents = nurture.
- **Family studies:** Compare biological relatives across generations.

> **Exclusion:** You do NOT need to know genotype, phenotype, DNA, chromosomes, dominant/recessive genes.

## 1.2 Overview of the Nervous System

**Central Nervous System (CNS):** Brain + Spinal Cord. Processes information.

**Peripheral Nervous System (PNS):** All nerves outside CNS. Relays messages.

| System | Function | Control |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Somatic** | Voluntary movement, sensory info | Voluntary |
| **Autonomic** | Involuntary (heart, glands, digestion) | Involuntary |

## 1.3 The Neuron and Neural Firing (Full Detail)

### Neuron Types:
- **Sensory neurons:** Carry info from senses TO CNS.
- **Motor neurons:** Carry info FROM CNS to muscles/glands.
- **Interneurons:** Connect sensory and motor neurons within CNS (spinal cord).

**Reflex arc:** Sensory → Interneuron (in spinal cord) → Motor. Brain not involved = faster.

**Glial cells (glia):** Support cells. Provide structure, insulation (myelin), communication, waste transport. Damage to glia = multiple sclerosis (myelin sheath destroyed → slow neural transmission).

### Neural Transmission Process (Order of events):
1. **Resting potential:** Neuron is polarized (more negative inside, -70mV). Not firing.
2. **Threshold:** Excitatory signals reach minimum level needed to trigger action potential.
3. **Depolarization:** Sodium ions rush in, inside becomes positive. Action potential fires.
4. **All-or-none principle:** Neuron either fires fully or not at all (no partial firing). Stronger stimulus = more frequent firing, not stronger firing.
5. **Repolarization/Refractory period:** Potassium ions exit, neuron returns to resting state. Cannot fire again during absolute refractory period.

**Reuptake:** Unused neurotransmitters are reabsorbed by the sending neuron.

> **Exclusion:** Sodium-potassium pump details are outside scope.

### Hindbrain (Brainstem):
| Structure | Function |
| :--- | :--- |

| **Pons** | Sleep, arousal, facial expressions, connects hindbrain to midbrain. |

| Structure | Function |

| **Hypothalamus** | "4 F's": Fighting, Fleeing, Feeding, Mating. Regulates body temperature, hunger, thirst, hormones (connects to pituitary). |
| **Pituitary gland** | Master gland. Releases hormones that control other glands. |
| **Hippocampus** | Formation of new explicit (declarative) memories. Damage = anterograde amnesia (can't form new memories). |
| **Amygdala** | Fear, aggression, emotional processing. Damage = no fear response. |

| Lobe | Location | Functions | Key Areas |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Frontal** | Forehead | Executive function, planning, decision-making, personality, speech production | **Prefrontal cortex** (judgment). **Motor cortex** (movement). **Broca's area** (speech production) |

- **Broca's area:** Speech PRODUCTION. Damage = Broca's aphasia (can understand, can't speak fluently).
- **Wernicke's area:** Speech COMPREHENSION. Damage = Wernicke's aphasia (speaks fluently but nonsense; can't understand).

### Split Brain Research:
- **Corpus callosum** connects hemispheres. Severed to treat severe epilepsy.
- Left hemisphere = language, logic, right visual field, right hand.
- Right hemisphere = spatial, emotion, left visual field, left hand.
- **Contralateral organization:** Left visual field → right hemisphere. Right visual field → left hemisphere.

**Plasticity:** Brain rewires itself. Damaged area function can be taken over by other areas. Greatest in childhood.

**Brain Research Methods:**
- **EEG (electroencephalogram):** Records electrical activity (brain waves). Good for timing, not location.
- **fMRI (functional MRI):** Measures blood flow (oxygen use). Good for location, not timing.
- **Lesioning:** Deliberately damaging brain area in animals to study function.
- **Case studies:** Phineas Gage (rod through frontal lobe → personality change).

## 1.5 Sleep (Full Detail)

**Consciousness:** Awareness of internal and external world. Continuous spectrum (alert → daydreaming → asleep → coma).

**Circadian rhythm:** ~24-hour cycle. Regulated by **melatonin** (released by pineal gland at night). Disrupted by jet lag, shift work.

### Stages of Sleep (NREM 1→2→3→REM→cycle repeats)

| Stage | Type | EEG Pattern | Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Stage 1** | NREM | Theta waves | Light sleep. **Hypnagogic sensations** (falling feeling, hallucinations). Lasts ~5-10 min |
| **Stage 2** | NREM | Theta waves + sleep spindles, K-complexes | Deeper. Sleep spindles = memory consolidation. ~20 min |
| **Stage 3** | NREM | Delta waves (slow wave) | Deep sleep. Hard to wake. Restoration, growth hormone release. ~30 min |
| **REM** | REM | Beta waves (like awake) | **Paradoxical sleep** (brain active, body paralyzed). Dreaming occurs here. Increases in duration each cycle. |

**REM Rebound:** If deprived of REM sleep, next sleep has more and longer REM cycles.

### Dream Theories:
- **Activation-synthesis theory (Hobson & McCarley):** Dreams are random brain stem bursts (activation); cortex tries to make sense of them (synthesis). No meaning.
- **Consolidation theory:** Dreams help consolidate and organize memories.

> **Exclusion:** Freud's psychoanalytic dream theory (manifest/latent content) is NOT tested.

### Why Sleep?
- **Memory consolidation:** Strengthens neural connections, transfers info from hippocampus to cortex.
- **Restoration:** Restores depleted resources, clears waste (glymphatic system), releases growth hormone.

### Sleep Disorders (Only these 5 tested):

| Disorder | Symptoms |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Insomnia** | Difficulty falling/staying asleep. Most common. |
| **Narcolepsy** | Sudden, uncontrollable sleep attacks. May include cataplexy (sudden muscle weakness triggered by emotion). |
| **REM Sleep Behavior Disorder** | Act out dreams (lack of muscle paralysis during REM). Dangerous. |
| **Sleep Apnea** | Stop breathing during sleep. Wake up gasping. Chronic fatigue. |
| **Somnambulism** | Sleepwalking. Occurs during NREM Stage 3. |

## 1.6 Sensation (Full Detail)

**Sensation vs Perception:**
- **Sensation:** Detection of physical energy by sense organs.
- **Perception:** Interpretation of sensory information by brain.

**Absolute threshold:** Minimum stimulus intensity detected 50% of the time.
- Vision: Candle flame 30 miles away.
- Hearing: Tick of watch 20 feet away.

**Difference threshold (Just Noticeable Difference / JND):** Minimum difference between two stimuli detected 50% of the time.

**Weber's Law:** JND is a constant proportion (not constant amount).
- Example: If you can tell 1 lb vs 1.1 lb (10% difference), you can tell 10 lb vs 11 lb (still 10%).

**Signal Detection Theory:** Detection depends on sensitivity AND decision criteria (motivation, expectations, fatigue).

**Sensory adaptation:** Diminished sensitivity due to constant stimulation (e.g., not feeling your clothes after wearing them all day).

**Sensory interaction:** Senses work together. Smell + taste = flavor.

**Synesthesia:** One sense triggers another (e.g., hearing colors, tasting sounds). Rare, neurological.

### VISION (Full Detail)

**Pathway:** Light → Cornea → Pupil (iris controls size) → Lens → Retina → Optic nerve → Thalamus → Occipital lobe.

**Accommodation:** Lens changes shape to focus on near/far objects. Nearsightedness (myopia) = eyeball too long. Farsightedness (hyperopia) = eyeball too short.

**Retina cells:**
| Cell Type | Location | Function | Color? | Light Level |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Rods** | Periphery (not fovea) | Shape, movement, peripheral vision | No (black/white/gray) | Low light (night vision) |
| **Cones** | Fovea (center) | Color, detail, acuity | Yes (red, green, blue) | High light (daylight) |

**Blind spot:** Where optic nerve exits retina. No photoreceptors. Brain fills in the gap.

**Dark adaptation:** Rods become more sensitive in low light (takes ~30 min). Cones adapt faster (~10 min).

**Color Vision Theories:**
1.  **Trichromatic theory (Young-Helmholtz):** Three types of cones (red, green, blue). Combination produces all colors. Explains color blindness (missing one cone type).
2.  **Opponent-process theory (Hering):** Three opponent pairs (red/green, blue/yellow, black/white). Explains afterimages (stare at red → green afterimage).

**Both theories are correct:** Cones do trichromatic (retina level). Ganglion cells do opponent-process (optic nerve level).

**Color vision deficiency (color blindness):** Most common = red-green (missing red or green cones). More common in males (X-linked).

**Visual disorders from brain damage:**
- **Prosopagnosia (face blindness):** Cannot recognize faces. Damage to fusiform gyrus (temporal lobe).
- **Blindsight:** Damage to occipital lobe → blind in part of visual field. But can still guess location of objects (unconscious vision via subcortical pathways).

### AUDITION (Full Detail)

**Sound properties:**
- **Pitch:** Frequency (wavelength). Hz. Higher frequency = higher pitch.
- **Loudness:** Amplitude (height of wave). Decibels (dB).

**Pathway:** Sound waves → Pinna (outer ear) → Auditory canal → Eardrum (tympanic membrane) → Ossicles (hammer, anvil, stirrup in middle ear) → Oval window → Cochlea (inner ear) → Hair cells (transduce) → Auditory nerve → Thalamus → Temporal lobe.

**Pitch perception theories:**
- **Place theory:** Different pitches stimulate different places on cochlea (basilar membrane). Explains high-frequency sounds.
- **Frequency theory:** Neurons fire at same frequency as sound wave. Explains low-frequency sounds.
- **Volley theory:** Groups of neurons fire in rapid succession to explain frequencies between 100-4000 Hz.

**Sound localization:** Brain compares timing and loudness difference between two ears.
- Horizontal plane: Difference in timing (microseconds).
- Vertical plane: Difference in loudness and pinna filtering.

**Hearing loss:**
| Type | Location | Cause |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Conduction deafness** | Middle ear (ossicles, eardrum) | Damage to mechanical parts. Can be helped with hearing aid. |
| **Sensorineural deafness** | Inner ear (cochlea, hair cells, auditory nerve) | Damage to hair cells or nerve. Cannot be repaired. Cochlear implant may help. |

---

### CHEMICAL SENSES (Olfaction & Gustation)

**Olfaction (Smell):**
- **ONLY sense that bypasses the thalamus.** Goes directly to olfactory bulb → amygdala/hippocampus (why smells trigger strong emotions/memories).
- **Pheromones:** Chemical signals between same species (in animals; human evidence is weak).

**Gustation (Taste):**
- **Five basic tastes (plus one):** Sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami (savory/meaty), oleogustus (fat).
- Taste buds (papillae) on tongue contain taste receptor cells.
- **Supertasters:** More taste buds → more sensitive to bitter. Genetic.

**Interaction:** Most of "taste" is actually smell. Pinch nose while eating → barely taste.

---

### BODY SENSES

**Touch (Tactile):** Pressure, warmth, cold, pain. Receptors in skin. "Hot" = simultaneous activation of warm and cold receptors.

**Pain:**
- **Gate control theory (Melzack & Wall):** Spinal cord has "gate" that can block or allow pain signals to brain. Rubbing injury (non-painful input) closes gate → less pain.
- **Phantom limb sensation:** Sensation/pain in amputated limb. Brain still expects input from that area.

**Vestibular sense (Balance):** **Semicircular canals** in inner ear (filled with fluid). Head movement → fluid moves → hair cells bend → brain knows head position. Motion sickness = mismatch between vestibular and visual input.

**Kinesthesis (Body position):** Receptors in muscles, joints, tendons. Know where body parts are without looking. Touch nose with eyes closed = kinesthesis.

# UNIT 2: COGNITION (Complete Detail)

## 2.1 Perception (Full Detail)

**Bottom-up processing:** Starting with raw sensory data and building up to perception. "I see lines and shadows; that must be a face."

**Top-down processing:** Using prior knowledge and expectations to interpret sensations. "I expect to see a face, so I see one in this random pattern."

**Schemas:** Mental frameworks that organize and interpret information. Example: Your "restaurant schema" tells you to expect a menu, a waiter, and food.

**Perceptual set:** Readiness to perceive something in a particular way based on expectations. Example: If told to look for numbers, you see "13." If told to look for letters, you see "B."

**Gestalt principles (German for "whole"):** The whole is greater than the sum of its parts. We automatically organize visual information:

| Principle | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Figure-ground** | Distinguish object (figure) from background (ground) | Vase/faces illusion |
| **Proximity** | Group nearby objects together | Three pairs of dots instead of six dots in a row |
| **Similarity** | Group similar-looking objects together | All red dots perceived as one group |
| **Closure** | Fill in gaps to see complete object | Circle with a bite taken out still looks like a circle |
| **Continuity** | Perceive smooth, continuous patterns | See a zigzag line, not two separate lines |

**Selective attention:** Focusing on one thing while ignoring others.

**Cocktail party effect:** Ability to filter out background noise but still hear your name mentioned.

**Inattentional blindness:** Failing to see visible objects when attention is elsewhere. Example: Gorilla in the basketball video.

**Change blindness:** Failing to notice changes in environment. Example: Person swapping while you watch a video.

**Depth perception:** Ability to see 3D space.

| Depth Cue | Type | Definition |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Retinal disparity** | Binocular | Each eye sees slightly different image. Greater difference = closer object. |
| **Convergence** | Binocular | Eyes turn inward as object gets closer. Brain senses muscle tension. |
| **Relative size** | Monocular | Smaller object appears farther away. |
| **Interposition (overlap)** | Monocular | Object that blocks another is closer. |
| **Relative clarity** | Monocular | Haze/detail loss = farther away. |
| **Texture gradient** | Monocular | Texture becomes finer/denser in distance. |
| **Linear perspective** | Monocular | Parallel lines converge at vanishing point. |
| **Relative height** | Monocular | Objects higher in visual field appear farther. |

**Perceptual constancy:** Perceiving objects as stable despite changes in sensory input.
- **Size constancy:** Car looks smaller far away, but you know it's still car-sized.
- **Shape constancy:** Door looks trapezoidal when open, but you know it's rectangular.
- **Color constancy:** Perceiving same color under different lighting.

**Visual illusions:** Demonstrate that perception is not always accurate.
- **Müller-Lyer illusion:** Arrows with inward vs outward fins. Culture influences perception (carpentered world hypothesis).
- **Ponzo illusion:** Two horizontal lines on railroad tracks. Top line appears longer (linear perspective).
- **Ames room:** Distorted room makes people look giant or tiny.

## 2.2 Thinking, Problem-Solving, Judgments, and Decision-Making (Full Detail)

**Concepts:** Mental categories grouping similar objects, events, or people.

**Prototype:** Best example of a concept. For "bird," your prototype might be a robin or sparrow (not a penguin or ostrich).

**Schemas (review from 2.1):** Frameworks for organizing information.
- **Assimilation:** Adding new info to existing schema without changing it. Child sees a dog → calls it "dog" → sees a cat → calls it "dog" (assimilating cat into dog schema).
- **Accommodation:** Changing schema to incorporate new info. Child learns cats are different → creates new "cat" schema.

**Problem-solving strategies:**

| Strategy | Definition | Pros/Cons |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Algorithm** | Step-by-step procedure that guarantees a solution | Time-consuming but certain |
| **Heuristic** | Mental shortcut that doesn't guarantee solution but is faster | Quick but error-prone |
| **Trial and error** | Trying random solutions until one works | Inefficient but can find novel solutions |
| **Insight** | Sudden "aha!" realization of solution | Often occurs after incubation period |

**Heuristics (specific types you MUST know):**
- **Representativeness heuristic:** Judging probability by how much something matches a stereotype. "He wears glasses and reads books; he must be a librarian" (ignores that there are far more farmers than librarians).
- **Availability heuristic:** Judging probability by how easily examples come to mind. "More words start with R than have R as the third letter" (false; you can think of more words that start with R, so it seems true).

**Cognitive biases that cause errors:**

| Bias | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Confirmation bias** | Seeking evidence that confirms beliefs, ignoring contradictory evidence | Only reading news sources that agree with you |
| **Hindsight bias** | "I knew it all along" phenomenon after learning the outcome | After election, saying you knew the winner all along |
| **Overconfidence** | Overestimating the accuracy of our knowledge/judgments | 82% of drivers think they're above average |
| **Belief perseverance** | Clinging to beliefs even when evidence disproves them | Still believing something after it's been proven false |
| **Framing effect** | Decisions influenced by how options are presented | "90% survival rate" vs "10% death rate" (same, but different choices) |
| **Gambler's fallacy** | Believing past events affect independent random events | After 5 coin flips of heads, believing tails is "due" |
| **Sunk-cost fallacy** | Continuing with something because of past investment | Staying in bad relationship because "we've been together 3 years" |

**Mental set:** Tendency to approach problems using prior successful strategies (can hinder creativity).

**Functional fixedness:** Inability to see new uses for familiar objects. The candle problem (boxes of thumbtacks).

**Executive functions:** Cognitive processes for goal-directed behavior: planning, inhibition, task switching, working memory, decision-making.

**Creativity:** Generating novel, useful ideas.
- **Divergent thinking:** Generating many possible solutions (brainstorming). Associated with creativity.
- **Convergent thinking:** Narrowing down to single best solution. Associated with intelligence tests.
- Hindered by **functional fixedness** and **mental set.**

## 2.3 Introduction to Memory (Full Detail)

**Three basic processes:** Encoding (getting info in), Storage (keeping info), Retrieval (getting info out).

**Types of memory:**

| Type | Subtype | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Explicit (Declarative)** | Episodic | Personal experiences/events | Your 10th birthday party |
| | Semantic | Facts/knowledge | Paris is capital of France |
| **Implicit (Non-declarative)** | Procedural | Skills/habits | Riding a bike, typing |
| | Classical conditioning | Conditioned responses | Fear of dentist |
| | Priming | Prior exposure influences response | Completing "so_p" as "soap" after seeing "soap" earlier |
| **Prospective memory** | Remembering to do future actions | Taking medication, attending appointment |

**Biological basis of memory:**
- **Long-term potentiation (LTP):** Repeated stimulation strengthens neural connections (synaptic plasticity). "Neurons that fire together wire together."
- **Hippocampus:** Critical for forming new explicit memories. Damage = anterograde amnesia.
- **Cerebellum:** Critical for procedural memories (classical conditioning of eye blink, motor skills).
- **Amygdala:** Emotional memories (fear conditioning).
- **Basal ganglia:** Procedural memories (habit formation).

**Memory models:**

**1. Multi-store model (Atkinson-Shiffrin):**

| Store | Duration | Capacity | Encoding | Forgetting |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sensory memory** | <1 sec (iconic), <4 sec (echoic) | All sensory input | Raw sensory info | Decay |
| **Short-term memory (STM)** | ~20-30 sec without rehearsal | 7 ± 2 items (Miller) | Acoustic (sound) | Decay, interference |
| **Long-term memory (LTM)** | Potentially permanent | Unlimited | Semantic (meaning) | Retrieval failure |

- **Iconic memory (visual):** Lasts <1 second. Sperling's partial report experiment demonstrated capacity and rapid decay.
- **Echoic memory (auditory):** Lasts 3-4 seconds.

**2. Working memory model (Baddeley & Hitch):** Updates STM as an active processing system.

| Component | Function |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Central executive** | Director, allocates attention, coordinates other systems |
| **Phonological loop** | Verbal/auditory info (inner voice) |
| **Visuospatial sketchpad** | Visual/spatial info (inner eye) |
| **Episodic buffer** (added later) | Integrates info from other systems into integrated episodes |

**3. Levels of processing model (Craik & Lockhart):** Deeper processing = better memory.

| Level | Type | Example | Retention |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Shallow | **Structural** (physical appearance) | Is the word in CAPITALS? | Poor |
| Intermediate | **Phonemic** (sound) | Does it rhyme with "late"? | Medium |
| Deep | **Semantic** (meaning) | Would it be used in a courtroom? | Best |

## 2.4 Encoding Memories (Full Detail)

**Automatic processing:** Unconscious encoding of incidental information (time, space, frequency). No effort required.

**Effortful processing:** Conscious, deliberate encoding that requires attention and effort.

**Mnemonic devices:** Memory aids that organize information.

| Mnemonic | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Method of loci** | Visualizing items in familiar locations | Imagine to-do list items placed around your house |
| **Peg-word system** | Rhyming words as "pegs" to hang info | One = bun, two = shoe. Visualize bun with first item |
| **Acronyms** | First letters form a word | ROYGBIV (colors of rainbow) |
| **Acrostics** | First letters form a sentence | Please Excuse My Dear Aunt Sally (order of operations) |
| **Chunking** | Organizing items into familiar groups | 1-9-9-4-0-2-2-4 becomes 1994-02-24 |
| **Hierarchies** | Organizing into broad categories and subcategories | Animal → Mammal → Primate → Human |

**Spacing effect:** Distributed practice (studying over time) produces better long-term retention than massed practice (cramming).

**Testing effect:** Retrieval practice (taking tests) improves memory more than restudying.

**Serial position effect (Ebbinghaus):**

| Effect | Position | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primacy effect** | Beginning of list | More time to rehearse → transferred to LTM |
| **Recency effect** | End of list | Still in STM |

**Next-in-line effect:** Poor memory for what someone said right before your turn (attention shifts to self).

**Encoding specificity principle:** Retrieval is better when encoding and retrieval contexts match.

## 2.5 Storing Memories (Full Detail)

**Sensory memory:** Briefly holds sensory information. Iconic (<1 sec), Echoic (3-4 sec). Large capacity but very short duration.

**Short-term memory:** Holds small amount of information briefly. Capacity: 7±2 items. Duration: 20-30 seconds without rehearsal.

**Working memory:** Active manipulation of STM content. Central executive directs attention, phonological loop holds words, visuospatial sketchpad holds images.

**Long-term memory:** Unlimited capacity, potentially permanent duration.
- **Maintenance rehearsal:** Repeating information to keep it in STM (does not effectively transfer to LTM).
- **Elaborative rehearsal:** Connecting new information to existing knowledge (meaningful processing) effectively transfers to LTM.

**Amnesia:**

| Type | Definition | Cause |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Anterograde amnesia** | Cannot form new explicit memories | Damage to hippocampus (e.g., patient HM) |
| **Retrograde amnesia** | Cannot retrieve past memories | Head trauma, often recovers |
| **Infantile amnesia** | Cannot remember first 3-4 years of life | Immature hippocampus, language development |
| **Alzheimer's disease** | Progressive memory loss, cognitive decline | Plaques, tangles, acetylcholine depletion |

**Highly superior autobiographical memory (HSAM):** Extremely rare ability to recall every day of one's life in detail. Suggests biological basis for superior storage.

---

## 2.6 Retrieving Memories (Full Detail)

**Recall:** Retrieving information without cues (essay question, fill-in-the-blank). Harder.

**Recognition:** Identifying correct information from options (multiple choice). Easier.

**Retrieval cues:** Stimuli that help retrieve memories.
- **Context-dependent memory:** Easier to retrieve memories when in same physical environment as encoding. State-dependent learners: Studying underwater → better recall underwater.
- **State-dependent memory:** Easier to retrieve when same internal state (mood, drug state, arousal level).
- **Mood-congruent memory:** Easier to recall memories that match current mood. When sad, remember sad events.

**Retrieval practice (testing effect):** Actively retrieving information improves later recall more than restudying.

**Metacognition:** Awareness and understanding of one's own thought processes. Knowing what you know and don't know.

---

## 2.7 Forgetting and Other Memory Challenges (Full Detail)

**Ebbinghaus's forgetting curve:** Rapid initial forgetting, then levels off. Without rehearsal, most forgetting occurs within first hour.

**Reasons for forgetting:**

| Reason | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Encoding failure** | Info never entered LTM | What does a penny look like? |
| **Storage decay** | Memories fade over time | Forgetting high school French |
| **Retrieval failure** | Info is stored but cannot be accessed | Tip-of-the-tongue phenomenon |

**Interference theory:** Forgetting because other memories interfere.

| Type | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Proactive interference** | Old information blocks new information | Old password makes you forget new password |
| **Retroactive interference** | New information blocks old information | New phone number makes you forget old one |

**Repression (psychodynamic):** Unconsciously forgetting traumatic memories to defend ego. Controversial; many memory researchers doubt its existence.

**Memory errors/distortions:**

| Error | Definition | Researcher |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Misinformation effect** | Leading questions distort memory | Loftus (car crash study: "smashed" vs "hit" changed speed estimates and false memory of broken glass) |
| **Source amnesia (source misattribution)** | Remembering the fact but forgetting where you learned it | "I know it's true, but I don't remember who told me" |
| **Constructive memory** | Memories are built/constructed, not replayed exactly | Two witnesses to same event tell different stories |
| **Imagination inflation** | Imagining an event increases confidence it happened | Imagining breaking a window makes you think you actually did |
| **False memories** | Entirely fabricated memories that feel real | Lost in the mall technique (Loftus) implanted false childhood memory |
| **Flashbulb memories** | Vivid, detailed memories of shocking events (often inaccurate) | 9/11, JFK assassination. People are confident but often wrong |

**Eyewitness testimony problems:** Misinformation effect, weapon focus (weapon draws attention away from face), cross-race effect (harder to recognize faces of other races).

## 2.8 Intelligence and Achievement (Full Detail)

### Theories of Intelligence

| Theorist | Theory | Definition |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Spearman** | **g factor (general intelligence)** | Single underlying factor influences all cognitive abilities |
| | **s factor (specific abilities)** | Specific skills unique to particular tasks |
| **Thurstone** | **Primary mental abilities** | 7 distinct abilities (verbal comprehension, numerical, spatial, etc.) |
| **Guilford** | **Structure of intellect** | 150 distinct abilities (operations x content x products) |
| **Gardner** | **Multiple intelligences** | 8+ independent intelligences: linguistic, logical-mathematical, musical, spatial, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic. Possibly existential |
| **Sternberg** | **Triarchic theory** | 3 types: **Analytical** (academic problem-solving), **Creative** (novel tasks), **Practical** (everyday problems, "street smarts") |
| **Cattell** | **Fluid vs Crystallized** | **Fluid** (ability to reason quickly, declines with age). **Crystallized** (accumulated knowledge, stays stable) |
| **Goleman** | **Emotional intelligence** | Perceiving, understanding, managing, and using emotions |

**g factor** is supported by factor analysis: people who score high on one test tend to score high on all tests.

### Measuring Intelligence

**Early tests:**
- **Binet-Simon (1905):** First modern intelligence test. Identified French children needing extra help. Mental age concept.
- **Stanford-Binet (Terman):** American revision. Introduced IQ formula: (Mental Age / Chronological Age) × 100.

**Wechsler tests (WAIS, WISC, WPPSI):** Most widely used today. Gives Verbal IQ, Performance IQ, and Full Scale IQ. Uses deviation IQ (comparison to age group, not mental/chronological ratio).

**Psychometric properties (MUST KNOW):**

| Property | Definition | How measured |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Standardization** | Consistent testing procedures and norms | Representative sample establishes normal distribution (mean = 100, SD = 15 for most tests) |
| **Reliability** | Consistency of results | **Test-retest reliability** (same results over time). **Split-half reliability** (correlation between two halves of test) |
| **Validity** | Test measures what it claims to measure | **Content validity** (covers domain). **Construct validity** (measures intended construct). **Predictive validity** (predicts outcomes like job performance) |

**Flynn Effect:** IQ scores have been rising ~3 points per decade. Causes: better nutrition, healthcare, education, test-taking skills, environmental complexity.

**Group differences in IQ:**
- IQ scores vary more **within** groups than **between** groups.
- Average differences exist between racial/ethnic groups (controversial, largely explained by environmental factors).
- **Stereotype threat (Steele & Aronson):** Fear of confirming a negative stereotype about your group impairs performance. Can explain some group differences.
- **Stereotype lift:** Performance boost from knowing your group is stereotyped as good at something.

**Uses/misuses of intelligence tests:**
- Positives: Identify students needing services (gifted, learning disabilities).
- Negatives: Used historically to limit immigration (Ellis Island), sterilization (eugenics), military placement, educational tracking.
- **Achievement tests:** Measure what you already learned (AP exam).
- **Aptitude tests:** Predict future performance (SAT, ACT).

**Mindset (Dweck):**
- **Fixed mindset:** Intelligence is static, unchanging. Avoid challenges, give up easily.
- **Growth mindset:** Intelligence can grow with effort. Embrace challenges, persist through setbacks.

## 3.1 Themes and Methods in Developmental Psychology

**Three major themes/questions:**

| Theme | Question |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Nature vs Nurture** | Is development primarily genetic or environmental? |
| **Stability vs Change** | Do early traits persist or do people change over time? |

**Research designs in developmental psychology:**

| Design | Method | Pros | Cons |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Cross-sectional** | Different ages tested at same time | Quick, cheap, no attrition | Cohort effects (differences due to generation, not age) |
| **Longitudinal** | Same group tested repeatedly over time | No cohort effects, tracks individual change | Expensive, time-consuming, attrition (dropout) |
| **Sequential** | Multiple cross-sectional studies over time | Combines best of both | Complex, expensive |

## 3.2 Physical Development Across the Lifespan

**Prenatal development:**
- **Teratogens:** Harmful agents (alcohol, drugs, viruses, radiation) that cause birth defects.
- **Fetal Alcohol Syndrome (FAS):** Caused by prenatal alcohol exposure. Symptoms: facial abnormalities, small head, intellectual disability, behavioral problems.

> **Exclusion:** Stages of prenatal development (zygote, embryo, fetus) are NOT tested.

**Infancy/childhood reflexes:**

| Reflex | Stimulus | Response | Disappears |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Rooting** | Touch cheek | Turn head, open mouth to suck | ~3-4 months |
| **Sucking** | Object in mouth | Suck rhythmically | Persists |
| **Moro (startle)** | Sudden noise or loss of support | Arch back, throw arms out, bring in | ~6 months |
| **Babinski** | Stroke sole of foot | Toes fan out | ~1 year (adults toes curl) |
| **Grasping** | Touch palm | Grip tightly | ~5-6 months |

**Motor development:** Proceeds in orderly sequence (cephalocaudal = head to toe, proximodistal = center to periphery).
- Typical sequence: Lift head → roll over → sit unsupported → crawl → walk (around 12 months). Timing varies, order consistent.

**Visual cliff (Gibson & Walk):** Infants will not crawl over apparent drop-off. Demonstrates depth perception develops by the time baby can crawl (~6-8 months).

**Critical periods:** Specific time window when certain skills must develop. Example: Language exposure needed in early childhood. If not, permanent deficits (Genie the feral child).

**Imprinting (Lorenz):** Critical period shortly after birth when some animals (geese) form attachment to first moving object they see.

**Adolescence:**
- **Puberty:** Sexual maturation, primary and secondary sex characteristics.
- **Menarche:** First menstrual period (females).
- **Spermarche:** First ejaculation (males).
- **Adolescent growth spurt:** Rapid increase in height/weight. Females start ~2 years earlier than males.

**Adulthood:**
- Physical peak in 20s-30s. Gradual decline: sensory acuity, reaction time, muscle strength, flexibility, fertility (women → menopause ~50s).
- Primary aging: inevitable biological decline. Secondary aging: preventable decline from lifestyle (exercise, diet, smoking).

## 3.3 Gender and Sexual Orientation

**Sex:** Biological categories (male, female, intersex). Chromosomes (XX, XY), hormones, anatomy.

**Gender:** Socially constructed roles, behaviors, expressions, identities.

**Gender identity:** One's internal sense of being male, female, neither, both, etc.

**Gender role:** Cultural expectations for behavior based on perceived sex.

**Gender socialization:** Process of learning gender roles. Parents, peers, schools, media.

**Sexual orientation:** Pattern of romantic/sexual attraction (heterosexual, homosexual, bisexual, asexual, etc.). Not a choice.

No single cause: combination of genetic, hormonal (prenatal androgen exposure), and environmental factors.

## 3.4 Cognitive Development Across the Lifespan

### Piaget's Stage Theory

| Stage | Age | Major achievements | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sensorimotor** | 0-2 years | **Object permanence** (understanding objects exist when out of sight). Develops around 8 months. Goal-directed behavior, cause-effect understanding. | No symbolic thought, no object permanence early |
| **Preoperational** | 2-7 years | Symbolic thinking (pretend play, language). **Theory of mind** (understanding others have different perspectives) begins. | **Egocentrism:** Cannot see other's perspective (Three mountains task). **Animism:** Attributing life to inanimate objects. **Lack conservation:** Cannot understand quantity stays same despite appearance change (conservation of liquid, number, volume). **Irreversibility:** Cannot mentally reverse operations. |
| **Concrete operational** | 7-11 years | **Conservation** (understands). **Reversibility** (can reverse mentally). **Classification** (hierarchical categories). **Seriation** (order items logically). | Cannot think abstractly or hypothetically |
| **Formal operational** | 11+ years | Abstract reasoning, hypothetical thinking, deductive logic (comes up with all possible combinations). Piaget said not all adults achieve this. | |

**Schemas:** Mental frameworks. **Assimilation:** Incorporate new info into existing schema. **Accommodation:** Modify schema to fit new info.

**Egocentrism examples:**
- Three mountains task: Child cannot describe what doll sees.
- Animism: "The sun is following me because it's happy."
- Egocentric speech: Talking to self without taking listener's perspective.

**Criticisms of Piaget:**
- Underestimated children's abilities (conservation may appear earlier with simpler tasks).
- Stage transitions are more gradual.
- Cultural differences (some cultures don't reach formal operational thinking).

### Vygotsky's Sociocultural Theory

**Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD):** Gap between what child can do alone vs with guidance.

**Scaffolding:** Temporary support from more knowledgeable other (parent, teacher, peer). Gradually removed as child becomes independent.

**Private speech:** Talking to oneself to guide thinking. For Vygotsky: essential step in development (not egocentric).

**Key difference from Piaget:** Piaget emphasized child as independent explorer. Vygotsky emphasized social interaction and cultural tools.

### Cognitive changes in adulthood

| Type | Definition | Trajectory |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Crystallized intelligence** | Accumulated knowledge, vocabulary, facts | Remains stable or increases with age |
| **Fluid intelligence** | Ability to reason quickly, solve novel problems, process information | Declines in late adulthood |
| **Processing speed** | How quickly mental operations performed | Declines with age |
| **Working memory capacity** | Holding/manipulating info temporarily | Declines with age |
| **Dementia** | Progressive cognitive decline (not normal aging) | Most common: Alzheimer's disease |

## 3.5 Communication and Language Development

**Components of language:**

| Component | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Phoneme** | Smallest unit of sound | /b/, /p/, /ch/ (English has ~44) |
| **Morpheme** | Smallest unit of meaning | "Dog" (1), "Dogs" (2: dog + s) |
| **Semantics** | Meaning of words and sentences | Understanding "He kicked the bucket" |
| **Syntax** | Rules for combining words | "Dog bites man" vs "Man bites dog" |
| **Grammar** | Entire system of rules | |
| **Generativity** | Finite rules create infinite possibilities | Never heard "purple dogs fly underwater" before but understand it |

**Stages of language development (universal across cultures):**

| Stage | Age | Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Crying/cooing** | 0-4 months | Different cries for different needs. Cooing (vowel sounds) |
| **Babbling** | 4-12 months | Consonant-vowel combinations ("ba-ba-ba"). Deaf babies babble with hands |
| **One-word (holophrastic)** | 12-18 months | Single words convey full sentences ("Milk!" = I want milk) |
| **Telegraphic (two-word)** | 18-24 months | Two-word phrases with essential meaning ("Daddy go," "More cookie") |
| **Early sentences** | 2-3 years | Longer, grammatical errors (overregularization: "I goed to store") |
| **Adult-like speech** | 5+ years | Complex sentences, vocabulary grows rapidly |

**Overregularization:** Applying grammatical rules to exceptions. "I runned" instead of "I ran." Shows learning rules, not just imitating.

**Theories of language development:**

| Theorist | Theory | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Skinner (behaviorist)** | Learning theory | Language learned through imitation, reinforcement, and association |
| **Chomsky (nativist)** | Universal grammar | Humans born with **Language Acquisition Device (LAD)**. Explains rapid learning, overregularization, and that all languages share universal grammar structure |
| **Interactionist** | Social interaction | Language emerges from interplay of biological readiness and social environment. Vygotsky's ZPD |

**Sensitive period for language:** First 5-7 years. Children learn languages effortlessly. After puberty, much harder (accent, fluency deficits). Evidence: Genie (feral child), second language learners.

## 3.6 Social-Emotional Development Across the Lifespan

### Attachment Theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth)

**Attachment:** Emotional bond between infant and caregiver.

**Harlow's monkey studies:** Infant monkeys preferred cloth mother (comfort) to wire mother (food). Demonstrated contact comfort > feeding for attachment.

**Ainsworth's Strange Situation:** Lab procedure measuring attachment quality.

| Attachment style | % (US) | Child behavior | Caregiver pattern |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Secure** | ~60% | Explores when caregiver present, distressed when leaves, happy on return, calmed by caregiver | Sensitive, responsive, consistent |
| **Insecure-avoidant** | ~15% | Ignores caregiver, little distress when leaves, ignores on return, treats stranger like caregiver | Rejecting, distant, unresponsive to distress |
| **Insecure-anxious (resistant)** | ~10% | Clingy, distressed when leaves, ambivalent on return (seeks comfort then pushes away), not easily calmed | Inconsistent (sometimes responsive, sometimes not) |
| **Disorganized** | ~15% | Confused, contradictory behavior (freezing, rocking). No coherent strategy | Frightening, abusive, or neglectful |

**Secure attachment** predicts better outcomes: social competence, emotional regulation, resilience.

**Temperament:** Biologically based individual differences in emotional reactivity and self-regulation. Present from birth. Easy, difficult, slow-to-warm-up (Thomas & Chess).

### Parenting Styles (Baumrind, Maccoby & Martin)

Two dimensions: **Warmth** (acceptance vs rejection) and **Control** (demanding vs undemanding).

| Parent Style | Warmth | Control | Child outcomes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Authoritative** | High | High (appropriate limits) | **Best outcomes:** Self-reliant, socially competent, high self-esteem, good grades |
| **Authoritarian** | Low | High (strict obedience) | Anxious, withdrawn, lower self-esteem, less social competence |
| **Permissive** | High | Low (few rules) | Impulsive, low self-control, entitlement, poor boundaries |
| **Uninvolved/Neglectful** | Low | Low | Worst outcomes: behavioral problems, poor social skills, low self-esteem |

**Important:** Parenting styles must consider cultural context. Authoritarian parenting not harmful in some cultures.

### Ecological Systems Theory (Bronfenbrenner)

| System | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Microsystem** | Direct environment | Family, school, peers, daycare |
| **Mesosystem** | Interactions between microsystems | Parent-teacher conference, home-school connection |
| **Exosystem** | Indirect environment affecting child | Parent's workplace, community resources |
| **Macrosystem** | Cultural values, laws, economics | Individualism vs collectivism, poverty, political system |
| **Chronosystem** | Time/historical context | Growing up during pandemic vs before |

### Erikson's Psychosocial Stages

| Stage | Age | Crisis | Positive resolution |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1 | 0-1.5 | Trust vs Mistrust | Trust if needs met reliably |
| 2 | 1.5-3 | Autonomy vs Shame/Doubt | Independent with self-control |
| 3 | 3-5 | Initiative vs Guilt | Take initiative, plan activities |
| 4 | 5-12 | Industry vs Inferiority | Competence through school/social success |
| 5 | 12-18 | **Identity vs Role Confusion** | Coherent sense of self (most important for teens) |
| 6 | 18-40 | Intimacy vs Isolation | Form close relationships |
| 7 | 40-65 | Generativity vs Stagnation | Guide next generation (parenting, mentoring) |
| 8 | 65+ | Integrity vs Despair | Look back with satisfaction vs regret |

> **Exclusion:** Freud's psychosexual stages are NOT tested.

### Adolescent Social Development

**Identity formation (Marcia):** Two dimensions: Crisis (exploration) and Commitment.

| Status | Crisis | Commitment | Description |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Diffusion** | No | No | No direction, apathetic |
| **Foreclosure** | No | Yes | Accepted parent/authority values without exploration |
| **Moratorium** | Yes | No | Actively exploring, not yet committed |
| **Achievement** | Yes | Yes | Explored and committed |

**Imaginary audience (Elkind):** Adolescent egocentrism; belief that everyone is watching and judging them.

**Personal fable:** "It won't happen to me" invincibility. Leads to risk-taking.

**Possible selves:** Future-oriented self-representations (hoped-for, feared selves).

**ACEs (Adverse Childhood Experiences):** Traumatic events before age 18. Cumulative ACEs predict poorer physical and mental health outcomes.

### Adulthood Social Development

**Social clock:** Culturally expected timing of life events (marriage, children, career). Departure can cause stress.

**Emerging adulthood (Arnett):** 18-25, distinct period in some cultures. Identity exploration, instability, self-focus, feeling "in-between."

**Intimacy:** Close relationships. Childhood attachment style predicts adult romantic attachment.

**Generativity:** Concern for next generation. Midlife. Raising children, mentoring, contributing to community.

## 3.7 Classical Conditioning (Full Detail)

**Pavlov's experiment:** Dogs salivated (UCR) to food (UCS). Neutral stimulus (bell) paired with food. After pairing, bell alone (CS) caused salivation (CR).

### Vocabulary (MUST KNOW)

| Term | Abbrev | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Unconditioned stimulus** | UCS | Naturally triggers response without learning | Food |
| **Unconditioned response** | UCR | Natural automatic response to UCS | Salivation to food |
| **Neutral stimulus** | NS | Does not trigger response before conditioning | Bell |
| **Conditioned stimulus** | CS | Previously neutral stimulus that now triggers response | Bell after pairing |
| **Conditioned response** | CR | Learned response to CS | Salivation to bell |

**Acquisition:** Initial learning of the association. CS must be presented before UCS (forward pairing).

**Extinction:** CS presented without UCS; CR decreases and eventually stops.

**Spontaneous recovery:** After extinction, CS presented again → CR reappears (weakened).

**Generalization:** CR occurs to stimuli similar to CS. Different bell → salivation.

**Discrimination:** CR does NOT occur to stimuli different from CS. No salivation to buzzer.

**Higher-order conditioning:** CS1 (bell) → CR. Pair new NS (light) with CS1 → light alone becomes CS2 → CR. Weaker than first-order.

**Applications:**
- **Counterconditioning:** Replace fear response with relaxation. Systematic desensitization (phobia treatment).
- **Taste aversion (Garcia):** One-trial learning. Biological preparedness: Rats learn to avoid poisoned food (not just any stimulus). Evolutionary adaptive.
- **Conditioned emotional responses (Watson):** Little Albert learned fear of rat (UCS = loud noise, CS = rat, UCR/CR = fear). Demonstrated emotional conditioning in humans (unethical by today's standards).

**Habituation:** Decreased response to repeated stimulus (not conditioning — no new association. You stop noticing background noise).

> **Exclusion:** Delayed, trace, simultaneous, backward conditioning, expectancy theory NOT tested.

---

## 3.8 Operant Conditioning (Full Detail)

**B.F. Skinner:** Behavior operates on environment; consequences determine future behavior.

**Law of Effect (Thorndike):** Behaviors followed by satisfying consequences are strengthened (stamped in). Unpleasant consequences weaken behavior (stamped out).

### Reinforcement and Punishment (MUST MEMORIZE EXACTLY)

| Term | Effect on behavior | Type | Operation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Positive reinforcement** | INCREASES | Add something desirable | Give candy for cleaning room |
| **Negative reinforcement** | INCREASES | Remove something aversive | Stop nagging when room is clean |
| **Positive punishment** | DECREASES | Add something aversive | Spanking for bad behavior |
| **Negative punishment** | DECREASES | Remove something desirable | Take away phone for bad behavior |

**Common confusion prevention:**
- "Positive" = adding something. "Negative" = removing something.
- Reinforcement = INCREASES behavior. Punishment = DECREASES behavior.
- **Negative reinforcement is NOT punishment.** Negative reinforcement INCREASES behavior (by removing bad thing).

**Primary reinforcers:** Innately reinforcing (food, water, warmth, sex, relief from pain). No learning needed.

**Secondary (conditioned) reinforcers:** Learn to value them through association with primary reinforcers (money, grades, praise, gold stars).

**Reinforcement schedules:**

| Schedule | Description | Pattern | Example | Resistance to extinction |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Continuous** | Reinforce every correct response | Fast acquisition | Praise every time child says "please" | Lowest |
| **Fixed ratio (FR)** | Reinforce after fixed number of responses | High rate, post-reinforcement pause | $10 for every 10 shirts sewn | Medium |
| **Variable ratio (VR)** | Reinforce after unpredictable number of responses | Highest, most consistent rate, no pause | Slot machine, gambling | **Highest** |
| **Fixed interval (FI)** | Reinforce after fixed time, first response | Scalloped graph (crescendo before time) | Weekly quiz: study right before | Low |
| **Variable interval (VI)** | Reinforce after unpredictable time | Slow, steady response rate | Pop quizzes, checking email | Medium |

**Shaping:** Reinforcing successive approximations toward target behavior. Used for complex behaviors (teaching dog to roll over, child to speak).

**Instinctive drift (Breland & Breland):** Trained animals revert to innate behaviors. Raccoons "wash" food despite training. Shows biological constraints on conditioning.

**Superstitious behavior (Skinner):** Accidentally reinforced behavior produces superstitious rituals. Pigeons turned, bowed, etc. thinking it caused food.

**Learned helplessness (Seligman):** Dogs given unavoidable shocks stopped trying to escape later when escape was possible. Model for human depression (perceived lack of control).

**Application:**
- **Token economies:** Secondary reinforcers (tokens) exchanged for primary reinforcers. Used in schools, prisons, psychiatric hospitals.

**Classical vs Operant Conditioning (MUST DISTINGUISH):**

| | Classical | Operant |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Behavior** | Involuntary, reflexive (salivation, fear) | Voluntary, emitted (studying, working) |
| **Association** | Between two stimuli (CS-UCS) | Between behavior and consequence |
| **Respondent** | Response elicited by stimulus | Response emitted by organism |
| **Example** | Pavlov's dogs | Skinner's rats |
| **Key figure** | Pavlov | Skinner |

---

## 3.9 Social, Cognitive, and Neurological Factors in Learning

**Observational learning (Bandura):** Learning by watching others (models).

**Bobo doll experiment:** Children watched adult hit doll → children imitated aggressive behavior. Demonstrated learning without direct reinforcement.

**Vicarious conditioning:** Learning consequences by watching others get reinforced/punished.

**Model characteristics that increase imitation:** Similarity, prestige, power, competence, warmth.

**Cognitive factors in learning (Tolman):**

| Concept | Definition | Evidence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Latent learning** | Learning that occurs without reinforcement but is not immediately demonstrated | Rats ran maze without reward; when reward introduced, they performed as well as rats always rewarded (had learned cognitive map) |
| **Cognitive map** | Mental representation of physical space | Rats navigate maze without trial-and-error |
| **Insight learning** | Sudden "aha" realization of solution (Köhler's chimpanzees) | Chimps stacked boxes to reach banana, suddenly solved problem |

**Biological constraints on learning:**
- **Preparedness:** Some associations learned more easily (taste aversion, fear of snakes/spiders).
- **Contrapreparedness:** Some associations very difficult (pigeons conditioned to avoid food when sick — they associate illness with taste, not visual cue).

# UNIT 4: SOCIAL PSYCHOLOGY AND PERSONALITY (Complete Detail)

## 4.1 Attribution Theory and Person Perception

**Attribution theory (Heider, Kelley):** How people explain causes of behavior.

**Two types:**
- **Dispositional (internal) attribution:** Behavior caused by personality, traits, ability. "He's lazy."
- **Situational (external) attribution:** Behavior caused by environment, circumstances. "The traffic was bad."

**Attribution biases:**
- **Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE):** Overestimate dispositional causes for OTHERS' behavior, underestimate situational. You trip = "there's a crack in the sidewalk." They trip = "they're clumsy."
- **Actor-observer bias:** Attribute own behavior to situation, others' behavior to disposition.
- **Self-serving bias:** Attribute success to internal factors, failure to external factors. "I passed because I'm smart. I failed because the test was unfair."
- **Optimistic explanatory style:** Positive events = internal, stable, global. Negative events = external, temporary, specific.
- **Pessimistic explanatory style:** Positive events = external, temporary, specific. Negative events = internal, stable, global.

**Locus of control (Rotter):**
- **Internal locus:** You control your outcomes (effort, decisions).
- **External locus:** Fate, luck, others control outcomes.

**Person perception:**
- **Mere exposure effect (Zajonc):** Repeated exposure to stimulus increases liking. Works for faces, songs, products.
- **Self-fulfilling prophecy:** Expectation causes behavior that makes expectation come true. Teacher expects student to be smart → pays more attention → student does better.
- **Social comparison (Festinger):** Evaluate ourselves by comparing to others. **Upward comparison** = comparing to better (motivation or envy). **Downward comparison** = comparing to worse (feeling better about self).
- **Relative deprivation:** Feeling worse off compared to others, even if objectively okay.

---

## 4.2 Attitude Formation and Attitude Change

**Attitude:** Evaluation (positive or negative) toward something.

**Components of attitude (ABC):** Affective (emotion), Behavioral (action), Cognitive (belief).

**Stereotype:** Generalized belief about a group. Can be positive or negative. Cognitive component.
**Prejudice:** Negative attitude (feeling) toward group based on stereotype. Affective component.
**Discrimination:** Negative behavior toward group. Behavioral component.

**Implicit attitudes:** Unconscious, automatic evaluations. Measured with **Implicit Association Test (IAT)**.

**Explicit attitudes:** Conscious, controlled evaluations.

**Biases in group perception:**
- **In-group bias:** Favoring your own group.
- **Out-group homogeneity bias:** Perceiving out-group members as more similar to each other than in-group members ("They all look alike").
- **Just-world hypothesis:** Belief that world is fair; people get what they deserve (leads to blaming victims).
- **Ethnocentrism:** Believing your culture is superior to others.

**Attitude change:**
- **Belief perseverance:** Clinging to belief despite contradictory evidence.
- **Confirmation bias:** Seeking evidence that confirms beliefs.
- **Cognitive dissonance (Festinger):** Discomfort from conflicting attitudes/behaviors. Motivated to reduce by changing attitude or behavior.

**Cognitive dissonance classic study:** Participants paid $1 or $20 to lie about boring task being interesting. $1 group rated task as more fun (changed attitude to justify lying). $20 group had external justification, so attitude didn't change.

**Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM) of persuasion:**

| Route | Process | Likelihood of change | Persistence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Central route** | Careful thinking, evaluation of arguments | High | Long-lasting |
| **Peripheral route** | Superficial cues (attractiveness, celebrity, length) | Low | Temporary |

**Halo effect:** One positive trait influences perception of other traits (attractive people assumed smarter, kinder).

**Persuasion techniques:**
- **Foot-in-the-door:** Small request → then large request (compliance increases).
- **Door-in-the-face:** Large request (rejected) → then smaller actual request (compliance increases).
- **Lowballing:** Get agreement, then increase cost (car sales).

## 4.3 Psychology of Social Situations

**Social norms:** Rules for acceptable behavior in group.

**Social roles:** Expected behavior for position in group. Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment: College students assigned prisoners/guards. Guards became abusive, prisoners became distressed. Study stopped early. Dramatically demonstrated power of social roles.

**Conformity (Asch):** Changing behavior to match group norms.

Asch's line experiment: Participants gave obviously wrong answer because group did. Factors increasing conformity: group size (3-5 optimal), unanimity, public response, collectivist culture, low self-esteem.

**Normative social influence:** Conform to gain approval/avoid rejection.

**Informational social influence:** Conform because assume others know more.

**Obedience (Milgram):** Following orders from authority figure.

Milgram's shock experiment: "Teacher" shocked "learner" (confederate) when wrong. 65% went to 450 volts (lethal label). Factors increasing obedience: authority proximity, legitimate authority, remote victim, no role model for defiance.

**Group phenomena:**
- **Social facilitation:** Presence of others improves performance on easy tasks, impairs performance on difficult tasks (Zajonc: arousal strengthens dominant response).
- **Social loafing (Ringelmann):** Put less effort in group than alone (pulling rope). Less in larger groups, tasks with pooled outcomes.
- **Deindividuation:** Loss of self-awareness in groups (mobs, riots, anonymous online). Aggression increases.
- **Group polarization:** Group discussion strengthens initial tendencies (risky shift if initially risk-prone).
- **Groupthink (Janis):** Group prioritizes harmony over critical thinking, leading to bad decisions (Bay of Pigs, Challenger).
- **Diffusion of responsibility:** Less likely to take action when others are present.
- **False consensus effect:** Overestimating how many share your beliefs/behaviors.

**Prosocial behavior:**
- **Altruism:** Helping with no apparent benefit or even cost to self.
- **Bystander effect (Latane & Darley):** Less likely to help when others present.
- **Kitty Genovese case (38 witnesses, no one helped initially) sparked research.
- **Steps to helping:** Notice → interpret as emergency → feel responsible → know how to help → act.
- **Social exchange theory:** Helping based on cost-benefit analysis.
- **Reciprocity norm:** Help those who help us.
- **Social responsibility norm:** Help those who need help (even if can't reciprocate).

**Superordinate goals:** Shared goals requiring cooperation reduce intergroup conflict (Sherif's Robbers Cave experiment).

**Social trap:** Individual self-interest leads to collective harm (Tragedy of the Commons).

## 4.4 Psychodynamic and Humanistic Theories of Personality

### Psychodynamic Theory (Freud)

**Unconscious mind:** Drives personality, contains repressed thoughts, wishes, conflicts.

**Personality structure:**
- **Id:** Present at birth. Pleasure principle. Instant gratification. Unconscious. "I want."
- **Ego:** Develops ~2-3. Reality principle. Mediates id and superego. "I plan."
- **Superego:** Develops ~5. Morality, conscience. Internalized rules. "I should."

**Defense mechanisms (MUST KNOW):**

| Mechanism | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Repression** | Unconsciously forgetting painful memories | No memory of childhood abuse |
| **Projection** | Attributing own unacceptable feelings to others | Cheater accuses partner of cheating |
| **Displacement** | Redirecting impulse to safer target | Angry at boss → kick dog |
| **Rationalization** | Creating logical excuses for irrational behavior | "I didn't study because the test isn't fair" |
| **Reaction formation** | Acting opposite of true feelings | Homophobe actually has repressed homosexual feelings |
| **Denial** | Refusing to accept reality | "I don't have a drinking problem" |
| **Regression** | Reverting to earlier developmental stage | Adult throws tantrum |
| **Sublimation** | Channeling unacceptable impulses into acceptable activities | Aggression → sports. Creativity from repressed desires |

**Projective tests:** Measure unconscious by presenting ambiguous stimuli.
- **Rorschach inkblot test:** Describe what you see. Low reliability/validity.
- **Thematic Apperception Test (TAT):** Tell story about ambiguous pictures.

> **Exclusion:** Freud's psychosexual stages are NOT tested.

### Humanistic Theory (Rogers, Maslow)

**Humanistic assumptions:** People are inherently good, strive for growth, free will.

**Self-concept:** Your perception of yourself.

**Congruence:** Match between self-concept and reality.

**Incongruence:** Mismatch → anxiety.

**Unconditional positive regard:** Acceptance regardless of behavior. Essential for healthy development.

**Conditional positive regard:** Acceptance only when meeting conditions. Leads to conditions of worth → incongruence.

**Self-actualizing tendency:** Innate drive to reach full potential.

> **Exclusion:** Maslow's hierarchy of needs is NOT tested.

## 4.5 Social-Cognitive and Trait Theories of Personality

### Social-Cognitive Theory (Bandura)

**Reciprocal determinism:** Behavior, internal factors (cognition), and environment mutually influence each other.

**Self-efficacy:** Belief in your ability to succeed. Influences motivation, persistence, resilience.

**Self-esteem:** Overall evaluation of self-worth.

**Locus of control (Rotter, also in 4.1):** Internal vs external.

### Trait Theories

**Trait:** Enduring characteristic that produces consistent behavior.

**Big Five (OCEAN) (McCrae & Costa):**

| Trait | Low score | High score |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **O**penness | Conventional, prefers routine | Curious, creative, open to new experiences |
| **C**onscientiousness | Disorganized, impulsive | Organized, responsible, self-disciplined |
| **E**xtraversion | Introverted (reserved, quiet) | Outgoing, social, energetic |
| **A**greeableness | Suspicious, uncooperative | Trusting, helpful, compassionate |
| **N**euroticism (Emotional Stability) | Emotionally stable, calm, resilient | Anxious, moody, insecure |

**Personality assessment:**
- **Self-report inventories (MMPI, NEO-PI-R):** Standardized questionnaires. Objective, reliable, valid. MMPI used for clinical diagnosis.
- **Projective tests (Rorschach, TAT):** Low reliability/validity.
- **Factor analysis:** Statistical technique to identify underlying traits (how Big Five identified).

**Person-situation debate (Mischel):** Traits don't always predict behavior (situation matters). Later resolution: Both matter; traits predict average behavior across situations.

## 4.6 Motivation

**Drive-reduction theory (Hull):** Physiological need creates drive (tension) → behavior to satisfy need → homeostasis.

**Homeostasis:** Body's tendency to maintain internal equilibrium.

**Arousal theory:** Seek optimal level of arousal (not too high, not too low).

**Yerkes-Dodson law:** Moderate arousal = optimal performance. Simple tasks: higher arousal OK. Complex tasks: lower arousal better.

**Self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan):** Three innate needs: competence, autonomy, relatedness.

**Intrinsic motivation:** Internal rewards (enjoyment, interest, satisfaction).

**Extrinsic motivation:** External rewards (money, grades, praise).

**Overjustification effect:** Extrinsic rewards can decrease intrinsic motivation (if already high interest).

**Incentive theory:** Behavior motivated by external rewards/incentives.

**Instincts:** Fixed action patterns in animals. Humans have few (if any) true instincts.

**Lewin's motivational conflicts:**

| Conflict | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Approach-approach** | Choose between two desirable options | Two good colleges |
| **Avoidance-avoidance** | Choose between two undesirable options | Study for boring test or get bad grade |
| **Approach-avoidance** | Single option with pros and cons | Job with high pay but long hours |

**Sensation-seeking (Zuckerman):** Need for varied, novel, complex experiences. Four components: thrill/adventure seeking, experience seeking, disinhibition, boredom susceptibility.

**Eating motivation:**
- **Ghrelin:** Hunger hormone (stomach).
- **Leptin:** Satiety hormone (fat cells). Signals fullness to hypothalamus.
- **Set point theory:** Body maintains genetically programmed weight.
- External factors: Food availability, social cues, time of day.

## 4.7 Emotion

**Emotion components:** Physiological arousal, expressive behaviors, conscious experience (feelings).

**Emotion theories:**

| Theory | Order | Explanation | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **James-Lange** | Physiological → Emotion | "I'm scared because my heart is pounding" | See bear → heart races → feel fear |
| **Cannon-Bard** | Simultaneous (physiological + emotion) | Thalamus sends signals simultaneously | See bear → heart races AND feel fear at same time |
| **Schachter-Singer (Two-factor)** | Physiological + Cognitive label → Emotion | Arousal + interpretation = emotion | See bear → heart races + interpret as "danger" → fear |
| **Zajonc-LeDoux** | Emotion before cognition (low road) | Some emotional responses bypass conscious thought | Fear before you know why |
| **Lazarus** | Cognitive appraisal first | Must interpret stimulus before emotion | Appraise bear as threat → fear |

**Facial feedback hypothesis (Ekman, Izard):** Facial expressions influence emotional experience. Smiling (even forced) makes you feel happier.

**Universality of emotion:** Six basic emotions recognized across cultures: anger, disgust, sadness, happiness, surprise, fear. Research mixed; some argue for additional.

**Display rules:** Cultural norms for emotional expression. Vary by culture, gender, age, social context.

**Broaden-and-build theory (Fredrickson):** Positive emotions broaden cognitive capacity and build resources. Negative emotions narrow focus.

**Emotional expression:**
- Nonverbal communication: facial expressions, body language, tone of voice.
- **Microexpressions:** Very brief (1/25 sec) facial expressions revealing true emotion.
- **Emotional contagion:** Catching others' emotions.

> **Exclusion:** Specific names of emotion theories (James-Lange, etc.) are NOT tested according to CED, but knowing them helps understand the distinctions. The CED says "specific names of theories of emotion are outside scope" but the differences are tested.

# UNIT 5: MENTAL AND PHYSICAL HEALTH (Complete Detail)

## 5.1 Introduction to Health Psychology

**Health psychology:** How psychological factors affect physical health and illness.

**Stress:** Response to perceived threat or challenge.

**Stressors:** Events triggering stress response.

| Type | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Eustress** | Positive, motivating stress (starting new job) |
| **Distress** | Negative, debilitating stress |
| **Traumatic stress** | Severe, life-threatening events (PTSD) |
| **Daily hassles** | Minor everyday stressors (traffic, deadlines). Cumulative effect significant |
| **ACEs (Adverse Childhood Experiences)** | Abuse, neglect, household dysfunction. Higher ACEs → poorer health outcomes |

**General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS) (Selye):** Three-stage physiological response to stress.

| Stage | Physiological response |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Alarm** | Sympathetic activation, fight-or-flight (cortisol, adrenaline release) |
| **Resistance** | Continued coping attempts, adaptation (prolonged arousal) |
| **Exhaustion** | Resource depletion, illness susceptibility, burnout |

**Tend-and-befriend (Taylor):** Alternative stress response (more common in women). Nurturing and seeking social support, not fighting or fleeing.

**Coping strategies:**

| Strategy | Definition | Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Problem-focused coping** | Address the stressor directly | Make plan, study more, talk to boss |
| **Emotion-focused coping** | Manage emotional response | Meditation, deep breathing, seek comfort, distraction |

**Health effects of stress:**
- Immune suppression (more colds, slower healing)
- Cardiovascular: hypertension, heart disease
- Gastrointestinal: ulcers, IBS
- Mental health: anxiety, depression

## 5.2 Positive Psychology

**Positive psychology (Seligman):** Study of human flourishing, happiness, well-being, strengths.

**Subjective well-being:** Person's evaluation of their life satisfaction and balance of positive/negative emotions.

**Gratitude interventions:** Regularly expressing gratitude increases well-being (gratitude journal, letters).

**Signature strengths (VIA classification):** 24 character strengths organized into 6 virtues: wisdom, courage, humanity, justice, temperance, transcendence. Using strengths increases happiness.

**Posttraumatic growth:** Positive psychological change after trauma (improved relationships, appreciation of life, personal strength, spiritual development, new possibilities).

**Resilience:** Ability to bounce back from adversity.

## 5.3 Explaining and Classifying Psychological Disorders

**Defining abnormality (no single definition, but these factors):**
- **Dysfunction:** Interferes with daily life.
- **Distress:** Causes emotional pain (to self or others).
- **Deviance:** Deviation from cultural norms (context matters).
- **Danger:** Harm to self or others (not always present).

**DSM (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders):** Published by American Psychiatric Association. Lists diagnostic criteria, prevalence, course. Currently DSM-5-TR (2022).

**ICD (International Classification of Diseases):** Published by WHO. Medical coding system including mental disorders.

**Pros of diagnosis:** Common language, research, treatment matching, validation.

**Cons of diagnosis:** Stigma, labeling effects, self-fulfilling prophecy, cultural bias.

**Perspectives on causes of disorders:**

| Perspective | Cause of disorder |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Biological** | Genetics, neurotransmitters, brain structure |
| **Psychodynamic** | Unconscious conflicts, early childhood experiences |
| **Behavioral** | Maladaptive learned associations (classical/operant) |
| **Cognitive** | Maladaptive thoughts, beliefs, cognitive distortions |
| **Humanistic** | Lack of unconditional positive regard, blocked growth |
| **Sociocultural** | Social inequity, discrimination, poverty, cultural factors |
| **Evolutionary** | Mismatch between ancestral and modern environment |

**Biopsychosocial model:** Integrative approach: biological + psychological + social factors.

**Diathesis-stress model:** Genetic vulnerability (diathesis) + environmental stressor = disorder.

**Eclectic approach:** Clinicians use multiple perspectives to treat.

## 5.4 Selection of Categories of Psychological Disorders

> **Note:** Only these specific disorders are tested. Do not memorize disorders not listed.

### Neurodevelopmental Disorders (onset in developmental period)

| Disorder | Key symptoms |
| :--- | :--- |
| **ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder)** | Inattention, hyperactivity, impulsivity. Symptoms present before age 12. |
| **Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)** | Persistent deficits in social communication/interaction, restricted/repetitive behaviors, interests, activities. Spectrum = wide variation in functioning. |

### Schizophrenia Spectrum

**Positive symptoms (excess/added):**
- **Delusions:** False beliefs. Types: persecution ("they're out to get me"), grandeur ("I'm Jesus"), reference (events personally directed), control (external force controlling thoughts/actions).
- **Hallucinations:** False perceptions (usually auditory: hearing voices).
- **Disorganized speech (word salad):** Incoherent, jumping between topics.
- **Disorganized motor behavior:** Catatonia (excitement or stupor).

**Negative symptoms (deficit/removed):**
- **Flat affect:** No emotional expression.
- **Alogia:** Reduced speech.
- **Avolition:** Lack of goal-directed behavior.
- **Anhedonia:** Inability to feel pleasure.
- **Catatonic stupor:** No movement, unresponsive.

**Causes:** Dopamine hypothesis (excess dopamine, especially positive symptoms). Genetics (80% heritability). Prenatal/environmental factors (birth complications, season of birth effect).

### Depressive Disorders

| Disorder | Duration | Key features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)** | At least 2 weeks | Depressed mood OR anhedonia (loss of pleasure) + 4+ other symptoms (sleep, appetite, fatigue, worthlessness, concentration, psychomotor changes, suicidal thoughts) |
| **Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia)** | At least 2 years | Chronic, less severe depression. Functioning not as impaired as MDD |

**Causes:** Neurotransmitters (serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine). Genetic (twin studies: ~40% heritability). Cognitive (Beck's negative triad: self, world, future). Learned helplessness (Seligman's animal model).

### Bipolar Disorders

| Disorder | Key features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Bipolar I** | At least one manic episode (7+ days, severe, usually hospitalization). Often with depressive episodes. |
| **Bipolar II** | Hypomanic episodes (less severe, 4+ days) + depressive episodes. No full manic episode. |

**Manic episode:** Elevated/irritable mood, increased energy, grandiosity, decreased need for sleep, rapid speech, racing thoughts, risky behavior.

### Anxiety Disorders

| Disorder | Key features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Specific phobia** | Intense fear of specific object/situation (acrophobia = heights, arachnophobia = spiders). Recognizes fear excessive. |
| **Agoraphobia** | Fear of places where escape difficult (public transport, open spaces, enclosed spaces, crowds, being outside home alone). |
| **Panic disorder** | Recurrent unexpected panic attacks (heart racing, sweating, shortness of breath, fear of dying/losing control). May develop agoraphobia. |
| **Social anxiety disorder (social phobia)** | Fear of negative evaluation, judgment, embarrassment in social situations. |
| **Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)** | Chronic excessive worry about multiple domains (health, work, family). Lasts 6+ months. Physical symptoms (muscle tension, fatigue, sleep disturbance). |
| **Culture-bound syndromes** | **Ataque de nervios** (Caribbean/Latino): uncontrollable crying, screaming, aggression, fainting. **Taijin kyofusho** (Japan): fear of offending/disgusting others with one's body or odor. |

**Causes:** Classical conditioning (learned fear), observational learning, overprotective parenting (GAD), neurotransmitter (GABA deficiency).

### Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders

| Disorder | Key features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **OCD** | **Obsessions** (intrusive, unwanted thoughts/images/urges). **Compulsions** (repetitive behaviors/mental acts to reduce anxiety). Common themes: contamination, symmetry, harm, forbidden thoughts. |
| **Hoarding disorder** | Persistent difficulty discarding possessions regardless of actual value. Causes clutter, impaired functioning. |

**Note difference:** OCD is about anxiety reduction. OCPD (personality disorder, Cluster C) is about perfectionism and control — not the same.

### Trauma and Stressor-Related Disorders

| Disorder | Key features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **PTSD (Posttraumatic Stress Disorder)** | Exposure to actual/threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence (direct, witnessed, or learning it happened to close person). Symptoms >1 month: intrusions (flashbacks, nightmares), avoidance (of reminders), negative mood/cognitions, hypervigilance/exaggerated startle, sleep disturbance. |

### Dissociative Disorders

| Disorder | Key features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Dissociative amnesia** | Inability to recall important autobiographical information (usually traumatic/ stressful). With or without fugue (purposeful wandering or confused travel). |
| **Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID)** | Disruption of identity with two or more distinct personality states (alters). Gaps in memory. Highly controversial. |

### Feeding and Eating Disorders

| Disorder | Key features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Anorexia nervosa** | Restriction of energy intake → significantly low body weight. Intense fear of weight gain. Disturbed body image (sees self as fat even when emaciated). Two subtypes: restricting (dieting/fasting) and binge-purge. |
| **Bulimia nervosa** | Recurrent episodes of binge eating (large amount + loss of control) AND compensatory behaviors (vomiting, laxatives, fasting, excessive exercise). Occurs at least once/week for 3 months. Typically normal weight or overweight. |

### Personality Disorders (3 clusters)

**General criteria:** Enduring, inflexible, pervasive pattern of inner experience/behavior deviating from culture. Onset adolescence/early adulthood. Causes distress/impairment.

| Cluster | Characteristics | Disorders |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **A: Odd/eccentric** | Suspicious, socially isolated, magical thinking | Paranoid, Schizoid, Schizotypal |
| **B: Dramatic/erratic** | Emotional, impulsive, dramatic | Antisocial, Histrionic, Narcissistic, Borderline |
| **C: Anxious/fearful** | Anxious, inhibited | Avoidant, Dependent, **Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder (OCPD)** |

**Important distinctions:**
- **Antisocial Personality Disorder (ASPD):** Disregard for/violation of others' rights, lack of remorse, deceitfulness, impulsivity, aggression. Must have conduct disorder before age 15.
- **Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD):** Instability in relationships, self-image, emotions. Intense fear of abandonment, impulsive, self-harm, suicidality.
- **OCPD vs OCD:** OCPD = personality (perfectionism, orderliness, control, no insight). OCD = anxiety disorder (obsessions/compulsions, ego-dystonic, insight present).

## 5.5 Treatment of Psychological Disorders

### General Principles

**Therapeutic alliance:** Collaborative, trusting relationship between therapist and client. Strong predictor of positive outcomes.

**Evidence-based interventions:** Treatments supported by research.

**Cultural humility:** Acknowledging and respecting cultural differences.

**Deinstitutionalization:** Movement from large mental hospitals to community-based care (1960s onward). Mixed outcomes: more freedom, but many homeless/unhoused mentally ill.

**Psychopharmacology revolution:** Antipsychotics, antidepressants, mood stabilizers allowed many patients to leave hospitals.

### Psychological Therapies by Perspective

**Psychodynamic therapy (Freud, modern variants):**
- **Goal:** Uncover unconscious conflicts.
- **Techniques:** Free association (say whatever comes to mind), dream interpretation, analysis of transference (projecting feelings onto therapist).
- **Duration:** Traditional = years. Modern psychodynamic = shorter-term.

**Humanistic therapy (Rogers): Person-centered therapy**
- **Goal:** Self-actualization, congruence.
- **Therapist role:** Provide **unconditional positive regard**, empathy, genuineness.
- **Techniques:** Active listening (restating, clarifying, reflecting), non-directive (client leads).
- **Outcomes:** Client does most work; therapist facilitates growth.

**Behavioral therapies (behaviorism): Action and learning focus**
- **Goal:** Change maladaptive behaviors (not focus on thoughts/feelings).
- **Applied Behavior Analysis:** Apply conditioning principles.

**Specific behavioral techniques:**
- **Systematic desensitization (phobias):** Hierarchy of fear + relaxation training. Counterconditioning. Developed by Wolpe.
- **Flooding:** Full-intensity exposure (no gradual hierarchy).
- **Aversion therapy:** Pair undesirable behavior with aversive stimulus (alcohol + nausea meds).
- **Token economy:** Reward desired behavior with tokens (exchangeable). Used in institutions, schools.
- **Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP):** OCD. Expose to trigger, prevent compulsion.

**Cognitive therapies (Beck, Ellis):**
- **Goal:** Change maladaptive thoughts.
- **Beck's Cognitive Therapy (for depression):** Cognitive triad (negative views of self, world, future). Identify/ challenge cognitive distortions (all-or-nothing, overgeneralization, catastrophizing).
- **Ellis's Rational Emotive Behavior Therapy (REBT):** ABCDE model: Activating event → Belief → Consequence → Dispute → Effect.

**Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT):**
- Most empirically supported therapy for many disorders.
- **Combines:** Cognitive restructuring + behavioral activation + exposure.
- **Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT - Linehan):** For BPD. Combines CBT + mindfulness + emotion regulation.

### Biomedical Therapies

**Psychopharmacology:**

| Category | Mechanism | Examples | Used for |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Antipsychotics** | Block dopamine (antagonists) | Haloperidol, chlorpromazine, clozapine | Schizophrenia (positive symptoms) |
| **Side effect:** Tardive dyskinesia (involuntary movements) from long-term use |
| **Antidepressants** | Increase serotonin/norepinephrine | SSRIs (Prozac, Zoloft, Paxil), SNRIs, MAOIs, tricyclics | Depression, anxiety, OCD |
| **Antianxiety (anxiolytics)** | Increase GABA activity | Benzodiazepines (Xanax, Valium), buspirone | Anxiety disorders |
| **Risk:** Dependence, withdrawal |
| **Mood stabilizers** | Unknown mechanism | Lithium, valproate | Bipolar disorder (prevents manic episodes) |

**Brain stimulation interventions:**
- **Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT):** Brief electrical current through brain while anesthetized. Most effective for severe, treatment-resistant depression. Memory side effects (retrograde/anterograde amnesia) usually temporary.
- **Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS):** Magnetic fields stimulate specific brain areas. Non-invasive, few side effects. For depression.
- **Psychosurgery (rare):** Lesioning specific brain areas. **Lobotomy** (popular 1930s-1950s, rarely performed now). Modern deep brain stimulation (Parkinson's, OCD).

### Other Therapies and Considerations

**Group therapy:** One or more therapists working with multiple clients. Advantages: support, feedback, social skills practice, cheaper than individual. Led to development of self-help groups (AA).

**Family therapy:** Views problems in family system context.

**Hypnosis:**
- **Effective for:** Pain management, anxiety.
- **Not effective for:** Memory retrieval (produces false memories), age regression.

**Ethical principles for therapists (APA Code):**
- **Nonmaleficence:** Do no harm.
- **Beneficence:** Do good; help others.
- **Fidelity:** Trust, responsibility.
- **Integrity:** Honesty, accuracy.
- **Justice:** Fair, equal treatment.
- **Respect for rights and dignity:** Confidentiality, informed consent.
