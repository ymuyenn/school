## LO 8.I: Explain the causes and effects of the Vietnam War.

**Thesis:** The Vietnam War was caused by **containment policy** (fear that fall of Vietnam would lead to communist domination of Southeast Asia – Domino Theory), **French defeat at Dien Bien Phu** (1954), **division of Vietnam at 17th parallel** (Geneva Accords 1954), **failure of elections** (South Vietnam refuses to hold reunification elections), and **Gulf of Tonkin incident** (1964); its effects included **58,000 US deaths**, **defeat of South Vietnam** (1975), **War Powers Act** (1973) limiting presidential authority, **the "Vietnam Syndrome"** (hesitancy to use military force), **loss of trust in government** (Pentagon Papers, My Lai), and **the end of the draft** (1973).

**Body Evidence:**

- **Causes – French colonial war (KC-8.1.D.ii):** France tries to reclaim Vietnam after WWII. Ho Chi Minh (communist, nationalist) leads Viet Minh resistance. Battle of Dien Bien Phu (March-May 1954) – Viet Minh surround 15,000 French troops; French surrender. US pays 80% of French war costs ($2.5 billion).
- **Causes – Geneva Accords (July 1954) (KC-8.1.D.ii):** Vietnam divided at 17th parallel. Ho Chi Minh (North, communist). Emperor Bao Dai (South, anti-communist). Elections scheduled for July 1956 to reunify. South Vietnam (Ngo Dinh Diem) refuses to hold elections (fears Ho will win). US supports Diem with $1 billion in aid (1954-1960).
- **Causes – Domino Theory (KC-8.1.I.B.ii):** Eisenhower press conference (April 7, 1954) – "You have a row of dominoes set up, you knock over the first one, and what will happen to the last one is the certainty that it will go over very quickly." If Vietnam falls to communism, Laos, Cambodia, Thailand, Burma, Indonesia, Philippines, Malaysia will follow.
- **Causes – Gulf of Tonkin incident (Aug 2 and 4, 1964):** USS Maddox reports being attacked by North Vietnamese torpedo boats (second attack later disputed). LBJ asks Congress for resolution. Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (Aug 7, 1964) – passed 416-0 (House), 88-2 (Senate). Authorizes president to "take all necessary measures to repel armed attack" – effectively declaration of war.
- **Causes – Escalation:** Operation Rolling Thunder (March 1965-Oct 1968) – 1 million tons of bombs dropped on North Vietnam. US troop levels: 23,000 (1964), 184,000 (1965), 385,000 (1966), 485,000 (1967), 536,000 (1968). Draft calls: 500,000 (1965), 1 million (1966), 2 million (1967), 3 million (1968).
- **Effects – Human cost (KC-8.1.B.ii):** US: 58,220 killed, 153,303 wounded, 2,500 missing. South Vietnamese (ARVN): 250,000 killed. North Vietnamese (NVA) and Viet Cong: 1.1 million killed. Civilians: 2 million killed (North and South). Agent Orange (chemical defoliant sprayed 1962-1971) – 2.1 million acres defoliated; 3 million Vietnamese have health problems; 500,000 children born with birth defects. US veterans: 300,000 diagnosed with PTSD; 100,000 died by suicide (more than combat deaths).
- **Effects – Tet Offensive (Jan 30-Feb 24, 1968):** 70,000 North Vietnamese/Viet Cong attack 100 cities (including US Embassy in Saigon). US/Vietnamese forces defeat attack (50,000 enemy casualties). Walter Cronkite broadcasts: "We are mired in stalemate." LBJ: "If I've lost Cronkite, I've lost Middle America." LBJ announces he will not seek re-election (March 31, 1968).
- **Effects – Anti-war movement (KC-8.1.II.B):** March on Pentagon (Oct 1967) – 100,000 protesters; "flower power" photo. Moratorium marches (Oct 15, 1969) – 2 million protesters nationwide. Cambodia bombings (1970) – 150,000 protest at Washington Mall; 100,000 at Yale. Kent State shootings (May 4, 1970) – National Guard kills 4 students, wounds 9. Jackson State (May 14, 1970) – police kill 2 students, wound 12. Student strike – 4 million students strike; 500 colleges closed.
- **Effects – My Lai Massacre (March 16, 1968):** Charlie Company (Americal Division) enters village of My Lai. Soldiers kill 500 unarmed Vietnamese civilians (mostly women, children, elderly). Cover-up for 18 months. Lt. William Calley convicted (1971) of murdering 22 civilians; sentenced to life; reduced to 20 years; served 3 years house arrest. Hugh Thompson (helicopter pilot) lands between US soldiers and civilians; testifies against Calley; receives Army's Soldier's Medal (1998).
- **Effects – Pentagon Papers (1971):** Daniel Ellsberg leaks 7,000-page secret study to NY Times. Reveals systematic lying to Congress/public about Vietnam (Gulf of Tonkin fabricated, secret bombing of Cambodia, assessments overly optimistic). Supreme Court rules (6-3) NY Times can publish (June 30, 1971) – "prior restraint" unconstitutional.
- **Effects – War Powers Act (1973) (KC-8.1.II.C.ii):** Passed over Nixon's veto. Requires president to notify Congress within 48 hours of deploying troops. Troops must be withdrawn within 60 days (90 with extension) unless Congress declares war or authorizes force. Limits president's ability to wage war without Congress. Used by every president since (often ignored).
- **Effects – End of draft (1973):** Last draft call (Dec 7, 1972). All-volunteer military begins (1973). 2.2 million drafted during Vietnam (1965-1972). Draft evasion: 500,000 "draft dodgers" flee to Canada (20,000 settle permanently). 250,000 evade draft but stay in US (some later pardoned by Carter 1977).
- **Effects – Fall of Saigon (April 30, 1975):** North Vietnamese tanks enter Saigon. Last helicopter evacuates US embassy (crowds of Vietnamese trying to climb walls). 125,000 Vietnamese evacuated (Operation Frequent Wind). Images of helicopter on embassy roof iconic. South Vietnam surrenders. Vietnam unified under communist rule.

## LO 8.J: Explain the causes and effects of continuing policy debates about the role of the federal government over time.

**Thesis:** Policy debates about the role of the federal government from 1945 to 1980 centered on **the welfare state** (New Deal vs. Great Society), **civil rights** (federal enforcement vs. states' rights), **the Vietnam War** (presidential power vs. congressional authority), **the economy** (Keynesianism vs. supply-side), and **environmental regulation** (conservation vs. preservation), leading to the expansion of federal power under LBJ (Great Society) followed by a conservative backlash under Nixon (New Federalism) and Reagan (Reagan Revolution).

**Body Evidence:**

- **Great Society vs. New Deal (KC-8.2.III.B.i):** LBJ's Great Society (1964-1968) – 60 programs, $10 billion. Medicare (1965) – health insurance for elderly (19 million enrolled 1966). Medicaid (1965) – health insurance for poor (10 million enrolled 1966). Head Start (1965) – preschool for low-income children (800,000 enrolled 1966). HUD (1965) – Department of Housing and Urban Development; $8 billion for urban renewal. Model Cities (1966) – $2 billion for 150 cities. Civil Rights Act (1964) – Title VII prohibits employment discrimination; creates EEOC. Voting Rights Act (1965) – bans literacy tests; federal registrars.
- **New Federalism (KC-8.2.III.C):** Nixon's "New Federalism" (1969-1974) – revenue sharing ($30 billion to states 1972-1980). States decide how to spend federal money (block grants). Family Assistance Plan (1969) – guaranteed income for poor ($1,600/family/year); fails in Senate. Nixon vetoes child care bill (1971) – "strengthens family, not federal government."
- **Civil rights – federal vs. state (KC-8.2.III.F):** Eisenhower sends troops to Little Rock (1957). Kennedy federalizes National Guard to integrate Alabama (1963). Wallace stands in schoolhouse door (June 11, 1963) – "segregation now, segregation tomorrow, segregation forever." Civil Rights Act (1964) – uses commerce clause to ban segregation. Voting Rights Act (1965) – suspends literacy tests; federal registrars replace state registrars.
- **Executive power – Vietnam and Watergate (KC-8.1.II.C.ii, KC-8.2.III.E):** Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964) – LBJ uses as declaration of war. Nixon's "imperial presidency" – impounds funds, claims executive privilege, orders wiretaps without warrants. U.S. v. Nixon (1974) – unanimous (8-0) orders Nixon to release tapes; rejects "absolute" executive privilege. Nixon resigns (Aug 8, 1974). Ford pardons Nixon (Sept 8, 1974) – "our long national nightmare is over."
- **Economic policy – Keynesianism (KC-8.2.III.C):** Kennedy tax cut (1964) – reduces top marginal rate from 91% to 70%; economy grows 5% (1964-1966). Johnson's "guns and butter" spending – Vietnam War ($100 billion) + Great Society ($10 billion) → inflation (5% by 1968). - **Environmental regulation (KC-8.2.II.D):** EPA created (Dec 2, 1970) – regulates air, water, land pollution. Clean Air Act (1970) – $50 billion for air quality standards. Clean Water Act (1972) – $20 billion for wastewater treatment. Endangered Species Act (1973) – protects 1,300 species and their habitats. Love Canal (1978) – 21,000 tons of toxic waste buried under school; Superfund created (1980) – $1.6 billion for toxic waste cleanup.
- **Conservative backlash – Reagan Revolution (KC-8.2.III.C, KC-8.2.III.F):** Reagan's 1964 speech "A Time for Choosing" – supports Goldwater; attacks welfare state. Reagan elected governor of California (1966) – cuts welfare, taxes. Reagan's 1980 campaign – "Government is not the solution to our problem; government is the problem." Reagan cuts taxes (25% across board, 1981), cuts domestic spending (food stamps, student loans, public housing), increases defense spending ($1.6 trillion 1981-1985).

## LO 8.K: Explain the continuities and changes in immigration patterns over time.

**Thesis:** Immigration patterns after WWII changed dramatically due to **the Hart-Celler Act of 1965** (abolishing national origins quotas), **refugee policies** (Hungarian Revolution 1956, Cuban Revolution 1959, Vietnam War 1975), and **economic globalization** (demand for skilled and unskilled labor), leading to a shift from European to Asian and Latin American immigration, while continuity persisted in **chain migration** (family reunification) and **nativist backlash** (California Proposition 187 1994, English-only movement).

**Body Evidence:**

- **Continuity – Quota system (1924-1965):** National origins quotas favor Northern/Western Europeans (Britain, Ireland, Germany, Scandinavia). Asians banned. Southern/Eastern Europeans (Italy, Poland, Greece, Turkey) limited to 5,000/year. 1960: 75% of immigrants from Europe.
- **Change – Hart-Celler Act (1965) (KC-8.3.I.C):** Signed by LBJ at Statue of Liberty (Oct 3, 1965). Abolishes 1924 quotas. Creates 20,000 per country limit (Eastern Hemisphere). 120,000 per hemisphere cap (later 290,000 worldwide). Priorities: family reunification (74% of visas), skilled labor (20% of visas), refugees (6% of visas). Immediate effect: immigration doubles (300,000/year 1960s → 600,000/year 1970s → 1 million/year 1980s).
- **Change – Asian immigration (KC-8.3.I.C):** 1965: 5,000 Asians/year. 1980: 200,000 Asians/year. Chinese: 3,000 (1965) → 30,000 (1980). Filipino: 5,000 → 40,000. Korean: 2,000 → 30,000. Vietnamese: 125,000 "boat people" (1975-1980). Indian: 5,000 → 50,000 (many doctors, engineers). Filipino nurses recruited to US hospitals (1970s-1980s): 100,000.
- **Change – Latin American immigration (KC-8.3.I.C):** Mexican: 50,000 (1965) → 200,000 (1980) → 1 million (1990s). Cuban: 300,000 "Freedom Flights" (1965-1973) → 125,000 Mariel boatlift (1980). Dominican: 10,000 (1965) → 100,000 (1980). Salvadoran: 5,000 (1970s) → 500,000 (1980s) fleeing civil war (1980-1992). Guatemalan: 100,000 fleeing civil war (1960-1996).
- **Change – Refugee policies (KC-8.3.I.C):** Displaced Persons Act (1948) – admits 400,000 European refugees (WWII displaced persons). Hungarian Revolution (1956) – 38,000 refugees admitted. Cuban Revolution (1959) – 500,000 admitted. Refugee Act (1980) – creates federal refugee program; raises annual ceiling to 50,000. Vietnam War (1975) – 125,000 Vietnamese evacuated. Soviet Jewry (1970s-1980s) – 200,000 admitted.
- **Continuity – Chain migration:** Family reunification priority (74% of visas) means one immigrant sponsors siblings, parents, children. Creates ethnic enclaves (Little Saigon, Koreatown, Little India, Chinatown, Little Havana). 1990: 40% of immigrants from Asia; 40% from Latin America; 15% from Europe; 5% from Africa/Middle East.
- **Continuity – Nativist backlash (KC-8.3.I.C, KC-8.2.II.C):** California Proposition 187 (1994) – denies education, health care, social services to undocumented immigrants; passes 59-41%; struck down by courts. English-only movement – 27 states declare English official language (1980-2010). Immigration Reform and Control Act (1986) – amnesty for 3 million undocumented immigrants; employers must verify work eligibility (I-9 forms).
- **Demographic effects:** Foreign-born population: 5% (1965), 8% (1980), 12% (2000), 14% (2020). 1980: 40% of immigrants from Europe; 40% from Latin America; 15% from Asia; 5% from Africa/Middle East. 2020: 10% from Europe; 50% from Latin America; 30% from Asia; 10% from Africa/Middle East.

## LO 8.M: Explain the various ways in which the federal government responded to the calls for the expansion of civil rights.

Board 1954, Loving v. **Body Evidence:**

Head Start (1965) – 800,000 low-income children. Upward Bound (1965) – college prep for low-income students.

Virginia (1967) – unanimous (9-0) strikes down bans on interracial marriage. Green v. Bradley (1974) – limits busing across school district lines (Detroit case; protects suburban schools). Regents of UC v. Bakke (1978) – upholds affirmative action but bans racial quotas.
- **Limited enforcement:** 1970: 90% of Southern Black students still attend segregated schools. 1980: 40% still attend segregated schools (resegregation begins 1990s). Civil rights complaints backlog: 100,000 (EEOC, 1975). Justice Department Civil Rights Division has 200 lawyers (1970) – too few to enforce.

## LO 8.O: Explain how and why policies related to the environment developed and changed from 1968 to 1980.

**Thesis:** Environmental policies from 1968 to 1980 developed and changed from **conservation** (wise use) to **regulation** (EPA, Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act) to **preservation** (Endangered Species Act, Wilderness Act) due to **environmental disasters** (Santa Barbara oil spill 1969, Cuyahoga River fire 1969, Love Canal 1978), **activism** (Earth Day 1970, Greenpeace), and **scientific evidence** (DDT harms birds, CFCs deplete ozone), leading to the creation of EPA (1970) and major legislation, followed by a conservative backlash (Reagan's "Sagebrush Rebellion").

**Body Evidence:**

- **Environmental disasters (KC-8.2.II.D):** Santa Barbara oil spill (Jan 28, 1969) – 3 million gallons; 35-mile slick kills 10,000 birds. Cuyahoga River fire (June 22, 1969) – river catches fire (13th time, but this one televised); Time magazine cover "The Filthy River." Love Canal (1978) – toxic waste buried under school; 21,000 tons; 241 families evacuate. Three Mile Island (1979) – partial meltdown; evacuations; loss of trust in nuclear power.
- **Legislation – Clean Air Act (1970) (KC-8.2.II.D):** Sets national ambient air quality standards (NAAQS) for 6 pollutants (lead, ozone, carbon monoxide, nitrogen oxide, sulfur dioxide, particulates). Requires states to develop implementation plans. $50 billion for pollution control. By 1980: lead emissions down 80%, sulfur dioxide down 30%, particulates down 40%.
- **Legislation – Clean Water Act (1972) (KC-8.2.II.D):** Sets goal of "fishable and swimmable" waters by 1983. Requires permits for discharging pollutants (NPDES). $20 billion for sewage treatment plants. By 1980: 50% of US waterways meet standards (up from 20% 1972).
- **Legislation – Endangered Species Act (1973) (KC-8.2.II.D):** Protects 1,300 species (bald eagle, grizzly bear, wolves, spotted owl, manatee). Prohibits any action that harms listed species or their habitat. Used to stop Tellico Dam (Tennessee, 1978) – saves snail darter (controversial, Congress exempts dam later). Supreme Court upholds (TVA v. Hill, 1978) – "the plain intent of Congress... was to halt and reverse the trend toward species extinction, whatever the cost."
- **Legislation – National Environmental Policy Act (1969):** Requires environmental impact statements (EIS) for any major federal project. By 1980: 10,000 EIS filed (most for highway, dam, pipeline, logging projects).
- **Executive action – EPA created (Dec 2, 1970) (KC-8.2.II.D):** Combines 15 existing pollution programs (from Interior, Agriculture, HEW, FDA, Atomic Energy Commission). First administrator: William Ruckelshaus. Budget $1 billion (1970), $5 billion (1980). Staff 6,000 (1970), 15,000 (1980). Enforces Clean Air, Clean Water, Toxic Substances, FIFRA (pesticides).
- **Activism (KC-8.2.II.D):** First Earth Day (April 22, 1970) – 20 million participants; 10,000 schools; 2,000 colleges; 1,000 communities. Sierra Club membership: 100,000 (1960), 1 million (1980). Greenpeace founded (1971) – protests nuclear testing, whaling. Natural Resources Defense Council (1970) – environmental law; 500,000 members. Environmental Defense Fund (1967) – uses science and law; bans DDT (1972).
- **Sagebrush Rebellion (1979-1982):** Western states (NV, WY, UT, AZ, NM, ID, MT) oppose federal land ownership (80% of NV, 50% of UT, 45% of ID, 40% of WY). Demand transfer of federal lands to states. Supported by Reagan (1980). Federal Land Policy and Management Act (1976) – declares federal lands will remain in federal ownership.
- **Limits of environmental policy:** 1980: 50% of US lakes and rivers still unsafe for swimming/fishing. 40% of air monitors exceed NAAQS. 100 million Americans live in areas with unsafe air. Reagan cuts EPA budget 20% (1981). Anne Gorsuch (EPA head, 1981-1983) tries to dismantle EPA; resigns after scandal.

## LO 8.P: Explain the effects of the growth of religious movements over the course of the 20th century.

**Thesis:** The growth of religious movements from 1945 to 1980 included **the rise of evangelical Christianity** (Billy Graham, National Association of Evangelicals), **the growth of Catholicism** (John F. Kennedy elected 1960, Vatican II 1962-1965), **the emergence of new religious movements** (Unification Church, Hare Krishna, Scientology), **the revival of Islam** (Nation of Islam, Malcolm X, Louis Farrakhan), and **the growth of Judaism** (Conservative, Reform, Orthodox), leading to increased political activism (Moral Majority, Christian Coalition) and cultural conflict (Scopes trial 1925 → evolution bans in 1960s-1970s, abortion 1973, school prayer 1962-1963).

**Body Evidence:**

- **Evangelical Christianity (KC-8.3.II.C):** Billy Graham (1949-2005) – preaches to 215 million people in 185 countries; counsels every president from Truman to Obama. National Association of Evangelicals (1942) – 45,000 churches; 30 million members. Fuller Theological Seminary (1947) – 4,000 students. Christianity Today (1956) – 100,000 circulation. Campus Crusade for Christ (1951) – 25,000 staff; 200 countries. Explo '72 (Dallas) – 80,000 attendees. Jesus movement (1960s-1970s) – "Jesus freaks" (hippie evangelicals).
- **Catholicism:** JFK elected president (1960) – first Catholic. Vatican II (1962-1965) – Mass in English (not Latin); ecumenism (dialogue with other religions); affirms religious liberty. Catholic population: 30 million (1950), 50 million (1980). National Catholic Reporter (1964) – liberal voice; 100,000 circulation. Catholic Worker movement (1933) – Dorothy Day; 200 communities (1980). Priests for Equal Justice (1960s-1970s) – Catholic clergy active in civil rights, anti-war.
- **New religious movements:** Unification Church (Moonies) – Sun Myung Moon (1960s-2012); mass weddings. Hare Krishna – A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami (1966); chants in airports. Scientology – L. Ron Hubbard (1954); Tom Cruise member. Children of God (1968) – sexual abuse, criminal charges. Transcendental Meditation (Maharishi Mahesh Yogi, 1959) – The Beatles (1968); 5 million practitioners.
- **Nation of Islam (KC-8.2.II.B):** Elijah Muhammad (1934-1975) – preaches Black supremacy, separatism, self-reliance. Malcolm X (1952-1964) – membership grows from 500 to 50,000. Muhammad Ali joins (1964). Louis Farrakhan leads after 1978. Million Man March (1995) – 500,000 attendees.

Aguillard (1987) – 7-2 strikes down Louisiana law requiring creation science.

## LO 8.Q: Explain the extent to which the events of the period from 1945 to 1980 reshaped national identity.

**Thesis:** The events of 1945 to 1980 reshaped American national identity from a **confident, unified, postwar consensus** to a **fragmented, cynical, conflicted** identity by 1980, due to **the Vietnam War** (shattering the myth of moral superiority and military invincibility), **Watergate** (destroying trust in the presidency), **the civil rights movement** (exposing systemic racism), **the counterculture** (rejecting traditional values), **the feminist movement** (challenging patriarchy), **economic stagnation** (ending postwar prosperity), and **the rise of the New Right** (polarizing politics), although the "American Dream" of homeownership, education, and upward mobility remained aspirational for many.

**Body Evidence:**

- **Confidence shattered – Vietnam (KC-8.1.I.B.ii):** Tet Offensive (1968) – shows US cannot win. My Lai Massacre (1968) – US soldiers kill 500 civilians. Pentagon Papers (1971) – government lied. Saigon falls (1975) – US loses. "Vietnam Syndrome" – reluctance to use military force (1973-1991).
- **Trust shattered – Watergate (KC-8.2.III.E):** Nixon resigns (1974) – first president to resign. Ford pardons Nixon. Trust in government: 75% (1964), 25% (1974). Gallup: 70% of Americans have "little or no confidence" in presidency (1975). "I'm not a crook" – Nixon's lie becomes symbol of political corruption.
- **Economic confidence shattered – Stagflation (KC-8.3.I.A, KC-8.3.I.D):** 1970s: high inflation (12% 1974, 13% 1979) + high unemployment (9% 1975, 7% 1979) + slow growth (2%/year 1973-1979). Oil shocks (1973, 1979) – gasoline quadruples; gas lines. Middle class wages stagnate (1973-1979). "Misery index" (inflation + unemployment) peaks at 22% (1980). Confidence in economy: 50% (1965), 10% (1980).
- **Racial identity reshaped (KC-8.2.I):** Civil Rights Act (1964) – outlawed segregation. Voting Rights Act (1965) – enfranchised Blacks. Fair Housing Act (1968) – banned housing discrimination. By 1980: 25% of Southern schools desegregated (up from 2% 1965). Black elected officials: 100 (1965), 5,000 (1980). But: white flight to suburbs, de facto segregation, wealth gap persists (Black median income 60% of white, 1980). Black national identity ("Black is beautiful") emerges.
- **Gender identity reshaped (KC-8.2.II.A):** 19th Amendment (1920) gave vote. 1960s-1970s feminism: 50% of women in workforce (1980). Women's median wage: 60% of men's (1980) – up from 50% (1960). Divorce rate doubles (1960-1980). Single mothers: 20% of families (1980). Abortion legal (Roe 1973). ERA fails (1982). "Superwoman" ideal emerges (career, marriage, children).
- **Sexual identity reshaped (KC-8.2.II.A):** Stonewall (1969) – birth of gay liberation. Gay pride parades (1970). First openly gay elected officials (Harvey Milk 1977). American Psychiatric Association removes homosexuality from mental disorders list (1973). 50% of Americans think homosexuality should be legal (1980) – up from 20% (1970). But: Anita Bryant's anti-gay campaign (1977), Briggs Initiative (California 1978) – fails.
- **Political identity polarized (KC-8.2.III.C, F):** New Deal coalition fragments (1968-1980). Democrats: labor, urban ethnics, Blacks, intellectuals, South (until 1960s). Republicans: business, Sun Belt, evangelicals, white ethnics (Reagan Democrats). "Reagan Democrat" (blue-collar white voter) swings to GOP (1980). 1980 election: Reagan wins 489 electoral votes (44 states); Carter wins 49 (6 states + DC).
- **American Dream – Continuities (KC-8.3.I.B):** Homeownership: 44% (1940), 65% (1980). College education: 5% (1940), 20% (1980). Middle class: 30% (1940), 60% (1980). Real median income: $20,000 (1950), $35,000 (1980). Poverty rate: 30% (1950), 12% (1980).
- **American Dream – Limits:** Wealth gap widens after 1973. Top 1%: 20% of wealth (1970), 30% (1980). Bottom 50%: 5% of wealth (1970), 3% (1980). Decline of labor unions: 35% of workers unionized (1955), 20% (1980). Deindustrialization: 5 million manufacturing jobs lost (1970-1980).
