## LO 7.A: Explain the context in which America grew into its role as a world power.

**Contextualization:** By 1890, the United States had become the world's largest industrial power, producing more steel, coal, and manufactured goods than Britain, Germany, and France combined. But this industrial might required foreign markets to sell surplus goods and foreign sources of raw materials (rubber, oil, tin, copper, sugar). At the same time, the "closing of the frontier" (Census of 1890 declared the frontier closed) led some Americans to worry that the nation's "rugged individualism" would fade without new frontiers to conquer. The ideology of **Manifest Destiny** (the belief that Americans were divinely ordained to expand across the continent) was extended overseas: the US would now "civilize" the "backward" peoples of the Caribbean, Central America, and the Pacific.

**Thesis:** The context in which America grew into its role as a world power from 1890 to 1945 was shaped by **economic factors** (the need for foreign markets and raw materials), **ideological factors** (Manifest Destiny, Social Darwinism, the "White Man's Burden," American exceptionalism), **geopolitical factors** (the "closing of the frontier," competition with European empires), and **specific events** (the Spanish-American War 1898, the acquisition of the Philippines, Guam, Puerto Rico, and Hawaii, the construction of the Panama Canal 1904-1914, World War I 1914-1918, and World War II 1941-1945).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Economic Context – The Need for Foreign Markets and Raw Materials (KC-7.1.I, KC-7.3.I.A):**
- By 1890, American factories produced more goods than Americans could consume. The US needed foreign markets to sell surplus goods (textiles, machinery, steel, agricultural products). Senator Albert Beveridge (Indiana) argued: "American factories are making more than the American people can use; American soil is producing more than they can consume. Fate has written our policy for us; the trade of the world must and shall be ours."
- The US also needed foreign sources of raw materials: rubber (from Brazil, Congo, Malaya), oil (from Mexico, Venezuela, the Middle East), tin (from Bolivia, Malaya), copper (from Chile), sugar (from Cuba, Hawaii, the Philippines), and fruit (bananas from Central America – the "Banana Republics").
- The **Panic of 1893** (the worst depression to that date) intensified the search for foreign markets. The depression was caused by overproduction, railroad bankruptcies, and the collapse of the gold standard (the "run on the gold reserves"). 500 banks failed, 15,000 businesses failed, 4 million workers were unemployed (20% of the labor force).

**Ideological Context – Manifest Destiny, Social Darwinism, and the "White Man's Burden" (KC-7.3.I.A, KC-7.3.I.A, KC-7.3.I.A):**
- **Manifest Destiny** : The belief that Americans were divinely ordained to expand across the continent (John O'Sullivan, 1845). After the frontier closed (1890), Manifest Destiny was extended overseas. The US would now "civilize" the "backward" peoples of the Caribbean, Central America, and the Pacific.
- **Social Darwinism** : The belief that "survival of the fittest" applied to nations and races. The "fittest" races (Anglo-Saxons, Nordics) were destined to dominate the "unfit" races (Africans, Asians, Native Americans, Latin Americans). Josiah Strong (a Congregationalist minister) wrote *Our Country: Its Possible Future and Its Present Crisis* (1885): "The Anglo-Saxon race is the representative of two great ideas: civil liberty and pure Christianity. It is the race that has been entrusted with the mission of disciplining the world."
- **The "White Man's Burden" (Rudyard Kipling, 1899)** : Kipling (a British poet) urged the US to take up the "burden" of civilizing the "savage" peoples of the Philippines. The poem was addressed to the US (after it acquired the Philippines). It was used by imperialists to justify colonialism: "Take up the White Man's burden – Send forth the best ye breed – Go bind your sons to exile – To serve your captives' need."
- **Alfred Thayer Mahan (1840-1914)** : A US Navy captain and historian. He wrote *The Influence of Sea Power Upon History, 1660-1783* (1890). Mahan argued that great nations have great navies; great navies require overseas bases (coaling stations, repair yards); overseas bases require colonies. He urged the US to build a modern "steel navy," acquire colonies (Hawaii, the Philippines, Cuba, Puerto Rico), and build a canal across Central America (the Panama Canal). Mahan's ideas were influential (President Theodore Roosevelt was a follower).

**Geopolitical Context – The "Closing of the Frontier" and Competition with Europe (KC-7.3.I.A, KC-7.3.I.A):**
- The **Census of 1890** declared the frontier "closed" (no more continuous line of settlement). This alarmed many Americans. Frederick Jackson Turner delivered his "Frontier Thesis" (1893): the frontier had shaped American character (individualism, democracy, ingenuity, violence). The closing of the frontier would lead to social unrest and class conflict (labor unions, Populism, socialism). The solution: acquire new frontiers overseas (imperialism).
- **European imperialism** : By 1890, European powers (Britain, France, Germany, Russia, Italy, Belgium, Portugal, Spain) had colonized most of Africa, Asia, and the Pacific. The US was late to the game. To compete with European empires, the US needed colonies of its own. Senator Henry Cabot Lodge (Massachusetts) argued: "The great nations are rapidly absorbing for their future expansion and their present defense all the waste places of the earth. As one of the great nations of the world, the United States must not fall out of the line of march."

**Specific Events – The Spanish-American War (1898) (KC-7.3.I.C):**
- **Causes** :
  - **Cuban rebellion** : Cuba (a Spanish colony) had been fighting for independence (1868-1878, 1895-1898). The Spanish brutally suppressed the rebellion (concentration camps, mass starvation). "Yellow journalism" (sensational, exaggerated news stories) by William Randolph Hearst (New York *Journal*) and Joseph Pulitzer (New York *World*) inflamed American public opinion.
  - **The *Maine* explosion (February 15, 1898)** : The US battleship *Maine* exploded in Havana harbor (killing 266 sailors). The cause was unknown (a mine? an accident?). Hearst's *Journal* blamed Spain ("Remember the Maine! To hell with Spain!").
  - **The de Lôme letter (February 9, 1898)** : A private letter from the Spanish ambassador (Dupuy de Lôme) was intercepted. The letter called President McKinley "weak and a bidder for the admiration of the crowd." It outraged Americans.
  - **The "Yellow Press"** : Hearst (quoted in a telegram to artist Frederic Remington): "You furnish the pictures, and I'll furnish the war."
- **The war (April-August 1898)** :
  - **The Pacific theater** : Commodore George Dewey (US Navy) attacked the Spanish fleet in Manila Bay (Philippines) on May 1, 1898. He destroyed the Spanish fleet. The US occupied Manila (with the help of Filipino rebels led by Emilio Aguinaldo).
  - **The Caribbean theater** : The US Navy blockaded Cuba. US troops (including Theodore Roosevelt's "Rough Riders") landed in Cuba. The **Battle of San Juan Hill** (July 1, 1898) was a US victory (though the "Rough Riders" did most of the fighting; Roosevelt was posthumously awarded the Medal of Honor in 2001). The Spanish fleet tried to escape from Santiago harbor (July 3) and was destroyed.
  - **The Treaty of Paris (December 10, 1898)** : Spain ceded the Philippines, Guam, and Puerto Rico to the US. Cuba became an independent country (but the US retained the right to intervene – the Platt Amendment, 1901). The US paid Spain $20 million for the Philippines.
- **The Philippine-American War (1899-1902)** : The Filipinos (led by Emilio Aguinaldo) did not want to be colonized by the US. They declared independence (June 12, 1898) and fought a guerrilla war against US forces (125,000 US troops; 4,300 US soldiers killed; 20,000 Filipino soldiers killed; 200,000 Filipino civilians killed). The war was brutal: the US used "water cure" (waterboarding), concentration camps, and scorched-earth tactics. The US won; the Philippines became a US colony until 1946.

**Specific Events – The Acquisition of Hawaii (1898) (KC-7.3.I.C):**
- **Hawaii** : The Hawaiian Islands were a constitutional monarchy (King Kalakaua, Queen Lili'uokalani). American sugar planters (Sanford Dole) controlled the economy. In 1893, American planters (with the help of US Marines) overthrew Queen Lili'uokalani. They established a "Republic of Hawaii" (with Sanford Dole as president). President Grover Cleveland (a Democrat) refused to annex Hawaii (he called the overthrow "an act of war"). President William McKinley (a Republican) annexed Hawaii in 1898 (by a joint resolution of Congress, not a treaty, because the treaty lacked the votes). Hawaii became a US territory (1900). It became the 50th state in 1959.

**Specific Events – The Panama Canal (1904-1914) (KC-7.1.I, KC-7.1.I):**
- The US wanted a canal across Central America to connect the Atlantic and Pacific oceans. A canal would allow the US Navy to move ships quickly between the two oceans (essential for defending the Philippines and Puerto Rico).
- **The Hay-Herrán Treaty (1903)** : The US negotiated a treaty with Colombia (which owned Panama) to build a canal. The Colombian Senate rejected the treaty (they wanted more money). President Theodore Roosevelt was furious. He encouraged Panamanian rebels to declare independence from Colombia (1903). The US Navy prevented Colombian troops from suppressing the rebellion. The US recognized the "Republic of Panama" within hours.
- **The Hay-Bunau-Varilla Treaty (1903)** : The US negotiated a treaty with Panama (represented by a French engineer, Philippe Bunau-Varilla). The US paid Panama $10 million (plus $250,000/year) for a 10-mile-wide "Canal Zone" (the right to build the canal and control the zone "in perpetuity"). Panama had no say in the treaty (it was a US colony in all but name).
- **The Panama Canal** : Construction (1904-1914) cost $375 million ($5 billion in today's dollars). 50,000 workers (mostly from the Caribbean – Barbados, Jamaica, Martinique, Guadeloupe) built the canal. 5,600 workers died (from disease – malaria, yellow fever – and accidents). The canal opened in 1914. It was a triumph of American engineering (the "big ditch").
- **The Roosevelt Corollary to the Monroe Doctrine (1904)** : President Roosevelt announced that the US had the right to intervene in the affairs of Latin American countries if they were "unable to pay their debts" or "unable to maintain order." The Corollary was used to justify US interventions in the Dominican Republic (1905), Nicaragua (1910, 1912, 1926-1933), Haiti (1915-1934), and Mexico (1914, 1916-1917). It turned the Monroe Doctrine (no European interference) into a justification for US imperialism ("the policeman of the Western Hemisphere").

**Specific Events – World War I (1914-1918) and Its Aftermath (KC-7.3.II.A, KC-7.3.II.B, KC-7.3.II.C, KC-7.3.II.D, KC-7.3.II.E):**
- **US neutrality (1914-1917)** : President Woodrow Wilson declared the US neutral. Most Americans favored neutrality (they did not want to fight another European war). But the US traded with both sides (the Allies – Britain, France, Russia; the Central Powers – Germany, Austria-Hungary, Turkey). The British navy blockaded Germany; the Germans used "unrestricted submarine warfare" (U-boats) to sink ships trading with Britain.
- **The *Lusitania* (May 7, 1915)** : A German U-boat sank the British passenger liner *Lusitania* (off the coast of Ireland). 1,198 people died (including 128 Americans). The *Lusitania* was carrying ammunition (it was a legitimate military target). The American public was outraged. President Wilson demanded that Germany stop unrestricted submarine warfare. Germany agreed (temporarily).
- **The Zimmermann Telegram (January 1917)** : The British intercepted a telegram from German Foreign Secretary Arthur Zimmermann to the German ambassador in Mexico. Zimmermann proposed a German-Mexican alliance: Mexico would attack the US; Germany would help Mexico recover "lost territory" (Texas, New Mexico, Arizona). The telegram was leaked to the American press. Americans were outraged.
- **US entry into the war (April 6, 1917)** : Wilson asked Congress for a declaration of war ("The world must be made safe for democracy"). Congress declared war (82-6 in the Senate; 373-50 in the House). Wilson's "Fourteen Points" (January 1918) were an idealistic vision for the post-war world (open diplomacy, freedom of the seas, free trade, disarmament, self-determination for ethnic groups, a League of Nations).
- **The American Expeditionary Force (AEF)** : General John J. Pershing commanded the AEF (2 million US soldiers in France). The AEF played a decisive role in the Allied victory (the Meuse-Argonne Offensive, September-November 1918, was the largest battle in US history – 1.2 million US troops, 26,000 killed). The war ended on November 11, 1918 (Armistice Day).
- **The Treaty of Versailles (1919)** : The "Big Four" (Wilson of the US, Lloyd George of Britain, Clemenceau of France, Orlando of Italy) negotiated the treaty. Wilson wanted a "peace without victory" (no reparations, no annexations, self-determination, a League of Nations). Clemenceau wanted revenge (Germany must pay). The treaty was a compromise: Germany lost territory (Alsace-Lorraine, Eupen-Malmédy, North Schleswig, West Prussia, Posen, Silesia, Danzig, Memel); Germany lost its colonies (Togo, Cameroon, South-West Africa, Tanganyika, New Guinea, the Marshall Islands); Germany's army was limited to 100,000 men; Germany had to pay reparations (132 billion gold marks – $33 billion); Germany had to accept "war guilt" (Article 231 – Germany alone was responsible for the war). The treaty also included the League of Nations (Wilson's "baby").
- **The Senate rejects the Treaty of Versailles (1919-1920)** : The US Senate (led by Henry Cabot Lodge) opposed the League of Nations because it might require the US to go to war without Congressional approval. Wilson refused to compromise (he was too ill – he suffered a stroke in September 1919). The Senate rejected the treaty (49-35 in November 1919; 49-35 again in March 1920). The US never joined the League of Nations. The US negotiated separate peace treaties with Germany, Austria, and Hungary (1921).

**Specific Events – World War II (1941-1945) (KC-7.3.III, KC-7.3.III.A, KC-7.3.III.C.i, KC-7.3.III.C.ii, KC-7.3.III.D, KC-7.3.III.E):**
- **US neutrality (1939-1941)** : President Franklin D. Roosevelt declared the US neutral. But he was sympathetic to the Allies (Britain, France, China, Russia). The US passed the **Lend-Lease Act (1941)** , allowing the US to "lend" weapons and supplies to "any country whose defense the President deems vital to the defense of the United States" (Britain, China, Russia). The US also imposed economic sanctions on Japan (oil, steel, scrap metal) to protest Japan's invasion of China (1937) and its occupation of French Indochina (1941).
- **Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941)** : Japan attacked the US Pacific fleet at Pearl Harbor (Hawaii). 2,403 Americans were killed; 1,178 wounded; 21 ships were sunk or damaged; 300 airplanes destroyed. The US declared war on Japan (December 8). Germany and Italy declared war on the US (December 11).
- **The "Grand Alliance"** : The US, Britain, and the Soviet Union (the "Big Three") agreed to defeat Germany first (the "Europe first" strategy). The US and Britain would fight Germany; the US would fight Japan (with help from China, Australia, New Zealand, and Britain).
- **The war in Europe (1942-1945)** :
  - **North Africa (Operation Torch, November 1942)** : US and British forces invaded French North Africa (Morocco, Algeria). They defeated the German "Afrika Korps" (Erwin Rommel).
  - **Italy (1943-1945)** : US and British forces invaded Sicily (July 1943) and Italy (September 1943). Italy surrendered; but German forces occupied northern Italy. The Allies fought a bloody campaign up the Italian peninsula (Monte Cassino, Anzio).
  - **D-Day (June 6, 1944)** : The Allied invasion of Normandy (France). 156,000 US, British, Canadian, and other troops landed on 5 beaches (Utah, Omaha, Gold, Juno, Sword). The fighting was brutal (2,500 US troops died on Omaha Beach alone). The Allies broke out of Normandy (July 1944) and liberated Paris (August 1944).
  - **The Battle of the Bulge (December 1944-January 1945)** : Germany's last offensive. German forces broke through the American lines in the Ardennes forest (Belgium). The US 101st Airborne Division held Bastogne. The US counterattacked and defeated the Germans.
  - **The fall of Germany (April-May 1945)** : Soviet forces reached Berlin (April 1945). Hitler committed suicide (April 30). Germany surrendered on May 7, 1945 (V-E Day – Victory in Europe Day).
- **The war in the Pacific (1942-1945)** :
  - **The Philippines (1942)** : US forces (Edward King) surrendered to Japan (May 1942). 75,000 US and Filipino troops were forced to march 65 miles (the "Bataan Death March"). 10,000 died (starvation, disease, execution).
  - **The "island-hopping" strategy** : The US Navy (Admiral Chester Nimitz) and Army (General Douglas MacArthur) bypassed heavily defended islands and captured lightly defended islands (Tarawa, Makin, Kwajalein, Eniwetok, Saipan, Tinian, Guam, Peleliu, Iwo Jima, Okinawa). The fighting was brutal (the Marines suffered 25,000 casualties at Iwo Jima; the US suffered 75,000 casualties at Okinawa).
  - **The bombing of Japan** : The US Army Air Forces (General Curtis LeMay) firebombed Japanese cities (Tokyo, March 9-10, 1945 – 100,000 civilians killed). The US dropped atomic bombs on **Hiroshima (August 6, 1945)** and **Nagasaki (August 9, 1945)** . 200,000 civilians died (most instantly; others from radiation poisoning). Japan surrendered on August 15, 1945 (V-J Day – Victory over Japan Day).
- **The United Nations** : The US, Britain, the Soviet Union, and China (the "Four Policemen") created the United Nations (1945) to replace the failed League of Nations. The UN Charter was signed in San Francisco (June 26, 1945). The US Senate ratified the UN Charter (89-2) – a sharp contrast with the League of Nations.

## LO 7.B: Explain the similarities and differences in attitudes about the nation's proper role in the world.

**Contextualization:** In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Americans debated the proper role of the United States in the world. Should the US remain isolated from foreign entanglements (the tradition of George Washington's Farewell Address and the Monroe Doctrine)? Or should the US become an imperial power (acquiring colonies, building a navy, intervening in the affairs of other nations)? These debates took place in the context of the Spanish-American War (1898), the Philippine-American War (1899-1902), the construction of the Panama Canal (1904-1914), World War I (1914-1918), and the rise of fascism and militarism in Europe and Asia (1920s-1930s).

**Thesis:** Attitudes about the nation's proper role in the world from 1890 to 1945 were divided between **imperialists** (expansionists) who argued that the US should acquire colonies, build a navy, and civilize "backward" peoples (Theodore Roosevelt, Alfred Thayer Mahan, Henry Cabot Lodge, Albert Beveridge); and **anti-imperialists** (isolationists) who argued that imperialism violated American ideals (self-determination, consent of the governed, no taxation without representation) and would lead to militarism, corruption, and racial mixing (Mark Twain, Andrew Carnegie, William Jennings Bryan, the Anti-Imperialist League).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Imperialists – Arguments for Expansion (KC-7.3.I.A):**
- **Economic arguments** : The US needed foreign markets for surplus goods, foreign sources of raw materials, and foreign investment opportunities. Senator Albert Beveridge (Indiana): "American factories are making more than the American people can use; American soil is producing more than they can consume. Fate has written our policy for us; the trade of the world must and shall be ours."
- **Geopolitical arguments** : The US needed a navy to protect its trade and its coastline. A navy required overseas bases (coaling stations, repair yards). Bases required colonies. Captain Alfred Thayer Mahan: *The Influence of Sea Power Upon History* (1890). Theodore Roosevelt: "Speak softly and carry a big stick; you will go far."
- **Ideological arguments** : The US had a "manifest destiny" to spread democracy, Christianity, and Anglo-Saxon civilization. Senator Albert Beveridge: "God has made us the master organizers of the world... He has made us adepts in government that we may administer government among savage and senile peoples." Josiah Strong: "The Anglo-Saxon race is the representative of two great ideas: civil liberty and pure Christianity... It is the race that has been entrusted with the mission of disciplining the world."
- **Racial arguments** : "Scientific racism" (Social Darwinism) held that Anglo-Saxons (Nordics) were the "fittest" race and were destined to dominate the "unfit" races (Africans, Asians, Native Americans, Latin Americans). The "White Man's Burden" (Rudyard Kipling) was a duty, not a choice.

**Imperialists – Advocates:**
- **Theodore Roosevelt (1858-1919)** : Assistant Secretary of the Navy (1897-1898); Rough Rider (Spanish-American War); Governor of New York (1899-1900); Vice President (1901); President (1901-1909). Roosevelt was a leading imperialist. He supported the Spanish-American War, the Philippine-American War, and the construction of the Panama Canal. He added the "Roosevelt Corollary" to the Monroe Doctrine (1904). He won the Nobel Peace Prize (1906) for mediating the Russo-Japanese War (1905).
- **Alfred Thayer Mahan (1840-1914)** : US Navy captain, historian, and strategist. *The Influence of Sea Power Upon History, 1660-1783* (1890) and *The Interest of America in Sea Power, Present and Future* (1897). Mahan's ideas were influential (Theodore Roosevelt, Henry Cabot Lodge, William McKinley, Woodrow Wilson).
- **Henry Cabot Lodge (1850-1924)** : US Senator from Massachusetts (1893-1924). Lodge was a leading imperialist. He supported the Spanish-American War, the annexation of the Philippines, and the construction of the Panama Canal. He opposed the League of Nations (after Wilson made it a partisan issue). He wrote: "The United States is the world's best hope... but it cannot hope to realize its destiny if it turns its back on the world."
- **Albert Beveridge (1862-1927)** : US Senator from Indiana (1899-1911). Beveridge was a leading imperialist. He gave famous speeches (the "March of the Flag" speech, 1898): "God has not been preparing the English-speaking and Teutonic peoples for a thousand years for nothing... He has made us the master organizers of the world... He has made us adepts in government that we may administer government among savage and senile peoples."

**Anti-Imperialists – Arguments Against Expansion (KC-7.3.I.B):**
- **Self-determination** : The US was founded on the principle of "consent of the governed." Imperialism (ruling a people without their consent) violated that principle. The Declaration of Independence: "Governments are instituted among men, deriving their just powers from the consent of the governed." The Philippines, Cuba, Puerto Rico, and Guam had not consented to US rule.
- **No taxation without representation** : The US had fought a revolution against British "taxation without representation." Yet the US was now doing the same thing to the Filipinos, Puerto Ricans, and Guamanians. Senator George Hoar (Massachusetts): "If we are to govern these people without their consent, we are no better than the British government against which we rebelled."
- **Militarism and corruption** : Imperialism would lead to a large standing army and navy, which would threaten liberty (the "military-industrial complex" – President Eisenhower, 1961). It would also lead to corruption (the "spoils system" extended to the colonies). Mark Twain: "We have gone there to conquer, not to redeem... I am an anti-imperialist. I am opposed to having the eagle put its talons on any other land."
- **Racial mixing** : Imperialism would bring Americans into contact with "inferior" races, leading to racial mixing (miscegenation) and the decline of the Anglo-Saxon "race." Senator Benjamin Tillman (South Carolina): "We are Anglo-Saxons, and we propose to keep the country Anglo-Saxon... We do not want the Philippines; we do not want the half-breeds and mongrels of the Orient."
- **The cost of empire** : The Philippine-American War cost $600 million (in 1902 dollars) and 4,300 American lives (plus 200,000 Filipino civilians). The war was brutal (the US used waterboarding, concentration camps, and scorched-earth tactics). The "water cure" (waterboarding) was exposed by the American press (the "water cure" was used on Filipinos). The war damaged America's reputation as a "beacon of liberty."

**Anti-Imperialists – Advocates:**
- **Mark Twain (1835-1910)** : The "Lincoln of American literature." Twain was a leading anti-imperialist. He wrote "The War Prayer" (1905) – a satire of American imperialism. He wrote "To the Person Sitting in Darkness" (1901) – criticizing the US for betraying its ideals. He was a vice president of the Anti-Imperialist League (1901-1910).
- **Andrew Carnegie (1835-1919)** : The steel magnate. Carnegie was a leading anti-imperialist. He offered to buy the Philippines from the US for $20 million (the same amount the US had paid Spain). He wrote "Distant Possessions" (1898): "The taste for empire is a colossal evil." He was a vice president of the Anti-Imperialist League.
- **William Jennings Bryan (1860-1925)** : The "Great Commoner." Bryan was a leading anti-imperialist. He resigned as Secretary of State (Woodrow Wilson's cabinet) in 1915 to protest Wilson's "preparedness" (expansion of the army and navy). He was a peace activist. He was a vice president of the Anti-Imperialist League.
- **The Anti-Imperialist League (1898-1920)** : The League included prominent Americans: Grover Cleveland (former president), John Sherman (former secretary of state), Carl Schurz (former secretary of the interior), Charles Eliot (president of Harvard), Jane Addams (settlement house leader), Samuel Gompers (AFL leader). The League was a coalition of "liberals" (anti-imperialists who opposed war and colonialism) and "racists" (white supremacists who opposed the annexation of "inferior" peoples). The League folded after World War I (the US acquired no new colonies after 1917). But the League's legacy is the idea that imperialism is incompatible with American ideals.

**The "Isolationist vs. Interventionist" Debate (1918-1941) (KC-7.3.II.D, KC-7.3.II.E):**
- **Isolationism** : The US should stay out of European affairs (George Washington's Farewell Address, the Monroe Doctrine). The US should not join the League of Nations (1920). The US should pass Neutrality Acts (1935, 1936, 1937, 1939) to prevent "entanglement" in foreign wars.
- **Isolationist advocates** : Senator William Borah (Idaho), Senator Hiram Johnson (California), Senator Gerald Nye (North Dakota), the America First Committee (1940-1941, including Charles Lindbergh, the famous aviator). Lindbergh argued that the US should not fight a "war for democracy" because Germany was "unbeatable" and the Soviet Union was a greater threat than Germany (a pro-Nazi stance).
- **Interventionism** : The US should take an active role in world affairs (Theodore Roosevelt's "big stick," Woodrow Wilson's "League of Nations," Franklin Roosevelt's "arsenal of democracy"). The US should support the Allies (Britain, France, China, Russia) against the Axis powers (Germany, Italy, Japan). The US should "lend-lease" weapons and supplies to Britain and Russia. The US should prepare for war (the "preparedness" movement, 1915-1917; the "two-ocean navy" act, 1940; the draft (Selective Service Act, 1940)).
- **Interventionist advocates** : President Woodrow Wilson (1913-1921), President Franklin D. Roosevelt (1933-1945), Secretary of State Cordell Hull (1933-1944), the "Committee to Defend America by Aiding the Allies" (1940-1941), "Fight for Freedom" (1941).

## LO 7.C: Explain the effects of the Spanish-American War.

**Contextualization:** The Spanish-American War (1898) lasted only 10 weeks (April-August). It was a "splendid little war" (Secretary of State John Hay). But its effects were profound. The US acquired its first overseas colonies (the Philippines, Guam, Puerto Rico); it also acquired Hawaii (annexed 1898). The US became an imperial power (a "world power"). It also became entangled in a brutal counterinsurgency war in the Philippines (1899-1902). And it reasserted its dominance over the Western Hemisphere (the Platt Amendment, 1901; the Roosevelt Corollary, 1904).

**Thesis:** The effects of the Spanish-American War included **the acquisition of the Philippines, Guam, Puerto Rico, and Hawaii** (the US became an imperial power), **the suppression of the Filipino independence movement** (the Philippine-American War, 1899-1902), **the Platt Amendment (1901)** (the US retained the right to intervene in Cuba, to lease Guantánamo Bay, and to veto Cuban treaties), **the "Insular Cases" (1901-1904)** (the Supreme Court ruled that "the Constitution does not follow the flag" – that is, the full rights of the Constitution (citizenship, trial by jury, habeas corpus) did not automatically apply to the inhabitants of the new territories), and **the rise of the US as a naval power** (the "Great White Fleet" world tour, 1907-1909; the construction of the Panama Canal, 1904-1914).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Effect 1 – Acquisition of the Philippines, Guam, Puerto Rico, and Hawaii (KC-7.3.I.C):**
- **The Philippines** : The US paid Spain $20 million for the Philippines (Treaty of Paris, 1898). The Philippines became a US colony (1898-1946). The US appointed a governor general (William Howard Taft, 1901-1904; Henry Stimson, 1927-1929; Douglas MacArthur, 1935-1937). The US built schools, roads, hospitals, and a modern army. The US also suppressed the Filipino independence movement (the Philippine-American War, 1899-1902).
- **Guam** : The US seized Guam from Spain (June 21, 1898). Guam became a US territory (1898-present). It was a naval base (Apra Harbor). It was occupied by Japan (1941-1944). It became an "unincorporated territory" (the Insular Cases, 1901-1904). Its inhabitants are US citizens (since 1950) but cannot vote for president.
- **Puerto Rico** : The US seized Puerto Rico from Spain (July 25, 1898). Puerto Rico became a US territory (1898-present). It was a military base (San Juan). It was invaded by Germany (1917) – the US responded by strengthening its naval presence. Puerto Rico is an "unincorporated territory" (the Insular Cases). Its inhabitants are US citizens (since 1917) but cannot vote for president. Puerto Rico has a "commonwealth" status (1952). It has its own constitution and elects its own governor (but the US Congress can overrule its laws). Puerto Rico has voted several times on statehood (1967, 1993, 1998, 2012, 2017, 2020 – all non-binding; statehood has never been approved).
- **Hawaii** : The US annexed Hawaii (1898) (see LO 7.A). Hawaii became a US territory (1900). It became the 50th state in 1959.

**Effect 2 – The Philippine-American War (1899-1902) (KC-7.3.I.C):**
- **Background** : The Filipinos (led by Emilio Aguinaldo) had been fighting for independence from Spain (1896-1898). They allied with the US against Spain (the US promised to recognize Filipino independence). After the Treaty of Paris (1898), the US annexed the Philippines. Aguinaldo declared independence (June 12, 1898) and proclaimed the "First Philippine Republic."
- **The war** : The US refused to recognize the Republic. The Filipinos attacked US forces (February 4, 1899). The US sent 125,000 troops (10% of the US Army). The Filipinos fought a guerrilla war (ambushes, sabotage, assassination). The US used "water cure" (waterboarding), concentration camps (to isolate the Filipinos from the guerrillas), and scorched-earth tactics (burning villages, destroying crops). The war was brutal and racist (American soldiers called Filipinos "niggers" and "gooks").
- **Casualties** : 4,300 US soldiers killed; 2,800 wounded; 20,000 Filipino soldiers killed; 200,000 Filipino civilians killed (from starvation, disease, and violence). The war was the deadliest and most expensive (in proportion to the population) in US history (until Vietnam).
- **The war ends (1902)** : President Theodore Roosevelt declared the war over (July 4, 1902). Aguinaldo was captured (1901) and forced to swear allegiance to the US (he later ran for president of the Philippines in 1935 – he lost). Small-scale guerrilla resistance continued until 1913.
- **Legacy** : The Philippine-American War was a "forgotten war." It was overshadowed by World War I. It damaged America's reputation as a "beacon of liberty." It emboldened anti-imperialists (Mark Twain, Andrew Carnegie, William Jennings Bryan). It also provided a "dress rehearsal" for Vietnam (guerrilla war, "hearts and minds," "counterinsurgency," "search and destroy," "waterboarding"). Many of the tactics used in the Philippines were also used in Vietnam.

**Effect 3 – The Platt Amendment (1901) (KC-7.3.I.C):**
- **Background** : Cuba was a Spanish colony (1492-1898). The Teller Amendment (1898) declared that the US would not annex Cuba ("the United States hereby disclaims any disposition or intention to exercise sovereignty, jurisdiction, or control over said island"). But the US wanted to control Cuba (to protect American investments – sugar, tobacco, mining, railroads). The Platt Amendment was a compromise.
- **The Platt Amendment (1901)** : It was attached to the Cuban constitution (the US threatened to keep its troops in Cuba if the amendment was not adopted). The amendment gave the US the right to:
  - Intervene in Cuban affairs (to protect "life, property, and individual liberty")
  - Lease naval bases (Guantánamo Bay – the US still holds the lease, paying Cuba $4,085 per year; Cuba has not cashed the checks since 1959)
  - Veto Cuban treaties with foreign nations
  - Control Cuban debt (the US would approve any loans)
  - The amendment also prohibited Cuba from forming an alliance with a foreign power, from going into debt beyond its ability to pay, and from allowing foreign powers to establish military bases on the island.
- **Repeal** : The Platt Amendment was repealed in 1934 (the Good Neighbor Policy, Franklin D. Roosevelt). But the US retained the lease on Guantánamo Bay.

**Effect 4 – The "Insular Cases" (1901-1904):**
- **Background** : The US acquired new territories (the Philippines, Guam, Puerto Rico, and later Hawaii, Alaska, the Panama Canal Zone, the US Virgin Islands, American Samoa, the Northern Mariana Islands). Did the Constitution apply to these territories? Did the inhabitants have the same rights as US citizens (due process, trial by jury, habeas corpus, freedom of speech, freedom of religion, the right to bear arms)?
- **The Insular Cases** : The Supreme Court ruled (8-1) that "the Constitution does not follow the flag" – that is, the full rights of the Constitution do not automatically apply to the inhabitants of the "unincorporated territories" (territories not intended for statehood). The inhabitants were "foreign in a domestic sense" (they had a "different culture," "different language," "different religion," "different race"). Congress could decide their rights. The Court also ruled that the inhabitants of "incorporated territories" (Alaska, Hawaii, the Panama Canal Zone) had the same rights as US citizens.
- **Effects** : The Insular Cases established a legal distinction between "incorporated" and "unincorporated" territories. They also upheld racial discrimination ("the inhabitants of the Philippines are not 'civilized' in the Anglo-Saxon sense"). The Insular Cases have never been overturned. Puerto Rico, Guam, the US Virgin Islands, American Samoa, and the Northern Mariana Islands are still "unincorporated territories." Their inhabitants are US citizens (except American Samoa) but cannot vote for president and have no voting representation in Congress.

**Effect 5 – The Rise of the US as a Naval Power (KC-7.3.I.C):**
- **The "Great White Fleet" (1907-1909)** : President Theodore Roosevelt sent 16 battleships (the "Great White Fleet" – painted white) on a world tour (1907-1909). The fleet visited 20 ports (Rio de Janeiro, Valparaíso, San Francisco, Honolulu, Auckland, Sydney, Melbourne, Manila, Yokohama, Colombo, Suez, Naples, Gibraltar, New York). The tour demonstrated American naval power and projected American "goodwill" (gunboat diplomacy).
- **The Panama Canal (1904-1914)** : see LO 7.A
- **The US Navy in World War I (1917-1918)** : The US Navy played a key role in the Atlantic (anti-submarine warfare – convoy system) and the Mediterranean (blockading the Austro-Hungarian navy). The US Navy also transported 2 million US soldiers to France.
- **The US Navy in World War II (1941-1945)** : The US Navy defeated the Japanese navy at Midway (1942), the Coral Sea (1942), Leyte Gulf (1944), and Okinawa (1945). The US Navy also built the "two-ocean navy" (1940-1945) – 1,200 warships, 50,000 landing craft, 100 aircraft carriers. The US Navy was the largest and most powerful navy in the world by 1945.

## LO 7.D: Compare the goals and effects of the Progressive reform movement.

**Contextualization:** The Progressive Era (c. 1890-1920) was a period of widespread social activism and political reform. Progressives responded to the excesses of industrial capitalism (monopolies, child labor, unsafe working conditions, slums, political corruption) by calling for government action at the local, state, and federal levels. Progressives were not a unified movement; they included social reformers (Jane Addams, Jacob Riis), political reformers (Robert La Follette, Hiram Johnson, Charles Evans Hughes), antitrust reformers (Louis Brandeis, William Howard Taft, Theodore Roosevelt), conservationists (John Muir, Gifford Pinchot), and women's suffragists (Susan B. Anthony, Elizabeth Cady Stanton, Carrie Chapman Catt, Alice Paul).

**Thesis:** The goals of the Progressive reform movement included **political democracy** (direct primaries, direct election of senators, initiative, referendum, recall, women's suffrage), **social justice** (temperance, child labor laws, factory safety laws, workers' compensation, minimum wage laws, maximum hour laws, pure food and drug laws), and **government regulation** (antitrust enforcement, railroad regulation, banking reform, conservation, municipal ownership of utilities); the effects of Progressive reform included **the 16th, 17th, 18th, and 19th Amendments** (income tax, direct election of senators, prohibition, women's suffrage), **the breakup of some monopolies** (Standard Oil, American Tobacco), **the creation of regulatory agencies** (the Federal Reserve, the Federal Trade Commission, the Food and Drug Administration, the National Park Service), and **the expansion of state and federal power** to regulate the economy and protect the welfare of citizens.

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Goal 1 – Political Democracy (KC-7.1.II.D, KC-7.1.II.D, KC-7.1.II.D, KC-7.1.II.D):**
- **Direct primaries** : Before the Progressive Era, party bosses chose candidates (in "smoke-filled rooms"). Progressives demanded "direct primaries" (voters choose candidates). Wisconsin (1903) was the first state to adopt direct primaries; by 1917, 44 states had them.
- **Direct election of senators** : Before the 17th Amendment (1913), US senators were chosen by state legislatures (not by the people). The "Senate was a millionaire's club" (a "rich man's club"). Progressives demanded direct election of senators. The 17th Amendment (1913) provided for "the election of senators by the people."
- **Initiative, referendum, and recall** :
  - **Initiative** : Citizens could propose laws (by petition) and force a vote on them (without going through the legislature). South Dakota (1898) was the first state to adopt the initiative.
  - **Referendum** : Citizens could vote directly on laws passed by the legislature (to veto them). Oregon (1902) was the first state to adopt the referendum.
  - **Recall** : Citizens could remove an elected official from office (by petition and election). Oregon (1908) was the first state to adopt the recall.
- **Women's suffrage** : The 19th Amendment (1920) granted women the right to vote. Wyoming (1869) was the first territory to grant women's suffrage; Wyoming (1890) was the first state. The National American Woman Suffrage Association (NAWSA, led by Carrie Chapman Catt) and the National Woman's Party (NWP, led by Alice Paul) campaigned for the amendment. The amendment was ratified in 1920. It was a major victory for the Progressive movement.

**Goal 2 – Social Justice (KC-7.1.II.A, KC-7.1.II.A, KC-7.1.II.A, KC-7.1.II.A, KC-7.1.II.B, KC-7.1.II.B):**
- **Temperance (prohibition)** : The 18th Amendment (1919) prohibited "the manufacture, sale, or transportation of intoxicating liquors." The Women's Christian Temperance Union (WCTU, founded 1874) and the Anti-Saloon League (founded 1893) campaigned for prohibition. They argued that alcohol caused poverty, crime, family violence, and moral decay. Prohibition was a failure (it led to bootlegging, speakeasies, and organized crime). It was repealed by the 21st Amendment (1933).
- **Child labor laws** : By 1900, 1.7 million children under 16 worked for wages. They worked in textile mills (the "lint-heads"), coal mines (the "breaker boys"), glass factories (the "glory hole"), canneries, and sweatshops. Progressives campaigned for state child labor laws (73 laws between 1902 and 1915). The Keating-Owen Act (1916) banned the interstate shipment of goods made by child labor (the Supreme Court struck it down in 1918). The Fair Labor Standards Act (1938) established a federal minimum wage and banned child labor (after this period).
- **Factory safety laws** : The **Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire (1911)** killed 146 garment workers (mostly young Jewish and Italian women). The doors were locked (to prevent theft). The fire escape collapsed. The fire department's ladders reached only the 6th floor (the fire was on the 8th, 9th, and 10th floors). The fire led to the passage of factory safety laws (sprinkler systems, fire drills, unlocked doors, fire escapes, and "building codes").
- **Workers' compensation laws** : Before workers' compensation, injured workers had to sue their employers (and prove negligence). Most lost. Progressives campaigned for "workers' compensation" (no-fault insurance for workplace injuries). Wisconsin (1911) was the first state to adopt workers' compensation; by 1920, 42 states had it.
- **Minimum wage laws** : Massachusetts (1912) was the first state to adopt a minimum wage law (for women and children). By 1923, 15 states had minimum wage laws (the Supreme Court struck them down in 1923, but they were re-enacted in the 1930s).
- **Maximum hour laws** : The Supreme Court upheld a 10-hour day for women (*Muller v. Oregon*, 1908) and a 10-hour day for bakers (*Lochner v. New York*, 1905 – the Court struck down a 10-hour day for bakers, ruling that it violated the "right to contract" under the 14th Amendment; *Lochner* was later overruled). Progressives campaigned for the 8-hour day for federal workers (the 8-hour day was established for federal workers in 1868; for federal contractors in 1892; for the Post Office in 1912; for the railroad industry in 1916; for all interstate commerce workers in 1938).
- **Pure food and drug laws** : Upton Sinclair's *The Jungle* (1906) exposed unsanitary conditions in the Chicago meatpacking industry ("I aimed at the public's heart and by accident I hit it in the stomach"). The **Pure Food and Drug Act (1906)** prohibited "the manufacture, sale, or transportation of adulterated or misbranded food, drugs, medicines, and liquors." The **Meat Inspection Act (1906)** authorized the USDA to inspect meat (the "Federal Meat Inspection Service").

**Goal 3 – Government Regulation (KC-7.1.II.B, KC-7.1.II.B, KC-7.1.II.B, KC-7.1.II.C):**
- **Antitrust enforcement** : The Sherman Antitrust Act (1890) was weak and poorly enforced. President Theodore Roosevelt ("Trust-Buster") sued 44 trusts (including the Northern Securities Company (railroad monopoly), Standard Oil, and American Tobacco). The Supreme Court ordered the breakup of Standard Oil (1911) and American Tobacco (1911). President William Howard Taft ("Trust-Buster") sued 90 trusts (more than Roosevelt). President Woodrow Wilson signed the **Clayton Antitrust Act (1914)** , which prohibited price discrimination, exclusive dealing, and interlocking directorates (the "third degree").
- **Railroad regulation** : The Interstate Commerce Act (1887) was weak (the ICC had no power to set rates). The **Elkins Act (1903)** prohibited rebates (discounts) and required railroads to publish their rates. The **Hepburn Act (1906)** gave the ICC the power to set maximum rates (the "long-and-short haul clause").
- **Banking reform** : The Panic of 1907 (a run on banks) led to the creation of the **Federal Reserve System (1913)** . The Fed was a central bank (the "lender of last resort"). It was designed to prevent bank failures and stabilize the economy. The Fed was responsible for monetary policy (controlling the money supply) and bank regulation. The **Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC)** was created in 1933 (after the bank failures of the Great Depression).
- **Conservation** : President Theodore Roosevelt set aside 150 million acres of public land for national forests (the "Working Man's National Park"), national parks (Yosemite, Sequoia, Mount Rainier, Crater Lake, Mesa Verde), and national monuments (the Grand Canyon, Devil's Tower, Muir Woods, the Gila Cliff Dwellings). The **Newlands Reclamation Act (1902)** funded irrigation projects (dams, canals, reservoirs) to "reclaim" arid land for agriculture. The **National Park Service (1916)** was created to manage the national parks.
- **Municipal ownership of utilities** : Many cities owned their water, gas, electricity, and streetcar systems (the "public ownership" movement). The "city beautiful" movement (Daniel Burnham, Frederick Law Olmsted) created parks, boulevards, and civic centers to improve the quality of urban life.

**Effects of Progressive Reform (KC-7.1.II.B, KC-7.1.II.B, KC-7.1.II.B, KC-7.1.II.B, KC-7.1.II.B):**
- **The 16th Amendment (1913)** : The income tax. The Supreme Court had struck down an income tax in 1895. The 16th Amendment allowed Congress to "lay and collect taxes on incomes, from whatever source derived, without apportionment among the several states." The income tax was designed to "soak the rich" (the top tax rate was 7% in 1913, 70% in 1918, 90% in 1950). It became the largest source of federal revenue.
- **The 17th Amendment (1913)** : Direct election of senators (see above).
- **The 18th Amendment (1919)** : Prohibition (see above). The 21st Amendment (1933) repealed it.
- **The 19th Amendment (1920)** : Women's suffrage (see above).
- **The Federal Reserve System (1913)** : see above.
- **The Federal Trade Commission (FTC, 1914)** : The FTC enforced antitrust laws and protected consumers from "unfair methods of competition."
- **The Food and Drug Administration (FDA, 1906)** : The FDA enforced the Pure Food and Drug Act and the Meat Inspection Act.
- **The National Park Service (NPS, 1916)** : The NPS managed the national parks and monuments.
- **The breakup of monopolies** : Standard Oil (1911) was broken into 34 separate companies (Exxon, Mobil, Chevron, Amoco, Sohio, etc.). American Tobacco (1911) was broken into 16 separate companies (Liggett & Myers, Lorillard, R.J. Reynolds, P. Lorillard, etc.). The "trust-busting" era did not end monopoly; it simply replaced one monopoly with a few large competitors (oligopoly).
- **The expansion of state and federal power** : The Progressive movement expanded the power of government to regulate the economy, protect the welfare of citizens, and conserve natural resources. This was a sharp departure from the laissez-faire ideology of the Gilded Age. The "New Deal" (1933-1938) and the "Great Society" (1964-1968) built on the Progressive legacy.

## LO 7.E: Compare attitudes toward the use of natural resources from 1890 to 1945.

**Contextualization:** The "closing of the frontier" (1890) and the rapid depletion of natural resources (forests, water, minerals, wildlife) led to a debate about how to use America's natural resources. "Conservationists" (Gifford Pinchot, President Theodore Roosevelt) believed that resources should be used "sustainably" (for the greatest good for the greatest number for the longest time). "Preservationists" (John Muir, the Sierra Club) believed that resources should be preserved "forever" (as wilderness, for its own sake).

**Thesis:** Attitudes toward the use of natural resources from 1890 to 1945 were divided between **conservationists** (Gifford Pinchot, Theodore Roosevelt, the US Forest Service) who advocated for the "wise use" of resources (multiple use – timber, grazing, mining, recreation, water power) and **preservationists** (John Muir, the Sierra Club) who advocated for the "protection" of resources (wilderness – no logging, no grazing, no mining, no dams); the conservationists won most of the policy battles (the Newlands Reclamation Act 1902, the US Forest Service 1905, the National Park Service 1916), but the preservationists succeeded in protecting some wilderness areas (Yosemite, Sequoia, Mount Rainier, Crater Lake, Mesa Verde, the Grand Canyon).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Conservationists – "Wise Use" (KC-7.1.II.C):**
- **Gifford Pinchot (1865-1946)** : The first chief of the US Forest Service (1905-1910). Pinchot defined conservation as "the wise use of the earth and its resources for the lasting good of men." He believed that resources should be used "sustainably" (for the "greatest good for the greatest number for the longest time"). He supported logging, grazing, mining, and water power (but with regulation). He opposed "preservation" (setting aside land as wilderness, with no use). Pinchot was a Republican (he served as governor of Pennsylvania, 1923-1927, 1931-1935). He wrote *The Fight for Conservation* (1910).
- **President Theodore Roosevelt (1901-1909)** : "The conservation of our natural resources is the most weighty question now before this nation." Roosevelt set aside 150 million acres of public land for national forests (the "Working Man's National Park"), national parks (Yosemite, Sequoia, Mount Rainier, Crater Lake, Mesa Verde), and national monuments (the Grand Canyon, Devil's Tower, Muir Woods, the Gila Cliff Dwellings). He created the **US Forest Service (1905)** and appointed Pinchot as its chief. He supported the **Newlands Reclamation Act (1902)** , which funded irrigation projects (dams, canals, reservoirs) to "reclaim" arid land for agriculture.
- **The US Forest Service (1905)** : The Forest Service managed the national forests (150 million acres) for "multiple use" (timber, grazing, mining, recreation, water power). The Forest Service also fought wildfires (Smokey Bear, 1944). The Forest Service was a model for other federal land management agencies (the Bureau of Land Management, the National Park Service).
- **The Newlands Reclamation Act (1902)** : The Act funded irrigation projects in the arid West. The money came from the sale of public land (the "Reclamation Fund"). The Act led to the construction of the Roosevelt Dam (Arizona), the Shoshone Dam (Wyoming), the Arrowrock Dam (Idaho), the Owyhee Dam (Oregon), the Klamath Project (Oregon-California), and the Grand Valley Project (Colorado). The Act transformed the West (allowing agriculture in areas that were previously desert).

**Preservationists – "Wilderness" (KC-7.1.II.C):**
- **John Muir (1838-1914)** : A Scottish-American naturalist and writer. Muir was the founder of the **Sierra Club (1892)** . He believed that nature was sacred (a "cathedral of the wilderness"). He opposed the "wise use" of nature (logging, grazing, mining, dams). He advocated for "preservation" (setting aside land as wilderness, with no human use). Muir wrote *The Mountains of California* (1894), *Our National Parks* (1901), *The Yosemite* (1912), *Travels in Alaska* (1915). He was a friend of President Theodore Roosevelt (they camped together in Yosemite in 1903).
- **The Sierra Club (1892)** : The Sierra Club was the first environmental organization in the United States. It advocated for the preservation of wilderness (the "wilderness idea"). It also organized hiking, camping, and mountaineering trips. It lobbied for the creation of national parks (Yosemite, Sequoia, Mount Rainier, Crater Lake, Mesa Verde, the Grand Canyon, Glacier, Rocky Mountain, Yellowstone). The Sierra Club later expanded its mission to include "environmental justice" (the protection of the environment for all people, not just the wealthy).
- **The Hetch Hetchy controversy (1908-1913)** : The city of San Francisco wanted to build a dam in the Hetch Hetchy Valley (Yosemite National Park) to supply water and hydroelectric power. Muir and the Sierra Club opposed the dam (they wanted to preserve the valley as wilderness). Pinchot and Roosevelt supported the dam (they argued that the valley was not as beautiful as Yosemite Valley, and that water for a growing city was a "higher use"). Congress approved the dam (1913). The O'Shaughnessy Dam was completed in 1923. The Hetch Hetchy Valley was flooded. The controversy split the conservation movement (Pinchot vs. Muir). It also led to the creation of the National Park Service (1916) to manage the parks and prevent such decisions from being made by Congress (which often favored "development" over "preservation").

**The National Park Service (1916) (KC-7.1.II.C):**
- The **National Park Service (NPS)** was created by the **National Park Service Organic Act (1916)** . The Act declared that the NPS should "conserve the scenery and the natural and historic objects and the wild life therein and to provide for the enjoyment of the same in such manner and by such means as will leave them unimpaired for the enjoyment of future generations." The NPS was a compromise between conservation (use) and preservation (non-use). The NPS manages national parks (61), national monuments (84), national historic sites (79), national battlefields (11), national seashores (10), and other units (total 423 units). The NPS also manages the National Register of Historic Places.

**The Conflict Between Conservation and Preservation (KC-7.1.II.C):**
- **The Grand Canyon** : President Theodore Roosevelt declared the Grand Canyon a national monument (1908). Congress declared it a national park (1919). The park was protected from mining, logging, and dams. (The "Bridge Canyon Dam" was proposed in the 1960s; the Sierra Club fought it; the dam was never built.)
- **The Everglades** : The Everglades were drained for agriculture (sugar, vegetables, cattle) in the early 20th century. The **Everglades National Park** was established in 1934 (dedicated 1947). The park protected the remaining wetlands (but the damage was already done; the Everglades ecosystem was severely damaged).
- **The Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA, 1933)** : President Franklin D. Roosevelt created the TVA to build dams, generate electricity, control floods, and promote economic development in the Tennessee Valley. The TVA built 29 dams (including the Norris Dam, the Wheeler Dam, the Wilson Dam, the Pickwick Landing Dam, the Kentucky Dam). The TVA was a "conservation" project (multiple use: flood control, navigation, electricity, recreation, fertilizer). The TVA also displaced 15,000 families (including 4,000 Black families) and flooded 1 million acres.

The Wilderness Act created the National Wilderness Preservation System (9 million acres in 1964; 110 million acres today). Wilderness areas are "untrammeled by man" (no roads, no buildings, no logging, no mining, no dams, no motorized vehicles, no motorized equipment). This was a victory for the preservationists.

## LO 7.F: Explain the causes and consequences of U.S. involvement in World War I.

**Contextualization:** When World War I began in Europe (August 1914), President Woodrow Wilson declared the United States neutral. Most Americans favored neutrality (they did not want to fight another European war). But the US was not neutral in practice: it traded with the Allies (Britain, France, Russia) and refused to trade with the Central Powers (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Turkey). Germany responded with "unrestricted submarine warfare" (U-boats) to sink ships trading with Britain. The sinking of the *Lusitania* (1915) and the *Sussex* (1916) outraged Americans. Germany agreed to stop unrestricted submarine warfare (the "Sussex Pledge," 1916). But in January 1917, Germany announced that it would resume unrestricted submarine warfare (to starve Britain into submission). At the same time, the British intercepted the "Zimmermann Telegram" (a German proposal to ally with Mexico against the US). Wilson asked Congress for a declaration of war (April 2, 1917). Congress declared war on April 6, 1917.

**Thesis:** The causes of US involvement in World War I included **unrestricted submarine warfare** (the sinking of American ships by German U-boats), the **Zimmermann Telegram** (a German proposal to ally with Mexico against the US), and the **ideology of "making the world safe for democracy"** (President Wilson's idealistic vision); the consequences of US involvement included **the defeat of Germany** (the American Expeditionary Force tipped the balance of the war in favor of the Allies), **the Treaty of Versailles** (which imposed harsh terms on Germany, including reparations and the "war guilt" clause), **the League of Nations** (Wilson's "baby"), and **the Senate's rejection of the Treaty** (the US never joined the League).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Causes – Unrestricted Submarine Warfare (KC-7.3.II.A):**
- **The British blockade** : Britain used its navy to blockade Germany (cutting off trade). The US wanted to trade with both sides (but the British blockade made trade with Germany impossible). Germany responded with "unrestricted submarine warfare" (U-boats would sink any ship trading with Britain, including neutral ships).
- **The *Lusitania* (May 7, 1915)** : A German U-boat sank the British passenger liner *Lusitania* (off the coast of Ireland). 1,198 people died (including 128 Americans). The *Lusitania* was carrying ammunition (it was a legitimate military target). The American public was outraged. President Wilson demanded that Germany stop unrestricted submarine warfare. Germany agreed (temporarily) – the "Arabic Pledge" (1915) and the "Sussex Pledge" (1916) promised not to sink passenger ships without warning.
- **The *Sussex* (March 24, 1916)** : A German U-boat torpedoed the French passenger ferry *Sussex* (in the English Channel). 50 people died (including 4 Americans). Wilson threatened to break off diplomatic relations with Germany. Germany issued the "Sussex Pledge" (promising not to sink merchant ships without warning and not to sink passenger ships at all). The pledge was conditional: Germany would resume unrestricted submarine warfare if the US did not persuade Britain to end its blockade.
- **The resumption of unrestricted submarine warfare (January 31, 1917)** : Germany announced that it would sink any ship (neutral or belligerent) in the war zone around Britain. Germany believed that unrestricted submarine warfare would starve Britain into submission within 6 months (before the US could send troops to Europe). The US broke off diplomatic relations with Germany (February 3, 1917).

**Causes – The Zimmermann Telegram (1917) (KC-7.3.II.A):**
- **The telegram** : The British intercepted a telegram from German Foreign Secretary Arthur Zimmermann to the German ambassador in Mexico (January 16, 1917). Zimmermann proposed a German-Mexican alliance: Mexico would attack the US (to recover "lost territory" – Texas, New Mexico, Arizona); Germany would provide financial and military support. The telegram also suggested that Mexico should invite Japan to join the alliance.
- **The leak** : The British leaked the telegram to the American press (February 28, 1917). The American public was outraged. President Wilson released the telegram to Congress (March 1, 1917). The telegram was a "smoking gun" – proof that Germany was plotting against the US.
- **The "Zimmermann Telegram" was a major factor in pushing the US into the war** . Wilson asked Congress for a declaration of war on April 2, 1917. Congress declared war on April 6, 1917 (82-6 in the Senate; 373-50 in the House).

**Causes – "Making the World Safe for Democracy" (KC-7.3.II.A):**
- Wilson's war message was idealistic: "The world must be made safe for democracy. Its peace must be planted upon the tested foundations of political liberty. We have no selfish ends to serve. We desire no conquest, no dominion. We seek no indemnities for ourselves, no material compensation for the sacrifices we shall freely make. We are but one of the champions of the rights of mankind."
- Wilson's "Fourteen Points" (January 8, 1918) outlined his vision for the post-war world:
  1. Open diplomacy (no secret treaties)
  2. Freedom of the seas (unrestricted trade)
  3. Free trade (lower tariffs)
  4. Disarmament (reduce arms)
  5. Self-determination for colonial peoples (the people should choose their own government)
  6. Self-determination for ethnic groups in Europe (the "Balkan" question)
  7. Self-determination for Belgium (Germany must evacuate)
  8. Self-determination for Alsace-Lorraine (France should recover it)
  9. Self-determination for Italy (Italian-speaking areas should be part of Italy)
  10. Self-determination for Austria-Hungary (ethnic groups should have autonomy)
  11. Self-determination for the Balkans (Serbia should have access to the sea)
  12. Self-determination for the Dardanelles (the straits should be open to all nations)
  13. Self-determination for Poland (Poland should be independent)
  14. A "League of Nations" (an international organization to prevent future wars)
- Wilson's idealism was popular in the US and Europe. But the Allies (Britain, France, Italy) were not idealistic; they wanted revenge (reparations, colonies, disarmament of Germany).

**Consequences – The Defeat of Germany (KC-7.3.II.B):**
- **The American Expeditionary Force (AEF)** : General John J. Pershing commanded the AEF. 2 million US soldiers served in France; 1.2 million saw combat. The AEF was an "independent" army (not integrated into the British or French armies). Pershing insisted on US divisions fighting under US command.
- **The Meuse-Argonne Offensive (September-November 1918)** : The largest battle in US history (1.2 million US troops, 26,000 killed, 95,000 wounded). The AEF broke through the German lines (at the Meuse River and the Argonne Forest). The German army was exhausted, demoralized, and outnumbered. The German High Command asked for an armistice (cease-fire).
- **The Armistice (November 11, 1918)** : The armistice went into effect at 11:00 AM on November 11, 1918 ("the eleventh hour of the eleventh day of the eleventh month"). Germany surrendered. The war was over.

**Consequences – The Treaty of Versailles (1919) (KC-7.3.II.C):**
- **The "Big Four"** : Wilson (US), Lloyd George (Britain), Clemenceau (France), Orlando (Italy). The "Big Four" negotiated the treaty. Wilson wanted a "peace without victory" (no reparations, no annexations, self-determination, a League of Nations). Clemenceau wanted revenge (Germany must pay). The treaty was a compromise.
- **The treaty's terms** :
  - **Territory** : Germany lost Alsace-Lorraine (to France), Eupen-Malmédy (to Belgium), North Schleswig (to Denmark), West Prussia and Posen (to Poland), Silesia (to Poland), Danzig (to the League of Nations), Memel (to Lithuania), and its colonies (Togo, Cameroon, South-West Africa, Tanganyika, New Guinea, the Marshall Islands, the Marianas, the Carolines).
  - **Military** : Germany's army was limited to 100,000 men (no tanks, no airplanes, no poison gas). Germany's navy was limited to 15,000 men (no submarines, no battleships). Germany was forbidden to have an air force. The Rhineland was demilitarized (no German troops).
  - **Reparations** : Germany had to pay reparations (132 billion gold marks – $33 billion). The reparations were to be paid over 30 years. Germany defaulted in 1923 (the "Ruhr crisis"). The Dawes Plan (1924) and the Young Plan (1929) reduced the reparations and extended the payment period.
  - **War guilt** : Article 231 of the treaty (the "war guilt" clause) declared that Germany (and Austria) were responsible for causing the war. This clause was used to justify reparations. It was deeply resented by Germans (who believed that the treaty was a "diktat" – a dictated peace).
- **The League of Nations** : The treaty created the League of Nations (an international organization to prevent future wars). Wilson was the "father" of the League. The League had no army; it relied on collective security (an attack on one member is an attack on all members). The US never joined the League (see below).

**Consequences – The Senate's Rejection of the Treaty (1919-1920) (KC-7.3.II.C):**
- **The "Lodge Reservations"** : Senator Henry Cabot Lodge (Massachusetts) was the chairman of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee. Lodge was a Republican; Wilson was a Democrat. Lodge opposed the League of Nations because Article X of the League Covenant (collective security) might require the US to go to war without Congressional approval. Lodge proposed "reservations" (amendments) to the treaty:
  - The US would assume no obligation to go to war (Article X) unless Congress declared war.
  - The US would not be bound by decisions of the League Council (the "executive committee" of the League) unless Congress approved.
- **Wilson's refusal to compromise** : Wilson refused to accept any changes to the treaty (he believed that the treaty was "sacred"). He went on a nationwide speaking tour (September 1919) to rally support for the treaty. He collapsed in Pueblo, Colorado (September 25, 1919) and suffered a stroke (October 2, 1919). He was paralyzed on his left side and partially blind. His wife Edith managed the White House (she decided who saw the president and what documents he saw).
- **The Senate votes (November 19, 1919)** : The Senate rejected the treaty (with the Lodge Reservations) (39-55). The Senate also rejected the treaty (without reservations) (38-53). The Senate voted again (March 19, 1920) – 49-35 (still short of the 2/3 majority needed). The US never joined the League of Nations.
- **The "Knox-Porter Resolution" (1921)** : The US negotiated separate peace treaties with Germany, Austria, and Hungary. The treaties were ratified by the Senate (July 1921). The US did not join the League of Nations.

**Other Consequences – The Home Front (KC-7.3.II.A, KC-7.3.II.A, KC-7.3.II.A, KC-7.3.II.A, KC-7.3.II.A, KC-7.3.II.A, KC-7.3.II.A, KC-7.3.II.A):**
- **The Selective Service Act (1917)** : The US drafted 2.8 million men (age 21-30, later age 18-45). The draft was controversial (the "draft riots" in New York City, 1917 – 100,000 people protested; the "Green Corn Rebellion" in Oklahoma, 1917 – 300 farmers refused to register; they were arrested). The Draft was upheld by the Supreme Court (*Selective Draft Law Cases*, 1918).
- **The "Liberty Loan" bonds** : The US government sold $20 billion in bonds (to finance the war). Hollywood stars (Charlie Chaplin, Mary Pickford, Douglas Fairbanks) appeared in "Liberty Loan" rallies. Children bought "Thrift Stamps" (25 cents). The "Four-Minute Men" gave speeches (4 minutes) before movies, urging Americans to buy bonds.
- **The "Espionage Act" (1917) and the "Sedition Act" (1918)** : These laws prohibited "disloyal, profane, scurrilous, or abusive language" about the US government, the Constitution, the flag, the uniform, or the war effort. 2,000 people were prosecuted; 1,000 were convicted. The most famous case was *Schenck v. United States* (1919): Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes wrote that free speech could be limited when it presented a "clear and present danger" – "The most stringent protection of free speech would not protect a man in falsely shouting fire in a theater and causing a panic."
- **The "Industrial Workers of the World (IWW)"** : The IWW (the "Wobblies") opposed the war (they called it a "capitalist war"). The IWW was suppressed by the federal government (the "Espionage Act"). 100 IWW leaders were arrested (1917); they were tried and convicted (1918); they were sentenced to 20 years in prison (most were pardoned in 1923).
- **The "Palmer Raids" (1919-1920)** : Attorney General A. Mitchell Palmer (a Quaker) arrested 10,000 suspected "radicals" (anarchists, communists, socialists, union members). 600 were deported (including Emma Goldman, Alexander Berkman). The "Red Scare" (1919-1920) was a period of intense anti-communist hysteria (the "Bolshevik Revolution" in Russia, 1917, gave rise to fears of a communist takeover in the US).
- **The "Great Migration" (1916-1970)** : The war cut off immigration from Europe (the "Immigration Act of 1917" required a literacy test; the "Emergency Quota Act of 1921" and the "Immigration Act of 1924" limited immigration). Northern factories needed workers. 1.5 million African Americans moved from the rural South to the urban North (Chicago, Detroit, Cleveland, New York, Philadelphia, Pittsburgh, Boston, St. Louis, Milwaukee, Gary, Buffalo). The "Great Migration" reshaped American cities and created the "Harlem Renaissance" (1920s).
- **The "Harlem Renaissance" (1920s)** : The Harlem Renaissance was a flowering of African American art, literature, music, and theater. Writers (Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Claude McKay, Jean Toomer, Countee Cullen), musicians (Duke Ellington, Louis Armstrong, Bessie Smith, Billie Holiday, Jelly Roll Morton, Fats Waller), and artists (Aaron Douglas, Jacob Lawrence, Romare Bearden) emerged. The Harlem Renaissance was a "coming of age" for African American culture.
- **The "Liberty Cabbage" and "Liberty Sausage"** : German culture was suppressed (German language schools were closed; German books were banned; German music was banned; "sauerkraut" became "liberty cabbage"; "hamburger" became "liberty sausage"; German measles became "liberty measles"; German shepherds became "Alsace dogs"); the "American Protective League" (250,000 volunteers) spied on "disloyal" Americans (they checked draft registrations, reported "suspicious" activity, and enforced the "Espionage Act").

## LO 7.G: Explain the causes and effects of international and internal migration patterns over time.

**Contextualization:** Between 1890 and 1945, the US experienced massive international and internal migration. International migration: 15 million immigrants arrived from Europe (Italy, Poland, Russia, Austria-Hungary, Greece, Turkey, the Baltic states, and the Balkan states) and Asia (China, Japan, the Philippines, India). Internal migration: African Americans moved from the rural South to the urban North (the "Great Migration"); white Southerners moved to the North and West (the "Hillbilly Highway"); Dust Bowl refugees moved from the Great Plains to California (the "Okies"); and farmers moved to cities (rural-to-urban migration).

**Thesis:** International and internal migration patterns from 1890 to 1945 were caused by **push factors** (poverty, persecution, war, famine, environmental disaster) and **pull factors** (industrialization, jobs, land, freedom, family reunification); the effects of these migrations included **the passage of restrictive immigration laws** (the Chinese Exclusion Act 1882, the Gentlemen's Agreement 1907, the Immigration Act of 1917, the Emergency Quota Act 1921, the Immigration Act of 1924), **the creation of ethnic neighborhoods** (Little Italy, Chinatown, Little Poland, Little Russia, Little Germany, Greektown, Koreatown, Japantown, Little India), **the growth of cities** (New York, Chicago, Philadelphia, Detroit, Cleveland, Boston, St. Louis, Pittsburgh, San Francisco, Los Angeles), **the "Great Migration" and the Harlem Renaissance** (African American culture), and **the "Dust Bowl" and the "Okie" migration** (the "Grapes of Wrath").

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**International Migration – Causes (KC-7.2.II.A.i, KC-7.2.II.A.i, KC-7.2.II.A.i):**
- **Push factors** :
  - **Poverty** : Southern Italy, Poland, Russia, and Austria-Hungary were poor. Overpopulation, landlessness, and unemployment drove people to emigrate.
  - **Persecution** : Russian Jews fled pogroms (state-sponsored massacres) in the Russian Empire (1881-1882, 1903-1906, 1919-1921). Armenians fled persecution in the Ottoman Empire (the Armenian Genocide, 1915-1923). Greeks fled persecution in Turkey (the "Asia Minor Disaster," 1922-1923).
  - **War** : World War I (1914-1918) displaced millions of Europeans. The Russian Revolution (1917) and the Russian Civil War (1917-1922) drove refugees to the US. The Mexican Revolution (1910-1920) drove 500,000-1 million Mexicans to the US.
  - **Famine** : The Irish Potato Famine (1845-1852) was a "push" factor (but it was before 1890). The "Great Famine" (1932-1933) in the Soviet Union (Holodomor – "death by hunger") drove few to the US (the Soviet government prevented emigration).
- **Pull factors** :
  - **Industrialization** : Factories, mills, mines, and railroads needed workers. Wages were higher in the US than in Europe. A factory worker in Chicago earned $500/year; a farm laborer in Poland earned $100/year.
  - **Land** : The Homestead Act (1862) offered 160 acres of free land (for 5 years of residency and improvement). Railroads sold land for $2-5 per acre (on credit).
  - **Freedom** : The US was a democracy (European countries were monarchies or empires). The US had freedom of speech, freedom of the press, freedom of religion, and freedom of assembly (the Bill of Rights). The US had no state religion (no "established church"). The US had no draft (until 1917-1918, and 1940).
  - **Family reunification** : "Chain migration" – immigrants followed family members, friends, and neighbors who had already emigrated.

**International Migration – Effects (KC-7.2.II.A.ii, KC-7.2.II.A.ii):**
- **Restrictive immigration laws** :
  - **Chinese Exclusion Act (1882)** : Banned Chinese laborers (skilled and unskilled) for 10 years. Renewed 1892, made permanent 1902, repealed 1943 (China was an ally in World War II).
  - **Gentlemen's Agreement (1907)** : Japan agreed to stop issuing passports to laborers; the US agreed to desegregate San Francisco schools (Japanese children were sent to "Chinese" schools). The agreement was in effect until 1924 (the Immigration Act of 1924 banned Japanese immigration).
  - **Immigration Act of 1917** : Required a literacy test (immigrants must be able to read and write in their own language). Created an "Asiatic Barred Zone" (banned immigration from most of Asia). The Act also increased the "head tax" ($8 per immigrant).
  - **Emergency Quota Act (1921)** : Limited immigration to 3% of each nationality's population in the US (based on the 1910 census). This favored Northern and Western Europeans (Britain, Ireland, Germany, Scandinavia) and discriminated against Southern and Eastern Europeans (Italy, Poland, Russia, Greece, Turkey).
  - **Immigration Act of 1924 (Johnson-Reed Act)** : Limited immigration to 2% of each nationality's population in the US (based on the 1890 census). The 1890 census had fewer Southern and Eastern Europeans (because they arrived after 1890). The Act also banned all Asian immigration (including Japanese). The Act did not limit immigration from the Western Hemisphere (Canada, Mexico, Central America, South America). The quotas were not abolished until 1965 (the Hart-Celler Act).
- **Ethnic neighborhoods** :
  - **Little Italy** : New York City (Mulberry Street, the "Italian Ghetto"), Chicago (Taylor Street), Boston (North End), Philadelphia (South Philadelphia), San Francisco (North Beach).
  - **Chinatown** : San Francisco (Grant Street), New York (Mott Street), Chicago (Cermak Road), Boston (Harrison Avenue), Los Angeles (Broadway).
  - **Little Poland** : Chicago (Milwaukee Avenue, the "Polish Triangle"), Detroit (Hamtramck), Buffalo (Broadway), Milwaukee (Mitchell Street).
  - **Little Russia** : New York City (East 7th Street, the "Ukrainian Village"), Chicago (Ukrainian Village), San Francisco (Russian Hill).
  - **Little Germany** : New York City (Kleindeutschland, "Little Germany," on the Lower East Side; destroyed by the General Slocum steamship disaster, 1904), Chicago (Lincoln Square), Milwaukee (Beer Town), St. Louis (Soulard), Cincinnati (Over-the-Rhine).
  - **Greektown** : Chicago (Halsted Street), Baltimore (Greektown), Detroit (Greektown).
  - **Koreatown** : Los Angeles (Olympic Boulevard, 6th Street), New York (Murray Hill, 32nd Street).
  - **Japantown** : San Francisco (Fillmore Street, 1820-1942; the term "Japantown" was not used until the 1960s; before that, it was "the Western Addition" or "Nihonmachi"), Los Angeles (Little Tokyo), Chicago (Lakeview), Seattle (Nihonmachi), Portland (Nihonmachi).
  - **Little India** : New York City (Jackson Heights, Queens), Chicago (Devon Avenue), Los Angeles (Artesia, "Little India").
- **Growth of cities** : New York: 3.4 million (1900) → 5.6 million (1920) → 7.9 million (1940). Chicago: 1.7 million (1900) → 2.7 million (1920) → 3.4 million (1940). Philadelphia: 1.3 million (1900) → 1.8 million (1920) → 1.9 million (1940). Detroit: 285,000 (1900) → 1 million (1920) → 1.6 million (1940). Cleveland: 381,000 (1900) → 796,000 (1920) → 878,000 (1940). Boston: 560,000 (1900) → 748,000 (1920) → 770,000 (1940). St. Louis: 575,000 (1900) → 748,000 (1920) → 816,000 (1940). Pittsburgh: 321,000 (1900) → 588,000 (1920) → 671,000 (1940). San Francisco: 342,000 (1900) → 506,000 (1920) → 770,000 (1940). Los Angeles: 102,000 (1900) → 576,000 (1920) → 1.5 million (1940).

**Internal Migration – The "Great Migration" (1916-1970) (KC-7.2.II.C):**
- **Causes** :
  - **Push factors** : Jim Crow segregation (separate but unequal), disenfranchisement (poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses), racial violence (lynching, the KKK, the Colfax massacre, the Hamburg massacre, the Wilmington coup, the Atlanta race riot, the Springfield race riot), the "boll weevil" (a beetle that destroyed cotton crops), the "Great Mississippi Flood of 1927" (displaced 300,000 African Americans), and the "Great Depression" (1930s).
  - **Pull factors** : Industrialization (factories in the North needed workers), higher wages (a factory worker in Chicago earned $2-3 per day; a farm laborer in Alabama earned $0.50-1.00 per day), and the "New Negro" (the "Harlem Renaissance" – the "coming of age" of African American culture).
- **Effects** :
  - **The "Harlem Renaissance" (1920s)** : see LO 7.F
  - **The "New Negro"** : The "New Negro" was proud, assertive, and "race conscious." He (and she) rejected the "accommodationist" stance of Booker T. Washington (the "Atlanta Compromise"). He (and she) embraced the "talented tenth" (W.E.B. Du Bois) – the 10% of African Americans who were "educated, cultured, and responsible" should lead the race.
  - **The growth of Black cities** : Harlem (New York) (pop. 200,000 in 1930), Bronzeville (Chicago) (pop. 200,000 in 1930), Black Bottom (Detroit), Roxbury (Boston), South Side (Philadelphia), the "Hill District" (Pittsburgh).
  - **The "Great Migration" and the "Great Depression"** : The Depression halted the "Great Migration" (jobs dried up). It resumed in the 1940s (World War II) and 1950s-1960s (the "Second Great Migration").

**Internal Migration – The "Dust Bowl" and the "Okie" Migration (1930s) (KC-7.2.II.B.i):**
- **Causes** : The "Dust Bowl" was a period of severe dust storms (1930-1936) on the Great Plains (the "Southern Plains" – Texas, Oklahoma, Kansas, Colorado, New Mexico). The dust storms were caused by drought (lack of rain) and over-farming (farmers had plowed up the native prairie grass, which held the soil in place). The "Black Blizzards" (dust storms) buried farmhouses, destroyed crops, and killed livestock. 500,000 people were displaced ("Okies" – the nickname for refugees from Oklahoma, Texas, Arkansas, Missouri, Kansas, and Colorado).
- **Effects** :
  - **The "Okie" migration** : 300,000 "Okies" moved to California (the "Grapes of Wrath" – John Steinbeck, 1939). They worked as migrant farm laborers (picking cotton, picking fruit, harvesting vegetables). They were despised by Californians (the "Okies" were "white trash," "poor white trash," "the dust bowl refugees," "the unemployable," "the diseased").
  - **The "Grapes of Wrath" (John Steinbeck, 1939)** : The novel is a realistic portrayal of the "Okie" migration (the Joad family). It won the Pulitzer Prize (1940). It was made into a film (1940, directed by John Ford, starring Henry Fonda). The novel was controversial: it was banned in some schools and libraries (for "vulgar language" and "portraying California growers in a negative light").
  - **The "Dust Bowl" and the "New Deal"** : The "New Deal" (1933-1938) created the **Soil Conservation Service (SCS, 1935)** to prevent soil erosion. The **Resettlement Administration (RA, 1935)** and the **Farm Security Administration (FSA, 1937)** provided loans and assistance to "dust bowl" farmers (and to "Okie" migrants). The **Drought Relief Service (DRS, 1935)** provided food, clothing, and medical care.

**Internal Migration – Rural-to-Urban Migration (KC-7.2.II.B.i, KC-7.2.II.B.i):**
- **Causes** : Industrialization (factories and mills offered higher wages than farms), education (high schools and colleges were in cities), urbanization (cities offered entertainment, culture, and social services), and the "Great Depression" (farmers lost their land; they moved to cities to find work).
- **Effects** : The proportion of Americans living in cities (2,500+ population) grew from 40% (1900) to 51% (1920) to 56% (1940). By 1940, a majority of Americans lived in cities (urbanization). The "city" defined America (not the "frontier," not the "farm").

## LO 7.H: Explain the causes and effects of the innovations in communication and technology in the United States over time.

**Contextualization:** Between 1890 and 1945, a series of technological innovations transformed American life: the automobile, the airplane, the radio, the phonograph, the motion picture, the telephone (already invented, but its use expanded dramatically), the electric light (already invented, but its use expanded), and the computer (the "Mark I" at Harvard, 1944). These innovations changed how Americans lived, worked, traveled, communicated, and were entertained.

**Thesis:** Innovations in communication and technology from 1890 to 1945 were caused by **the rise of industrial capitalism** (which funded research and development), **the growth of the middle class** (which had disposable income to buy new products), and **the "Second Industrial Revolution"** (the development of electricity, internal combustion, and chemicals); their effects included **the "automobile revolution"** (Henry Ford's Model T, the assembly line, the creation of suburbs, the decline of public transportation, the rise of the oil industry), **the "aviation revolution"** (the Wright Brothers, the "Golden Age of Flight," Charles Lindbergh, Amelia Earhart, the "air mail," the "airline industry"), **the "radio revolution"** (Guglielmo Marconi, Lee De Forest, the "wireless," the "broadcasting" of news, music, sports, and entertainment), **the "phonograph revolution"** (Thomas Edison, Emile Berliner, the "phonograph," the "gramophone," the "record player"), and **the "motion picture revolution"** (Thomas Edison, the Lumière brothers, the "Kinetoscope," the "Cinématographe," the "nickelodeon," the "movie palace," the "Hollywood studio system").

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Innovation 1 – The Automobile (KC-7.1.I.A):**
- **Causes** : The internal combustion engine (Gottlieb Daimler, Karl Benz, 1885), the "horseless carriage" (Henry Ford, 1896), the "Quadricycle" (Ford's first car), the "Model T" (Ford, 1908), the "assembly line" (Ford, 1913). The "Model T" was affordable ($850 in 1908, $290 in 1925). 15 million Model T's were sold (1908-1927). The "assembly line" reduced the time to build a car from 12 hours to 90 minutes. Ford paid his workers $5 per day (twice the average wage). Ford's "welfare capitalism" (the "5 dollar day") was designed to reduce turnover, prevent unionization, and allow his workers to buy his cars.
- **Effects** :
  - **The "automobile revolution"** : Car registrations: 8,000 (1900), 1 million (1910), 8 million (1920), 23 million (1930), 32 million (1940). 90% of American households owned a car by 1940 (at least one).
  - **The creation of suburbs** : The car allowed people to live in suburbs (Levittowns, "white flight") and commute to work in the city. The "suburbanization" of America changed the "American Dream" (a single-family home with a lawn, a garage, and a car).
  - **The decline of public transportation** : Streetcars, trolleys, buses, and trains lost passengers to the car. The "General Motors streetcar conspiracy" (1930s-1940s) – GM and other companies bought streetcar systems and replaced them with buses (and then sold the buses). This weakened public transportation and strengthened the automobile industry.
  - **The rise of the oil industry** : The car needed gasoline. The "Texas oil boom" (1901, Spindletop) – oil production: 500,000 barrels (1900), 100 million barrels (1910), 500 million barrels (1920), 1 billion barrels (1930). The "Seven Sisters" (Standard Oil of New Jersey, Royal Dutch Shell, Anglo-Persian Oil Company, Gulf Oil, Texaco, Standard Oil of California, Standard Oil of New York) dominated the global oil industry.
  - **The rise of the rubber industry** : The car needed tires. Rubber was imported from Brazil and Southeast Asia. The "rubber boom" (1910) – the US imported 100,000 tons of rubber (1900), 500,000 tons (1920), 1 million tons (1940). The "Goodyear," "Goodrich," "Firestone," and "Uniroyal" companies dominated the tire industry.
  - **The rise of the highway system** : The "Lincoln Highway" (1913) was the first transcontinental highway (New York to San Francisco). The "Route 66" (1926) was the "mother road" (Chicago to Los Angeles). The "Federal Highway Act (1921)" provided federal funding for highways. The "Interstate Highway System" (1956) was built after WWII.
  - **The "gas station" and the "motel"** : The car created the "gas station" (the "service station") and the "motel" (the "motor hotel" – a hotel for car travelers). The "drive-in" restaurant, the "drive-in" movie theater, the "drive-through" bank, and the "suburban shopping mall" (1950s) were all created by the car.

**Innovation 2 – The Airplane (KC-7.1.I.A):**
- **Causes** : The Wright Brothers (Orville and Wilbur) flew the first powered airplane at Kitty Hawk, North Carolina (December 17, 1903). The flight lasted 12 seconds and covered 120 feet. By 1905, the Wright Flyer III could fly for 30 minutes. The "Wright brothers" were bicycle mechanics; they designed their own engine and propellers. They also invented "wing warping" (a method of controlling the plane's roll – later replaced by ailerons).
- **Effects** :
  - **The "aviation revolution"** : By 1910, the US had 100 planes. By 1918 (WWI), the US had 10,000 planes. By 1930, the US had 50,000 planes.
  - **The "air mail" (1918)** : The US Post Office began air mail service. The "air mail" was carried by the "Air Mail Service" (1918-1926). It was later replaced by the "Contract Air Mail Act" (1925) and the "Air Commerce Act" (1926).
  - **The "airline industry"** : The "Ford Trimotor" (1926-1933) was the first successful passenger plane (the "Tin Goose"). The "Boeing 247" (1933) and the "Douglas DC-3" (1935) were faster, more comfortable, and more economical. The "DC-3" carried 21 passengers (later 28 passengers) and had a range of 1,500 miles. By 1940, the "DC-3" was the most popular airliner in the world.
  - **The "Golden Age of Flight" (1920s-1930s)** : Charles Lindbergh (the "Spirit of St. Louis") flew solo across the Atlantic (New York to Paris, 1927 – 33.5 hours). Amelia Earhart was the first woman to fly solo across the Atlantic (1932). She disappeared over the Pacific (1937) – her fate is unknown. The "Wiley Post" (1933) flew around the world (7 days, 19 hours). The "Howard Hughes" (1938) flew around the world (3 days, 19 hours).
  - **World War I (1914-1918)** : The plane was used for reconnaissance (spying), bombing, and "dogfights" (air-to-air combat). The "Red Baron" (Manfred von Richthofen) was a German ace (80 kills). American aces: Eddie Rickenbacker (26 kills), "Frank Luke" (18 kills).
  - **World War II (1939-1945)** : The plane was used for strategic bombing (the "B-17 Flying Fortress," the "B-24 Liberator," the "B-29 Superfortress"), tactical bombing (the "B-25 Mitchell," the "B-26 Marauder"), fighter planes (the "P-51 Mustang," the "P-38 Lightning," the "P-47 Thunderbolt"), and naval aviation (the "F4U Corsair," the "F6F Hellcat," the "SBD Dauntless").

**Innovation 3 – The Radio (KC-7.2.I.A):**
- **Causes** : Guglielmo Marconi (Italian) invented the "wireless" (radio) in 1895. He sent the first transatlantic radio signal (1901). Lee De Forest (American) invented the "Audion" (a vacuum tube that amplified radio signals) in 1906. This made "voice" radio possible (not just Morse code).
- **Effects** :
  - **The "radio revolution"** : By 1920, there were 100 radio stations (the first commercial station was KDKA in Pittsburgh, 1920). By 1930, there were 600 radio stations. By 1940, there were 900 radio stations. 90% of American households owned a radio (by 1940).
  - **The "broadcasting" of news, music, sports, and entertainment** : The "radio" became a "medium" of mass communication. It "connected" the nation. News: The "Hindenburg" disaster (1937) was covered live. Music: The "Grand Ole Opry" (1925) – country music; the "Metropolitan Opera" (1931) – classical music; the "Hit Parade" (1935) – popular music. Sports: The "World Series" (1922); the "Rose Bowl" (1926); the "Kentucky Derby" (1925). Entertainment: The "Amos 'n' Andy" (1928-1960) – a comedy show (racist stereotypes); the "Lone Ranger" (1933-1954) – a western; the "Shadow" (1937-1954) – a mystery; the "War of the Worlds" (1938) – a radio drama (by Orson Welles) that caused a panic (people believed that Martians were invading New Jersey).
  - **The "radio" as a "campaign" tool** : President Franklin D. Roosevelt used "fireside chats" (1933-1944) to speak directly to the American people ("my friends"). 30 million people listened. The "fireside chats" were a model for presidential communication.
  - **The "radio" as a "wartime" tool** : World War II (1941-1945) – "The World Today" (Edward R. Murrow) covered the war. "Tokyo Rose" (Iva Toguri) – a Japanese-American woman who broadcast propaganda for Japan (she was convicted of treason in 1949, but was pardoned in 1977). The "Voice of America" (VOA, 1942) broadcast American news and music to the world.

**Innovation 4 – The Phonograph (KC-7.2.I.A):**
- **Causes** : Thomas Edison invented the "phonograph" (1877) – the first machine to record and reproduce sound. Emile Berliner invented the "gramophone" (1887) – the first machine to play flat discs (records). The "Victor Talking Machine Company" (1901) – the "Victrola" – was the most popular phonograph.
- **Effects** :
  - **The "phonograph revolution"** : By 1900, 1 million phonographs were sold. By 1920, 10 million phonographs were sold. By 1940, 30 million phonographs were sold.

## LO 7.J: Explain the causes of the Great Depression and its effects on the economy.

**Contextualization:** The Great Depression (1929-1941) was the worst economic crisis in American history. By 1933, 25% of the workforce was unemployed (15 million workers). 5,000 banks failed. 100,000 businesses failed. 1 million families lost their farms. 2 million people became homeless. The Depression was not caused by a single event (the stock market crash of October 1929) but by a combination of structural weaknesses in the American economy.

**Thesis:** The Great Depression was caused by **overproduction** (factories and farms produced more goods than Americans could afford to buy), **uneven distribution of wealth** (the richest 1% owned 51% of the nation's wealth; the poorest 50% owned 1%), **speculation and buying on margin** (people bought stocks with borrowed money, creating a bubble), **bank failures** (when banks failed, depositors lost their savings), **the stock market crash (1929)** (which wiped out $30 billion in wealth), **the Hawley-Smoot Tariff (1930)** (which raised tariffs to 60%, causing other nations to retaliate and reducing international trade), and **Federal Reserve policy** (the Fed raised interest rates in 1931-1932, worsening the depression); its effects on the economy included **mass unemployment** (25% of the workforce), **deflation** (prices fell 30%), **bank runs** (depositors withdrew their savings, causing more banks to fail), **the "Hoovervilles"** (shantytowns named for President Hoover), **the "Bonus Army" (1932)** (20,000 WWI veterans marched on Washington to demand their bonuses; they were dispersed by the army, using tear gas and bayonets), and **the "New Deal" (1933-1938)** (President Franklin D. Roosevelt's program to provide relief, recovery, and reform).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Cause 1 – Overproduction (KC-7.1.I, KC-7.1.I):**
- **Agricultural overproduction** : Farmers had been using new technology (the tractor, the combine, the reaper, the thresher) to increase production. They also planted more acres. The result: a "glut" of wheat, corn, cotton, and other commodities. Prices fell. Farmers could not pay their debts. They lost their farms. By 1932, 1 million families had been foreclosed.
- **Industrial overproduction** : Factories produced more goods (cars, radios, refrigerators, washing machines, clothes, shoes, cigarettes) than Americans could afford to buy. The "middle class" was too small to absorb the surplus. The "working class" was too poor. The result: unsold inventory, layoffs, and falling wages.

**Cause 2 – Uneven Distribution of Wealth (KC-7.1.I.C):**
- The richest 1% of Americans owned 51% of the nation's wealth. The richest 5% owned 70%. The poorest 50% owned 1%. The wealthy saved their money (they did not spend it). The poor had no money to spend. The middle class was too small to drive the economy.
- The "rich" were "rentiers" (they lived off interest, dividends, and rent). They did not "invest" in new factories (because factories were already overproducing). They speculated on the stock market (see below).

**Cause 3 – Speculation and Buying on Margin (KC-7.1.I.C):**
- **Buying on margin** : An investor could buy a stock with 10% down (margin) and borrow the rest (90%) from a broker. If the stock went up, the investor made a profit (minus interest). If the stock went down, the investor lost his investment (and still owed the broker). The "margin" was a loan. By 1929, 1 million Americans had bought stocks on margin. The "margin" created a "bubble" (prices were inflated by borrowed money).
- **Speculation** : People bought stocks not as "investments" (to hold for the long term) but as "speculation" (to sell quickly for a profit). They bought "hot tips" (from shoeshine boys, barbers, cab drivers, farmers, and other "amateurs"). They also bought "stock pools" (secret agreements to drive up the price of a stock). By 1929, the "stock market" was a "casino."

**Cause 4 – The Stock Market Crash (October 1929) (KC-7.1.I.C):**
- **"Black Thursday" (October 24, 1929)** : The market fell 11% at the opening. A group of bankers (led by J.P. Morgan Jr.) bought $30 million worth of stock to stop the panic. It worked (temporarily).
- **"Black Monday" (October 28, 1929)** : The market fell 13% (the second worst one-day percentage decline). The "bankers' pool" did not intervene.
- **"Black Tuesday" (October 29, 1929)** : The market fell 12% (the worst one-day percentage decline until 1987). 16 million shares were traded (a record). The ticker tape ran behind (by 2 hours). The market had lost 40% of its value (from its September high). By 1932, the market had lost 90% of its value (from its September 1929 high).
- **Effects of the crash** : The crash wiped out $30 billion in wealth. It also wiped out the "margin" accounts (investors who had bought on margin lost everything). It also wiped out the "bankers' pool" (J.P. Morgan Jr. lost $100 million). The crash triggered a "run" on the banks (see below).

**Cause 5 – Bank Failures (KC-7.1.I.C):**
- **Bank runs** : When the stock market crashed, depositors feared that their banks would fail. They rushed to withdraw their savings. Banks had only a fraction of their deposits in cash (the rest were invested in loans and securities). They could not pay all depositors at once. They failed.
- **Bank failures** : 5,000 banks failed between 1929 and 1933. When a bank failed, its depositors lost their savings. There was no deposit insurance (the FDIC was created in 1933). 9 million depositors lost their savings. The money supply fell by 30%. The "multiplier effect" (when one bank fails, it causes other banks to fail) was devastating.
- **The "Federal Reserve" made it worse** : The Fed (the central bank) could have acted as "lender of last resort" (loaning money to banks to keep them open). It did not. The Fed raised interest rates in 1931-1932 (to protect the gold standard). This made it harder for banks to borrow money. It also reduced the money supply (which caused deflation). Milton Friedman (a Nobel Prize-winning economist) argued that the Fed's "mistake" turned a "recession" into a "depression."

**Cause 6 – The Hawley-Smoot Tariff (1930) (KC-7.1.I.C):**
- The **Hawley-Smoot Tariff (1930)** raised tariffs on 20,000 imported goods to 60% (the highest in American history). It was designed to protect American farmers and manufacturers from foreign competition. Instead, it provoked retaliation from other nations.
- **Retaliation** : Canada, Cuba, Mexico, Britain, France, Germany, Italy, Spain, and other nations raised tariffs on American goods. American exports fell 66% (from $5.2 billion in 1929 to $1.7 billion in 1933). The "Great Depression" became a "global depression." International trade collapsed.

**Cause 7 – Federal Reserve Policy (1931-1932):**
- The Fed raised interest rates in 1931-1932 to protect the gold standard (the dollar). This reduced the money supply (which caused deflation). Deflation (falling prices) made debts harder to repay (because the value of the dollar increased). It also discouraged consumption (people waited for prices to fall further). The "deflationary spiral" worsened the depression.

**Effects – Mass Unemployment (KC-7.1.III, KC-7.1.III):**
- Unemployment: 3% (1929), 9% (1930), 16% (1931), 24% (1932), 25% (1933). 15 million workers were unemployed (1933). Another 10 million were "underemployed" (working part-time). Unemployment remained above 14% until 1941 (when the US entered WWII).
- **The "human" cost** : Families lost their homes (foreclosures, evictions). They moved in with relatives, lived in shantytowns ("Hoovervilles"), or lived in the streets (the "homeless"). Children went hungry (the "breadlines," the "soup kitchens"). People died of starvation (though the "New Deal" prevented mass starvation). People also died of "diseases of poverty" (tuberculosis, pneumonia, influenza, whooping cough, diphtheria, scarlet fever, polio). The "suicide rate" increased (by 30% between 1929 and 1933). The "marriage rate" fell (by 20%). The "birth rate" fell (by 30%). The "divorce rate" fell (because couples could not afford to live apart).

**Effects – Deflation (KC-7.1.III):**
- Prices fell 30% between 1929 and 1933. A basket of food that cost $1 in 1929 cost $0.70 in 1933. This sounds good, but it was not. Deflation meant that farmers could not sell their crops (prices were too low). Deflation meant that businesses could not make a profit (prices were too low). Deflation meant that wages fell (workers were paid less). Deflation also increased the real value of debt (debts became harder to repay).

**Effects – The "Hoovervilles" (1930-1933) (KC-7.1.III):**
- "Hoovervilles" were shantytowns (named after President Herbert Hoover, who was blamed for the Depression). They were built from scrap lumber, corrugated tin, cardboard, tarpaper, and other "found" materials. They had no running water, no electricity, no sewer, no garbage collection. They were "home" to 2 million homeless Americans. The largest "Hooverville" was in St. Louis (pop. 10,000). There were "Hoovervilles" in Central Park (New York), Anacostia (Washington, D.C.), and the "Boulder Dam" project (later renamed Hoover Dam).

**Effects – The "Bonus Army" (1932) (KC-7.1.III):**

**Effects – President Hoover's Response (KC-7.1.III):**

The "Emergency Relief and Construction Act (1932)" provided federal funds for public works (roads, bridges, dams, hospitals) and loans to states for unemployment relief. These policies were too little, too late.
- **Hoover was blamed for the Depression** . "Hoovervilles," "Hoover blankets" (newspapers), "Hoover flags" (empty pockets turned inside out), "Hoover hogs" (jackrabbits), "Hoover leather" (cardboard). He was defeated by Franklin D. Roosevelt (1932) in a landslide (57% to 40%).

**Effects – President Franklin D. Roosevelt and the "New Deal" (1933-1938) (KC-7.1.III, KC-7.1.III.A, KC-7.1.III.B, KC-7.1.III.C):**
- The "New Deal" was Roosevelt's program to provide "relief, recovery, and reform." See LO 7.K.

## LO 7.K: Explain how the Great Depression and the New Deal impacted American political, social, and economic life over time.

**Contextualization:** The New Deal (1933-1938) was a series of federal programs, laws, and agencies designed to provide "relief" (immediate help for the unemployed, the poor, the elderly, and the disabled), "recovery" (stimulating the economy, creating jobs, raising prices, increasing demand), and "reform" (changing the economic system to prevent another depression). The New Deal was not a socialist revolution (it did not nationalize the means of production). But it was a "welfare state" (it created a "safety net" for Americans).

**Thesis:** The Great Depression and the New Deal impacted American political, social, and economic life by **expanding the role of the federal government** (the New Deal created agencies that regulated the economy, protected workers, and provided a "safety net"), **creating the "New Deal coalition"** (a political alliance of labor unions, African Americans, urban ethnic groups, the poor, and the South), **legitimizing the "welfare state"** (the government had a responsibility to care for its citizens), **establishing the "Social Security" system** (old-age pensions, unemployment insurance, aid to dependent children), **regulating the stock market and banking system** (the SEC, the FDIC), **promoting "industrial recovery"** (the NRA, the PWA, the WPA, the CCC, the TVA), and **transforming the American landscape** (the "New Deal" built roads, bridges, dams, schools, hospitals, post offices, courthouses, and parks).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**The "First New Deal" (1933-1934) – Relief and Recovery (KC-7.1.III.A, KC-7.1.III.A, KC-7.1.III.A, KC-7.1.III.A):**
- **The "Bank Holiday" (March 6-13, 1933)** : Roosevelt closed all banks for 8 days to stop the "bank runs." He then allowed only "sound" banks to reopen (with federal approval). The "bank holiday" restored confidence in the banking system (depositors stopped withdrawing their money). The "Emergency Banking Act (1933)" gave the president the power to regulate banks.
- **The "Glass-Steagall Act (1933)"** : Created the "Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC)" to insure bank deposits (up to $2,500, later $5,000, $10,000, $15,000, $20,000, $25,000, $50,000, $100,000, $250,000). The FDIC ended "bank runs" (depositors knew their money was safe). The Act also separated "commercial banking" (loans to businesses) from "investment banking" (stocks, bonds). This prevented banks from using depositors' money to speculate on the stock market. The Act was repealed in 1999 (the "Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act").
- **The "Federal Emergency Relief Administration (FERA, 1933)"** : Provided federal grants to states for unemployment relief (welfare). $500 million was distributed (free money). The "Emergency Relief Administration (ERA)" was a state agency that administered the funds.
- **The "Civilian Conservation Corps (CCC, 1933)"** : Hired unemployed young men (age 18-25) to work on conservation projects (tree planting, soil conservation, flood control, building parks, building roads, building bridges, building dams). 3 million men worked for the CCC. They lived in camps (CCC camps). They were paid $30 per month ($25 went home to their families). The CCC was one of the most popular New Deal programs.
- **The "Public Works Administration (PWA, 1933)"** : Provided federal funds for "public works" (roads, bridges, dams, schools, hospitals, post offices, courthouses, airports, harbors, sewer systems, water systems). The PWA spent $6 billion on 34,000 projects. The "Grand Coulee Dam" (Washington), the "Bonneville Dam" (Oregon), the "Boulder Dam" (Arizona-Nevada, later renamed Hoover Dam), the "Key West Highway" (Florida), and the "Triborough Bridge" (New York) were built by the PWA.
- **The "National Industrial Recovery Act (NIRA, 1933)"** : Created the "National Recovery Administration (NRA)" to promote "industrial recovery." The NRA established "codes of fair competition" (minimum wages, maximum hours, child labor bans, price controls, production quotas). The NRA also allowed workers to form unions and bargain collectively (Section 7a). The NRA was declared unconstitutional by the Supreme Court (1935) – *Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States* (the "sick chicken" case).
- **The "Agricultural Adjustment Act (AAA, 1933)"** : Paid farmers to reduce production (to raise prices). Farmers plowed under 10 million acres of cotton (1933) and slaughtered 6 million pigs (1933). The AAA was declared unconstitutional by the Supreme Court (1936) – *United States v. Butler* (the AAA was "a coercive regulation of agriculture"). A revised AAA was passed in 1938.
- **The "Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA, 1933)"** : Built dams, generated electricity, controlled floods, and promoted economic development in the Tennessee Valley (a region of 7 states: Tennessee, Virginia, North Carolina, Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Kentucky). The TVA built 29 dams (including the Norris Dam, the Wheeler Dam, the Wilson Dam, the Pickwick Landing Dam, the Kentucky Dam). The TVA also sold fertilizer (to improve soil). The TVA was a "model" for other "regional development" programs (the "Columbia River Project," the "Central Valley Project," the "Missouri River Project").

**The "Second New Deal" (1935-1938) – Reform (KC-7.1.III.A, KC-7.1.III.B, KC-7.1.III.B, KC-7.1.III.B, KC-7.1.III.B):**
- **The "Works Progress Administration (WPA, 1935)"** : Hired unemployed workers (men and women) to build public works (roads, bridges, dams, schools, hospitals, post offices, courthouses, airports, harbors, sewer systems, water systems, parks, playgrounds, swimming pools, tennis courts, baseball fields). The WPA also hired "white collar" workers (actors, artists, musicians, writers, photographers, historians, librarians). The "Federal Writers' Project" produced state guidebooks. The "Federal Theater Project" produced plays (including "The Cradle Will Rock," "It Can't Happen Here," "The Living Newspaper"). The "Federal Arts Project" painted murals (in post offices, courthouses, schools, hospitals). The "Historical Records Survey" indexed old documents. The "WPA" employed 8.5 million workers. It spent $11 billion. It was the largest New Deal agency.
- **The "Social Security Act (1935)"** : Created a "safety net" for Americans. It had 3 components:
  - **Old-age pensions** : A retirement fund for workers (age 65). Workers paid into the fund (payroll tax). The fund paid out monthly benefits. The "pay-as-you-go" system (current workers pay for current retirees) is still in effect.
  - **Unemployment insurance** : A fund for workers who lost their jobs (temporary). Workers paid into the fund (payroll tax). The fund paid out weekly benefits (for 26 weeks).
  - **Aid to dependent children (ADC, later AFDC, later TANF)** : A welfare program for children whose father was deceased, disabled, absent, or unemployed. The program was later expanded to include mothers.
  - The Act also included "Aid to the Blind" and "Aid to the Disabled." The Act was a "social insurance" program (not a "charity"). It was funded by payroll taxes (not by general revenues). It was (and is) popular. It was the foundation of the "welfare state."
- **The "National Labor Relations Act (NLRA, 1935)" – the "Wagner Act"** : Guaranteed workers the right to form unions, bargain collectively, and strike. It also created the "National Labor Relations Board (NLRB)" to enforce the act. The "Wagner Act" was a "Magna Carta" for labor unions. It led to a surge in union membership (from 3 million in 1933 to 8 million in 1939 to 15 million in 1945). It also led to the "Congress of Industrial Organizations (CIO)" (a union of "industrial" workers, not just "craft" workers).
- **The "Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA, 1938)"** : Established a federal minimum wage ($0.25 per hour), a maximum work week (44 hours, later 40 hours), and a ban on child labor (under age 16, except for agriculture). The "FLSA" was a "reform" of the "sweatshop" system. It was the last major New Deal law.
- **The "United States Housing Authority (USHA, 1937)"** : Provided federal loans for "public housing" (low-rent apartments for the poor). The USHA built 200,000 units of public housing (the "projects"). The "projects" were later criticized for concentrating poverty, crime, and dysfunction.

**Political Impact – The "New Deal Coalition" (KC-7.1.III.C):**
- The "New Deal coalition" was a political alliance of: (1) labor unions (the AFL, the CIO), (2) African Americans (the "Great Migration" had made them a political force; they switched from the Republican Party (the "party of Lincoln") to the Democratic Party (the "party of the New Deal")), (3) urban ethnic groups (Irish, Italian, Polish, Jewish, German, Greek, Slavic – they were "Catholic" or "Jewish" and voted Democratic), (4) the poor (the "unemployed," the "homeless," the "elderly," the "disabled" – they supported the New Deal), (5) the South (white Southerners voted Democratic to protect "states' rights" and "white supremacy" – they were "Dixiecrats").
- The "New Deal coalition" dominated American politics from 1932 to 1968. It elected Franklin D. Roosevelt (1932, 1936, 1940, 1944), Harry Truman (1948), John F. Kennedy (1960), and Lyndon B. Johnson (1964). It also controlled Congress for most of that period. The "New Deal coalition" was eventually broken by the "civil rights movement" (the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 drove white Southerners to the Republican Party).

**Social Impact – The "Welfare State" (KC-7.1.III.C):**
- The "New Deal" created a "welfare state" (the government has a responsibility to care for its citizens). The "welfare state" was a "safety net" for the poor, the elderly, the disabled, the unemployed, and the "dependent." It was a "compromise" between capitalism and socialism (it did not nationalize the means of production, but it did regulate the economy and provide a safety net).
- The "welfare state" was popular. It was also controversial. Critics (Republicans, conservatives, business leaders) argued that the "welfare state" was "socialism" (it would destroy American individualism). They also argued that it was "inefficient" (it wasted money) and "immoral" (it encouraged "dependency").
- The "welfare state" was expanded by Lyndon B. Johnson's "Great Society" (1964-1968) – Medicare, Medicaid, the "War on Poverty," and by Barack Obama's "Affordable Care Act" (2010). It was trimmed by Ronald Reagan's "Reagan Revolution" (1981-1989) – cuts to welfare, food stamps, aid to dependent children, and public housing.

**Economic Impact – The "New Deal" and the Economy:**
- The "New Deal" did not end the Great Depression. Unemployment was still 14% in 1940 (down from 25% in 1933). The economy did not recover until World War II (1941-1945) – when the government spent $300 billion on the war (more than the "New Deal" ever spent). The "war economy" (full employment, wage and price controls, rationing, massive federal spending) ended the Depression.
- The "New Deal" did, however, "restructure" the economy. It created the "welfare state." It regulated the stock market (the SEC) and the banking system (the FDIC). It protected labor unions (the Wagner Act). It established a federal minimum wage (the FLSA). It built the "social safety net" (Social Security). It also built the "infrastructure" of the nation (roads, bridges, dams, schools, hospitals, post offices, courthouses, parks). The "New Deal" was a "revolution" in American government.

## LO 7.L: Explain how and why U.S. participation in World War II transformed American society.

**Contextualization:** World War II (1941-1945) was the most transformative event in American history since the Civil War. The US mobilized its economy and society for "total war." 16 million Americans served in the military (5 million volunteered; 11 million were drafted). The war cost $300 billion (more than the "New Deal" had spent in 10 years). The war also ended the Great Depression (unemployment fell to 1.2% in 1944). And it transformed American society: women entered the workforce ("Rosie the Riveter"); African Americans demanded their rights (the "Double V" campaign); Japanese Americans were interned (120,000 people); the "Sun Belt" grew (California, Texas, Florida, Arizona, New Mexico); and the US emerged as the world's dominant superpower.

**Thesis:** US participation in World War II transformed American society by **ending the Great Depression** (war spending created full employment), **mobilizing the economy** (factories converted to war production; the "military-industrial complex" was born), **creating opportunities for women** ("Rosie the Riveter" – women worked in factories, shipyards, aircraft plants, and military support roles), **creating opportunities for minorities** (African Americans served in segregated units, including the Tuskegee Airmen and the 761st Tank Battalion; Japanese Americans served in the 442nd Regimental Combat Team; Native Americans served as "code talkers"; Mexican Americans served in combat), **leading to debates over racial segregation** (the "Double V" campaign – victory over fascism abroad and racism at home – laid the groundwork for the civil rights movement), and **resulting in the internment of Japanese Americans** (120,000 people, 2/3 of whom were US citizens, were forced into "relocation centers" (concentration camps) – a violation of civil liberties).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Effect 1 – Ending the Great Depression (KC-7.3.III.B):**
- **War spending** : The US spent $300 billion on the war (1941-1945). That was more than the "New Deal" had spent in 10 years ($50 billion). The "war economy" created full employment (unemployment fell from 14% in 1940 to 1.2% in 1944). The "war economy" also created inflation (prices rose 30% between 1941 and 1945). But inflation was controlled by "price controls" (the Office of Price Administration) and "rationing" (gasoline, meat, butter, sugar, coffee, shoes, tires).
- **The "war production"** : Factories converted from civilian goods to war goods. The "Big Three" automakers (Ford, General Motors, Chrysler) built tanks, planes, and jeeps (the "Willys MB" – the "jeep"). The "shipyards" (Kaiser Shipyards) built "Liberty ships" (2,700 were built). The "aircraft plants" (Boeing, Lockheed, Douglas, Grumman, North American) built 300,000 planes. The "munitions plants" (DuPont, Hercules, Olin) built bombs, shells, and gunpowder. The "Manhattan Project" (1942-1945) built the atomic bomb.
- **The "military-industrial complex"** : The "military-industrial complex" (a term coined by President Eisenhower in 1961) was the "permanent" alliance between the military and the defense industry. It was born during WWII (when the government spent billions on weapons, ships, planes, and bases). It continued during the Cold War (the "atomic age," the "arms race," the "space race").

**Effect 2 – Mobilizing the Economy (KC-7.3.III.B):**
- **The "War Production Board (WPB, 1942)"** : Directed the conversion of factories from civilian to war production. It also allocated raw materials (steel, copper, aluminum, rubber, oil) and set production quotas. It banned the production of new cars (the "last" new cars were made in 1942; the "next" new cars were made in 1946). It also banned the production of "non-essential" goods (washing machines, refrigerators, radios, bicycles, typewriters, vacuum cleaners, toasters).
- **The "Office of War Mobilization (OWM, 1943)"** : Coordinated the war effort (the WPB, the OPA, the OWI, the War Manpower Commission, the War Labor Board, the Navy, the Army, the Air Force). It was the "super-agency" of the war economy.
- **The "War Manpower Commission (WMC, 1942)"** : Recruited workers for war industries (including women, African Americans, and Mexican Americans). It also drafted men (the "Selective Service System").

**Effect 3 – Creating Opportunities for Women (KC-7.3.III.C.i):**
- **"Rosie the Riveter"** : The iconic image of a woman working in a factory (the "Rosie" was a symbol of American womanhood). 6 million women entered the workforce (1941-1945). They worked as welders, riveters, electricians, mechanics, truck drivers, crane operators, and shipbuilders. They also served in the military (the "WACs" – Women's Army Corps, the "WAVES" – Women Appointed for Volunteer Emergency Service, the "SPARS" – Coast Guard women's reserve). They were paid less than men (50-60% of men's wages). After the war, most women were fired (to make room for returning veterans). The "Rosie" experience, however, changed women's expectations. They had tasted independence. The "women's movement" of the 1960s-1970s had its roots in WWII.
- **The "GI Bill" (1944)** : The "Servicemen's Readjustment Act" provided:
  - **Education** : College tuition ($500 per year) and a living stipend ($50-75 per month). 2.2 million veterans used the GI Bill to go to college (the "GI Bill" created the "middle class").
  - **Housing** : Low-interest mortgages (no down payment) for veterans. 5 million veterans used the GI Bill to buy homes (the "GI Bill" created the "suburbs").
  - **Unemployment** : 52 weeks of unemployment benefits ($20 per week). 1 million veterans used the GI Bill for unemployment.
  - **Small business loans** : 2 million veterans used the GI Bill to start businesses.
  - The GI Bill was "racist" (it was administered by the states, and Southern states denied benefits to Black veterans). It was also "sexist" (it was for men only). The "GI Bill" was a "success" (it created the "greatest generation" and the "middle class").

**Effect 4 – Creating Opportunities for Minorities (KC-7.3.III.C.i, KC-7.3.III.C.ii):**
- **African Americans** : The "Double V" campaign (victory over fascism abroad, victory over racism at home) was a slogan promoted by the *Pittsburgh Courier* (a Black newspaper). African Americans served in segregated units (the "Tuskegee Airmen" – the 332nd Fighter Group; the "761st Tank Battalion" – the "Black Panthers," the first Black tank unit to see combat; the "452nd Anti-Aircraft Artillery Battalion"; the "555th Parachute Infantry Battalion" – the "Triple Nickles"). They also served in the "merchant marine" and the "WACs" (the "6888th Central Postal Directory Battalion" – the "Six Triple Eight"). They faced discrimination (they were often assigned to "service" units, not "combat" units). They also faced violence (the "Detroit race riot" of 1943 – 34 killed, 433 wounded; the "Mobile shipyard riot" of 1943 – 3 killed, 50 wounded; the "Beaumont riot" of 1943 – 2 killed, 100 wounded).
  - **Executive Order 8802 (1941)** : Prohibited racial discrimination in defense industries. It also created the "Fair Employment Practices Committee (FEPC)" to investigate complaints. The order was issued after A. Philip Randolph (the head of the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters) threatened a "March on Washington" (100,000 people). The "March" was cancelled, but the "FEPC" was created.
- **Japanese Americans** : See below.
- **Native Americans** : 25,000 Native Americans served in the military (including "code talkers" – Navajo, Comanche, Cherokee, Choctaw, Lakota, Meskwaki, Mohawk, Hopi). The "Navajo code talkers" used their language to encrypt military messages. The code was never broken. The "code talkers" were honored with the Congressional Gold Medal (2008).
- **Mexican Americans** : 500,000 Mexican Americans served in the military (including the "Fighting 65th" – the 65th Infantry Regiment from Puerto Rico; the "158th Infantry Regiment" – the "Bushmasters"; the "141st Infantry Regiment" – the "1st Texas"). They also worked in war industries (the "bracero" program – 4 million Mexican workers were recruited to work on farms and railroads). The "Zoot Suit Riots" (1943) – white sailors attacked Mexican American youths wearing "zoot suits" (baggy pants, long jackets). The sailors claimed the "zoot suits" were "unpatriotic" (they used too much fabric). The riots were "racist."

**Effect 5 – Debates over Racial Segregation (KC-7.3.III.C.i):**
- The "Double V" campaign (above) laid the groundwork for the "civil rights movement." African Americans returned from the war expecting "first class" citizenship. They were determined to get it. The "Congress of Racial Equality (CORE)" was founded in 1942 (by James Farmer, Bayard Rustin, and others). CORE used "nonviolent civil disobedience" (sit-ins, freedom rides) to challenge segregation. The "NAACP" grew from 50,000 members (1940) to 500,000 members (1946). It also won a major victory in *Smith v. Allwright* (1944) (the Supreme Court struck down the "white primary").
- **The "GI Bill" and "racial discrimination"** : The "GI Bill" was administered by the states. Southern states denied benefits to Black veterans (they could not go to white colleges; there were no Black colleges; they were denied housing loans; they were denied unemployment). This led to a "push" for civil rights.

**Effect 6 – The Internment of Japanese Americans (KC-7.3.III.C.i):**
- **Executive Order 9066 (February 19, 1942)** : Authorized the military to create "exclusion zones" (areas from which "any or all persons" could be removed). The order was aimed at Japanese Americans (on the West Coast). 120,000 Japanese Americans (2/3 were US citizens) were forced from their homes. They were sent to "relocation centers" (concentration camps) in the interior of the US (California, Arizona, Utah, Idaho, Colorado, Wyoming, Arkansas). The camps were surrounded by barbed wire and guarded by armed soldiers. The conditions were harsh (they lived in barracks; they had to share bathrooms; they had no privacy; they were fed "government" food; they were cold in the winter; they were hot in the summer). They lost their homes, businesses, farms, and possessions. They lost their dignity.
- **Korematsu v. United States (1944)** : The Supreme Court upheld the internment (6-3). Justice Hugo Black wrote the majority opinion: "Korematsu was not excluded from the military area because of hostility to him or his race. He was excluded because we are at war with the Japanese Empire, because the properly constituted military authorities feared an invasion of our West Coast, and because they decided that the military urgency of the situation demanded that all citizens of Japanese ancestry be segregated from the West Coast." Justice Frank Murphy wrote a dissenting opinion: "This exclusion of 'all persons of Japanese ancestry, both alien and non-alien,' goes over the 'brink of constitutional power' and falls into the ugly abyss of racism." The internment was "a legalization of racism."
- **The "internment" was a "violation" of civil liberties** . It was later condemned by the US government. The "Civil Liberties Act of 1988" apologized to Japanese Americans and provided $20,000 (in reparations) to each surviving internee. 82,000 survivors received $1.6 billion.

**Effect 7 – The "Sun Belt" Growth (KC-7.3.III.C.ii):**
- The "Sun Belt" (California, Texas, Florida, Arizona, New Mexico, Nevada, Colorado, Georgia, North Carolina, South Carolina, Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Arkansas, Oklahoma) grew rapidly during the war. The war brought military bases (the "air force" bases, the "army" bases, the "navy" bases), defense plants (the "Lockheed" plant in Burbank, the "Boeing" plant in Seattle, the "Douglas" plant in Long Beach, the "North American" plant in Inglewood, the "Consolidated" plant in San Diego), and shipyards (the "Kaiser" shipyards in Richmond, California; the "Bethlehem" shipyards in San Francisco; the "Newport News" shipyards in Virginia). The "Sun Belt" grew even faster after the war (air conditioning, cheap land, low taxes, "right to work" laws, anti-union sentiment).

**Effect 8 – The US as a Superpower (KC-7.3.III.E):**
- After the war, the US was the world's dominant superpower. It had the "atomic bomb" (the "Manhattan Project" cost $2 billion). It had the world's largest navy, the world's largest air force, and a large army. It also had a "global" economy (the "Bretton Woods" system, 1944, made the "dollar" the world's "reserve currency"). The US also became the "leader of the free world" (the "Cold War" with the Soviet Union).

## LO 7.M: Explain the causes and effects of the victory of the United States and its allies over the Axis powers.

**Contextualization:** World War II (1939-1945) was the deadliest war in human history (60-85 million deaths). The US entered the war after Japan attacked Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941). The US joined the "Allies" (Britain, the Soviet Union, China, and 50 other nations) against the "Axis" (Germany, Italy, Japan, and smaller nations). The war was fought on two fronts: Europe (against Germany) and the Pacific (against Japan).

**Thesis:** The victory of the United States and its allies over the Axis powers was caused by **Allied cooperation** (the "Big Three" – the US, Britain, the Soviet Union – coordinated their military strategies), **technological and scientific advances** (radar, sonar, the proximity fuze, penicillin, the atomic bomb), **the contributions of servicemen and women** (16 million Americans served; 405,000 died; 671,000 were wounded), **the "island-hopping" campaign in the Pacific** (the US bypassed heavily fortified islands and captured lightly defended islands, bringing it within bombing range of Japan), the **D-Day invasion** (June 6, 1944, the Allied invasion of Normandy, France), and the **use of atomic bombs** (Hiroshima and Nagasaki, August 1945) which hastened the end of the war and sparked debates about the morality of using atomic weapons.

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Cause 1 – Allied Cooperation (KC-7.3.III.A, KC-7.3.III.D):**
- The "Big Three" (Franklin D. Roosevelt of the US, Winston Churchill of Britain, Joseph Stalin of the Soviet Union) met three times during the war:
  - **Tehran Conference (November-December 1943)** : The "Big Three" agreed to a "second front" in Europe (the D-Day invasion, June 1944). They also agreed to the partition of Germany after the war.
  - **Yalta Conference (February 1945)** : The "Big Three" agreed to:
    - The partition of Germany into four zones (American, British, French, Soviet)
    - The "de-Nazification" and "demilitarization" of Germany
    - The creation of the United Nations (April 1945)
    - The entry of the Soviet Union into the war against Japan (in exchange for territory in Asia)
  - **Potsdam Conference (July-August 1945)** : The "Big Three" (Truman (US), Churchill (Britain, replaced by Attlee), Stalin) agreed to:
    - The "demilitarization," "de-Nazification," and "democratization" of Germany
    - The "reparations" from Germany (the Soviet Union would receive $10 billion)
    - The "Potsdam Declaration" (demanding Japan's unconditional surrender; threatening "prompt and utter destruction")
- **Allied strategy** : "Europe first" (defeat Germany before Japan). The strategy was controversial (the US wanted to focus on Japan; Britain wanted to focus on Germany). The "Europe first" strategy was adopted at the "Arcadia Conference" (December 1941).

**Cause 2 – Technological and Scientific Advances (KC-7.3.III.D):**
- **Radar** : Used by the Allies to detect enemy aircraft, ships, and submarines. The "Battle of Britain" (1940) was won by radar (the British "Chain Home" radar system). The "US Navy" used radar to win the "Battle of the Philippine Sea" (1944).
- **Sonar** : Used by the Allies to detect enemy submarines (U-boats). The "convoy system" (ships traveling together, protected by destroyers with sonar) defeated the U-boats in the Atlantic.
- **The proximity fuze** : A device that detonated a shell when it was near its target (not when it hit it). It was used by the US Navy against Japanese aircraft (the "Battle of the Philippine Sea," the "Battle of Leyte Gulf"). It was also used by the US Army in ground combat (the "Battle of the Bulge"). It was a "top secret" weapon.
- **Penicillin** : An antibiotic that prevented infection. It was mass-produced by the US (1943-1945). It saved the lives of 500,000 soldiers (and many civilians).
- **The atomic bomb** : See below.

**Cause 3 – Contributions of Servicemen and Women (KC-7.3.III.C.ii):**
- 16 million Americans served in the military (5 million volunteered; 11 million were drafted). 405,000 Americans died (including 7,000 civilians – the "WWII" death toll). 671,000 were wounded. The "deadliest" battles:
  - **Battle of the Bulge (December 1944-January 1945)** : 19,000 US soldiers killed, 47,000 wounded.
  - **Battle of Okinawa (April-June 1945)** : 12,000 US soldiers killed, 36,000 wounded (plus 100,000 Japanese soldiers killed, 100,000 Okinawan civilians killed).
  - **Battle of Iwo Jima (February-March 1945)** : 7,000 US soldiers killed, 20,000 wounded (plus 20,000 Japanese soldiers killed).
  - **Normandy landings (D-Day, June 6, 1944)** : 4,400 Allied soldiers killed (including 2,500 Americans).
- **Women in the military** : 350,000 women served in the "WACs" (Women's Army Corps), the "WAVES" (Women Appointed for Volunteer Emergency Service), the "SPARS" (Coast Guard women's reserve), the "Women Marines," and the "Women's Air Force Service Pilots (WASPs)." They served as nurses, clerks, drivers, mechanics, radio operators, telephone operators, and pilots (the "WASPs" flew planes from factories to bases). They were not allowed to fly combat missions.

**Cause 4 – The "Island-Hopping" Campaign in the Pacific (KC-7.3.III.D):**
- The "island-hopping" strategy (also called "leapfrogging") was devised by General Douglas MacArthur and Admiral Chester Nimitz. The Allies bypassed heavily fortified Japanese islands (like Truk, Rabaul, and Palau) and captured lightly defended islands (like Tarawa, Makin, Kwajalein, Eniwetok, Saipan, Tinian, Guam, Peleliu, Iwo Jima, Okinawa). The captured islands were used as "bases" for bombers (the B-29 "Superfortress").
- **Key battles** :
  - **Battle of Midway (June 4-7, 1942)** : The turning point in the Pacific. The US Navy (Admiral Chester Nimitz) defeated the Japanese navy (Admiral Isoroku Yamamoto). The US sank 4 Japanese aircraft carriers (Akagi, Kaga, Soryu, Hiryu) and 1 cruiser. The Japanese sank 1 US carrier (the Yorktown). The battle ended Japan's offensive and gave the US the initiative.
  - **Battle of Guadalcanal (August 1942-February 1943)** : The first Allied offensive. The US Marines landed on Guadalcanal (in the Solomon Islands). They fought a brutal jungle campaign against the Japanese. The US won (after 6 months). The "Tokyo Express" (Japanese naval forces) tried to reinforce the island; they failed.
  - **Battle of Leyte Gulf (October 23-26, 1944)** : The largest naval battle in history. The US Navy (Admiral William "Bull" Halsey) defeated the Japanese navy. The Japanese lost 4 aircraft carriers, 3 battleships, 6 cruisers, 12 destroyers, and 10,000 men. The US lost 1 light carrier, 2 escort carriers, 2 destroyers, and 3,000 men. The battle destroyed the Japanese navy.
  - **Battle of Iwo Jima (February-March 1945)** : The US Marines captured Iwo Jima (a volcanic island). The battle was brutal (the Japanese had built a network of tunnels, bunkers, and caves). The US flag was raised on Mount Suribachi (the iconic photograph). 7,000 US soldiers died; 20,000 Japanese soldiers died.
  - **Battle of Okinawa (April-June 1945)** : The last major battle. The US captured Okinawa (a Japanese island). The battle was brutal (the Japanese used "kamikaze" suicide planes). 12,000 US soldiers died; 36,000 were wounded; 100,000 Japanese soldiers died; 100,000 Okinawan civilians died. The battle convinced President Truman that an invasion of Japan would be "too costly" (the "Operation Downfall" invasion was planned for November 1945; it was expected to cost 500,000-1 million American lives).

**Cause 5 – The D-Day Invasion (June 6, 1944) (KC-7.3.III.D):**
- "D-Day" was the Allied invasion of Normandy, France (Operation Overlord). It was the largest amphibious invasion in history. 156,000 Allied troops (American, British, Canadian, and others) landed on 5 beaches (Utah, Omaha, Gold, Juno, Sword). 6,000 ships (including 1,000 warships) and 10,000 aircraft supported the invasion.
- **The fighting** : The Allies faced heavy resistance from German forces (the "Atlantic Wall"). The US "Rangers" scaled the cliffs at Pointe du Hoc (to destroy German gun batteries). The US "1st Infantry Division" ("the Big Red One") landed on Omaha Beach (the most heavily defended beach). 2,500 US soldiers were killed, wounded, or missing at Omaha Beach. The British and Canadian forces landed on Gold, Juno, and Sword beaches (with fewer casualties).
- **The "breakout"** : The Allies secured the beaches (by the end of June). They "broke out" of Normandy (July 1944). They liberated Paris (August 25, 1944). They pushed the Germans back into Germany (by the end of 1944).

**Cause 6 – The Use of Atomic Bombs (KC-7.3.III.D):**
- The "Manhattan Project" (1942-1945) developed the atomic bomb. The project was led by General Leslie Groves (US Army) and J. Robert Oppenheimer (scientific director). The "Trinity test" (July 16, 1945) was the first atomic explosion (in Alamogordo, New Mexico). The "Little Boy" (uranium) and "Fat Man" (plutonium) bombs were built.
- **Hiroshima (August 6, 1945)** : A B-29 "Enola Gay" (pilot: Colonel Paul Tibbets) dropped "Little Boy" on Hiroshima (a city of 350,000 people). 80,000 people died instantly; 60,000 died later (from radiation poisoning, burns, and injuries).
- **Nagasaki (August 9, 1945)** : A B-29 "Bockscar" (pilot: Major Charles Sweeney) dropped "Fat Man" on Nagasaki (a city of 260,000 people). 40,000 people died instantly; 40,000 died later. The "bomb" was dropped 3 days after Hiroshima (the US did not give Japan time to surrender).
- **Japan surrenders (August 15, 1945)** : Emperor Hirohito announced Japan's surrender (August 15, 1945). The formal surrender was signed on the USS Missouri (September 2, 1945). V-J Day (Victory over Japan Day) was celebrated.
- **Debate over the use of the atomic bomb** : Supporters argued that the bomb saved lives (it prevented the invasion of Japan, which would have cost 500,000-1 million American lives and 5-10 million Japanese lives). Opponents argued that the bomb was immoral (it killed civilians, it was not necessary – Japan was already defeated, it was a "terror" weapon). They also argued that the bomb was used to intimidate the Soviet Union (the "Cold War" had already begun). The debate continues.

## LO 7.N: Explain the consequences of U.S. involvement in World War II.

**Contextualization:** World War II ended in 1945. The US was the victor. It was also the world's dominant superpower. It had the atomic bomb. It had a global economy (the "dollar" was the world's "reserve currency"). It also had a "global" military presence (bases in Europe, Asia, the Pacific, the Caribbean, the Middle East, North Africa, and the Arctic). The US became the "leader of the free world" (the "Cold War" with the Soviet Union).

**Thesis:** The consequences of US involvement in World War II included **the emergence of the US as the world's most powerful nation** (the "American Century"), **the beginning of the Cold War** (the US vs. the Soviet Union), **the creation of the United Nations** (to prevent future wars), **the "Marshall Plan" (1948)** (the US rebuilt Western Europe to prevent the spread of communism), **the "Truman Doctrine" (1947)** (the US would "contain" communism), **the "Berlin Airlift" (1948-1949)** (the US supplied West Berlin after the Soviet Union blockaded it), **the "NATO" alliance (1949)** (the US formed a military alliance with Western Europe), **the "Korean War" (1950-1953)** (the US fought to "contain" communism in Asia), and **the "Red Scare" (1947-1957)** (the US feared communist subversion at home).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Consequence 1 – The US as the World's Dominant Superpower (KC-7.3.III.E):**
- The US had the atomic bomb (the Soviet Union would develop its own bomb in 1949). The US had the world's largest navy, the world's largest air force, and a large army. The US had a "global" economy (the "Bretton Woods" system, 1944, made the "dollar" the world's "reserve currency"). The US was the "leader of the free world" (the "Cold War" with the Soviet Union).

**Consequence 2 – The Beginning of the Cold War:**
- The "Cold War" was a state of "geopolitical tension" between the US (and its allies) and the Soviet Union (and its allies). It was called "cold" because there was no direct military conflict between the US and the Soviet Union (they fought "proxy" wars in Korea, Vietnam, and Afghanistan). The Cold War lasted from 1947 (the "Truman Doctrine") to 1991 (the collapse of the Soviet Union).
- **Causes** : The Soviet Union occupied Eastern Europe (Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria, East Germany). The US believed that the Soviet Union was trying to spread communism. The Soviet Union believed that the US was trying to destroy it. The "Yalta Conference" (February 1945) and the "Potsdam Conference" (July-August 1945) revealed the differences between the US and the Soviet Union.

**Consequence 3 – The Creation of the United Nations (KC-7.3.III.E):**
- The "United Nations (UN)" was created on April 25, 1945 (the "UN Charter" was signed on June 26, 1945). The UN had 51 members (the "Allies" and other nations). Its purpose was to "save succeeding generations from the scourge of war." It had a "Security Council" (5 permanent members – the US, the Soviet Union, Britain, France, China – with veto power) and a "General Assembly" (all members). The US Senate ratified the UN Charter (89-2) – a sharp contrast with the League of Nations (which was rejected).

**Consequence 4 – The "Marshall Plan" (1948) (KC-7.3.III.E):**
- The "Marshall Plan" (the "European Recovery Program") was a US program to rebuild Western Europe (1948-1952). It provided $13 billion (in economic aid) to 16 countries. The plan was named for Secretary of State George C. Marshall (a former Army general). The plan was designed to:
  - Rebuild the European economy (to prevent the spread of communism)
  - Create markets for American goods (to prevent a post-war depression)
  - Strengthen the "free world" against the Soviet Union
- The Soviet Union refused to participate (and forced its satellites to refuse). The "Marshall Plan" was a success: Western Europe recovered; communism did not spread; trade boomed.

**Consequence 5 – The "Truman Doctrine" (1947) (KC-7.3.III.E):**
- The "Truman Doctrine" was a US policy to "contain" communism (to prevent its spread). It was announced by President Harry S Truman (March 12, 1947) in response to the Greek Civil War (1946-1949) and the Soviet threat to Turkey. Truman asked Congress for $400 million in aid to Greece and Turkey. Congress approved. The "Truman Doctrine" was the "official" start of the Cold War.

**Consequence 6 – The "Berlin Airlift" (1948-1949) (KC-7.3.III.E):**
- The Soviet Union blockaded West Berlin (June 1948 – May 1949). The Soviets cut off all road, rail, and canal traffic to West Berlin (a city of 2 million people). The US and Britain responded with an "airlift" (they flew food, fuel, medicine, and other supplies into West Berlin). 2.3 million tons of supplies were delivered (by 275,000 flights). The US lost 70 pilots (to crashes). The Soviet Union lifted the blockade (May 12, 1949). The "Berlin Airlift" was a US victory.

**Consequence 7 – The "NATO" Alliance (1949) (KC-7.3.III.E):**
- The "North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO)" was a military alliance between the US, Canada, and 10 Western European nations (Britain, France, Belgium, the Netherlands, Luxembourg, Portugal, Italy, Norway, Denmark, Iceland). The treaty (signed April 4, 1949) stated that "an armed attack against one or more of them... shall be considered an attack against them all." NATO was the "military arm" of the "free world." The Soviet Union created its own alliance – the "Warsaw Pact" (1955) – in response.

**Consequence 8 – The "Korean War" (1950-1953) (KC-7.3.III.E):**
- North Korea (communist, supported by the Soviet Union and China) invaded South Korea (capitalist, supported by the US) on June 25, 1950. The US (under the flag of the UN) sent troops to defend South Korea. The war was a "stalemate." It ended with an armistice (July 27, 1953). 37,000 Americans were killed; 103,000 were wounded. The "Korean War" was a "containment" war.

**Consequence 9 – The "Red Scare" (1947-1957) (KC-7.3.III.E):**
- The "Red Scare" was a period of intense anti-communist hysteria (the "Second Red Scare," the "McCarthy Era"). Americans feared that communists had infiltrated the US government, the military, the media, and the schools. Senator Joseph McCarthy (R-Wisconsin) claimed that there were 205 communists in the State Department. He was a "demagogue." He was eventually censured by the Senate (1954). The "Red Scare" led to the "blacklisting" of 500 actors, writers, and directors (the "Hollywood Ten"). It also led to the "execution" of Julius and Ethel Rosenberg (1953) for "conspiracy to commit espionage" (they were accused of passing atomic secrets to the Soviet Union).

## LO 7.O: Compare the relative significance of the major events of the first half of the 20th century in shaping American identity.

**Contextualization:** The first half of the 20th century was a period of profound change for the United States. It became an imperial power (1898), a world power (1917-1918), a "cultural" power (the "Jazz Age," the "Harlem Renaissance"), a "depressed" nation (the "Great Depression"), a "New Deal" nation (the "welfare state"), a "war" nation (World War II), and a "superpower" (1945).

**Thesis:** The relative significance of the major events of the first half of the 20th century in shaping American identity can be ranked as follows: **most significant** was **World War II** (which made the US a superpower, ended the Great Depression, and established the US as the "leader of the free world"); **second** was **the Great Depression and the New Deal** (which created the "welfare state" and the "New Deal coalition"); **third** was **World War I** (which made the US a "world power" and ended its isolation); **fourth** was **the Spanish-American War** (which made the US an "imperial power"); **fifth** was **the "culture wars" of the 1920s** (which divided Americans over religion, race, ethnicity, and "modernity").

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Most Significant – World War II (KC-7.3.III):**
- World War II transformed the US from a "regional" power to a "global" superpower. It also ended the Great Depression (the "war economy" created full employment). It also created the "military-industrial complex." It also led to the "Cold War." It also established the US as the "leader of the free world." It also created the "American Dream" (the "GI Bill," the "suburbs," the "middle class"). It also created the "civil rights movement" (the "Double V" campaign). It also created the "women's movement" ("Rosie the Riveter"). It also created the "Sun Belt." It also led to the "internment of Japanese Americans" (a "violation" of civil liberties). It also led to the "atomic age" (the "Manhattan Project").

**Second Most Significant – The Great Depression and the New Deal (KC-7.1.III):**
- The Great Depression was the worst economic crisis in American history. It lasted 12 years. It created mass unemployment (25%). It created "Hoovervilles." It created the "Bonus Army." It led to the "New Deal." The "New Deal" created the "welfare state" (Social Security, unemployment insurance, aid to dependent children). It created the "New Deal coalition" (labor unions, African Americans, urban ethnic groups, the poor, the South). It also created the "modern" Democratic Party. It also "re-regulated" the economy (the SEC, the FDIC). It also built the "infrastructure" of the nation (roads, bridges, dams, schools, hospitals, post offices, courthouses, parks). It also created the "modern" presidency (Franklin D. Roosevelt). It also "transformed" American politics (the "New Deal" was a "revolution").

**Third Most Significant – World War I (KC-7.3.II):**
- World War I ended the US's "isolation" from world affairs. It made the US a "world power." It also led to the "Red Scare" (1919-1920). It also led to the "Great Migration" (African Americans moved from the South to the North). It also led to the "Harlem Renaissance." It also led to "Prohibition" (the 18th Amendment, 1919). It also led to "women's suffrage" (the 19th Amendment, 1920). It also led to the "Roaring Twenties." It also led to the "Lost Generation" (expatriate writers – Hemingway, Fitzgerald, Dos Passos, e.e. cummings).

**Fourth Most Significant – The Spanish-American War (KC-7.3.I):**
- The Spanish-American War made the US an "imperial power." The US acquired the Philippines, Guam, Puerto Rico, and Hawaii. It also made the US a "naval power" (the "Great White Fleet," the Panama Canal). It also led to the "Philippine-American War" (1899-1902) – the US suppressed Filipino independence. It also led to the "Platt Amendment" (1901) – the US controlled Cuba. It also led to the "Open Door Policy" (1899-1900) – the US demanded equal trading rights with China. It also led to the "Roosevelt Corollary" (1904) – the US became the "policeman of the Western Hemisphere." The Spanish-American War was the "coming out" of the US as a "global" power.

**Fifth Most Significant – The "Culture Wars" of the 1920s (KC-7.2.I.D):**
- The 1920s were a "culture war" between "traditionalists" (rural, Protestant, nativist, prohibitionist) and "modernists" (urban, Catholic/Jewish, immigrant, "wet" (anti-prohibition)). The "culture war" was fought over:
  - **Prohibition** : The 18th Amendment (1919) banned alcohol. The 21st Amendment (1933) repealed it. Prohibition was a "failure" (it led to bootlegging, speakeasies, and organized crime). It also "divided" Americans (the "wets" vs. the "drys").
  - **Evolution** : The "Scopes Monkey Trial" (1925) was a trial about teaching evolution in public schools. John Scopes (a high school teacher) was convicted of violating Tennessee's "Butler Act" (which banned the teaching of evolution). The trial pitted "science" (Clarence Darrow) against "religion" (William Jennings Bryan). It was a "symbolic" victory for "modernism" (though Scopes was convicted).
  - **Immigration** : The "Immigration Act of 1924" (the "Johnson-Reed Act") limited immigration (2% of each nationality's population in 1890). It also banned Asian immigration. It was a victory for "nativism."
  - **Race** : The "Harlem Renaissance" (1920s) was a "cultural" movement. It also "celebrated" African American identity. The "Ku Klux Klan" (the "Second Klan") had 5 million members (1925). It was "racist," "anti-Catholic," "anti-Semitic," "anti-immigrant," and "anti-foreign." The "Klan" was a "defense" of "white Protestant America."
  - **Gender** : The "flapper" was a "modern" woman (she wore short skirts, bobbed her hair, smoked cigarettes, drank alcohol, and danced the Charleston). The "flapper" was a "rebellion" against "Victorian" morality. The "19th Amendment" (1920) gave women the right to vote. The "Equal Rights Amendment" (proposed 1923) failed.
- The "culture wars" of the 1920s shaped the "culture wars" of the 21st century (the "culture wars" over abortion, gay rights, immigration, and religion).
