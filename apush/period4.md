## LO 4.A: Explain the context in which the republic developed from 1800 to 1848.

**Contextualization:** The election of Thomas Jefferson in 1800 marked the first peaceful transfer of power between political parties in modern history. Jefferson called it “the revolution of 1800” because it restored republican principles after the “tyranny” of the Alien and Sedition Acts. The period from 1800 to 1848 saw dramatic changes: the expansion of democracy (suffrage for all white men), the Market Revolution (transportation, technology, manufacturing), the Second Great Awakening (religious revival and reform movements), and westward expansion (Louisiana Purchase, Texas, Oregon, Mexican Cession).

**Thesis:** Although the United States in 1800 was still a fragile republic of 5.3 million people hugging the Atlantic coast, by 1848 it had been transformed by **the expansion of participatory democracy** (universal white male suffrage, growth of political parties), **the Market Revolution** (canals, railroads, factories, cotton gin), **the Second Great Awakening** (religious revivalism, reform movements including abolitionism and women’s rights), and **westward expansion** (Louisiana Purchase, Texas annexation, Mexican-American War), which together created a more democratic, more economically integrated, and more geographically expansive nation—but one deeply divided over slavery.

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Political Context – The “Revolution of 1800” and the Rise of Democracy (KC-4.1.I):**
- Jefferson’s election demonstrated that power could be transferred peacefully between parties (Federalist to Democratic-Republican)
- Jefferson reduced the size of the federal government: cut taxes (eliminated the whiskey tax, reduced other internal taxes), reduced the national debt (from $83 million to $57 million), cut the size of the army and navy (though kept the US Military Academy at West Point, founded 1802)
- Jefferson pardoned those convicted under the Sedition Act and allowed the Alien Friends Act to expire
- The **Marbury v. Madison (1803)** case: William Marbury (a “midnight judge” appointed by John Adams in his last days in office) sued Secretary of State James Madison for his commission. Chief Justice John Marshall ruled that the Judiciary Act of 1789 (which gave the Supreme Court the power to issue writs of mandamus) was unconstitutional. This established **judicial review** – the power of the Supreme Court to declare laws unconstitutional. Marshall’s ruling was a masterstroke: he denied Marbury his commission (avoiding a confrontation with Jefferson), but asserted the Court’s power to review congressional laws
- **Expansion of suffrage** : By the 1820s-1830s, most states had eliminated property qualifications for voting for white men. By 1840, over 90% of white men could vote (up from about 50-70% in 1800). New states entering the Union (Ohio 1803, Indiana 1816, Illinois 1818, Missouri 1821, etc.) wrote constitutions with universal white male suffrage
- **The “Common Man”** became the idealized political figure. Andrew Jackson’s election (1828) symbolized this shift. Jackson was the first president not from Virginia or Massachusetts (he was from Tennessee), the first to be born in a log cabin (though he was actually a wealthy planter), and the first to be elected by popular vote (most states had abolished the property requirement by 1828)

**Economic Context – The Market Revolution (KC-4.2.I, KC-4.2.II, KC-4.2.III):**
- **Transportation revolution** :
- **National Road (Cumberland Road)** : built 1811-1837, from Cumberland, Maryland to Vandalia, Illinois (620 miles). First federally funded highway
- **Erie Canal (1825)** : 363-mile canal from Albany to Buffalo, connecting the Hudson River to the Great Lakes. Slashed shipping costs from $100 per ton to $5 per ton. Turned New York City into the nation’s largest port and commercial center
- **Steamboats** : Robert Fulton’s *Clermont* (1807) demonstrated the feasibility of steam-powered boats. By the 1830s, steamboats were common on the Mississippi, Ohio, and other rivers
- **Railroads** : Baltimore & Ohio (B&O) chartered 1827, first passenger railroad. By 1840, there were over 3,000 miles of railroad track (mostly in the North)
- **Technological innovations** :
- **Cotton gin (1793)** : Eli Whitney. Made short-staple cotton profitable to clean. Cotton production: 3,000 bales (1790), 178,000 bales (1810), 4 million bales (1860). This dramatically increased the demand for enslaved labor and expanded slavery westward
- **Interchangeable parts** : Eli Whitney demonstrated for muskets (1801). Led to mass production and the “American System of Manufacturing”
- **Telegraph (1844)** : Samuel Morse sent “What hath God wrought?” from Washington to Baltimore. Revolutionized communication
- **Steel plow (1837)** : John Deere. Allowed farmers to cut through tough prairie sod, enabling settlement of the Great Plains
- **Mechanical reaper (1831, patented 1834)** : Cyrus McCormick. Allowed farmers to harvest wheat faster; increased agricultural productivity
- **McCormick’s reaper** and **Deere’s plow** transformed the Midwest into the “breadbasket of the world”
- **Factory system** :
- **Lowell Mills (Massachusetts)** : Francis Cabot Lowell built integrated textile mills (carding, spinning, weaving all under one roof). Employed “Lowell mill girls” (young women from New England farms who lived in company boarding houses, worked 12-14 hour days, and earned $2-4 per week). The Lowell system was paternalistic; but conditions declined in the 1830s-1840s, and workers organized strikes (1834, 1836)
- **Rhode Island system** : Samuel Slater built mills based on British models, employed entire families (including children)
- **Economic specialization by region** :
- **North** : manufacturing, finance, commerce
- **South** : cotton (King Cotton), slavery
- **West (Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Michigan, Wisconsin)** : wheat, corn, livestock (food for eastern cities and the South)
- The regions became economically interdependent (western food fed northern factory workers and southern slaves; southern cotton supplied northern textile mills), but this interdependence did not prevent political conflict

**Social and Religious Context – The Second Great Awakening (KC-4.1.III.A, KC-4.1.III.A):**
- The **Second Great Awakening (c. 1790-1840s)** was a Protestant religious revival that swept across the United States
- **Causes** :
- Reaction against the rationalism of the Enlightenment (Deism, Unitarianism)
- Anxiety about the social changes of the Market Revolution (urbanization, immigration, mobility)
- The “democratization” of American society (ordinary people wanted a religion that spoke to their emotions, not just their intellect)
- **Key figures** :
- **Charles Grandison Finney** (1792-1875) : the most famous revivalist of the era. He preached in upstate New York’s “Burned-Over District” (so called because it was so repeatedly “burned” by revivals). Finney rejected Calvinist predestination, emphasized free will and individual choice, and preached “perfectionism” (that Christians could achieve sinless perfection in this life, and therefore must work to perfect society). He also advocated for abolitionism and women’s right to pray and speak in mixed-sex gatherings
- **Lyman Beecher** (1775-1863) : Presbyterian minister who preached against Unitarianism, Catholicism, and “immorality” (drinking, gambling, prostitution). His daughter, Harriet Beecher Stowe, wrote *Uncle Tom’s Cabin* (1852)
- **Peter Cartwright** (1785-1872) : Methodist circuit rider who preached on the frontier (Kentucky, Tennessee, Illinois). He was a champion wrestler and once beat up a heckler during a sermon
- **Richard Allen** (1760-1831) : African American minister who founded the **African Methodist Episcopal (AME) Church** (1816), the first independent Black denomination in the United States
- **The “Burned-Over District”** (western New York, central New York) was a hotbed of revivalism, abolitionism, and new religious movements:
- **Mormonism (Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints)** : founded by Joseph Smith (1805-1844) in Palmyra, New York. Smith published the *Book of Mormon* (1830), which he said he translated from golden plates revealed to him by an angel. After facing persecution, Smith moved his followers to Kirtland, Ohio, then to Independence, Missouri, then to Nauvoo, Illinois. He was killed by a mob in Carthage, Illinois (1844). Brigham Young led the Mormons to the Great Salt Lake (Utah, 1847)
- **Millerism (Adventism)** : William Miller predicted that Christ would return on October 22, 1844. When he did not, the “Great Disappointment” led to the formation of the Seventh-day Adventist Church
- **Camp meetings** : large outdoor religious gatherings that lasted for days (sometimes weeks). The most famous was at Cane Ridge, Kentucky (1801), where 10,000-25,000 people attended. Camp meetings were emotional, ecstatic, and chaotic: people shouted, wept, fell down, danced, and spoke in tongues. They were open to everyone, including enslaved and free Blacks, and women

**Reform Movements (KC-4.1.III.B, KC-4.1.III.C):**
- The Second Great Awakening inspired a wave of “perfectionist” reform movements: if individuals could perfect themselves, they could also perfect society
- **Temperance** : the movement to ban alcohol. The American Temperance Society (1826) had 1.5 million members by 1835. Temperance advocates argued that alcohol caused poverty, crime, family violence, and moral decay. Some states passed prohibition laws (Maine 1851, others after this period)
- **Asylum and prison reform** : **Dorothea Dix** (1802-1887) visited jails and almshouses, discovering that the mentally ill were housed with criminals, chained to walls, and beaten. She campaigned for the creation of state-funded “insane asylums.” Between 1843 and 1852, she convinced 11 states to build mental hospitals
- **Abolitionism** : see LO 4.K
- **Women’s rights** : see LO 4.K
- **Education reform** : **Horace Mann** (1796-1859) , secretary of the Massachusetts Board of Education (1837-1848), created the first statewide public school system (common schools). He argued that public education was essential for democracy (to educate citizens) and social mobility. Massachusetts passed a compulsory attendance law (1852, first in the nation)
- **Utopian communities** : some reformers tried to build perfect societies from scratch
- **Brook Farm (1841-1847)** : transcendentalist commune in West Roxbury, Massachusetts, founded by George Ripley. Members shared work (farming, teaching, printing) equally and enjoyed intellectual activities (lectures, concerts, dances). It failed financially after a fire (1847)
- **New Harmony (1825-1829)** : secular socialist community in Indiana, founded by Robert Owen (a Welsh industrialist). It failed due to internal conflict and lack of religious belief (most Americans were religious)
- **Oneida Community (1848-1881)** : a perfectionist community in New York that practiced “complex marriage” (any man could have sex with any woman, as long as both consented; children were raised communally). It failed after its founder, John Humphrey Noyes, died
- **Shakers** : religious communities that practiced celibacy, gender equality, and communal ownership. They grew by converting new members and by adopting orphans. Shaker crafts (furniture, boxes, tools) are famous for their simplicity and quality

**Geographic Context – Westward Expansion (KC-4.3.I, KC-4.3.II):**
- **Louisiana Purchase (1803)** : Jefferson sent James Monroe and Robert Livingston to France to buy New Orleans for $10 million. Napoleon, needing money for his European wars and losing interest in a New World empire after the Haitian Revolution (1791-1804), offered to sell the entire Louisiana Territory (828,000 square miles) for $15 million (3 cents per acre). Jefferson doubted his constitutional authority (strict construction), but he went ahead anyway. The treaty was ratified by the Senate (24-7). The Louisiana Purchase doubled the size of the United States
- **Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804-1806)** : Meriwether Lewis and William Clark led a 33-member Corps of Discovery from St. Louis to the Pacific Ocean (Fort Clatsop, Oregon). They mapped the territory, recorded plant and animal species, established diplomatic relations with Native nations (though they often ignored Native sovereignty), and searched for a water route to the Pacific (unsuccessful). Sacagawea (a Shoshone woman, wife of French trader Toussaint Charbonneau) served as guide and interpreter
- **Zebulon Pike Expedition (1806-1807)** : explored the southern Louisiana Territory (Colorado, New Mexico). Pike discovered Pikes Peak. He was captured by Spanish authorities (who considered him a spy) and later released
- **Texas** : Mexico won independence from Spain (1821). The Mexican government encouraged American immigration to Texas (Coahuila y Tejas) as a buffer against Comanche raids. Stephen F. Austin brought 300 families (the “Old Three Hundred”) to Texas (1825). By 1830, there were 20,000 white Americans in Texas (and 5,000 enslaved Africans), outnumbering Tejanos (Mexican Texans) 10 to 1
- Mexico abolished slavery (1829) but exempted Texas (temporarily). Mexican law required immigrants to convert to Catholicism and become Mexican citizens. Most Americans ignored these laws
- **Texas Revolution (1835-1836)** : Americans in Texas revolted against Mexican President Antonio López de Santa Anna (who had centralized power and abolished the Mexican Constitution of 1824)
- **Alamo (March 6, 1836)** : Santa Anna’s army (1,800 troops) besieged the Alamo mission in San Antonio. 189 Texan defenders (including Davy Crockett, Jim Bowie, William B. Travis) held out for 13 days. Santa Anna’s forces stormed the Alamo, killing all defenders. “Remember the Alamo!” became a battle cry
- **Goliad Massacre (March 27, 1836)** : Santa Anna executed 342 Texan prisoners
- **Battle of San Jacinto (April 21, 1836)** : Sam Houston’s army (800 men) surprised Santa Anna’s forces (1,200 men), shouting “Remember the Alamo! Remember Goliad!” The Texans killed 630, captured 700, and forced Santa Anna to sign a treaty recognizing Texan independence (the treaty was not ratified by Mexico; Mexico never recognized Texan independence until 1848)
- The **Republic of Texas** (1836-1845) was an independent nation. It was not annexed by the US immediately because antislavery Northerners opposed adding a slave state, and because Mexico threatened war
- **Oregon** : The US and Britain jointly occupied Oregon (1818-1846). American missionaries (Jason Lee, Marcus and Narcissa Whitman) settled in the Willamette Valley. The Oregon Trail (blazed 1836-1842) brought thousands of settlers. By 1845, there were 5,000 Americans in Oregon, outnumbering British subjects. The US and Britain agreed to the 49th parallel boundary (Treaty of Oregon, 1846), avoiding war. “54-40 or Fight!” (Democratic slogan, 1844) demanded the US claim all of Oregon up to 54°40’ (the southern border of Russian Alaska). James K. Polk compromised
- **Mexican-American War (1846-1848)** : see LO 5.C (Period 5)

## LO 4.B: Explain the causes and effects of policy debates in the early republic.

**Contextualization:** Jefferson’s presidency (1801-1809) was marked by debates over the size and power of the federal government, the national bank, the tariff, and foreign policy (especially the Napoleonic Wars). After Jefferson, James Madison (1809-1817) and James Monroe (1817-1825) faced similar debates. The “Era of Good Feelings” (1817-1825) saw a brief lull in partisan conflict (the Federalist Party collapsed), but new debates emerged over tariffs, internal improvements, and slavery.

**Thesis:** Policy debates in the early republic were caused by fundamental disagreements over **the powers of the federal government** (loose vs. strict construction of the Constitution), **economic policy** (tariffs, national bank, internal improvements), **relations with European powers** (war with Britain 1812, the Monroe Doctrine), and **the expansion of slavery** (Missouri Crisis), leading to the formation of new political parties (Democratic-Republicans, National Republicans, Democrats, Whigs) and the temporary resolution of some issues through compromise (Missouri Compromise 1820).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Debate over the National Bank (KC-4.1.I.A):**
- The **First Bank of the United States (1791-1811)** was chartered for 20 years. Its charter expired in 1811; the Democratic-Republican majority in Congress refused to renew it (arguing it was unconstitutional and favored Northern commercial interests)
- The War of 1812 (see below) revealed the weakness of a central bank: the federal government could not borrow money easily, and inflation skyrocketed
- **Second Bank of the United States (1816-1836)** : Congress chartered a new national bank (with a 20-year charter). Its capital was $35 million (largest corporation in America). The bank was headquartered in Philadelphia, with branches throughout the states
- **Functions** : held federal deposits, issued banknotes (currency), made loans to the federal government and private businesses, and regulated state banks (by demanding that state banks redeem their notes in specie—gold or silver)
- **Opposition** : State bankers resented the Bank’s regulatory power; Westerners and Southerners believed the Bank was a “monster” that served Northern commercial interests; Andrew Jackson opposed the Bank and vetoed its recharter in 1832 (see LO 4.H)
- **McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)** : Maryland imposed a tax on the Baltimore branch of the Bank of the United States. James McCulloch, the branch cashier, refused to pay. The case went to the Supreme Court. Chief Justice John Marshall ruled:
- **Congress had the power to charter a bank** (even though the Constitution does not explicitly say so) because the “necessary and proper” clause granted Congress “implied powers”
- **Maryland could not tax the Bank** because “the power to tax is the power to destroy”; states could not destroy federal institutions
- This was a major victory for loose construction and federal power

**Debate over the Tariff (KC-4.1.I.A, KC-4.1.I.D):**
- **Tariff of 1789** : First protective tariff (8% average). Designed to raise revenue and protect infant industries
- **Tariff of 1816** : After the War of 1812, Congress raised tariffs to protect American manufacturers from cheap British imports (British mills were dumping goods at below cost to crush American competition). The tariff averaged 20-25%
- **Tariff of 1824** : Increased rates again. Southerners (who exported cotton and imported manufactured goods) protested that the tariff was unconstitutional and benefited Northern manufacturers at the expense of Southern planters
- **Tariff of 1828 (Tariff of Abominations)** : Raised rates to 45-50%. Southerners called it the “Tariff of Abominations.” Vice President John C. Calhoun (of South Carolina) wrote the **South Carolina Exposition and Protest** (1828), arguing that states had the right to **nullify** unconstitutional federal laws (nullification doctrine)
- **Nullification Crisis (1832-1833)** : see LO 4.H

**Debate over Internal Improvements (KC-4.1.I.A, KC-4.1.I.A):**
- **American System** : Proposed by Henry Clay (Kentucky, Speaker of the House). Three components:
- **Protective tariff** (to raise revenue and protect American industry)
- **Internal improvements** (roads, canals, harbors) to connect the nation and facilitate commerce
- **National bank** (to provide a stable currency and credit)
- Clay’s American System was designed to benefit all regions: the tariff would help Northern manufacturers; internal improvements would help the West (transportation) and the South (access to markets); the bank would stabilize the economy nationwide
- **Opposition** : Democratic-Republicans (strict constructionists) argued that the Constitution did not authorize the federal government to build roads and canals (internal improvements were states’ responsibilities). President James Monroe vetoed the Cumberland Road bill (1822) for this reason
- **Internal improvements were mostly left to states and private companies** (Erie Canal built by New York State; Baltimore & Ohio railroad built by private investors; National Road built by federal government but was an exception)

**Debate over Foreign Policy – The Napoleonic Wars and the War of 1812 (KC-4.1.I.A):**
- **Napoleonic Wars (1803-1815)** : Britain and France were at war. Both nations seized American ships and impressed American sailors (forced them into the British navy). Britain seized 10,000 American sailors between 1803-1812
- **Jefferson’s response – Embargo Act (1807)** : Jefferson banned all American trade with foreign nations (to pressure Britain and France to respect American neutrality). The embargo was a disaster: American exports fell from $108 million (1807) to $22 million (1808); New England’s economy collapsed; smuggling flourished. The embargo was repealed in 1809 (replaced by the Non-Intercourse Act, which banned trade with Britain and France only)
- **Macon’s Bill No. 2 (1810)** : Reopened trade with all nations; but if either Britain or France stopped violating American neutrality, the US would embargo the other. Napoleon claimed to have ended his violations; Madison embargoed Britain (1811)
- **War of 1812 (1812-1815)** : The US declared war on Britain (June 18, 1812). Causes:
- Impressment of American sailors
- British seizure of American ships and cargo
- British support for Native American resistance in the Northwest Territory (the “Indian problem”)
- “War hawks” (young Democratic-Republican congressmen from the South and West: Henry Clay, John C. Calhoun) demanded war to expand into Canada (to end British influence over Natives) and Spanish Florida (to seize land)
- **Key events of the War of 1812** :
- **US defeats in the Northwest** : British and Native forces (Tecumseh’s confederacy) captured Detroit (August 1812). General William Henry Harrison defeated Tecumseh at the Battle of the Thames (October 1813); Tecumseh was killed
- **Burning of Washington, D.C. (August 1814)** : British troops marched on Washington, burned the Capitol, the White House, and other public buildings. President James Madison and Dolley Madison fled (Dolley saved Gilbert Stuart’s portrait of George Washington)
- **Battle of Baltimore (September 1814)** : British bombarded Fort McHenry (Francis Scott Key wrote “The Star-Spangled Banner” after seeing the flag still flying in the morning)
- **Battle of New Orleans (January 1815)** : Andrew Jackson defeated a British invasion force (2,000 British killed/wounded; 70 Americans killed/wounded). The battle was fought two weeks after the Treaty of Ghent (signed December 24, 1814, but news had not reached New Orleans). Jackson became a national hero
- **Treaty of Ghent (1814)** : Ended the war. It was essentially a draw: no territory changed hands; the impressment issue was not mentioned (but the Napoleonic Wars had ended anyway); British support for Natives ended
- **Effects of the War of 1812** :
- Increased American nationalism (the “Second War of Independence”)
- Broke the power of Native nations in the Northwest Territory (Tecumseh’s confederacy collapsed)
- Spurred American manufacturing (the embargo and war had cut off imports)
- The Federalist Party collapsed (Federalists had opposed the war and talked of secession at the **Hartford Convention (1814-1815)** ; they were seen as unpatriotic)
- Led to the “Era of Good Feelings” (one-party rule by Democratic-Republicans)

**Foreign Policy – The Monroe Doctrine (1823) (KC-4.1.I.A, KC-4.3.I.A.ii):**
- **Context** : After the Napoleonic Wars, Spain’s Latin American colonies were revolting (Mexico, Gran Colombia, Peru, Argentina, Chile, etc.). The US recognized these new republics. European powers (France, Russia, Prussia, Austria) considered intervening to restore Spanish rule
- **Monroe Doctrine (December 2, 1823)** : President James Monroe’s annual message to Congress included a foreign policy declaration written by Secretary of State John Quincy Adams
- **“The American continents… are henceforth not to be considered as subjects for future colonization by any European powers.”**
- **“We should consider any attempt on their part to extend their system to any portion of this hemisphere as dangerous to our peace and safety.”** (Europe should not interfere in the Western Hemisphere)
- In return, the US would not interfere in European affairs or in existing European colonies
- **Significance** :
- The Monroe Doctrine was not international law; it was a unilateral declaration of US policy. The US had little power to enforce it (the British navy enforced it, because Britain wanted to trade with Latin America)
- The Monroe Doctrine became a cornerstone of US foreign policy (extended by Theodore Roosevelt’s “Roosevelt Corollary” in 1904)
- The doctrine asserted US leadership in the Western Hemisphere

**The Missouri Crisis (1819-1820) (KC-4.1.I.D, KC-4.3.II.C):**
- **Context** : Missouri applied for statehood as a slave state (1819). The territory had 2,000 slaves. The US had 22 states (11 free, 11 slave). Adding Missouri as a slave state would upset the balance in the Senate
- **Tallmadge Amendment (1819)** : Representative James Tallmadge (NY) proposed an amendment to the Missouri statehood bill that would:
- Prohibit further importation of slaves into Missouri
- Free all children born to enslaved mothers in Missouri after the age of 25 (gradual emancipation)
- The Tallmadge Amendment passed the House (Northern majority) but failed in the Senate (Southern majority)
- **Debate** : The Missouri Crisis exploded into a national debate. Northerners argued that Congress had the power to prohibit slavery in territories (citing the Northwest Ordinance). Southerners argued that Congress could not impose conditions on statehood and that each state had the right to decide its own domestic institutions (including slavery)
- **The Missouri Compromise (1820)** : Proposed by Henry Clay (“The Great Compromiser”)
- Missouri would be admitted as a **slave state**
- Maine (which had been part of Massachusetts) would be admitted as a **free state** (preserving the balance in the Senate: 12 free, 12 slave)
- **Slavery was prohibited in the Louisiana Territory north of 36°30’ latitude** (the southern border of Missouri), except for the state of Missouri itself
- **Significance** :
- The Missouri Compromise temporarily resolved the issue of slavery’s expansion
- It established that Congress had the power to regulate slavery in territories (a precedent that would be overturned by the Dred Scott decision 1857)
- It set a geographical line (36°30’) that would become a battleground in the 1850s (Kansas-Nebraska Act 1854 repealed it)
- Thomas Jefferson wrote that the Missouri Crisis “like a fire bell in the night, awakened and filled me with terror. I considered it at once as the knell of the Union.”

## LO 4.C: Explain how different regional interests affected debates about the role of the federal government in the early republic.

**Contextualization:** The early republic was not a monolithic nation; different regions (North, South, West) had different economic interests, social structures, and political priorities. These regional interests shaped debates over tariffs, internal improvements, the national bank, and slavery.

**Thesis:** Regional interests affected debates about the role of the federal government because **Northern states** (manufacturing, commerce) favored a strong federal government that could enact protective tariffs, fund internal improvements, and charter a national bank; **Southern states** (plantation agriculture, slavery) favored a weak federal government that would not interfere with slavery, would keep tariffs low (to keep the cost of imported goods down), and would leave internal improvements to states; and **Western states** (small farmers, frontier settlers) favored federal land policies, internal improvements (roads, canals to get their goods to market), and cheap land (the Preemption Act 1830, the Homestead Act 1862 after this period).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Northern Regional Interests (Manufacturing, Commerce, Finance) (KC-4.1.I.D, KC-4.2.III.D):**
- **Protective tariff** : Northern manufacturers wanted high tariffs to protect their industries from British competition. Example: the Tariff of 1816 (25%) and the Tariff of 1824 (higher) were supported by the North but opposed by the South
- **National bank** : Northern merchants and bankers wanted a stable national currency and credit system (the Bank of the United States). They saw state banks as unstable and inflationary
- **Internal improvements** : Northerners wanted federal funding for roads, canals, and harbors to connect Northern cities and transport goods. The Erie Canal (built by New York State, not federal) was a model; Northerners wanted federal aid for similar projects
- **Federal power** : Northerners generally favored loose construction of the Constitution (implied powers) because it allowed the federal government to act. They supported Supreme Court decisions (McCulloch v. Maryland, Gibbons v. Ogden) that expanded federal power
- **Slavery** : Most Northern states abolished slavery after the Revolution (to varying degrees). Northerners generally opposed the expansion of slavery into new territories (free soil), though many did not support immediate abolition in the South (fearing racial equality or economic disruption). The “free soil” movement argued that slavery should be restricted to the South; western territories should be reserved for free white farmers

**Southern Regional Interests (Plantation Agriculture, Slavery) (KC-4.1.I.D, KC-4.2.III.C, KC-4.2.III.D):**
- **Low tariff** : Southern planters exported cotton (to Britain) and imported manufactured goods (from Britain and the North). High tariffs (like the Tariff of 1824, Tariff of Abominations 1828) raised the cost of imported goods and provoked retaliation from Britain (which could reduce its purchase of Southern cotton). Southerners called the tariff “the Tariff of Abominations” and argued it was unconstitutional
- **Weak central government** : Southerners feared that a strong federal government might interfere with slavery (by abolishing it, restricting its expansion, or enforcing the Fugitive Slave Clause more vigorously). They favored strict construction of the Constitution and states’ rights
- **Nullification doctrine** : John C. Calhoun (South Carolina) argued that states had the right to “nullify” (declare void) federal laws they considered unconstitutional. This doctrine emerged from the tariff debates (see LO 4.H)
- **Slavery expansion** : Southerners wanted to expand slavery into new territories (Missouri, Texas, the Southwest) to maintain political power (the three-fifths clause gave the South extra representation in Congress) and to create new land for cotton cultivation (which exhausted the soil quickly)
- **Internal improvements** : Southerners generally opposed federal funding for internal improvements, arguing that they were a states’ responsibility. However, some Southerners supported specific projects that would benefit their region (e.g., the National Road, which connected the Potomac River to the Ohio River, benefiting Virginia and the West)

**Western Regional Interests (Small Farmers, Frontier Settlers) (KC-4.1.I.D, KC-4.2.III.D):**
- **Cheap land** : Westerners wanted the federal government to sell land at low prices (the Land Act of 1820 reduced the price to $1.25 per acre, minimum 80 acres). The **Preemption Act (1830)** allowed squatters (settlers who had built on land before it was surveyed and sold) to buy that land at the minimum price
- **Internal improvements** : Westerners needed roads, canals, and navigable rivers to transport their crops (wheat, corn, pork, whiskey) to eastern markets. The National Road (Cumberland Road) benefited the West; the Erie Canal (built by New York State) did as well. Westerners wanted federal funding for more projects
- **The “American System”** : Westerners supported Henry Clay’s American System (protective tariff, internal improvements, national bank) because it promised to benefit all regions, including the West
- **Slavery** : The West was divided. The Northwest Territory (Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Michigan, Wisconsin) was free soil (by the Northwest Ordinance). The Southwest (Kentucky, Tennessee, Missouri, Arkansas, Louisiana) allowed slavery. Westerners who were small farmers (often from the North) generally opposed slavery’s expansion (they did not want to compete with slave labor); Westerners who were planters (from the South) supported slavery’s expansion
- **Native American removal** : Westerners wanted the federal government to remove Native Americans from their lands to make room for white settlement. Andrew Jackson’s Indian Removal Act (1830) was popular in the West (see LO 4.H)

**Debates over Economic Policy (KC-4.1.I.D, KC-4.2.III.D):**
- **The American System** : Henry Clay (Kentucky) proposed a comprehensive plan to unify the nation’s economy (see LO 4.B)
- **Opposition to the American System** :
- Northerners (Radical Democrats, followers of Martin Van Buren) opposed the American System because they thought it benefited only wealthy capitalists, not workers
- Southerners opposed the American System because of the tariff (they called it the “American System of plunder”)
- Andrew Jackson opposed the American System because he distrusted the national bank (see LO 4.H) and favored states’ rights
- **Internal improvements debates** :
- President James Monroe vetoed the Cumberland Road bill (1822) because he believed the Constitution did not authorize federal funding for roads
- In **Gibbons v. Ogden (1824)** , John Marshall’s Supreme Court ruled that the federal government had the exclusive power to regulate interstate commerce (including navigation). This expanded federal power but did not require federal funding of internal improvements
- **President John Quincy Adams (1825-1829)** proposed a bold program of federal internal improvements (roads, canals, universities, scientific expeditions). Congress rejected most of it as too expensive and unconstitutional

**Debates over Slavery (KC-4.1.I.D, KC-4.3.II.C):**
- **Missouri Crisis (1819-1820)** : see LO 4.B
- **The “Gag Rule” (1836-1844)** : The House of Representatives adopted a resolution automatically tabling (killing) all petitions relating to slavery. This was a response to the flood of antislavery petitions sent to Congress by the American Anti-Slavery Society. John Quincy Adams (then a Massachusetts congressman) fought the gag rule, arguing that it violated the First Amendment right to petition. The gag rule was repealed in 1844
- **Abolitionist mail campaign (1835)** : The American Anti-Slavery Society sent thousands of antislavery pamphlets to the South. Southern postmasters refused to deliver them, and President Jackson authorized them to do so (violating federal law). This intensified sectional conflict

## LO 4.D: Explain how and why American foreign policy developed and expanded over time.

**Contextualization:** In 1800, the United States was a weak nation, sandwiched between British Canada, Spanish Florida, Spanish Louisiana, and the British navy. By 1848, the United States had doubled its territory (Louisiana Purchase), fought and won a war with Mexico, established trade with China and Japan (limited), and asserted its dominance over the Western Hemisphere (Monroe Doctrine).

**Thesis:** American foreign policy developed and expanded through **diplomatic efforts to acquire territory** (Louisiana Purchase 1803, Texas annexation 1845, Oregon Treaty 1846), **military actions** (War of 1812, Seminole Wars, Mexican-American War), **American Indian removal** (Treaty of Fort Jackson 1814, Indian Removal Act 1830, Trail of Tears), and **the Monroe Doctrine (1823)** , which asserted US dominance over the Western Hemisphere, all driven by a desire to expand trade, secure borders, and spread American institutions (including slavery).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Acquisition of Territory – Diplomacy and Purchase (KC-4.3.I.A.i):**
- **Louisiana Purchase (1803)** : see LO 4.A
- **Florida (Adams-Onís Treaty, 1819)** : Andrew Jackson invaded Florida (1817-1818) to suppress Native American (Seminole) raids and capture escaped slaves. Jackson’s actions (he executed two British subjects accused of arming Natives) provoked a diplomatic crisis. Secretary of State John Quincy Adams negotiated the Adams-Onís Treaty (1819):
- Spain ceded Florida to the US
- The US agreed to assume $5 million in claims by American citizens against Spain
- The US established a clear boundary with New Spain (Spanish Texas, New Mexico, California) at the Sabine River (Texas-Louisiana border) and the Red River (Oklahoma-Texas border)
- The US relinquished its claim to Texas (temporarily)
- **Texas annexation (1845)** : see LO 5.B (Period 5) – Texas was annexed as a slave state (despite Northern opposition). Mexico threatened war. The US declared war on Mexico after a border skirmish (the Thornton Affair, April 25, 1846)
- **Oregon Treaty (1846)** : The US and Britain agreed to the 49th parallel as the boundary between US Oregon Territory and British Canada (British Columbia). The US gained present-day Washington, Oregon, Idaho, and parts of Montana and Wyoming. The treaty avoided war (“54-40 or Fight!” was Democratic campaign slogan, but President Polk compromised)

**Military Actions (KC-4.3.I.A.ii):**
- **War of 1812 (1812-1815)** : see LO 4.B
- **First Seminole War (1817-1818)** : Andrew Jackson’s invasion of Florida – see above
- **Second Seminole War (1835-1842)** : The US attempted to remove Seminole Indians from Florida (under the Indian Removal Act of 1830). Seminoles, led by Osceola, resisted fiercely, using guerrilla tactics in the swamps. The war cost the US $40 million (the most expensive Indian war in US history) and thousands of lives. Most Seminoles were forcibly removed to Oklahoma (Indian Territory), but a few hundred remained in the Florida Everglades (never conquered)
- **Mexican-American War (1846-1848)** : see LO 5.C (Period 5)

**American Indian Removal (KC-4.3.I.A.ii, KC-4.3.I.A.ii):**
- **Treaty of Fort Jackson (1814)** : After defeating the Creek Nation at the Battle of Horseshoe Bend (1814), Andrew Jackson forced the Creek to cede 23 million acres of land in Georgia and Alabama (half of Creek territory)
- **Indian Removal Act (1830)** : Signed by Andrew Jackson. Authorized the president to negotiate treaties to exchange Native lands in the East for lands west of the Mississippi (Indian Territory, present-day Oklahoma). It did not authorize the forced removal of Natives (in theory, treaties had to be voluntary), but in practice, pressure, fraud, and force were used
- **Trail of Tears (1838-1839)** : The Cherokee Nation sued the state of Georgia (which had passed laws abolishing the Cherokee government and distributing Cherokee land to white settlers). The Supreme Court ruled in *Cherokee Nation v. Georgia* (1831) that the Cherokee were a “domestic dependent nation” and not a foreign state (so the Court lacked jurisdiction). In *Worcester v. Georgia* (1832), the Court ruled that Georgia could not impose its laws on the Cherokee. Andrew Jackson reportedly said, “John Marshall has made his decision; now let him enforce it.” Jackson ignored the ruling
- The Cherokee were forced to march 1,200 miles from Georgia, North Carolina, Tennessee, and Alabama to Indian Territory (Oklahoma). 4,000 of 16,000 Cherokee died from disease, starvation, and exposure
- Other nations also removed: Chickasaw, Choctaw, Muscogee (Creek), Seminole
- **Black Hawk’s War (1832)** : Sauk and Fox Indians (led by Black Hawk) resisted removal from Illinois. They were defeated by US forces; Black Hawk was captured, toured the East (where he met Andrew Jackson), then was imprisoned and later released

**The Monroe Doctrine (1823) (KC-4.3.I.A.ii):**
- See LO 4.B
- **Key effect** : Asserted US dominance over the Western Hemisphere, but was enforced by the British navy (not the US navy, which was small). It became the foundation of later US foreign policy in Latin America (Roosevelt Corollary 1904, Good Neighbor Policy 1930s)

**Trade with Asia (KC-4.3.I, KC-4.3.I):**
- **China trade** : After the American Revolution, US merchants (the “China traders”) sailed to Canton (Guangzhou) to trade for tea, silk, and porcelain. They used the “opium route” (selling Turkish opium to Chinese smugglers) to finance their purchases, but this was illegal and would lead to conflicts later
- **The *Empress of China* (1784)** : First American ship to trade with China (sailed from New York to Canton)
- **Pacific Northwest fur trade** : US ships (the “Boston men”) traded with Natives for sea otter pelts, which were sold in China for high prices
- **Missionaries** : American missionaries (Catholic and Protestant) traveled to Asia (China, India, Hawaii, later Japan) to convert souls. Their reports shaped American attitudes toward Asia
- **Japan** : The US wanted Japan to open its ports for trade and refueling (coal for steamships). President Millard Fillmore sent Commodore Matthew Perry (1853-1854) to Tokyo Bay with a squadron of “black ships” (steam-powered warships). Perry threatened to use force if Japan did not negotiate. The **Treaty of Kanagawa (1854)** opened two Japanese ports to US ships (for refueling and supplies) – after this period, but the groundwork was laid in the 1840s

**Limits of American Power:**
- The US could not enforce the Monroe Doctrine without the British navy
- Texas remained independent from 1836-1845 because the US feared war with Mexico and Northern opposition to slavery expansion
- Britain still had a presence in Oregon until 1846
- Russia had Alaska (sold to the US in 1867)
- The US did not control the Southwest (California, New Mexico, Arizona, Nevada, Utah, Colorado) until the Mexican-American War (1848)

## LO 4.E: Explain the causes and effects of the innovations in technology, agriculture, and commerce over time.

**Contextualization:** The Market Revolution (c. 1800-1850) was a period of rapid technological innovation, agricultural transformation, and commercial expansion. It connected the nation through canals, railroads, and steamboats; transformed manufacturing through the factory system; and increased agricultural productivity through new machines.

**Thesis:** Innovations in technology, agriculture, and commerce were caused by a combination of **entrepreneurship** (investors seeking profit), **government policies** (tariffs, land grants, patents), and **labor supply** (immigrants and rural migrants); their effects included **the creation of a national market** (regional specialization, interdependence), **the rise of factories and wage labor** (the “wage slave” vs. the independent artisan), **the expansion of slavery** (cotton gin), and **the transformation of daily life** (faster travel, communication, consumer goods, but also longer working hours, child labor, and economic instability).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Causes – Entrepreneurship, Government, and Labor (KC-4.2.I.A):**
- **Entrepreneurs** : Eli Whitney (cotton gin, interchangeable parts), Robert Fulton (steamboat), Samuel Slater (textile mills), Francis Cabot Lowell (Lowell mills), Cyrus McCormick (mechanical reaper), John Deere (steel plow), Samuel Morse (telegraph)
- **Government policies** :
- **Protective tariffs** (1816, 1824, 1828, 1832) encouraged domestic manufacturing by making imported goods more expensive
- **Patents** : The US Patent Office granted patents to inventors, protecting their intellectual property and encouraging innovation
- **Land grants** : The federal government gave land to railroad companies (5-10 miles on each side of the track) and canal companies, subsidizing their construction
- **State funding** : New York State funded the Erie Canal ($7 million, 1825). Other states funded internal improvements (Pennsylvania Main Line Canal, Ohio canals)
- **Labor supply** :
- **Immigrants** : 5 million immigrants arrived between 1820 and 1860 (mostly from Ireland, Germany, and Britain). They provided labor for canals, railroads, factories, and domestic service
- **Rural-to-urban migration** : Young women from New England farms moved to Lowell, Massachusetts, to work in textile mills (“Lowell mill girls”). Young men from rural areas moved to cities to work as clerks, laborers, and artisans
- **Child labor** : Children worked in factories (textile mills, glass factories, coal mines) from age 8 or younger. Labor reformers (like the Lowell Female Labor Reform Association) protested child labor, but it remained common until the early 20th century

**Technological Innovations – Transportation (KC-4.2.I.B, KC-4.2.I.C):**
- **Erie Canal (1825)** : 363 miles, 83 locks, cost $7 million. Slashed shipping costs from $100 per ton to $5 per ton. Reduced travel time from New York to Buffalo from 3 weeks (by wagon) to 8 days (by canal boat)
- **National Road (Cumberland Road)** : 620 miles from Maryland to Illinois (completed 1837). First federally funded highway. Cost $6 million
- **Steamboats** : Robert Fulton’s *Clermont* (1807) traveled from New York City to Albany (150 miles) in 32 hours. By 1820, there were 69 steamboats on the western rivers (Mississippi, Ohio). Steamboats could travel upstream (against the current), opening the interior to trade
- **Railroads** : The Baltimore & Ohio (B&O) was chartered in 1827; the first passenger railroad in the US. By 1840, the US had 3,000 miles of railroad track (mostly in the North). By 1850, 9,000 miles. Railroads were faster than canals (trains could travel 20-30 mph vs. 3-4 mph for canal boats) and could be built anywhere (canals required flat terrain and a water source)
- **Clipper ships** : Fast sailing ships (the *Flying Cloud* sailed from New York to San Francisco in 89 days, 1851). Clippers were used for the China trade (tea), the California gold rush, and the transatlantic passenger trade

**Technological Innovations – Communication (KC-4.2.I.B):**
- **Telegraph (1844)** : Samuel Morse sent “What hath God wrought?” from Washington to Baltimore (40 miles). By 1850, there were 12,000 miles of telegraph lines connecting major cities. By 1860, a transcontinental telegraph line connected St. Louis to San Francisco
- **Effect** : Telegraph allowed instant communication across long distances (no more waiting weeks for a letter). Businesses could coordinate operations; newspapers could report news faster; the stock market could react instantly

**Technological Innovations – Agriculture (KC-4.2.I.B):**
- **Cotton gin (1793)** : Eli Whitney. Separated cotton fibers from seeds. One operator could clean 50 pounds of cotton per day (vs. 1 pound per day by hand). Made short-staple cotton (which grew in the upland South) profitable. Cotton production exploded (3,000 bales in 1790, 178,000 in 1810, 4 million in 1860). The cotton gin increased the demand for enslaved labor (contradicting Whitney’s claim that it would reduce slavery)
- **Steel plow (1837)** : John Deere. Enabled farmers to cut through the tough sod of the Great Plains (the prairie sod was too thick for iron plows). This opened the Midwest to agriculture
- **Mechanical reaper (1834)** : Cyrus McCormick. Harvested wheat, cutting and bundling it. One reaper did the work of 5-10 men. Allowed farmers to harvest large wheat fields quickly (essential for western farms)
- **Threshing machine** : Separated grain from stalks. Increased productivity; allowed farmers to produce more grain with fewer workers

**Commercial Innovations – The National Market (KC-4.2.I.A, KC-4.2.I.D):**
- **Regional specialization** :
- **North** : manufacturing, finance, commerce (textiles, shoes, tools, clocks, furniture, ships, insurance, banking)
- **South** : cotton (plus tobacco, rice, sugar), slavery – exported to Britain and the North
- **West** : wheat, corn, livestock (pork, beef), whiskey – exported to the North (food for factory workers) and the South (food for slaves)
- **Interdependence** : The North depended on Southern cotton (for textile mills) and Western food (for factory workers). The South depended on Northern manufactured goods (textiles, machinery) and Western food. The West depended on Northern and Southern markets for its crops. This interdependence did not prevent political conflict; it intensified it (each region felt it was being exploited by the others)
- **New York City** became the commercial capital of the US (Erie Canal, transatlantic trade, banking, stock exchange). New York’s population grew from 60,000 (1800) to 515,000 (1850)
- **Consumer goods** : The Market Revolution produced a flood of consumer goods: textiles (clothing), shoes (made in Massachusetts factories), clocks (Connecticut), furniture, tools, guns (interchangeable parts), books, newspapers (penny press, 1830s), and later, canned goods, packaged food, and patent medicines

**Effects on Society (KC-4.2.II.A, KC-4.2.II.B, KC-4.2.II.A, KC-4.2.II.B, KC-4.2.II.C):**
- **Rise of factories and wage labor** : Before the Market Revolution, most Americans were farmers or artisans (self-employed). After, many worked in factories for wages. The “artisan republic” gave way to the “factory system.”
- **Decline of artisans** : Shoemakers, weavers, blacksmiths, and other craftsmen lost their independence. They could not compete with factory-produced goods. Some became factory workers (“wage slaves”); others became factory owners (the “middle class”)
- **Child labor** : Children worked in textile mills (Lawrence, Lowell, Fall River), coal mines (Pennsylvania), and glass factories (New Jersey). They worked 12-14 hour days, 6 days a week, in dangerous conditions. Reformers (like the New England Association of Farmers, Mechanics, and Other Workingmen) protested child labor, but state laws were weak until the 1880s-1900s
- **The “Lowell mill girls”** : Young women (ages 15-30) worked in Lowell mills for $2-4 per week (room and board deducted). They lived in company boarding houses with strict rules (curfews, church attendance, no alcohol). They worked 12-14 hour days, 6 days a week. They organized strikes (1834, 1836, 1845) and petitioned the Massachusetts legislature for a 10-hour day. Their wages declined in the 1840s, and many Irish immigrant women replaced them (lower pay)
- **The “middle class”** : A new class of managers, clerks, professionals (lawyers, doctors, engineers), and small business owners emerged. They lived in suburbs (first streetcar suburbs 1850s), sent their children to school (common schools), and embraced “separate spheres” ideology (women in the home, men in the workplace)
- **Labor unions** : Workers organized unions (National Trades’ Union 1834, Commonwealth v. Hunt 1842 – Massachusetts Supreme Court ruled unions were legal). However, courts often ruled against unions, and employers used “yellow-dog contracts” (workers had to agree not to join a union) and blacklists (lists of union organizers who were fired and denied work elsewhere)
- **Economic instability** : The Market Revolution brought booms and busts (panics of 1819, 1837, 1857). Banks failed, businesses went bankrupt, workers lost their jobs, and farmers lost their land. The federal government offered little relief (no unemployment insurance, no welfare). Many Americans blamed speculative banks, greedy capitalists, or the “monster” Bank of the United States

## LO 4.F: Explain how and why innovation in technology, agriculture, and commerce affected various segments of American society over time.

**Contextualization:** The Market Revolution did not affect all Americans equally. Some benefited enormously (merchants, factory owners, large planters, speculators); others lost their livelihoods (artisans, small farmers, some workers); and others were enslaved and had no voice at all (enslaved African Americans).

**Thesis:** Technological, agricultural, and commercial innovations affected various segments of American society differently: **white middle-class men** (managers, professionals) gained economic and political power; **white middle-class women** were idealized as the “angels of the home” (separate spheres); **white working-class men** (artisans, laborers) lost economic independence and organized labor unions to protest; **white working-class women** (Lowell mill girls, domestic servants) experienced wage labor and exploitation; **enslaved African Americans** were forced to work in cotton fields (their labor expanded due to the cotton gin); **free African Americans** faced discrimination and limited opportunities; **Native Americans** were dispossessed of their land to make room for white settlers (steel plow, reaper, canals); and **immigrants** (Irish, German) provided cheap labor for canals, railroads, and factories, facing nativist hostility.

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Effects on White Middle-Class Men (KC-4.2.II.B):**
- The “middle class” grew rapidly: managers (factory superintendents, railroad agents), clerks (accountants, salesmen), professionals (lawyers, doctors, engineers, teachers, ministers), and small business owners
- **Economic opportunities** : Salaries (not wages) allowed middle-class men to buy homes, send their children to school, and invest in new businesses
- **Political power** : Middle-class men were the backbone of the Whig Party (later Republican), which favored internal improvements, tariffs, and moral reform (temperance, abolitionism)
- **Ideology** : “Self-made man” (Horatio Alger’s “rags to riches” stories, though written after this period, reflected the ideal). They believed in education, hard work, thrift, and delayed gratification

**Effects on White Middle-Class Women – “Separate Spheres” (KC-4.2.II.C):**
- The Market Revolution created a new ideology: **separate spheres** – men worked in the public sphere (business, politics); women ruled the domestic sphere (home, children, religion, charity)
- **“The Cult of Domesticity”** : women were responsible for creating a peaceful, moral, and nurturing home (a refuge from the competitive, cutthroat world of commerce). Virtues: piety, purity, submissiveness, domesticity
- **Economic role** : Middle-class women did not work outside the home (except as teachers, governesses, or seamstresses in emergencies). They managed the household (servants, if they could afford them), raised children, attended church, performed charity work, and consumed (shopping was a female activity)
- **Education** : Middle-class girls received more education (female academies, seminaries) to become “instructors of their children” (republican motherhood). Emma Willard founded the Troy Female Seminary (1821); Mary Lyon founded Mount Holyoke Female Seminary (1837)
- **Limits** : Women could not vote, hold office, serve on juries, or sign contracts (coverture). A married woman’s legal identity was subsumed by her husband’s; she could not own property (until Married Women’s Property Acts, starting with Mississippi 1839, New York 1848), keep her wages, or sue or be sued separately from her husband

**Effects on White Working-Class Men (Artisans, Laborers) (KC-4.2.II.A, KC-4.2.II.A):**
- Before the Market Revolution, most artisans were self-employed (cobblers, weavers, blacksmiths, tailors, carpenters, printers). They controlled their own work hours, owned their own tools, and sold their own goods
- After the Market Revolution, “unskilled” workers (laborers) were hired by factories, railroads, and construction companies. They worked 12-14 hour days, 6 days a week, for low wages, with no job security
- **Skilled artisans** became “wage slaves”: they were hired by factory owners, lost control over their work, and were paid by the piece (piecework) or by the hour
- **Labor unions** : Workers organized unions to demand higher wages, shorter hours (10-hour day), and safer conditions. The **National Trades’ Union (1834)** was the first national labor organization. The **Lowell Female Labor Reform Association (1845)** organized women workers
- **Early strikes** : 1834 Lowell strike (mill girls protested wage cuts); 1835 Philadelphia general strike (workers in 20 occupations struck for 10-hour day); 1842 Lynn shoemakers’ strike
- **Legal status** : In *Commonwealth v. Hunt* (1842), the Massachusetts Supreme Court ruled that labor unions were legal (the first such ruling in the US). However, courts often issued injunctions against strikes, and employers used blacklists and “yellow-dog” contracts

**Effects on White Working-Class Women (Lowell Mill Girls, Domestic Servants) (KC-4.2.II.A, KC-4.2.II.C):**
- **Lowell mill girls** (see LO 4.E) were the first large-scale female industrial workforce. They lived in company boarding houses, worked 12-14 hours, earned $2-4 per week. They organized strikes and petitioned for a 10-hour day
- **Domestic servants** : Thousands of young women (and later Irish immigrants) worked as domestic servants (maids, cooks, nannies). They lived in their employers’ homes, worked long hours, had few rights, and were vulnerable to sexual harassment and exploitation
- **Factory women** were blamed for working outside the home (violating “separate spheres”). Their morality was questioned; some were accused of prostitution (the “factory girl” stereotype)

**Effects on Enslaved African Americans (KC-4.2.III.C, KC-4.3.II.B.ii):**
- The **cotton gin (1793)** made slavery more profitable than ever. Cotton production expanded into Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Arkansas, Missouri, Texas. The internal slave trade (slavery’s “Second Middle Passage”) transported 1 million enslaved people from the Upper South (Virginia, Maryland, Delaware, Kentucky) to the Deep South (Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Texas) between 1800-1860
- Enslaved people were forced to work in cotton fields from sunrise to sunset, supervised by overseers with whips. They lived in small cabins, ate meager rations (cornmeal, pork fat, molasses), had no legal rights, and could be sold at any time
- **Family separation** : The domestic slave trade broke up families (husbands separated from wives, parents from children). Solomon Northup (*12 Years a Slave*) was a free Black man kidnapped and sold into slavery in Louisiana (1841-1853)
- **Resistance** : Enslaved people resisted through covert means (working slowly, breaking tools, feigning illness) and overt means (running away, poisoning enslavers, rebellion). **Nat Turner’s Rebellion (1831)** : see LO 5.F (Period 5)
- **Free African Americans** (approx. 500,000 by 1860) lived mostly in the North (Philadelphia, New York, Boston, Cincinnati) but also in the Upper South (Baltimore, Richmond, Washington, D.C.). They faced discrimination: could not vote (most states), could not serve on juries, could not testify against whites, were barred from schools, hospitals, and public accommodations (segregation), and were excluded from many trades. Despite this, they built their own institutions: churches (AME, AME Zion), schools (African Free School), mutual aid societies, and newspapers (*Freedom’s Journal* 1827, *The North Star* 1847)

**Effects on Native Americans (KC-4.2.III.D):**
- Canals and railroads brought white settlers onto Native lands. The steel plow and mechanical reaper made those lands valuable. Natives were pressured to sell their lands (treaties) or forced to remove west of the Mississippi (Indian Removal Act 1830, Trail of Tears)
- **The “Five Civilized Tribes”** (Cherokee, Chickasaw, Choctaw, Creek, Seminole) were targeted for removal because they lived in the Southeast (Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Florida), which white settlers coveted for cotton plantations. They were called “civilized” because they had adopted some white customs (written constitutions, newspapers, schools, Christianity, chattel slavery)
- By 1840, most Native nations east of the Mississippi had been relocated to Indian Territory (Oklahoma). Their lands were taken by white settlers or sold to land speculators

**Effects on Immigrants (Irish, German) (KC-4.2.II.A):**
- **Irish immigrants** : Escaping the Great Famine (Potato Famine, 1845-1852, after this period but Irish immigration peaked 1840s-1850s). They were mostly Catholic, spoke English (but with an Irish accent), and were very poor. They worked as laborers (canals, railroads, docks), domestic servants, and factory workers. They were targets of nativist hostility (see LO 5.E, Period 5). They lived in slums (Five Points, New York City; Fort Hill, Boston)
- **German immigrants** : Escaping economic hardship and political unrest (failed revolutions 1848). They were mostly Lutheran or Catholic, spoke German, and were more skilled (farmers, brewers, bakers, carpenters, tailors, musicians). They settled in the Midwest (Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Wisconsin, Missouri, Texas) and established German-language churches, schools, and newspapers. They were less targeted by nativists (except during World War I)
- **Effects on the economy** : Immigrants provided cheap labor, allowing canals, railroads, and factories to be built. They also brought skills (German brewing, Irish construction) and cultural diversity (St. Patrick’s Day, Oktoberfest, sauerkraut, corned beef and cabbage)

## LO 4.G: Explain the causes and effects of the expansion of participatory democracy from 1800 to 1848.

**Contextualization:** In 1800, most states required voters to own property (land) or pay a certain amount of taxes. By 1840, almost all white men could vote, regardless of property ownership. This expansion of suffrage was part of a larger movement toward “Jacksonian Democracy”—the belief that ordinary people should participate in politics, hold office, and shape government policy.

**Thesis:** The expansion of participatory democracy from 1800 to 1848 was caused by **the elimination of property qualifications** (new western states adopted universal white male suffrage; eastern states followed to keep voters from moving west), **the rise of popular political parties** (Democratic-Republicans, Democrats, Whigs) that mobilized voters through campaigns, rallies, and newspapers, and **the contested election of 1824 and the “corrupt bargain”** (which galvanized Andrew Jackson’s supporters); its effects included **higher voter turnout** (80%+ in some states by 1840), **the election of Andrew Jackson (1828)** (the first “common man” president), **the spoils system** (rewarding political supporters with government jobs), **the rise of the Democratic Party** (Jackson) and the **Whig Party** (opposition to Jackson), and **the continued exclusion of women, African Americans, and Native Americans** from political participation.

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Causes – Elimination of Property Qualifications (KC-4.1.I):**
- **New western states** (Ohio 1803, Indiana 1816, Illinois 1818, Missouri 1821, etc.) wrote constitutions with **universal white male suffrage** (no property requirement). They wanted to attract settlers and compete with older states
- **Eastern states** (Massachusetts, New York, Virginia, etc.) revised their constitutions to eliminate or reduce property qualifications. They did not want to lose population (and political power) to the West
- **New York (1821)** : eliminated property qualifications for white men (but retained a $250 property requirement for African Americans — a racial qualification)
- **Massachusetts (1821)** : eliminated property qualifications for white men (but retained a taxpaying requirement)
- **Virginia (1830)** : reduced property qualifications but did not eliminate them (Virginia was the most conservative state; the planter elite controlled the government)
- **Rhode Island (1842)** : still used its colonial charter (which limited suffrage to landowners). This led to the **Dorr Rebellion (1842)** , in which Thomas Dorr and his followers attempted to force a new constitution through an extralegal convention. President John Tyler sent federal troops to Rhode Island, but the rebellion convinced the state to adopt a new constitution with expanded suffrage
- **By 1840** , over 90% of white men could vote (up from 50-70% in 1800). Property qualifications remained in a few states (Virginia, North Carolina, Rhode Island) but were low

**Causes – The Rise of Popular Political Parties (KC-4.1.I):**
- **Democratic-Republican Party** (Jefferson, Madison, Monroe) dominated the “Era of Good Feelings” (1817-1825). But after Monroe, the party split into factions
- **The “Corrupt Bargain” (1824)** : The election of 1824 had four candidates: Andrew Jackson (Tennessee), John Quincy Adams (Massachusetts), William H. Crawford (Georgia), and Henry Clay (Kentucky). Jackson won the most popular votes (vs. Adams) and the most electoral votes (99 vs. 84 for Adams, 41 for Crawford, 37 for Clay), but not a majority (131 needed). The election was thrown to the House of Representatives. Henry Clay (who had come in 4th) threw his support to John Quincy Adams. Clay became Adams’s Secretary of State. Jackson’s supporters called this a “corrupt bargain” (Clay sold his support for a cabinet position). Jackson was furious
- **Jackson’s campaign for 1828** : Jackson and his supporters (Martin Van Buren, John C. Calhoun, Thomas Hart Benton) built the **Democratic Party** from the ground up. They organized rallies, parades, barbecues, and local committees. They published newspapers (the *United States Telegraph*). They used the slogan “Adams can write, Jackson can fight.” They attacked Adams as an out-of-touch elitist who had purchased a billiard table (a sign of aristocratic luxury) for the White House
- **The election of 1828** : Jackson defeated Adams (178 electoral votes to 83). Voter turnout was 57% (up from 27% in 1824). Jackson’s victory was seen as the triumph of the “common man” over the “aristocracy”

**Causes – Jacksonian Ideology (KC-4.1.I):**
- **Rotation in office** (“spoils system”) : Jackson believed that ordinary citizens could hold government jobs. He replaced many federal officeholders with his supporters (about 10% of federal employees). He argued that “rotation in office” was democratic (preventing the creation of a permanent, corrupt bureaucracy). Critics called it the “spoils system” (“to the victor belong the spoils of the enemy”)
- **Opposition to the national bank** : Jackson believed the Bank of the United States was a “monster” that served the interests of wealthy capitalists, not ordinary farmers and workers (see LO 4.H)
- **Opposition to federal internal improvements** : Jackson vetoed the Maysville Road bill (1830) because it was a purely internal improvement in Kentucky (Henry Clay’s state). He argued that the federal government should not fund local projects (he was inconsistent; he supported some internal improvements, like the National Road)
- **Indian Removal** : Jackson supported Indian removal to open lands for white settlement (the Indian Removal Act of 1830, see LO 4.D)

**Effects – Higher Voter Turnout and Political Engagement (KC-4.1.I):**
- Voter turnout skyrocketed: 27% (1824), 57% (1828), 55% (1832), 58% (1836), 80% (1840). By 1840, over 80% of eligible white men voted in some states (the highest turnout in American history until the late 19th century)
- **Political campaigns** became mass spectacles: log cabin rallies (1840, “Tippecanoe and Tyler Too”), torchlight parades, barbecues, free alcohol (whiskey), campaign songs, and slogan-filled banners
- **Political parties** built permanent organizations: local committees, state central committees, national conventions (Democrats 1832, Whigs 1839). They distributed thousands of newspapers, pamphlets, and campaign biographies
- **The “penny press”** (1830s) made newspapers affordable for ordinary people (1 cent, vs. 6 cents for traditional papers). The *New York Sun* (1833), *New York Herald* (1835), and *New York Tribune* (1841) reached mass audiences with sensational stories, crime reports, and political news

**Effects – The Election of Andrew Jackson and the “Common Man” President (KC-4.1.I):**
- Jackson was the first president not from Virginia or Massachusetts (he was from Tennessee, a frontier state). He was the first to be born in a log cabin (though he was actually a wealthy planter with 150 slaves and a mansion called The Hermitage). He was the first to be nominated by a national party convention (Democrats, 1832)
- **Jackson’s personality** : He was a war hero (Battle of New Orleans, 1815). He was known as “Old Hickory” (tough as hickory wood). He trusted his instincts and distrusted experts (lawyers, bankers, politicians). He used the veto power more than all previous presidents combined (12 vetoes; previous presidents: 9 total). He asserted the power of the presidency against Congress and the Supreme Court

**Effects – The Spoils System (KC-4.1.I):**
- Jackson replaced about 10% of federal officeholders (about 900 out of 10,000). His successors (Martin Van Buren, William Henry Harrison, John Tyler, James K. Polk) continued the practice
- **Effects on government** : The spoils system created a more responsive government (officeholders owed their jobs to the president, not to a permanent bureaucracy). But it also led to corruption (officeholders embezzled funds, sold appointments, and used their positions for personal gain)
- **The assassination of James Garfield (1881)** was committed by a disappointed office-seeker (Charles Guiteau). This led to the Pendleton Civil Service Act (1883), which replaced the spoils system with a merit-based system

**Effects – Continuation of Exclusions (KC-4.1.I):**
- **Women** could not vote. The New Jersey constitution (1776) had allowed unmarried women and widows to vote if they met property requirements; the state legislature restricted suffrage to white men in 1807
- **African Americans** could not vote in most states. New York (1821) retained a $250 property requirement for Black men (while eliminating it for white men). Pennsylvania (1838) explicitly restricted suffrage to “white freemen.” The only states where Black men could vote on equal terms with whites were Massachusetts, Vermont, New Hampshire, Maine, Rhode Island (after 1842), and briefly New York (before 1821)
- **Native Americans** were not citizens and could not vote. The Naturalization Act of 1790 restricted naturalization to “free white persons.” Native Americans could only become citizens by treaty (some did), but most were not considered citizens until the Indian Citizenship Act of 1924

## LO 4.H: Explain the causes and effects of continuing policy debates about the role of the federal government from 1800 to 1848.

**Contextualization:** The period from 1800 to 1848 saw intense debates over the role of the federal government. Jefferson and his successors reduced the size of the federal government, but the Market Revolution and westward expansion forced new questions: Could the federal government charter a national bank? Could it fund internal improvements? Did states have the right to nullify federal laws? Could the president remove Native Americans from their lands?

**Thesis:** Continuing policy debates about the role of the federal government from 1800 to 1848 were caused by **conflicting visions of the Constitution** (strict vs. loose construction), **regional economic interests** (tariffs, internal improvements, banking), and **the expansion of slavery** (Missouri Crisis, gag rule, annexation of Texas); their effects included **the destruction of the Second Bank of the United States** (Jackson’s veto, deposit removal), **the Nullification Crisis** (South Carolina threatened secession; Jackson threatened force), **the Indian Removal Act and Trail of Tears** (federal government forcibly removed Natives), and **the rise of the Whig Party** (opposing Jackson’s “executive tyranny”).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Debate over the National Bank – The Bank War (KC-4.1.I.C):**
- **Second Bank of the United States (chartered 1816-1836)** : see LO 4.B
- **Andrew Jackson’s opposition** : Jackson hated the Bank. He believed it was unconstitutional (“The opinion of the [Supreme] Court… ought not to control the coordinate authorities of this government” – ignoring *McCulloch v. Maryland*). He believed it was a “monster” that served the interests of wealthy capitalists at the expense of ordinary farmers and workers. He believed it was corrupt (the Bank had made loans to members of Congress, possibly influencing their votes)
- **The Bank recharter bill (1832)** : Henry Clay (Jackson’s opponent) and Daniel Webster pushed a bill to recharter the Bank four years early (the charter expired in 1836). They wanted to force Jackson to veto it, making the Bank an issue in the 1832 election
- **Jackson’s veto** : Jackson vetoed the recharter bill. His veto message was a political manifesto: “It is to be regretted that the rich and powerful too often bend the acts of government to their selfish purposes… The bank of the United States is a private corporation… It is not owned by the people, but by a few wealthy stockholders.” Jackson argued that the president had the power to veto any bill he believed harmful, regardless of its constitutionality (this expanded the veto power)
- **The 1832 election** : Jackson defeated Clay (219 electoral votes to 49). Jackson interpreted his victory as a mandate to destroy the Bank
- **Removal of deposits (1833)** : Jackson ordered his Secretary of the Treasury to remove federal deposits from the Bank of the United States and deposit them in state-chartered “pet banks.” The Bank’s president, Nicholas Biddle, appealed to Congress. Henry Clay led an investigation. The Senate censured Jackson (1834) for assuming “authority and power not conferred by the Constitution” (the censure was expunged in 1837)
- **The Bank’s death** : Without federal deposits, the Bank of the United States lost its power. Its charter expired in 1836. It became a state-chartered bank (the Bank of the United States of Pennsylvania) and failed in 1841
- **Effects** :
- The “pet banks” (state banks) issued paper money recklessly, leading to inflation and land speculation
- Jackson issued the **Specie Circular (1836)** , requiring that public land be purchased with gold or silver (specie), not paper money. This reduced the money supply and contributed to the **Panic of 1837** (a severe depression that lasted until 1843)

**Debate over Tariffs – The Nullification Crisis (1832-1833) (KC-4.1.I.C):**
- **Tariff of Abominations (1828)** : see LO 4.B
- **South Carolina Exposition and Protest (1828)** : John C. Calhoun (Jackson’s vice president) anonymously wrote that states had the right to **nullify** (declare void) federal laws they considered unconstitutional
- **Tariff of 1832** : Reduced rates somewhat, but not enough for South Carolina
- **South Carolina’s Ordinance of Nullification (November 1832)** : A state convention declared the Tariffs of 1828 and 1832 “null, void, and no law” within South Carolina. It threatened to secede if the federal government attempted to collect tariffs
- **Jackson’s response** : Jackson was furious. He issued the **Proclamation to the People of South Carolina (December 1832)** , denying the right of nullification. “The Constitution of the United States… forms a government, not a league… To say that any state may at pleasure secede from the Union is to say that the United States is not a nation.” Jackson asked Congress for the **Force Bill (1833)** , authorizing him to use the army and navy to enforce federal law in South Carolina
- **Henry Clay’s compromise** : Clay proposed a compromise tariff (1833) that would gradually reduce rates over 10 years (to the level of the Tariff of 1816 by 1842). The Tariff of 1833 was passed by Congress, and the Force Bill was also passed (though not used). South Carolina repealed its nullification ordinance (but also “nullified” the Force Bill in a symbolic gesture)
- **Effects** :
- The Nullification Crisis established that the federal government would use force to enforce its laws (precedent for the Civil War)
- It exposed the deep sectional divide over tariffs (North vs. South)
- Calhoun resigned as vice president and became a senator from South Carolina. He continued to argue for states’ rights and nullification

**Debate over Internal Improvements (KC-4.1.I.C):**
- **Jackson’s Maysville Road veto (1830)** : Jackson vetoed a bill to fund a road in Kentucky (Henry Clay’s state), arguing that it was a local, not national, project. He believed the federal government should fund only projects of “national character” (roads connecting multiple states, canals, lighthouses, harbors)
- **Effects** : Jackson’s veto limited federal funding for internal improvements. States and private companies continued to build roads, canals, and railroads. But the lack of federal funding may have slowed the pace of internal improvements

**Debate over Indian Removal (KC-4.3.I.B):**
- **Indian Removal Act (1830)** : see LO 4.D
- **Cherokee resistance** : The Cherokee Nation sued Georgia (which had passed laws abolishing Cherokee government). The Supreme Court ruled in *Worcester v. Georgia* (1832) that Georgia’s laws had no force in Cherokee territory. Jackson ignored the ruling.
- **Treaty of New Echota (1835)** : A small faction of Cherokee leaders (without the authority of the Cherokee National Council) signed a treaty ceding Cherokee lands in exchange for lands in Indian Territory (Oklahoma). The treaty was ratified by the Senate by one vote (1836). The Cherokee Nation protested that the treaty was fraudulent
- **Trail of Tears (1838-1839)** : see LO 4.D
- **Effects** : The federal government forcibly removed 100,000 Native Americans from their lands between 1830 and 1850. Millions of acres were opened to white settlement (cotton plantations in the South, farms in the Midwest). Native nations were devastated (thousands died on the trail)

**The Rise of the Whig Party (KC-4.1.I.C):**
- The **Whig Party** (1834-1856) was formed in opposition to Andrew Jackson’s “executive tyranny”
- **Whig leaders** : Henry Clay (Kentucky), Daniel Webster (Massachusetts), William Henry Harrison (Ohio), John Quincy Adams (Massachusetts), Thaddeus Stevens (Pennsylvania), Abraham Lincoln (Illinois, elected to Congress 1846)
- **Whig ideology** :
- **Strong legislative branch** (Congress, not the president, should lead)
- **Internal improvements** (federal funding for roads, canals, harbors)
- **Protective tariff** (to protect American industry)
- **National bank** (to provide stable currency and credit)
- **Moral reform** (temperance, abolitionism, public education)
- **Loose construction** of the Constitution
- **Whig base** : Northern industrialists, merchants, professionals, middle-class reformers, evangelical Protestants (Congregationalists, Methodists, Baptists, Presbyterians), and some Southern planters (who opposed Jackson’s attacks on the Bank but supported slavery)
- **Whig electoral successes** : William Henry Harrison (1840), Zachary Taylor (1848), and later, Whig successors (Millard Fillmore, John Tyler was a Democrat who became a Whig after breaking with Jackson)
- **Whig failures** : Whigs were divided over slavery (Northern Whigs opposed slavery expansion; Southern Whigs supported it). This division led to the party’s collapse in the 1850s (replaced by the Republican Party)

## LO 4.I: Explain how and why a new national culture developed from 1800 to 1848.

**Contextualization:** After the War of 1812, Americans felt a surge of national pride. They had fought Britain twice (Revolution, 1812) and survived. They had expanded westward. They wanted to create a culture that was distinct from Europe—American literature, art, architecture, and philosophy.

**Thesis:** A new national culture developed from 1800 to 1848 because Americans sought to create a **distinct identity** separate from Europe (cultural nationalism), were influenced by **Romanticism** (emphasizing emotion, nature, and individual genius), and were inspired by **Transcendentalism** (a uniquely American philosophical movement that emphasized self-reliance, intuition, and the divinity of nature); this culture was expressed in **literature** (Washington Irving, James Fenimore Cooper, Edgar Allan Poe, Nathaniel Hawthorne, Herman Melville), **art** (Hudson River School), **architecture** (neoclassical, Greek Revival, Gothic Revival), and **popular culture** (minstrel shows, circuses, penny presses).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Cultural Nationalism (KC-4.1.II.B, KC-4.1.II.B, KC-4.1.II.B, KC-4.1.II.B, KC-4.1.II.C):**
- After the War of 1812, American writers and artists sought to create a national literature and art independent of British models
- **Noah Webster** : *American Dictionary of the English Language* (1828) – standardized American spelling (color not colour, theater not theatre, center not centre)
- **Washington Irving (1783-1859)** : *The Sketch Book of Geoffrey Crayon, Gent.* (1819-1820) included “Rip Van Winkle” and “The Legend of Sleepy Hollow.” Irving was the first American writer to achieve international fame. He set his stories in the American past (Dutch New York, the Hudson Valley)
- **James Fenimore Cooper (1789-1851)** : *The Leatherstocking Tales* (1823-1841) – *The Pioneers, The Last of the Mohicans, The Prairie, The Pathfinder, The Deerslayer*. He created the archetypal American frontier hero: Natty Bumppo (Hawkeye), a white man who lives among Natives, respects nature, and embodies American individualism
- **William Cullen Bryant (1794-1878)** : Poet; “Thanatopsis” (1817) and “To a Waterfowl” (1818). He was influenced by British Romantic poets (Wordsworth, Coleridge) but wrote about American landscapes
- **Henry Wadsworth Longfellow (1807-1882)** : Poet; “Paul Revere’s Ride” (1861), “The Song of Hiawatha” (1855), “Evangeline” (1847). He wrote about American history and folklore, though his style was traditional
- **Edgar Allan Poe (1809-1849)** : Poet and short story writer; “The Raven” (1845), “The Fall of the House of Usher” (1839), “The Tell-Tale Heart” (1843). He wrote Gothic horror and detective fiction (the first detective story, “The Murders in the Rue Morgue,” 1841). He explored dark psychological themes (madness, guilt, death)
- **Nathaniel Hawthorne (1804-1864)** : *The Scarlet Letter* (1850), *The House of the Seven Gables* (1851). He explored Puritan New England history, sin, guilt, and hypocrisy. His works were dark and psychological
- **Herman Melville (1819-1891)** : *Moby-Dick* (1851) – a complex, philosophical novel about obsession (Captain Ahab hunting the white whale). It was not popular during Melville’s lifetime but is now considered a masterpiece of American literature
- **Ralph Waldo Emerson (1803-1882)** and **Henry David Thoreau (1817-1862)** – see Transcendentalism below

**Transcendentalism (KC-4.1.II.C):**
- **Transcendentalism** was a philosophical, literary, and religious movement that emerged in New England (1830s-1840s)
- **Key ideas** :
- **Intuition over reason** : Truth can be known through intuition, not just logic or religious dogma
- **Divinity in nature** : God is present in nature; nature is a source of spiritual truth
- **Self-reliance** : Individuals should trust their own instincts and beliefs, not conform to society’s expectations
- **Individualism** : The individual conscience is the ultimate moral authority (this could justify opposition to slavery, war, or unjust laws)
- **Ralph Waldo Emerson** : “Nature” (1836) – the foundational text of Transcendentalism. “The American Scholar” (1837) – called on American writers to break free from European influence (the “Declaration of Intellectual Independence”). “Self-Reliance” (1841) – “Whoso would be a man must be a nonconformist.” “A foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds.”
- **Henry David Thoreau** : *Walden* (1854) – an account of his two years living in a cabin he built on Walden Pond (Concord, Massachusetts). He sought to live simply, close to nature, and discover the essential facts of life. *Civil Disobedience* (1849) – an essay arguing that individuals should resist unjust laws (he had been jailed for refusing to pay a poll tax to protest the Mexican-American War and slavery). “Under a government which imprisons any unjustly, the true place for a just man is also a prison.” Thoreau’s essay influenced Mohandas Gandhi (Indian independence) and Martin Luther King Jr. (civil rights)
- **Margaret Fuller (1810-1850)** : Edited *The Dial* (1840-1842), the Transcendentalist journal. *Woman in the Nineteenth Century* (1845) – a feminist work arguing for women’s intellectual, social, and political equality. She was the first American female foreign correspondent (for the *New York Tribune*). She died in a shipwreck off Fire Island, New York
- **Brook Farm (1841-1847)** : A Transcendentalist utopian community in West Roxbury, Massachusetts (see LO 4.A)
- **The “Transcendental Club”** : Emerson, Thoreau, Fuller, Bronson Alcott (father of Louisa May Alcott), George Ripley, Orestes Brownson, and others met regularly in Boston and Concord

**The Hudson River School of Art (KC-4.1.II.B):**
- The **Hudson River School** (c. 1825-1875) was the first American art movement. Painters depicted the American landscape (especially the Hudson River Valley, the Catskill Mountains, the Adirondacks, and later the Rocky Mountains) as majestic, sublime, and divinely inspired
- **Thomas Cole (1801-1848)** : Founder of the Hudson River School. “The Course of Empire” (1833-1836) – a five-painting series depicting the rise and fall of a classical civilization (meant as a warning to America). “The Oxbow” (1836) – a view of the Connecticut River Valley, contrasting wilderness (stormy, sublime) with settled farmland (peaceful, pastoral)
- **Asher B. Durand (1796-1886)** : “Kindred Spirits” (1849) – depicts Thomas Cole and William Cullen Bryant in the Catskill Mountains. “Progress” (1853) – shows the development of American civilization from wilderness to city
- **Frederic Edwin Church (1826-1900)** : Cole’s student. “The Heart of the Andes” (1859) – a massive (5x10 foot) painting of the South American landscape. “Niagara” (1857) – a dramatic view of the falls. Church traveled to South America and the Arctic to paint exotic landscapes
- **Albert Bierstadt (1830-1902)** : German-born; painted the Rocky Mountains, Yosemite, and the Sierra Nevada. “The Rocky Mountains, Lander’s Peak” (1863) – a dramatic, idealized view of the West
- **Themes** : The American landscape is a source of national pride; nature is a manifestation of God (the sublime); America’s destiny is to expand across the continent (Manifest Destiny)

**Architecture (KC-4.1.II.B):**
- **Neoclassical architecture** dominated public buildings (see LO 3.M). The US Capitol (completed 1826, dome added later), the White House (rebuilt after 1814 fire), the Treasury Building, and the Patent Office were built in neoclassical style
- **Greek Revival (1820s-1850s)** : Even more popular than neoclassical. Americans identified with ancient Greece (the birthplace of democracy). Greek Revival buildings had columns (often Doric or Ionic), pediments, and white-painted wood or stone. Examples: Second Bank of the United States (Philadelphia), Biltmore (Asheville, NC, after this period), and thousands of plantation mansions, courthouses, and churches
- **Gothic Revival (1840s-1870s)** : Based on medieval Gothic architecture (pointed arches, steep roofs, ornate decoration). Used for churches (Trinity Church, New York City 1846) and later for college buildings (Yale, Harvard) and homes (Lyndhurst, NY)
- **Thomas Jefferson’s University of Virginia (1819-1826)** : Jefferson designed the campus (the “Academical Village”) with a Rotunda (based on the Pantheon in Rome) at the head of a lawn lined with pavilions and student rooms. Jefferson’s design was neoclassical, but it also incorporated his ideas about education (the Rotunda housed the library, the center of learning; there was no chapel, emphasizing reason over religion)

**Popular Culture (KC-4.1.II.B):**
- **Minstrel shows** : The most popular form of entertainment in the 1840s-1850s. White performers in blackface (painted their faces with burnt cork) performed caricatures of African Americans (dancing, singing, telling jokes). Minstrel shows reinforced racial stereotypes (the “happy slave,” the “dandy,” the “mammy”) but also introduced American music (the banjo, the tambourine) and dance (the cakewalk) to white audiences
- **Circuses** : Traveling circuses (P. T. Barnum’s American Museum in New York City, 1841) featured animal acts (elephants, lions), acrobats, clowns, and “freak shows” (giants, dwarfs, bearded ladies, Siamese twins)
- **Theater** : Shakespeare was popular, but American plays also emerged (Dion Boucicault, *The Octoroon* 1859 about slavery; Anna Cora Mowatt, *Fashion* 1845 a satire of New York society)
- **Penny presses** : Mass-circulation newspapers (the *New York Sun*, *New York Herald*, *New York Tribune*) cost 1 cent (vs. 6 cents for traditional papers). They featured sensational stories (crime, scandal, disasters), sports, and political news. They reached a mass audience (immigrants, workers, clerks, middle-class families)
- **Baseball** : Early versions of baseball (the “New York game,” codified by Alexander Cartwright 1845) became popular in the 1840s. The first recorded baseball game (1846) was in Hoboken, New Jersey. The National Association of Base Ball Players was formed in 1857 (professional leagues after the Civil War)

## LO 4.J: Explain the causes of the Second Great Awakening.

**Contextualization:** In the late 18th century, American religion was characterized by rationalism (Deism, Unitarianism) and declining church membership. Many Americans were not attending church. The Second Great Awakening (c. 1790-1840s) reversed this trend, sparking a Protestant revival that swept across the nation.

**Thesis:** The Second Great Awakening was caused by **the rise of democratic and individualistic beliefs** (ordinary people wanted a religion that spoke to their emotions, not just their intellect), **a response to the rationalism of the Enlightenment** (Deism, Unitarianism, and universalism were too cold and abstract for many Americans), **changes to society caused by the Market Revolution** (urbanization, immigration, social mobility created anxiety and a search for meaning), and **greater social and geographical mobility** (Americans moving west lacked churches; camp meetings filled the void).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Cause 1 – The Rise of Democratic and Individualistic Beliefs (KC-4.1.III.A):**
- The American Revolution and the expansion of democracy (universal white male suffrage) encouraged ordinary people to think for themselves, including about religion
- **Arminianism** (the belief that individuals have free will to choose salvation) was more appealing than Calvinist predestination (the belief that God has already decided who will be saved, regardless of individual action). Charles Finney explicitly rejected predestination: “Sinners are bound to change their own hearts.” This message resonated with Jacksonian America (individualism, self-improvement, democratic choice)
- **The “common man”** rejected the elitism of educated, rationalist ministers (Unitarians, Congregationalists). They wanted ministers who spoke their language (plain, direct, passionate)

**Cause 2 – Response to Rationalism (KC-4.1.III.A):**
- **Deism** : Belief in a “clockmaker God” who created the universe but does not intervene in human affairs (popular among Enlightenment thinkers like Thomas Jefferson, Benjamin Franklin). Deists rejected miracles, the divinity of Christ, and the authority of the Bible. Most Americans found Deism too cold and abstract
- **Unitarianism** : Belief in the unity (not trinity) of God; Jesus was a great moral teacher but not divine. Unitarians emphasized reason, free will, and human goodness. They were influential among New England elites (Harvard, Boston). But their services were intellectual and restrained (no emotional preaching, no revivals)
- **Universalism** : Belief that all souls will eventually be saved (no eternal damnation). Universalists emphasized God’s love and mercy. But many Americans still believed in hell and feared damnation
- The Second Great Awakening was a reaction against these rationalist, intellectual religions. It offered emotional, experiential religion (conversion, rebirth, personal relationship with Jesus)

**Cause 3 – Changes to Society Caused by the Market Revolution (KC-4.1.III.A):**
- The Market Revolution disrupted traditional communities. People moved away from their families, to new towns and cities, where they felt isolated and anonymous
- **Urbanization** created social problems (poverty, crime, alcoholism, prostitution, gambling). Evangelicals blamed these problems on the decline of religion
- **Capitalism** created winners (factory owners, merchants, speculators) and losers (workers, artisans, farmers who lost their land). Evangelicals offered spiritual comfort (God’s plan) and moral reform (temperance, charity)
- **Anxiety about social change** : The rapid pace of change (canals, railroads, factories, immigration) made many Americans anxious. The Second Great Awakening offered certainty (God’s word, moral absolutes)

**Cause 4 – Greater Social and Geographical Mobility (KC-4.1.III.A):**
- Americans moved west (Kentucky, Tennessee, Ohio, Indiana, Illinois). They had no churches, no ministers, and no religious communities
- **Camp meetings** emerged as a solution. Thousands of settlers would gather (in a clearing, a field) for days of preaching, singing, and prayer. The first camp meetings were in Kentucky and Tennessee (Cane Ridge, 1801, had 10,000-25,000 attendees)
- **Itinerant preachers** (Methodist circuit riders, Baptist preachers) traveled on horseback from settlement to settlement, preaching wherever they could find an audience (churches, schoolhouses, barns, fields)
- **The “Burned-Over District”** (western New York) was a region of intense revivalism. The Erie Canal (1825) brought settlers and commerce; the region lacked established churches (Congregationalist, Presbyterian). Finney held revivals in Utica, Rochester, and Buffalo (1820s-1830s). The region produced new religious movements (Mormonism, Millerism, the Holiness Movement, the Oneida Community)

**Key Figures of the Second Great Awakening (KC-4.1.III.A):**
- **Charles Grandison Finney (1792-1875)** : see LO 4.A
- **Lyman Beecher (1775-1863)** : see LO 4.A
- **Peter Cartwright (1785-1872)** : Methodist circuit rider in Kentucky, Tennessee, Illinois. He was a champion wrestler and a fiery preacher. He claimed to have converted 12,000 souls. He opposed slavery, fought the “demon rum,” and debated a Catholic priest (whom he called the “agent of the Antichrist”)
- **Richard Allen (1760-1831)** : Founder of the AME Church (1816). He was born enslaved, bought his freedom (1783), and became a Methodist minister. White Methodists forced African American worshippers to sit in segregated galleries; Allen and others left to form their own denomination
- **Jarena Lee (1783-1864)** : African American woman preacher in the AME Church. She was the first woman to receive official authorization to preach (though she was not ordained). She traveled thousands of miles, preaching to mixed-race audiences
- **Francis Asbury (1745-1816)** : First bishop of the Methodist Episcopal Church in the US (appointed 1784). He traveled 270,000 miles on horseback, ordained 4,000 ministers, and preached 16,000 sermons. The Methodist Church grew from 15,000 members (1784) to 214,000 (1816) to 1.2 million (1844)

**Key Effects of the Second Great Awakening (KC-4.1.III.A, KC-4.1.III.A):**
- **Growth of evangelical denominations** : Methodists and Baptists grew rapidly. By 1850, the Methodist Church was the largest Protestant denomination in the US (1.3 million members), followed by the Baptists (1 million), Presbyterians (500,000), and Congregationalists (200,000)
- **New religious movements** : Mormonism, Millerism (Adventism), the Holiness Movement, the Oneida Community
- **Reform movements** : See LO 4.K
- **Sectional division** : The Second Great Awakening also divided churches over slavery. The Methodist Church split (1844) into Methodist Episcopal Church (North, antislavery) and Methodist Episcopal Church, South (pro-slavery). The Baptist Church split (1845) into Northern Baptists (American Baptist Missionary Union) and Southern Baptists (Southern Baptist Convention). The Presbyterian Church split (1837) into “Old School” (conservative, pro-slavery) and “New School” (revivalist, antislavery) – not a clean North-South split, but the New School Presbyterians split again in 1857 into North and South

## LO 4.K: Explain how and why various reform movements developed and expanded from 1800 to 1848.

**Contextualization:** The Second Great Awakening taught that individuals could perfect themselves and, by extension, perfect society. This “perfectionist” impulse, combined with the social dislocations of the Market Revolution, led to an explosion of reform movements: temperance (abolish alcohol), asylum and prison reform (treat the mentally ill humanely), abolitionism (end slavery), women’s rights (legal and political equality), education reform (public schools), and utopian communities (perfect societies).

**Thesis:** Reform movements developed and expanded from 1800 to 1848 because **the Second Great Awakening inspired “perfectionism”** (the belief that Christians must work to perfect society), **the Market Revolution created social problems** (alcoholism, poverty, crime, child labor, prostitution), and **the contradiction between American ideals (liberty, equality) and American realities (slavery, women’s subordination) became intolerable** to some reformers; these movements achieved **limited successes** (temperance laws in some states, asylum construction, abolition of slavery in the North, the Seneca Falls Convention for women’s rights) but also **faced fierce opposition** (mobs attacked abolitionists, Southern enslavers defended slavery as a “positive good”).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Temperance (KC-4.1.III.B):**
- **Causes** : Alcohol consumption skyrocketed in the early 19th century (the average American drank 7 gallons of pure alcohol per year — three times the modern rate). Whiskey was cheap; hard cider was a staple. Alcohol was blamed for poverty, crime, family violence, unemployment, and moral decay
- **American Temperance Society (1826)** : 1.5 million members by 1835. They signed pledges to abstain from alcohol (temperance = moderation initially, but soon teetotalism = complete abstinence)
- **Maine Law (1851)** : Maine passed the first state prohibition law (banned the manufacture and sale of alcohol). 12 other states passed similar laws in the 1850s (most were repealed or struck down by courts)
- **Effects** : The temperance movement reduced alcohol consumption (by 1860, it was down to 3 gallons per person). It also created a model for other reform movements (organization, petitions, lobbying, moral suasion)

**Asylum and Prison Reform (KC-4.1.III.B):**
- **Dorothea Dix (1802-1887)** : A schoolteacher who visited jails and almshouses in Massachusetts. She discovered that mentally ill people were housed with criminals, chained to walls, beaten, and left naked in unheated cells. She documented conditions and presented her findings to the Massachusetts legislature (1843). Between 1843 and 1852, she convinced 11 states to build state-funded mental hospitals (insane asylums)
- **Prison reform** : Reformers argued that prisons should rehabilitate, not just punish. Auburn Prison (New York, 1817) introduced the “Auburn system” (solitary confinement at night, silent work in groups during the day). Eastern State Penitentiary (Philadelphia, 1829) introduced the “Pennsylvania system” (total solitary confinement, meant to induce remorse). Both systems were brutal and ineffective, but they represented a new idea: the state had a responsibility to reform criminals, not just lock them up

**Abolitionism (KC-4.1.III.B.i, KC-4.3.II.B.i):**
- **Early antislavery** : The American Colonization Society (1816) proposed sending free African Americans to Liberia (West Africa). It was supported by some enslavers (who feared free Blacks would encourage slave revolts) and some antislavery whites (who despaired of racial equality). The ACS had little effect (only 12,000 Black Americans emigrated to Liberia)
- **William Lloyd Garrison (1805-1879)** : The most radical white abolitionist. He published *The Liberator* (1831-1865) – “I will be as harsh as truth and as uncompromising as justice… I will not equivocate – I will not excuse – I will not retreat a single inch – AND I WILL BE HEARD.” He demanded **immediate, unconditional, uncompensated emancipation** (no gradual emancipation, no compensation for enslavers). He denounced the Constitution as “a covenant with death and an agreement with hell” (because it protected slavery). He burned a copy of the Constitution at an abolitionist rally (1854)
- **The American Anti-Slavery Society (1833)** : Founded by Garrison and others. By 1838, it had 250,000 members and 1,350 local chapters. It flooded Congress with petitions (leading to the “gag rule,” see LO 4.C)
- **Frederick Douglass (1818-1895)** : The most famous Black abolitionist. He was born enslaved in Maryland, escaped to the North (1838), and became a lecturer for the American Anti-Slavery Society. He published his autobiography, *Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, an American Slave* (1845), which sold 30,000 copies in five years. He founded his own newspaper, *The North Star* (1847-1851), with the motto “Right is of no Sex – Truth is of no Color – God is the Father of us all, and we are all brethren.” He broke with Garrison when Garrison denounced the Constitution; Douglass argued that the Constitution was an antislavery document (the preamble’s “We the People” included Black Americans; the due process and equal protection clauses could be used to fight slavery)
- **Sojourner Truth (c. 1797-1883)** : Born enslaved in New York (Isabella Baumfree). She escaped to freedom (1826) and became a preacher and abolitionist. She delivered her famous “Ain’t I a Woman?” speech at the Ohio Women’s Rights Convention (1851) – “And ain’t I a woman? I have ploughed and planted and gathered into barns, and no man could head me – and ain’t I a woman?”
- **The Grimké Sisters (Sarah and Angelina)** : Daughters of a wealthy South Carolina enslaver. They moved to the North, became Quakers, and spoke against slavery. They were the first women to address a state legislature (Massachusetts, 1838). Angelina wrote *An Appeal to the Christian Women of the South* (1836), urging them to work for emancipation. Their public speaking was controversial (it was “unladylike” for women to speak in public to mixed audiences)
- **Underground Railroad** : A network of secret routes and safe houses used by enslaved people to escape to free states and Canada. **Harriet Tubman (c. 1822-1913)** escaped from slavery (1849) and returned to the South 13 times, leading 70+ enslaved people to freedom. She was called “Moses.” (The term “Underground Railroad” was coined in the 1830s; it was neither underground nor a railroad, but a loose network of sympathetic abolitionists)
- **Opposition to abolitionism** :
- Southerners passed laws prohibiting the distribution of abolitionist literature (South Carolina 1835). President Andrew Jackson authorized postmasters to refuse to deliver abolitionist pamphlets
- Mobs attacked abolitionist meetings (Boston, 1835; New York, 1834; Cincinnati, 1836). They destroyed printing presses (Elijah Lovejoy was killed by a mob in Alton, Illinois, 1837)
- The “gag rule” (1836-1844) prevented Congress from even discussing abolitionist petitions (see LO 4.C)
- **Limits** : Most Northerners opposed slavery’s expansion (free soil) but did not support immediate abolition. They feared that freed slaves would move north and compete for jobs; they also held racist beliefs (Black people were inferior). Abolitionists remained a small, unpopular minority

**Women’s Rights (KC-4.1.III.C):**
- **Causes** : Women who worked in the abolitionist movement (the Grimké sisters, Lucretia Mott, Elizabeth Cady Stanton) were told that they should not speak in public (it was “unladylike”), that they should defer to men, that their role was domestic (not political)
- **The “cult of domesticity”** (separate spheres) gave women moral authority (religion, charity, child-rearing) but denied them political and legal rights
- **Lucretia Mott (1793-1880)** and **Elizabeth Cady Stanton (1815-1902)** met at the World Anti-Slavery Convention in London (1840). Women delegates were denied seating and forced to sit in a gallery behind a curtain. They resolved to hold a women’s rights convention when they returned to America
- **Seneca Falls Convention (July 19-20, 1848)** : The first women’s rights convention in the United States (Seneca Falls, New York). 300 attendees (including 40 men, such as Frederick Douglass)
- **Declaration of Sentiments** : Modeled on the Declaration of Independence. “We hold these truths to be self-evident: that all men and women are created equal.” It listed 18 grievances (“He has never permitted her to exercise her inalienable right to the elective franchise”; “He has made her, if married, in the eye of the law, civilly dead”; “He has taken from her all right in property, even to the wages she earns”; “He has monopolized nearly all the profitable employments”)
- **The demand for suffrage** : The Declaration of Sentiments called for “the elective franchise” (the right to vote). This was the most controversial demand; some delegates thought it was too radical. Frederick Douglass argued strongly for it, and it passed (by a narrow margin)
- **After Seneca Falls** : The women’s rights movement held annual conventions (Worcester, MA; Syracuse, NY; etc.). They petitioned state legislatures for married women’s property rights (New York passed a Married Women’s Property Act in 1848, just days after Seneca Falls; other states followed). The movement for woman suffrage would continue into the 20th century (19th Amendment, 1920)

**Education Reform (KC-4.1.III.A):**
- **Horace Mann (1796-1859)** : Secretary of the Massachusetts Board of Education (1837-1848). He created the first statewide public school system (common schools). He argued that public education was essential for democracy (to educate citizens) and for social mobility (to give poor children a chance). Massachusetts passed a compulsory attendance law (1852)
- **McGuffey Readers (1836)** : William Holmes McGuffey’s textbooks (the *Eclectic Readers*) taught reading, grammar, and morality (honesty, hard work, thrift, patriotism). They were used in schools across the nation (120 million copies sold by 1920)
- **Oberlin College (1833)** : First college in the US to admit women (1837) and African Americans (1835). Oberlin was founded by Presbyterian ministers and was a center of abolitionism
- **Mount Holyoke Female Seminary (1837)** : Mary Lyon’s college for women (in Massachusetts). It was the first “seminary” (college) to offer women the same academic curriculum as men (as opposed to finishing schools, which taught manners and accomplishments)
- **Troy Female Seminary (1821)** : Emma Willard’s school (in New York). It offered a rigorous academic curriculum (math, science, history, philosophy) to women

**Utopian Communities (KC-4.1.III.A):**
- See LO 4.A (Brook Farm, New Harmony, Oneida, Shakers)
- **Socialist and communist ideas** : Some reformers (Robert Owen, Charles Fourier, Étienne Cabet) believed that private property was the cause of social problems. They founded utopian communities based on communal ownership (shared property). These communities failed because:
- They were too small (unable to compete with capitalist enterprises)
- They had difficulty attracting members (most Americans wanted private property)
- They were plagued by internal conflicts (sexual jealousy, property disputes, religious disagreements)

## LO 4.L: Explain the continuities and changes in the experience of African Americans from 1800 to 1848.

**Contextualization:** In 1800, most African Americans were enslaved (893,000 out of 1 million). By 1848, the number of enslaved people had grown to 3.2 million (out of 3.6 million African Americans). The cotton gin and westward expansion had made slavery more profitable and more entrenched. But there were also changes: Northern states had abolished slavery (gradually), a free Black community had emerged, and a militant abolitionist movement had developed.

**Thesis:** From 1800 to 1848, the experience of African Americans was marked by **the continued growth and entrenchment of slavery in the South** (cotton boom, domestic slave trade, harsher slave codes), **the rise of a free Black community in the North** (churches, schools, mutual aid societies, newspapers), **increased resistance by enslaved people** (Gabriel’s Rebellion 1800, Denmark Vesey 1822, Nat Turner 1831 – though Turner’s rebellion is in 1831, after the period start), and **the development of abolitionism** (Garrison, Douglass, the American Anti-Slavery Society), but also **persistent discrimination and violence** (gag rule, mob violence, restrictions on Black voting, segregation).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Continuities – Slavery in the South (KC-4.1.II.D, KC-4.1.III.B.ii, KC-4.3.II.A):**
- The **cotton boom** (after the cotton gin, 1793) transformed the South. Cotton was king. Slavery expanded westward into Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Arkansas, Missouri, Texas
- The **domestic slave trade** (the “Second Middle Passage”) transported 1 million enslaved people from the Upper South (Virginia, Maryland, Delaware, Kentucky) to the Deep South (Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Texas) between 1800 and 1860. Enslaved people were marched in coffles (chains) or shipped by sea. Families were separated (husband from wife, parent from child)
- **Slave codes** were strengthened:
- **Prohibited teaching enslaved people to read and write** (Virginia 1819, South Carolina 1834, Georgia 1829, Alabama 1832, Louisiana 1830). Learning to read the Bible was no defense
- **Prohibited enslaved people from assembling** (worship services, funerals, weddings) without a white person present
- **Prohibited enslaved people from owning firearms, selling goods, hiring themselves out, or leaving the plantation without a pass**
- **Increased penalties for resistance** : enslavers could kill rebellious slaves with impunity; states paid compensation for slaves executed for crimes (so enslavers had no financial incentive to prevent executions)
- **Enslaved resistance** : see below

**Changes – The Free Black Community in the North (KC-4.1.II.D):**
- By 1840, there were 386,000 free African Americans in the US (mostly in the North, but also in the Upper South – Baltimore, Richmond, Washington, D.C.)
- **Institutions** :
- **Churches** : AME (Richard Allen, 1816), AME Zion (New York, 1821). Black churches were the center of community life (worship, education, political organizing)
- **Schools** : African Free School (New York, 1787), Boston African American School (1820), Philadelphia Institute for Colored Youth (1837)
- **Mutual aid societies** : Free African Americans pooled their resources to provide sickness insurance, burial benefits, and aid to widows and orphans (the New York African Society for Mutual Relief, 1808)
- **Newspapers** : *Freedom’s Journal* (1827-1829) – first Black-owned and operated newspaper in the United States. Editors: John Russwurm and Samuel Cornish. *The North Star* (1847-1851) – Frederick Douglass’s newspaper
- **Discrimination** :
- **Voting** : Most Northern states restricted suffrage to white men (New York 1821, Pennsylvania 1838, New Jersey 1807). Only Massachusetts, Vermont, New Hampshire, Maine, and Rhode Island (after 1842) allowed Black men to vote on equal terms with whites
- **Segregation** : Schools, churches, theaters, and public accommodations were segregated. Black people could not ride in certain streetcars, stay in certain hotels, eat in certain restaurants
- **Employment** : Most free Black men worked as laborers, sailors, waiters, barbers, and cooks (not skilled trades, which were controlled by whites). Black women worked as domestic servants or laundresses
- **Violence** : Mobs attacked Black neighborhoods in Northern cities (Cincinnati 1829, Philadelphia 1834, 1838, 1842, New York 1834). The New York riots (1834) were sparked by abolitionist meetings; mobs destroyed Black churches, homes, and businesses

**Changes – Resistance by Enslaved People (KC-4.1.III.B.ii):**
- **Gabriel’s Rebellion (1800)** : see LO 3.O
- **Denmark Vesey’s conspiracy (1822)** : Vesey was a free Black carpenter in Charleston, South Carolina. He planned a massive slave rebellion (9,000 enslaved people were supposedly involved). The plot was betrayed; Vesey and 35 others were hanged. South Carolina passed stricter slave codes (prohibited hiring out slaves, prohibited slaves from assembling, required free Black sailors to be imprisoned while their ships were in port)
- **Nat Turner’s Rebellion (1831)** : Nat Turner, an enslaved preacher in Southampton County, Virginia, believed he was chosen by God to lead a rebellion. On August 21, 1831, Turner and 70 followers killed 60 white men, women, and children (the Turner family, the Whitehead family, the Travis family, etc.). The rebellion was crushed by the state militia; Turner was captured and hanged. 200 Black people (including many who were not involved) were killed by mobs in retaliation
- **Effects** : Virginia considered abolishing slavery (the state legislature debated gradual emancipation in 1831-1832). The proposal failed. Instead, Virginia passed even stricter slave codes (prohibited teaching slaves to read and write, prohibited free Blacks from owning firearms, required free Blacks to leave the state within 12 months of emancipation). Other Southern states followed

**Changes – The Abolitionist Movement (KC-4.1.III.B.i):**
- See LO 4.K
- **Free Black abolitionists** : David Walker (1796-1830) published *An Appeal to the Coloured Citizens of the World* (1829), calling for enslaved people to rise up against their enslavers. He denounced Thomas Jefferson’s racism (*Notes on the State of Virginia*). The *Appeal* was widely distributed in the South (banned, and Georgia offered a reward for Walker’s capture or death)
- **Southern abolitionism** : The Grimké sisters (see LO 4.K). Southern abolitionists were rare; they faced social ostracism, violence, and imprisonment

**Continuities – The Internal Slave Trade (KC-4.1.II.D):**
- See above
- **Solomon Northup (1808-1863)** : A free Black man from New York; he was kidnapped in Washington, D.C. (1841) and sold into slavery in Louisiana. He was enslaved for 12 years (hence the title of his memoir, *Twelve Years a Slave*, 1853). His book is a first-hand account of the domestic slave trade and the brutality of plantation slavery

## LO 4.M: Explain how geographic and environmental factors shaped the development of the South from 1800 to 1848.

**Contextualization:** The South was not a monolithic region. The Upper South (Virginia, Maryland, Delaware, Kentucky, Tennessee, North Carolina) and the Deep South (South Carolina, Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Texas, Arkansas) had different geographic and environmental conditions that shaped their economies, labor systems, and social structures.

**Thesis:** Geographic and environmental factors shaped the development of the South from 1800 to 1848 because **the fertility of the soil and the length of the growing season** determined which crops could be grown (tobacco in the Upper South, cotton in the Deep South, rice and sugar in the coastal lowlands), **the depletion of soil by tobacco and cotton** pushed planters westward (to new lands), and **the disease environment** (malaria, yellow fever) made the lowcountry deadly to whites (leading to absentee plantations, the task system, and Black majorities).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**The Upper South (Virginia, Maryland, Delaware, Kentucky, Tennessee, North Carolina) (KC-4.2.III.C, KC-4.3.II.B.ii, KC-4.3.II.A):**
- **Tobacco** was the traditional cash crop. But tobacco depleted soil nitrogen quickly (a field could be cultivated for only 3-4 years before yields declined). Planters needed new land
- **As tobacco prices declined in the early 19th century** , Upper South planters shifted to wheat (sold to the North and Europe) and to **selling enslaved people** to the Deep South (the domestic slave trade)
- **Overcultivation** : By 1840, much of the soil in Virginia, Maryland, and North Carolina was exhausted. Planters moved west (to Kentucky, Tennessee) or sold their land to smaller farmers
- **Most Southerners owned no enslaved people** (only 1/3 of Southern families owned slaves). In the Upper South, small farmers (yeomen) grew corn, wheat, and tobacco on small plots, using family labor
- **Attitudes toward slavery** : Many Upper South planters expressed ambivalence about slavery (Jefferson, Washington, Madison). But they did not free their slaves (except a few). The Upper South was also the site of the Turner rebellion (Virginia) and the Vesey conspiracy (Charleston is in the Deep South, but Vesey was a free Black man from Charleston)

**The Deep South (South Carolina, Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Texas, Arkansas) (KC-4.2.III.C, KC-4.3.II.A):**
- **Cotton** was king. The invention of the cotton gin (1793) made short-staple cotton (which could grow on the uplands, not just the lowcountry) profitable. Cotton production expanded westward into Georgia (1800s), Alabama and Mississippi (1810s-1820s), Louisiana and Arkansas (1830s-1840s), Texas (1840s-1850s)
- **Cotton was brutal on the soil** : Continuous cotton cultivation depleted nitrogen and led to erosion. Planters moved west every 15-20 years, abandoning exhausted lands
- **The “cotton belt”** : By 1840, the cotton belt stretched from South Carolina to Texas. The region produced 2 million bales of cotton per year (by 1840), 4 million bales by 1860
- **The domestic slave trade** supplied enslaved labor to the Deep South (see LO 4.L)
- **Attitudes toward slavery** : Deep South planters were more defensive of slavery. They shifted from calling it a “necessary evil” to a “positive good” (John C. Calhoun). They argued that slavery was beneficial for both races (enslaved people were “civilized” by Christianity and paternalistic care; white society avoided class conflict)

**Rice and Sugar Plantations (KC-4.2.III.C):**
- **Rice** was grown in the lowcountry of South Carolina and Georgia (coastal plains, swamps, tidal rivers). Rice required standing water (paddies) and a large, disciplined labor force. Enslaved people from the “Rice Coast” of West Africa (Senegambia, Sierra Leone, Windward Coast) had the knowledge to cultivate rice. The **task system** (see LO 2.C) gave enslaved people some autonomy (they could work their own gardens, trade, and rest after their daily tasks)
- **Sugar** was grown in Louisiana (after the Louisiana Purchase, 1803). Sugar cultivation was brutal (12-16 hour days, 6 days a week, during grinding season). The **gang system** (see LO 2.F) was used. The sugar harvest required a large workforce for a short period (September-January). Louisiana imported thousands of enslaved people from the Upper South and directly from Africa (illegally, after 1808)

**The “Southern Way of Life” – Ideology (KC-4.3.II.B.ii):**
- Southern leaders argued that slavery was part of the “Southern way of life.” They claimed that slavery was a “positive good”:
- **Economic** : Slavery provided labor for cotton, rice, and sugar – the foundation of the Southern economy and of American prosperity (cotton exports paid for imports)
- **Racial** : Black people were inferior and could not care for themselves; slavery “civilized” them
- **Biblical** : The Bible sanctioned slavery (the curse of Ham, the patriarchs owned slaves, Paul returned a runaway slave to his master, Philemon)
- **Paternalistic** : Enslavers cared for “their people” (sick, elderly, children) – a myth (most enslaved people were treated harshly, and the domestic slave trade broke up families)
- **John C. Calhoun (1782-1850)** : The leading spokesman for the “positive good” argument. He argued that slavery was “the most safe and stable basis for free institutions.” He claimed that the North’s wage labor system was worse than slavery (Northern workers were “wage slaves” who could be fired and starved; enslaved people were guaranteed food, shelter, and medical care – a false contrast)

**Disease Environment (KC-4.3.II.B.ii):**
- **Malaria** and **yellow fever** were endemic in the lowcountry (SC, GA, FL, LA). These diseases were spread by mosquitoes (though the link was not known until the 1880s). Whites had little resistance; they died in large numbers. Enslaved Africans and their descendants had some genetic resistance (sickle cell trait protects against malaria) or acquired immunity (if they survived yellow fever)
- **Effects** :
- **Black majorities** : In lowcountry parishes (SC, GA), enslaved people were 80-90% of the population
- **Absentee planters** : Wealthy planters often left during the “sickly season” (summer) for their “pine barrens” retreats (higher, cooler, less mosquito-infested) or traveled north to Newport, Philadelphia, or Europe
- **The task system** may have emerged partly because whites could not supervise enslaved people closely during the sickly season (they had to trust enslaved people to complete their tasks unsupervised)
- **Sugar, not rice** , was grown in Louisiana (less need for standing water – though sugar fields are still mosquito-prone)

**Westward Expansion (KC-4.3.II.A):**
- As soil was exhausted in the East, planters moved west. This expansion accelerated the domestic slave trade and intensified the demand for new territories (Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Texas)
- **The “Old Southwest”** (Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana) was settled in the 1810s-1830s. Cotton plantations spread across the Black Belt (a crescent of dark, rich soil stretching from central Alabama into Mississippi)
- **The annexation of Texas (1845)** and the Mexican-American War (1846-1848) would open new lands for cotton (Texas) and potentially for slavery (California, New Mexico – see Period 5)
- The expansion of slavery into new territories became the central political issue of the 1840s-1850s

## LO 4.N: Explain the extent to which politics, economics, and foreign policy promoted the development of American identity from 1800 to 1848.

**Contextualization:** By 1848, the United States had changed dramatically. It had doubled in size (Louisiana Purchase). It had fought and won a war with Mexico. Its citizens had moved west (Kentucky, Tennessee, Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, Missouri, Arkansas, Michigan, Wisconsin, Iowa, Texas, Oregon). A new generation of Americans had never known British rule. They thought of themselves not as “Americans” (provincials) but as “Americans” (citizens of a powerful, expanding republic).

**Thesis:** Politics, economics, and foreign policy promoted the development of American identity from 1800 to 1848 to a **significant but incomplete extent** : **politically** , the expansion of democracy (universal white male suffrage, Jacksonian democracy) created a sense that ordinary people ruled (not aristocrats or kings); **economically** , the Market Revolution created regional interdependence (national markets, canals, railroads) but also regional conflict (tariffs, banking, internal improvements); **geographically** , westward expansion (Louisiana Purchase, Texas, Oregon) and foreign policy (Monroe Doctrine, Mexican-American War) fostered a sense of national destiny (Manifest Destiny) and power (the US was becoming a continental empire); however, this American identity was **deeply exclusionary** (it was defined by white men, excluded women, African Americans, and Native Americans, and was built on the dispossession of Native lands and the expansion of slavery).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Political Development of American Identity (KC-4.1.I):**
- **Universal white male suffrage** : By 1840, over 90% of white men could vote (up from 50-70% in 1800). The expansion of democracy fostered the belief that America was uniquely democratic (the “nation of the common man”)
- **Jackson’s presidency** : Andrew Jackson was the first “common man” president (born in a log cabin, frontier hero, champion of the common people). His inauguration (1829) was mobbed by ordinary citizens who trashed the White House. This was a symbol of democratic change
- **The “American System”** : Henry Clay’s plan to unify the nation (protective tariff, internal improvements, national bank) was a political vision of national development
- **Limits** : Women could not vote; most African Americans could not vote; Native Americans were not citizens. The “Second Party System” (Democrats vs. Whigs) was bitterly partisan, but both parties agreed that the republic was white, male, and propertied

**Economic Development of American Identity (KC-4.2.III.D):**
- **Regional interdependence** : The North manufactured goods; the West grew food; the South produced cotton. The nation was economically integrated, creating a sense of national identity (we need each other)
- **The “Market Revolution”** created a national market. Farmers in Ohio sold wheat to millers in Massachusetts; cotton from Alabama was spun into cloth in Manchester, England, and Lowell, Massachusetts; New York bankers financed it all
- **Transportation** : The Erie Canal, the National Road, railroads, and steamboats connected the nation. Travel time and shipping costs fell dramatically. Americans could move west, send goods east, and visit relatives in other states
- **Limits** : Regional tensions over tariffs, slavery, and internal improvements divided the nation. The Nullification Crisis (1832-1833) threatened civil war. The Missouri Crisis (1819-1820) nearly broke the union

**Foreign Policy Development of American Identity (KC-4.3.I.A.ii, KC-4.3.I.A.ii, KC-4.3.I.A.ii, KC-4.3.I.A.ii, KC-4.3.I.A.ii):**
- **The Louisiana Purchase (1803)** doubled the size of the United States. The nation could now expand westward indefinitely (or so it seemed). This fostered a sense of Manifest Destiny – the belief that the United States was destined to expand across the continent (the “empire of liberty”)
- **The War of 1812** was called the “Second War of Independence.” America proved it could stand up to Britain (though the war was a draw). National pride soared. The Federalist Party (which had opposed the war) collapsed
- **The Monroe Doctrine (1823)** declared that the Western Hemisphere was closed to European colonization. The US asserted its leadership role in the Americas. Even though the US could not enforce the doctrine alone, Americans believed it was their right to do so
- **Texas annexation (1845)** and the **Mexican-American War (1846-1848)** added 1.2 million square miles to the United States (California, New Mexico, Arizona, Nevada, Utah, Colorado, Wyoming). The nation stretched from the Atlantic to the Pacific. “From sea to shining sea” became a reality
- **Manifest Destiny** : The phrase was coined by John O’Sullivan (1845). He argued that it was “the fulfillment of our manifest destiny to overspread the continent allotted by Providence for the free development of our yearly multiplying millions.” Manifest Destiny promoted American identity as a nation chosen by God (exceptionalism), destined to spread democracy (and white supremacy) across the continent
- **Limits** : The Mexican-American War was controversial. Abolitionists argued it was a slaveholders’ plot to expand slavery. Abraham Lincoln (then a congressman) opposed the war. The war also highlighted racial prejudice against Mexicans (many Americans viewed Mexicans as “mongrel,” Catholic, and unfit for self-government)

**Cultural Development of American Identity (KC-4.1.II.B, KC-4.1.II.C):**
- See LO 4.I (Hudson River School, Transcendentalism, literature)
- **Noah Webster’s dictionary** defined American English (color not colour, etc.)
- **The “American System” of manufacturing** (interchangeable parts) was a source of national pride (Eli Whitney, the Springfield Armory)
- **The Smithsonian Institution (1846)** was founded with a bequest from British scientist James Smithson (who never visited the United States). The Smithsonian became a national museum (art, history, science, technology)
- **Limits** : American culture still imitated Europe (neoclassical architecture, English literary forms, European-trained artists). Many Americans preferred European culture (they toured Europe, collected European art, sent their children to European schools)

**Exclusionary Nature of American Identity (KC-4.1.II, KC-4.1.II, KC-4.1.II, KC-4.1.II):**
- **Distinctive cultures of various groups** : The “American identity” was not shared by all. Enslaved African Americans developed their own culture (Gullah Geechee, spirituals, ring shout, family structures, religious syncretism). Free African Americans built their own institutions (churches, schools, mutual aid societies). Native Americans (the “Five Civilized Tribes”) tried to adapt (written constitutions, newspapers, schools, Christianity, chattel slavery) but were still forced to remove west. Immigrants (Irish, German) preserved their languages, religions, and customs
- **“American identity” in 1848 was white, male, Protestant, and free** . It excluded women, African Americans, Native Americans, and immigrants (especially Irish Catholics). This exclusionary identity would be challenged by the Civil War (1861-1865) and the civil rights movement (1950s-1960s)
