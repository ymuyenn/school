## LO 6.A: Explain the historical context for the rise of industrial capitalism in the United States.

**Contextualization:** When the Civil War ended in 1865, the United States was still primarily an agricultural nation. By 1898, it was the world's largest industrial power, producing more steel, coal, and manufactured goods than Britain, Germany, and France combined. This transformation—the rise of industrial capitalism—was driven by a combination of factors: abundant natural resources (coal, iron, oil, timber, copper, silver, gold), a massive labor force (immigrants from Europe and migrants from American farms), technological innovation (the Bessemer process, electricity, the telephone, the internal combustion engine), government policies (protective tariffs, land grants to railroads, a national banking system, and a judiciary that favored corporations), and ruthless business leaders (Andrew Carnegie, John D. Rockefeller, J.P. Morgan, Cornelius Vanderbilt) who consolidated industries into giant trusts and monopolies.

**Thesis:** The rise of industrial capitalism from 1865 to 1898 was caused by the convergence of **abundant natural resources** (coal, iron, oil, timber), **technological innovation** (Bessemer steel process, electricity, telegraph, telephone, refrigeration), **a growing labor force** (25 million immigrants arrived between 1865 and 1915), **pro-business government policies** (protective tariffs, land grants, the national banking system, and Supreme Court rulings that protected corporations), and **entrepreneurial "captains of industry"** (Carnegie, Rockefeller, Morgan, Vanderbilt, Duke, Swift, Armour) who created vertically and horizontally integrated trusts; its effects included **rapid economic growth** (the US became the world's largest industrial power by 1890), **the rise of a new class of millionaires** (the "robber barons" or "captains of industry"), **the growth of cities** (urban population tripled between 1860 and 1890), **the exploitation of labor** (low wages, long hours, child labor, dangerous working conditions), and **the rise of labor unions** (the Knights of Labor, the American Federation of Labor, the Pullman Strike, the Haymarket Riot).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Context – Abundant Natural Resources (KC-6.1.I.B.i):**
- **Coal** : The US had the world's largest coal reserves (Appalachia, Pennsylvania, Illinois, Ohio, West Virginia, Kentucky). Coal fueled steam engines (railroads, steamships) and powered factories (steel mills, textile mills, iron foundries). Coal production increased from 10 million tons (1860) to 250 million tons (1900).
- **Iron ore** : The Mesabi Range (Minnesota) was the world's richest iron ore deposit. The Great Lakes shipping route (through the Soo Locks, completed 1855) allowed iron ore to be shipped to steel mills in Pennsylvania, Ohio, and Illinois.
- **Oil** : Edwin Drake drilled the first commercial oil well in Titusville, Pennsylvania (1859). The Pennsylvania oil fields produced 3 million barrels per year by 1865. John D. Rockefeller's Standard Oil Company refined 90% of US oil by 1880.
- **Timber** : The Great Lakes states (Michigan, Wisconsin, Minnesota) and the Pacific Northwest (Washington, Oregon) had vast white pine and redwood forests. Timber was essential for building homes, factories, railroads, and ships.
- **Copper** : The Keweenaw Peninsula (Michigan) and Butte, Montana produced copper for electrical wiring.
- **Silver and gold** : The Comstock Lode (Nevada, 1859) produced $400 million in silver. The Black Hills (South Dakota, 1874) produced gold (the "Great Sioux War" was fought over the Black Hills). The Cripple Creek district (Colorado, 1890) produced gold.

**Context – Technological Innovation (KC-6.1.I.B.i):**
- **The Bessemer process (1856, introduced to US 1864)** : A method for mass-producing cheap steel (by blowing air through molten iron to remove impurities). Steel was stronger and more durable than iron. It made possible skyscrapers (the Home Insurance Building, Chicago 1885 – first skyscraper), long bridges (the Brooklyn Bridge, 1883 – steel cables), and railroads (steel rails lasted 10 times longer than iron rails).
- **Open-hearth process (1860s)** : Another method for producing high-quality steel (used for armor plate, naval guns, and structural beams). The US steel industry adopted open-hearth furnaces in the 1880s-1890s.
- **Electricity** : Thomas Edison invented the incandescent light bulb (1879), the phonograph (1877), and the motion picture camera (1891). He also developed the first electric power distribution system (Pearl Street Station, New York City, 1882). George Westinghouse developed alternating current (AC) power, which could transmit electricity over long distances. The "War of the Currents" (Edison DC vs. Westinghouse AC) was won by AC (Westinghouse).
- **The telephone** : Alexander Graham Bell invented the telephone (1876). He founded the Bell Telephone Company (1877), which later became AT&T. By 1900, there were 1.3 million telephones in the US.
- **The telegraph** : Samuel Morse's telegraph (1844) was improved by the invention of the teletype (1850s) and the stock ticker (1867). Western Union (founded 1851) dominated the telegraph industry.
- **Refrigeration** : Refrigerated railroad cars (the "reefer") allowed meatpackers (Gustavus Swift, Philip Armour) to slaughter cattle in Chicago and ship fresh meat to New York, Boston, and even London. Refrigeration also allowed the transport of fruits, vegetables, and dairy products.
- **The internal combustion engine** : Gottlieb Daimler (Germany) invented the first high-speed internal combustion engine (1885). Karl Benz built the first automobile (1885). Henry Ford would mass-produce automobiles in the early 20th century.
- **The cash register** : James Ritty invented the cash register (1879) to prevent employee theft. John H. Patterson's National Cash Register Company (NCR) mass-produced them.
- **The typewriter** : Christopher Sholes invented the typewriter (1868), manufactured by Remington. The typewriter created jobs for women ("typewriters" – clerical workers).
- **The calculating machine** : William Burroughs invented the adding machine (1885). Burroughs Corporation became a major office equipment manufacturer.

**Context – A Growing Labor Force (KC-6.1.II.B.ii, KC-6.2.I.A):**
- **Immigration** : Between 1865 and 1900, 10 million immigrants arrived in the US (mostly from Germany, Ireland, Britain, Scandinavia, and Canada). Between 1900 and 1915, another 15 million arrived (mostly from Southern and Eastern Europe – Italy, Poland, Russia, Austria-Hungary, Greece, Turkey). Immigrants provided cheap labor for railroads, factories, mines, steel mills, and slaughterhouses.
- **Internal migration** : Millions of Americans moved from farms to cities (rural-to-urban migration). Between 1860 and 1900, the number of cities with 50,000+ people grew from 16 to 78. New York City's population grew from 1 million (1860) to 3.4 million (1900). Chicago grew from 109,000 (1860) to 1.7 million (1900).
- **Child labor** : Children as young as 8 worked in factories, textile mills, coal mines, glass factories, and canneries. They worked 12-14 hour days, 6 days a week, for pennies per hour. By 1900, 1.7 million children under 16 worked for wages.
- **Women workers** : Women worked as domestic servants, textile workers, garment workers (sweatshops), clerical workers (typewriters, telephone operators), and teachers. Women earned 50-60% of what men earned for comparable work.

**Context – Pro-Business Government Policies (KC-6.1.I.A, KC-6.1.I.A, KC-6.1.I.A, KC-6.1.I.A):**
- **Protective tariffs** : The Morrill Tariff (1861) and subsequent tariffs (1862, 1864, 1870, 1875, 1883, 1890) protected American manufacturers from foreign competition (especially British steel and textiles). Tariffs averaged 40-50% on manufactured goods. Northern industrialists loved them; Southern farmers (who had to buy manufactured goods) hated them.
- **Land grants to railroads** : The Pacific Railway Acts (1862, 1864) gave railroad companies alternating sections of land (10-20 miles on each side of the track) and federal loans (in bonds) to build the transcontinental railroad. The Union Pacific and Central Pacific received 175 million acres of public land (more than the area of Texas).
- **The national banking system** : The National Banking Acts (1863, 1864) created a system of nationally chartered banks (subject to federal regulation) and a national currency (greenbacks). The system stabilized the banking industry and provided credit for industrial expansion.
- **Subsidies and tariffs for specific industries** : The Sugar Tariff (1862, later the McKinley Tariff 1890) protected American sugar growers (Louisiana, Hawaii). The Wool Tariff (1867) protected sheep ranchers. The Tin Plate Tariff (1890) protected American tin plate manufacturers. The Dingley Tariff (1897) raised rates to 57% – the highest in American history.
- **The Supreme Court protected corporations** :
  - *Santa Clara County v. Southern Pacific Railroad* (1886) : The Supreme Court ruled that the 14th Amendment's "equal protection" clause applied to corporations (as "legal persons"). This allowed corporations to challenge state regulations (labor laws, safety laws, tax laws) as unconstitutional.
  - *Munn v. Illinois* (1877) : The Court ruled that states could regulate private property (grain elevators, railroads) if it was "clothed with a public interest." But *Wabash, St. Louis & Pacific Railway Co. v. Illinois* (1886) ruled that states could not regulate interstate railroads (the Commerce Clause gave Congress exclusive power). This led to the Interstate Commerce Act (1887).
- **Laissez-faire ideology** : The dominant economic theory was laissez-faire (French for "let do" – let the market operate without government interference). Business leaders argued that government regulation was unnecessary and harmful. They cited Adam Smith's *Wealth of Nations* (1776) and Herbert Spencer's Social Darwinism (see LO 6.9).

**Context – "Captains of Industry" and "Robber Barons" (KC-6.1.I.B.ii, KC-6.1.I.D):**
- **Andrew Carnegie (1835-1919)** : Scottish immigrant. He worked his way up from a bobbin boy in a textile mill (earning $1.20 per week) to the owner of Carnegie Steel Company (the largest steel company in the world). He used **vertical integration** : he owned iron mines, coal mines, coke ovens, ore ships, railroads, and steel mills. This allowed him to control every stage of production (from raw material to finished product) and cut costs. He sold Carnegie Steel to J.P. Morgan for $480 million (1901). He spent his last years giving away his fortune (philanthropy) – he built Carnegie Hall (New York), Carnegie libraries (2,500), Carnegie Mellon University, and the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace.
- **John D. Rockefeller (1839-1937)** : Founder of Standard Oil Company (1870). He used **horizontal integration** : he bought out or drove out his competitors (through secret rebates from railroads, price wars, and outright bribery) until he controlled 90% of US oil refining. He created the **trust** (a legal device where stockholders in different companies transferred their shares to a board of trustees, who managed all the companies as a single entity). Standard Oil was a trust. The Ohio Supreme Court ordered the trust dissolved (1892), so Rockefeller created a **holding company** (Standard Oil of New Jersey) to circumvent the ruling. Rockefeller was attacked as a "robber baron" (he used ruthless tactics to crush competition). He retired in 1897 and devoted himself to philanthropy (he gave away $500 million – to the University of Chicago, Rockefeller University, the Rockefeller Foundation, and the General Education Board).
- **J.P. Morgan (1837-1913)** : The most powerful banker in America. He financed railroads (he reorganized bankrupt railroads into monopolies), steel (he bought Carnegie Steel and merged it with other steel companies to form US Steel, the first billion-dollar corporation, 1901), and electricity (he financed Edison's electric company and merged it with Thomson-Houston to form General Electric, 1892). Morgan also bailed out the US Treasury during the Panic of 1907 (he lent the government $50 million). He was famous for his statement: "The United States is good for whatever it chooses to pay."
- **Cornelius Vanderbilt (1794-1877)** : Made his fortune in steamships and then railroads. He merged the New York Central Railroad with the Hudson River Railroad to create a railroad empire connecting New York City to Chicago. He was a ruthless competitor (he slashed rates to drive rivals into bankruptcy). He left $100 million to his son William ("The Commodore").
- **Gustavus Swift (1839-1903)** and **Philip Armour (1832-1901)** : Meatpackers. They created **vertical integration** : they owned cattle ranches, slaughterhouses, refrigerated railroad cars, and distribution networks. They undercut local butchers and created a national market for fresh meat.
- **James B. Duke (1856-1925)** : Tobacco tycoon. He created the American Tobacco Company (1890), which controlled 90% of US cigarette production. He used aggressive advertising (he included coupons in cigarette packs that could be redeemed for prizes) and ruthless tactics (price wars, buyouts). The Supreme Court ordered the trust dissolved (1911). Duke gave $40 million to Trinity College (which was renamed Duke University).
- **The "Gospel of Wealth"** : Carnegie argued that the wealthy had a moral obligation to use their fortunes for the public good (philanthropy). He wrote "The Gospel of Wealth" (1889): "The man who dies thus rich dies disgraced." This was a justification for wealth inequality (the rich were "trustees" for the poor). Critics (including socialist Eugene Debs) called it hypocrisy.

**Effects – Economic Growth and the Rise of Big Business (KC-6.1.I, KC-6.1.I, KC-6.1.I):**
- **Gross National Product (GNP)** : The US GNP grew from $9 billion (1870) to $37 billion (1900) to $91 billion (1916). The US became the world's largest industrial power by 1890 (surpassing Britain).
- **Steel production** : 20,000 tons (1865) → 1.3 million tons (1880) → 10 million tons (1900) → 26 million tons (1915).
- **Railroad mileage** : 35,000 miles (1865) → 93,000 miles (1880) → 199,000 miles (1900) → 254,000 miles (1915). Railroads connected every region of the country and opened the West to settlement.
- **Corporate consolidation** : By 1900, 1% of corporations controlled 33% of all manufacturing output. The "trust" and "holding company" allowed a few men to control entire industries (oil, steel, sugar, tobacco, rubber, leather, whiskey, salt, lead, copper).
- **The "Millionaire's Club"** : The number of millionaires (in 1870 dollars) grew from 400 (1870) to 4,000 (1900). The richest 1% of Americans owned 51% of the nation's wealth; the richest 9% owned 71%; the bottom 50% owned 1%.

**Effects – Urbanization and the Rise of Cities (KC-6.2.I.A, KC-6.2.I.B):**
- **City growth** : New York (pop. 942,000 in 1870 → 3.4 million in 1900); Chicago (299,000 → 1.7 million); Philadelphia (674,000 → 1.3 million); St. Louis (310,000 → 575,000); Boston (250,000 → 560,000); Baltimore (267,000 → 508,000); San Francisco (149,000 → 342,000).
- **Tenements and slums** : Immigrants and rural migrants crowded into tenements (crowded, poorly ventilated, unsanitary apartment buildings). Jacob Riis's *How the Other Half Lives* (1890) photographed the slums of New York City (the "Bandit's Roost," "Mulberry Bend"). He documented the conditions that caused disease, crime, and poverty.
- **The "city beautiful" movement** : Some reformers (Frederick Law Olmsted, Calvert Vaux) designed parks and green spaces to improve urban life. Olmsted designed Central Park (New York), Prospect Park (Brooklyn), and the grounds of the US Capitol and the Biltmore Estate.

**Effects – Exploitation of Labor and the Rise of Labor Unions (KC-6.1.II.C, KC-6.1.II.B.i):**
- **Working conditions** : 12-14 hour days, 6 days a week. No sick leave, no vacation, no workers' compensation, no unemployment insurance. Factories were dangerous (the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire, 1911 – after this period – killed 146 women workers; the doors were locked to prevent theft). The annual death rate from industrial accidents was 35,000-50,000 (in the 1880s-1890s).
- **Wages** : The average wage was $400-500 per year (for men); women earned $200-300; children earned $50-100. A "living wage" for a family of four was $600-800 per year. Most families needed two or three wage earners.
- **The Knights of Labor (1869-1890s)** : The first national labor union. Its leader was Terence Powderly. The Knights welcomed all workers (skilled, unskilled, men, women, Black, white, immigrant). They demanded an 8-hour day, equal pay for equal work, the abolition of child labor, and the creation of worker-owned cooperatives. The Knights declined after the Haymarket Riot (1886) – they were blamed for the bombing (though they were not involved).
- **The American Federation of Labor (AFL, founded 1886)** : Led by Samuel Gompers. The AFL was a "trade union" (it organized skilled workers by craft – carpenters, plumbers, electricians, printers, cigar makers). It focused on "bread and butter" issues (higher wages, shorter hours, better working conditions). It used strikes, boycotts, and collective bargaining. It avoided political radicalism. The AFL was the most successful and enduring labor union. Membership grew to 1 million (1900), 2 million (1914), 4 million (1920).
- **The Haymarket Riot (May 4, 1886)** : A labor rally in Chicago's Haymarket Square (protesting police brutality against striking workers at the McCormick Harvesting Machine Company). Someone threw a bomb at the police; the police opened fire. 7 police officers and 4 workers were killed. 8 anarchist leaders (Albert Parsons, August Spies, Samuel Fielden, Oscar Neebe, Michael Schwab, Adolph Fischer, George Engel, Louis Lingg) were convicted of conspiracy (though the bomb thrower was never identified). Four were hanged (Parsons, Spies, Fischer, Engel; Lingg committed suicide in jail; Neebe, Schwab, Fielden were pardoned by Illinois Governor John Peter Altgeld, 1893). The Haymarket Riot damaged the labor movement (the Knights of Labor were blamed and collapsed). But it also created the 8-hour day movement (May Day, May 1, became International Workers' Day).
- **The Homestead Strike (1892)** : The Carnegie Steel Company plant in Homestead, Pennsylvania (near Pittsburgh). The union (the Amalgamated Association of Iron and Steel Workers) went on strike. Carnegie was in Scotland; his partner, Henry Clay Frick, hired 300 Pinkerton detectives to break the strike. A gun battle broke out; 9 workers and 3 Pinkerton men died. The Pennsylvania state militia was called in; the union was crushed. The strike lasted 5 months; the company rehired non-union workers. Carnegie's reputation was damaged (though he blamed Frick).
- **The Pullman Strike (1894)** : The Pullman Palace Car Company (in Pullman, Illinois, a company town near Chicago) cut wages but did not reduce rent. The American Railway Union (ARU), led by Eugene V. Debs, called a boycott (union members refused to handle Pullman cars). The boycott spread to 27 states; 250,000 workers joined. President Grover Cleveland sent federal troops to break the strike (he said the strike interfered with the mail). The soldiers clashed with strikers; 30 workers were killed. Debs was arrested, tried, and convicted (for violating a federal injunction against the strike). He was sentenced to 6 months in prison. While in prison, Debs read Karl Marx and became a socialist. He ran for president 5 times as the Socialist Party candidate (1900, 1904, 1908, 1912, 1920 – the last time from prison, running against Woodrow Wilson and Warren G. Harding).

## LO 6.B: Explain the causes and effects of the settlement of the West from 1877 to 1898.

**Contextualization:** After the Civil War, the federal government encouraged westward expansion through the Homestead Act (1862), the Pacific Railway Acts (1862, 1864), and the Indian Appropriation Acts (funding the reservation system and military campaigns). Between 1870 and 1890, the "Great Plains" (from the Mississippi River to the Rocky Mountains) were settled by miners (gold, silver, copper), cowboys (cattle drives from Texas to Kansas railheads), farmers (homesteaders on the "sod frontier"), and railroad workers (Irish and Chinese laborers building the transcontinental railroad). This settlement displaced Native Americans (the "Great Sioux War," the Nez Perce War, the Apache Wars) and destroyed the bison (30 million in 1800 → fewer than 1,000 by 1885).

**Thesis:** The settlement of the West from 1877 to 1898 was caused by **government policies** (the Homestead Act, the Pacific Railway Acts, the Indian Appropriation Acts), **economic opportunities** (gold, silver, copper, cattle, land, timber), and the **ideology of Manifest Destiny** (the belief that Americans were destined to occupy the continent); its effects included **the creation of new states** (Colorado 1876, North Dakota 1889, South Dakota 1889, Montana 1889, Washington 1889, Idaho 1890, Wyoming 1890, Utah 1896), **the destruction of Native American societies** (the Dawes Act 1887 broke up reservations into individual allotments, selling "surplus" land to white settlers; the Wounded Knee massacre 1890 ended Native resistance), **the decline of the open range** (barbed wire, 1874), **the rise of cattle kingdoms** (Texas longhorns, the Chisholm Trail, the Goodnight-Loving Trail), and **the transformation of the Great Plains from "desert" to "breadbasket"** (steel plow, mechanical reaper, windmills, dry farming techniques, the "bonanza farms" of the Red River Valley).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Causes – Government Policies (KC-6.1.I.A, KC-6.2.II.A, KC-6.2.II.D, KC-6.2.II.D, KC-6.2.II.D, KC-6.2.II.D):**
- **The Homestead Act (1862)** : see LO 5.B
- **The Pacific Railway Acts (1862, 1864)** : see LO 5.B
- **The Mining Act of 1866** : Allowed prospectors to stake claims on public land (mining claims, placer claims, lode claims). This led to the "rush" for gold and silver in the West (the Comstock Lode, the Black Hills, Cripple Creek, the Klondike – after this period).
- **The Timber Culture Act (1873)** : Gave 160 acres of public land to anyone who would plant 40 acres of trees (and keep them alive for 8 years). This encouraged settlement on the treeless Great Plains.
- **The Desert Land Act (1877)** : Gave 640 acres of public land to anyone who would irrigate it within 3 years. This encouraged settlement in arid regions (Nevada, Utah, Arizona, New Mexico, California, Oregon, Washington).
- **The Dawes Severalty Act (1887)** : see LO 6.3

**Causes – Economic Opportunities (KC-6.2.II.A):**
- **Gold and silver** : The Comstock Lode (Nevada, 1859) produced $400 million in silver. The Black Hills (South Dakota, 1874) produced gold and led to the Great Sioux War (1876-1877). The Cripple Creek district (Colorado, 1890) produced gold. The Klondike Gold Rush (Yukon Territory, Canada, 1896-1899) drew 100,000 prospectors (though it was outside the US).
- **Copper** : Butte, Montana, produced 30% of the world's copper by 1890. The Anaconda Copper Mining Company (founded 1881) became a monopoly.
- **Cattle** : Texas longhorns were driven north to Kansas railheads (Abilene, Dodge City, Ellsworth, Wichita) and then shipped east. The cattle kingdom (1860s-1880s) covered the Great Plains from Texas to Montana. The "open range" allowed cattle to graze on public land for free. Overgrazing, the "Great Die-Up" (winter of 1886-1887 killed 80% of cattle), and barbed wire (1874) ended the open range.
- **Land** : The Homestead Act offered 160 acres of free land (for a $10 filing fee, plus 5 years of residency and improvement). 1.6 million homesteaders claimed 270 million acres by 1900. Railroads sold land for $2-5 per acre (on credit). Land speculators bought huge tracts and sold them to settlers at a profit.
- **Timber** : The Pacific Northwest (Washington, Oregon) supplied lumber for building cities, houses, and railroads. The Weyerhaeuser Timber Company (founded 1900) controlled millions of acres.
- **Farming** : The "bonanza farms" of the Red River Valley (North Dakota, Minnesota) were huge (3,000-100,000 acres) and highly mechanized (steam-powered tractors, gang plows, reapers, threshers, binders). They grew wheat for export (to Europe). The bonanza farms were owned by corporations (not families). They made huge profits but collapsed in the Panic of 1893.

**Causes – Manifest Destiny and the "Closing of the Frontier" (KC-6.2.II.A):**
- **Manifest Destiny** : The belief that Americans were divinely ordained to occupy the entire continent (from the Atlantic to the Pacific). After the Civil War, Manifest Destiny was directed against Native Americans and Mexicans (in the Southwest).
- **The "Frontier Thesis" (Frederick Jackson Turner, 1893)** : The Census Bureau declared the frontier "closed" in 1890 (no more continuous line of settlement). Turner argued that the frontier had shaped American character (individualism, democracy, ingenuity, violence). He worried that the closing of the frontier would lead to social unrest and class conflict. His thesis was influential, though later historians criticized it for ignoring Native Americans, women, and minorities.

**Effects – New States (KC-6.2.II.A):**
- Colorado (1876, "Centennial State") : Gold (Pikes Peak Gold Rush, 1858-1859), silver, lead, zinc. Denver, Leadville, Cripple Creek.
- North Dakota (1889), South Dakota (1889) : Wheat, cattle, gold (Black Hills). The "Dakota Territory" was divided (because the Republicans wanted four new senators – two from each state – to control the Senate).
- Montana (1889) : Copper (Butte), gold (Helena, Virginia City), cattle, timber.
- Washington (1889) : Timber, fishing, wheat, apples, gold.
- Idaho (1890) : Silver (Coeur d'Alene), gold (Boise Basin), potatoes.
- Wyoming (1890) : Coal, oil, cattle, tourism (Yellowstone National Park, established 1872 – the first national park in the world).
- Utah (1896) : Mormons, copper (Bingham Canyon), silver, gold, "Gentiles" (non-Mormons) wanted statehood for political representation. Utah's statehood was delayed because of Mormon polygamy (the church banned polygamy in 1890).

**Effects – Destruction of Native American Societies (KC-6.2.II.D, KC-6.2.II.E):**
- **The Dawes Severalty Act (1887)** : The federal government broke up reservations (tribal land) into individual allotments (160 acres for each head of household, 80 acres for each adult, 40 acres for each child). The "surplus" land (90 million acres) was sold to white settlers. The goal was to "civilize" Native Americans (assimilate them into white society) by making them individual farmers. The Dawes Act was a disaster:
  - Native Americans lost 90 million acres of land (2/3 of their remaining territory).
  - Individual allotments were often too small and arid for farming.
  - Native Americans were not prepared for farming (many were hunter-gatherers or pastoralists).
  - The "surplus" land was sold cheaply to white settlers and railroad companies.
- **The Wounded Knee Massacre (December 29, 1890)** : The last major confrontation between the US army and Native Americans. The army was attempting to disarm a band of Lakota Sioux on the Pine Ridge Reservation (South Dakota). A gun accidentally discharged; the soldiers opened fire with rifles and Hotchkiss cannons. 150-300 Lakota men, women, and children were killed (the army admitted 150; the Lakota claim 300). The wounded were left to die in a blizzard (temperatures below zero). 20 Congressional Medals of Honor were awarded to the soldiers (some of which are still in dispute – the Lakota have asked for them to be rescinded).
- **The Ghost Dance** : A religious movement among Plains Indians (Paiute prophet Wovoka, 1889). The Ghost Dance promised that the buffalo would return, the white settlers would disappear, and the ancestors would rise from the dead. The US army feared the Ghost Dance was a prelude to an uprising (it was not). The attempt to suppress the Ghost Dance led to the Wounded Knee massacre.
- **Carlisle Indian Industrial School (1879-1918)** : Founded by Richard Henry Pratt (a former army officer). Pratt's motto: "Kill the Indian, save the man." Native American children were forced to attend boarding schools (off-reservation), forbidden to speak their native languages, wear Native clothing, or practice Native religions. They were given English names, taught English, and trained in manual trades (farming, carpentry, blacksmithing, sewing, cooking). Many children died of disease; many were abused. The boarding school system was a tool of assimilation (cultural genocide). It persisted into the 20th century (the last residential school closed in 1973).
- **"Indian Territory" (Oklahoma)** : The "Five Civilized Tribes" (Cherokee, Chickasaw, Choctaw, Creek, Seminole) were removed to Indian Territory (Oklahoma) in the 1830s. After the Civil War, the federal government forced them to cede land to other tribes (Plains Indians) and to white settlers. The "Land Run of 1889" opened up unassigned lands to white settlers (50,000 people rushed in to claim 160-acre plots). Oklahoma became a territory (1890) and a state (1907).

**Effects – The Decline of the Open Range (KC-6.2.II.B, KC-6.2.II.C):**
- **Barbed wire (Joseph Glidden, 1874)** : Cheap, effective fencing. Barbed wire allowed farmers to fence their fields (keeping cattle out). It also allowed ranchers to fence their range (keeping their cattle in). The open range (where cattle grazed on public land for free) was replaced by fenced pastures. This benefited large ranchers (who could afford to fence land) and hurt small ranchers (who could not).
- **The "Range Wars"** : Conflicts between large ranchers and small ranchers, ranchers and farmers, ranchers and sheep herders. The most famous was the Johnson County War (Wyoming, 1892): large ranchers hired Texas gunmen to kill small ranchers (who they accused of stealing cattle). The gunmen were captured and released (the Wyoming governor was a large rancher).
- **The "Great Die-Up" (1886-1887)** : An extremely harsh winter (blizzards, freezing temperatures) killed 80% of cattle on the northern plains (Montana, Dakota Territory, Wyoming). Cattle prices collapsed. The open range cattle industry never recovered. The "cattle kingdom" was replaced by smaller, fenced ranches.

**Effects – The Transformation of the Great Plains (KC-6.1.III.A, KC-6.1.III.A):**
- **Dry farming techniques** : Farmers adapted to the arid climate (less than 20 inches of rainfall per year) by planting drought-resistant crops (sorghum, corn, wheat) and using techniques to retain soil moisture (summer fallowing, deep plowing, harrowing). The introduction of Russian thistle (tumbleweed) and other weeds changed the ecology.
- **Windmills** : Windpumps pumped water from underground aquifers (the Ogallala Aquifer) for livestock, irrigation, and domestic use.
- **Steel plows (John Deere, 1837)** : Cut through the dense prairie sod (which was too tough for iron plows). The "soddy" (sod house) was a common dwelling on the treeless plains.
- **Mechanical reapers (Cyrus McCormick, 1834)** : Harvested wheat quickly and efficiently. The "combine" (combined reaper and thresher) appeared in the 1880s.
- **Railroads** : The transcontinental railroads (Union Pacific, Central Pacific, Northern Pacific, Great Northern, Santa Fe, Southern Pacific) brought settlers, supplies, and markets. They also charged high freight rates (which farmers resented – one cause of the Populist movement).
- **The "Garden of the World" myth** : Rain follows the plow – a popular belief that settlement and cultivation would increase rainfall (it did not). This led to over-optimistic settlement and, later, the Dust Bowl (1930s).

## LO 6.C: Explain how various factors contributed to continuity and change in the "New South" from 1877 to 1898.

**Contextualization:** After Reconstruction ended in 1877, Southerners (white Democrats, called "Redeemers" or "Bourbons") took control of state governments. They promised to create a "New South" – an industrial, diversified economy (not just cotton). They built textile mills, tobacco factories, steel mills, iron furnaces, and railroad lines. But the "New South" was also the "Old South" in many ways: sharecropping and tenant farming replaced slavery (most Black farmers remained landless and in debt); Jim Crow laws segregated society; African Americans were disenfranchised (poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses, white primaries); and violence (lynching, the KKK, the Wilmington coup) terrorized Black communities.

**Thesis:** The "New South" from 1877 to 1898 was characterized by both continuity and change: **change** (industrialization – textile mills, tobacco factories, steel mills, iron furnaces, railroad expansion; the rise of a new middle class – merchants, bankers, railroad managers, industrialists; the growth of cities – Atlanta, Birmingham, Nashville, Richmond, Charlotte; and the establishment of public school systems – for white and Black children, though segregated); **continuity** (the persistence of sharecropping and tenant farming – most Black farmers remained landless and in debt; the disenfranchisement of African Americans – poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses; the rise of Jim Crow segregation – Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) established "separate but equal"; and racial violence – lynching, the Colfax massacre, the Hamburg massacre, the Wilmington coup of 1898).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Change – Industrialization (KC-6.1.II.D):**
- **Textile mills** : The South became a center of textile manufacturing (cotton from Southern fields was spun into cloth in Southern mills). The "Piedmont Crescent" (from Greenville, South Carolina to Columbus, Georgia) housed hundreds of mills. Mill villages (company towns) provided housing, stores, churches, and schools for workers (mostly poor white farmers who had been displaced by falling cotton prices). Workers were paid low wages (50-75 cents per day) and worked 12-hour shifts.
- **Tobacco factories** : Durham, North Carolina, became a center of tobacco processing. James B. Duke (American Tobacco Company) consolidated the industry. Duke's factory in Durham (the "Bull Durham" factory) was a tourist attraction. The "roll your own" cigarette (with a paper wrapper) was invented in the 1880s. Cigarette consumption exploded (from 500 million in 1880 to 3 billion in 1900).
- **Steel and iron** : Birmingham, Alabama, became the "Pittsburgh of the South." The city had coal (for fuel), iron ore (for smelting), and limestone (as a flux). The Tennessee Coal, Iron, and Railroad Company (TCI) was a major employer.
- **Railroad expansion** : The South built 10,000 miles of railroad track (1865-1890). The "Southern Railway System" (a consolidation of smaller lines) connected the region to the rest of the nation.
- **Furniture** : High Point, North Carolina, became a center of furniture manufacturing.
- **Lumber** : The Southern pine forests (Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Texas) supplied lumber for construction (both in the South and nationwide). The "yellow pine" boom (1880s-1900s) attracted Northern investors.
- **Development of resources** : Coal (West Virginia, Kentucky, Tennessee, Alabama, Georgia), iron (Alabama), copper (Tennessee), phosphate (Florida, South Carolina, North Carolina), granite and marble (Georgia, Vermont). These resources were extracted by convict lease labor (mostly Black men, who were arrested for "vagrancy" and leased to private companies).

**Change – The Rise of a New Middle Class (KC-6.1.II.D):**
- **Merchants and bankers** : The "Redeemers" (white Democrats who took power after Reconstruction) were often merchants, bankers, railroad executives, and industrialists – not planters (though many were also planters). They promoted economic development (textile mills, railroads, banks, public schools) as a way to modernize the South.
- **Editor Henry Grady (1850-1889)** : The editor of the *Atlanta Constitution* (Georgia). Grady coined the phrase "New South" (1886). He argued that the South should industrialize, diversify, and abandon the "Old South" of slavery and plantation agriculture. He praised Northern capital and Northern investors (who built the textile mills). He also praised the "New South" as a place of racial harmony (which was a lie).
- **The "Cotton States and International Exposition" (Atlanta, 1895)** : A World's Fair-style exposition showcasing the "New South." Booker T. Washington gave his "Atlanta Compromise" speech (see LO 6.4). The exposition featured exhibits on Southern industry (textiles, tobacco, steel, timber, agriculture) and technology (telephones, electric lights, the "new woman").

**Change – The Growth of Cities (KC-6.1.II.D):**
- **Atlanta, Georgia** : The "capital of the New South." Atlanta was a railroad hub (the "Crossroads of the South") and a manufacturing center (textiles, furniture, machinery, Coca-Cola – invented 1886, bottled 1899). Atlanta's population grew from 21,789 (1870) to 89,872 (1900) to 154,839 (1910). The city hosted the Cotton States Exposition (1895).
- **Birmingham, Alabama** : Founded 1871 (named after Birmingham, England, the center of British steel production). Birmingham's population grew from 3,086 (1870) to 38,415 (1900) to 132,685 (1910). It produced pig iron, steel, and railroad equipment. It was also the site of convict leasing (mining coal, working in iron furnaces, building roads and railroads). The "Sloss Furnaces" (National Historic Landmark) operated from 1882 to 1971 (now a museum).
- **Nashville, Tennessee** : The "Athens of the South." Nashville was an educational center (Fisk University, Vanderbilt University, Meharry Medical College) and a publishing center (Gospel music, the "Grand Ole Opry" – though the Opry was founded 1925). It also hosted the Tennessee Centennial Exposition (1897).
- **Richmond, Virginia** : The "Capital of the Confederacy." Richmond was a manufacturing center (iron, steel, tobacco, flour) and a transportation hub (railroads, James River canal).
- **Charlotte, North Carolina** : A textile center (the "Lowell of the South"). Charlotte's population grew from 2,000 (1860) to 18,000 (1890) to 34,000 (1900).

**Change – Public Education (KC-6.1.II.D):**
- Before the Civil War, the South had almost no public schools (only private academies for the wealthy). Reconstruction governments established public school systems (for both Black and white children, segregated). The "Redeemer" governments maintained public schools but drastically underfunded Black schools.
- By 1900, 85% of Black adults were illiterate (down from 95% in 1865). By 1930, 25% of Black adults were illiterate. The Black literacy rate was rising, but it was still far below the white literacy rate (95%).
- Black communities built their own schools (with help from Northern missionaries, the Freedmen's Bureau, and Black churches). The "Rosenwald Fund" (Julius Rosenwald, CEO of Sears, Roebuck) built 5,000 Black schools in the South (1912-1932).
- Black colleges were founded: Howard University (1866, Washington, D.C.), Fisk University (1866, Nashville), Atlanta University (1866), Morehouse College (1867, Atlanta), Spelman College (1881, Atlanta), Hampton Institute (1868, Virginia, founded by the American Missionary Association to train Black teachers), Tuskegee Institute (1881, Alabama, founded by Booker T. Washington).
- **Continuity:** Black schools were poorly funded, often had substandard facilities (one-room shacks, hand-me-down textbooks), and were segregated by law (Plessy v. Ferguson, 1896).

**Continuity – Sharecropping and Tenant Farming (KC-6.1.II.D):**
- **Sharecropping** : A landowner (usually white) allowed a family (usually Black, but also poor white) to farm a plot of land in exchange for a share of the crop (usually 1/3 or 1/2 of the crop). The landowner provided seed, fertilizer, tools, and sometimes a mule. The sharecropper provided labor. At the end of the year, the sharecropper sold the crop, paid the landowner his share, and (hopefully) kept the rest.
- **Tenant farming** : A tenant farmer rented land (paying cash or a share of the crop). The tenant farmer provided his own seed, tools, and mule. Tenant farmers had more autonomy than sharecroppers (they could decide what to plant and where to sell the crop).
- **The crop lien system** : Sharecroppers and tenant farmers often had to borrow money (from the landowner or a local merchant) to buy seed, fertilizer, tools, and food. The loan was secured by a lien on the crop (the crop was the collateral). The interest rates were high (20-50%). If the crop failed (due to bad weather, pests, low prices), the farmer could not repay the loan and was trapped in debt. He could not leave the farm (the landowner would not release him until the debt was paid). This system was called "debt peonage" (a form of slavery). It affected both Black and white farmers.
- The sharecropping and crop lien system kept African Americans (and poor whites) in a state of virtual slavery. The "Southern economy" was still based on cotton (which depleted the soil), but the wealth was concentrated in the hands of landowners and merchants.

**Continuity – Disenfranchisement of African Americans (KC-6.3.II.C):**
- The 15th Amendment (1870) prohibited racial discrimination in voting. But Southern states found ways to circumvent it:
  - **Poll taxes** : A fee to vote ($1-2 per year). Most Black people (and many poor whites) could not afford it.
  - **Literacy tests** : Voters had to read and interpret a section of the state constitution (or a random passage from law). The test was administered by white registrars, who could fail Black applicants for trivial mistakes (and pass white applicants who were illiterate).
  - **Grandfather clauses** : Exempted from literacy tests any person whose grandfather had voted (before the 15th Amendment). This allowed poor white illiterates to vote while excluding most Black voters (because their grandfathers had been slaves and could not vote).
  - **White primaries** : The Democratic Party (which controlled the South) held primary elections that were open only to white voters. The general election was a formality (the Democratic candidate always won). This effectively disenfranchised African Americans.
  - **Understanding clauses** : A voter had to "understand" a section of the constitution to the satisfaction of the registrar. This was another subjective test.
- **The "Mississippi Plan" (1890)** : Mississippi was the first state to rewrite its constitution to disenfranchise African Americans (poll tax, literacy test, understanding clause). Other Southern states followed (South Carolina 1895, Louisiana 1898, North Carolina 1900, Alabama 1901, Virginia 1902, Georgia 1908, Oklahoma 1910).
- By 1910, Black voter turnout in the South had fallen from 90% (during Reconstruction) to <5%.

**Continuity – Jim Crow Segregation (KC-6.3.II.C):**
- **Jim Crow** : A system of racial segregation (de jure – by law) in the South. It covered public transportation (railroads, streetcars, steamboats), public accommodations (hotels, restaurants, theaters, restrooms, water fountains), housing (restrictive covenants), education (segregated schools), and public health (segregated hospitals, asylums, and even cemeteries). The term "Jim Crow" comes from a 19th-century minstrel show character (a caricature of a Black man).
- **Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)** : Homer Plessy (a light-skinned Black man, 1/8 Black) sat in a "whites only" railroad car in Louisiana (he was working with the Citizens Committee to test the law). He was arrested. The Supreme Court (7-1) upheld the Louisiana Separate Car Act (1890). Justice Henry Billings Brown wrote the majority opinion: "If one race be inferior to the other socially, the Constitution of the United States cannot put them upon the same plane." Justice John Marshall Harlan (the lone dissenter) wrote: "Our Constitution is color-blind, and neither knows nor tolerates classes among citizens... The thin disguise of 'equal' accommodations for passengers in railroad coaches will not mislead anyone, nor atone for the wrong this day done." The "separate but equal" doctrine was established (though in practice, "equal" was a lie – Black facilities were always inferior).
- **"Separate but equal" was a fiction** : In 1930, South Carolina spent $95 per white student and $11 per Black student. Mississippi spent $84 per white student and $7 per Black student. There were no Black law schools in the South (until the NAACP sued, leading to *Gaines v. Canada* 1938, *Sipuel v. Board of Regents* 1948, *Sweatt v. Painter* 1950).
- **"Jim Crow" was not limited to the South** . The Jim Crow era (1890s-1960s) also saw the rise of "sundown towns" (towns that expelled African Americans after dark) in the North and West. Many Northern cities had segregated neighborhoods through "redlining" (bank lending practices that discriminated against Black neighborhoods) and restrictive covenants (private agreements not to sell to Black people).

**Continuity – Racial Violence (KC-6.3.II.C):**
- **Lynching** : Murder by mob (hanging, burning, shooting, mutilation). Between 1882 and 1968, 4,743 people were lynched in the United States (3,446 were Black; 1,297 were white). The peak years were 1892-1903 (150-200 lynchings per year). Lynching was a form of racial terror (used to enforce white supremacy and suppress Black economic competition). Ida B. Wells (a Black journalist from Tennessee) investigated lynchings and published *Southern Horrors* (1892) and *The Red Record* (1895). She documented that lynchings were rarely for rape (as claimed by white supremacists) – most were for "insulting a white person," "trying to vote," "stealing a cow," "arson," "talking back to a white man," or "refusing to move off the sidewalk."
- **The Colfax massacre (1873)** : See LO 5.K
- **The Hamburg massacre (1876)** : See LO 5.K
- **The Wilmington coup (1898)** : White Democrats overthrew the elected biracial government of Wilmington, North Carolina. A mob burned the Black newspaper office (the *Daily Record*), killed 60-300 Black people, and forced the mayor, police chief, and aldermen to resign. They replaced them with white Democrats. This was the only successful coup d'état in American history.
- **The Atlanta race riot (1906)** : White mobs attacked Black neighborhoods in Atlanta, Georgia, after newspapers (owned by the "Redeemer" Hoke Smith) printed false stories of Black men assaulting white women. 25-40 Black people were killed (and several white people). The riot was a reaction to Black economic progress (Atlanta had a thriving Black middle class, with Black-owned banks, newspapers, and businesses).
- **The Springfield race riot (1908)** : In Springfield, Illinois (the hometown of Abraham Lincoln). White mobs attacked Black neighborhoods after a white woman accused a Black man of rape (the accusation was false). 7 Black people were killed, 2,000 fled the city. The riot led to the founding of the NAACP (National Association for the Advancement of Colored People, 1909).

**Continuity – The "Lost Cause" Mythology:**
- The "Lost Cause" was a historical revisionism movement that glorified the Confederacy and denied that slavery was the cause of the Civil War. It argued that:
  - The Confederacy was a noble "lost cause" (fighting for "states' rights," not slavery)
  - Slavery was a "positive good" (comfortable, paternalistic, benevolent)
  - Reconstruction was a "tragedy of corruption and misrule" (Northern carpetbaggers, Southern scalawags, and ignorant Black people)
  - The Ku Klux Klan was a heroic resistance movement (not a terrorist organization)
  - Confederate generals (Robert E. Lee, Stonewall Jackson, J.E.B. Stuart) were Christian saints
- The "Lost Cause" was promoted by the United Daughters of the Confederacy (UDC) and the Sons of Confederate Veterans (SCV). They erected Confederate monuments (500+ in public spaces), renamed schools, and lobbied for "Confederate Memorial Day." This mythology was taught in Southern schools (and even in some Northern schools) for generations. It was a tool of white supremacy and a way to resist the civil rights movement.

## LO 6.D: Explain the effects of technological advances in the development of the United States over time.

**Contextualization:** The period from 1865 to 1898 was an age of remarkable technological innovation. New inventions—the telephone, the light bulb, the phonograph, the motion picture camera, the typewriter, the cash register, the calculating machine, the refrigerator car, the automobile, the electric streetcar—transformed daily life, work, communication, and entertainment.

**Thesis:** Technological advances from 1865 to 1898 affected the development of the United States by **increasing industrial productivity** (the Bessemer process, the open-hearth furnace, the assembly line – though Henry Ford perfected it later), **transforming communication** (the telephone, the telegraph, the typewriter), **revolutionizing transportation** (the refrigerator car, the electric streetcar, the automobile – though the automobile would transform American society in the 20th century), **creating new industries** (electricity, oil refining, steel, chemicals, rubber), **changing the nature of work** (the "deskilling" of labor, the rise of clerical work for women), and **shaping popular culture** (the phonograph, the motion picture, spectator sports – baseball, football, boxing).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Effect 1 – Increased Industrial Productivity (KC-6.1.I.B.i):**
- The **Bessemer process** (1856, introduced to US 1864) and the **open-hearth furnace** (1860s) allowed the mass production of cheap, high-quality steel. Steel replaced iron in railroads (steel rails lasted 10 times longer than iron rails), bridges (the Brooklyn Bridge, 1883, used steel cables), ships (steel-hulled steamships), buildings (skyscrapers), and machinery (steam engines, locomotives, farm equipment). The US steel industry grew from 20,000 tons (1865) to 10 million tons (1900).
- **Interchangeable parts** : Eli Whitney (muskets, 1798) and the Springfield Armory (1840s) pioneered the "American System of Manufacturing." By the 1880s, manufacturers of sewing machines (Singer), bicycles, and farm equipment (McCormick, Deere) used interchangeable parts. This allowed for mass production, lower costs, and easier repair.
- **The "assembly line"** : Not perfected until Henry Ford (1913), but the principles of continuous flow and division of labor were applied to meatpacking (Swift, Armour) and canning (Campbell's, Heinz) in the 1880s-1890s.

**Effect 2 – Transformation of Communication (KC-6.2.I.A, KC-6.2.I.A):**
- **The telephone (Alexander Graham Bell, 1876)** : The telephone allowed instant voice communication over long distances. Bell formed the Bell Telephone Company (1877), which later became AT&T. By 1900, 1.3 million telephones were in use; by 1910, 7.9 million. The telephone created jobs for women (telephone operators) and changed business practices (managers could call subordinates, salesmen could call customers, customers could call companies).
- **The telegraph** : The telegraph (Samuel Morse, 1844) had already transformed communication. The "ticker tape" (stock ticker, 1867) allowed brokers to receive stock prices instantly. Western Union (founded 1851) monopolized the telegraph. By 1900, Western Union had 1.5 million miles of wire and 10,000 offices.
- **The typewriter (Christopher Sholes, 1868)** : The typewriter created jobs for women ("typewriters" – clerical workers, secretaries). By 1900, 200,000 women worked as clerical workers (up from almost none in 1870). The typewriter also standardized business correspondence (making it legible and professional).
- **The cash register (James Ritty, 1879)** : The cash register prevented employee theft (by recording each sale). John H. Patterson's National Cash Register Company (NCR) mass-produced cash registers and trained salesmen. NCR also pioneered "welfare capitalism" (company housing, sports teams, health programs) to prevent unionization.
- **The calculating machine (William Burroughs, 1885)** : The Burroughs adding machine automated bookkeeping. It created jobs for women ("computers" – people who performed calculations). The Burroughs Corporation became a major office equipment manufacturer.

**Effect 3 – Revolutionizing Transportation (KC-6.1.I.A, KC-6.2.I.A):**
- **The refrigerator car (the "reefer")** : Gustavus Swift (meatpacker) developed the refrigerator car (1878). It allowed fresh meat (and later fruits, vegetables, dairy) to be shipped long distances. Swift undercut local butchers and created a national market for meat. Chicago became the "hog butcher of the world."
- **The electric streetcar (Frank Sprague, 1888)** : Sprague's electric streetcar system (Richmond, Virginia, 1888) was the first successful large-scale electric streetcar system. It replaced horse-drawn streetcars and cable cars. Electric streetcars allowed cities to expand (streetcar suburbs – Roxbury, Dorchester, Jamaica Plain in Boston; Lake View, Hyde Park in Chicago; Brooklyn, Queens in New York). By 1900, there were 20,000 miles of electric streetcar track.
- **The automobile** : Karl Benz (Germany) built the first automobile (1885). Gottlieb Daimler (Germany) built the first high-speed internal combustion engine (1885). Charles Duryea (US) built the first successful gasoline-powered automobile (1893). Henry Ford would mass-produce automobiles in the early 20th century (the Model T, 1908). The (early) automobile was a toy for the rich (they cost $1,000-$2,000, when the average worker earned $500/year).
- **The bicycle** : The "safety bicycle" (with two wheels of equal size, chain drive, pneumatic tires – John Dunlop, 1888) was popular in the 1890s. The bicycle cost $100 (affordable for middle-class workers). Bicycles gave women mobility and independence (Susan B. Anthony said, "Let me tell you what I think of bicycling... it has done more to emancipate women than anything else in the world"). Bicycle clubs and bicycle racing were popular.

**Effect 4 – Creating New Industries (KC-6.1.I.B.i):**
- **Electricity** : Thomas Edison (light bulb, 1879; electric power distribution, 1882). George Westinghouse (alternating current, AC, 1886). By 1900, 50% of American cities had electric lighting; 25% of factories used electric motors. The electrical industry employed 100,000 workers by 1900. General Electric (Edison) and Westinghouse were the major companies.
- **Oil refining** : John D. Rockefeller's Standard Oil Company (1870) refined 90% of US oil by 1880. Oil was used for kerosene (lighting), lubricants (machinery), and (later) gasoline (automobiles). The US oil industry produced 3 million barrels (1865), 60 million barrels (1900).
- **Steel** : Andrew Carnegie's Carnegie Steel Company (1870s-1901) was the largest steel company in the world. It employed 20,000 workers. The steel industry was centered in Pittsburgh (iron ore from Minnesota, coal from Pennsylvania, limestone from Ohio).
- **Chemicals** : The chemical industry produced dyes, acids, fertilizers, and medicines. DuPont (founded 1802 as a gunpowder manufacturer) expanded into chemicals and explosives (dynamite – Alfred Nobel, 1867).
- **Rubber** : The rubber industry used natural rubber (from Brazil, Southeast Asia) to make tires (bicycles, automobiles), hoses, belts, and footwear. B.F. Goodrich (founded 1870) and Goodyear (founded 1898) were major companies.

**Effect 5 – Changing the Nature of Work (KC-6.2.I.E):**
- **The "deskilling" of labor** : The division of labor (each worker performs a single repetitive task) and the use of machines (that require less skill to operate) reduced the need for skilled artisans. A "machinist" (skilled) could be replaced by a "machine tender" (unskilled). This reduced wages and job security.
- **The rise of clerical work** : The typewriter, telephone, telegraph, cash register, and calculator created jobs for women as "typewriters" (clerical workers, secretaries, stenographers), telephone operators, and "computers" (people who performed calculations). By 1900, 1/3 of clerical workers were women.
- **The "sweatshop"** : The garment industry (clothing) used the "sweating system" (contractors hired workers to sew garments in their homes or in small, overcrowded, unsanitary workshops). Sweatshops paid by the piece (piecework) and required long hours (14-16 hours per day). They were common in immigrant neighborhoods (the Lower East Side of New York, the "Jewish ghetto"). The Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire (1911) killed 146 workers (mostly young Jewish and Italian women) – the doors were locked to prevent theft.

**Effect 6 – Shaping Popular Culture (KC-6.2.I.A):**
- **The phonograph (Thomas Edison, 1877)** : The phonograph recorded and reproduced sound. It was used for music, speeches, and entertainment (the "talking machine"). By 1900, phonographs and recordings (cylinders, then discs) were popular in homes and arcades.
- **The motion picture camera (Thomas Edison, 1891)** : Edison's "Kinetoscope" (1891) showed short films (20-30 seconds) to individual viewers through a peephole. The "Cinématographe" (Lumière brothers, France, 1895) projected films to an audience. By 1900, "nickelodeons" (storefront theaters, admission 5 cents) were popular in working-class neighborhoods.
- **Baseball** : The "National League" (founded 1876) was the first professional baseball league. The "American League" (founded 1901) competed with the National League. The first World Series was played in 1903. Baseball (the "national pastime") was popular among all social classes.
- **Football** : College football became popular in the 1880s-1890s. Walter Camp (Yale) helped develop the rules (the line of scrimmage, downs, points system). The Intercollegiate Football Association (1876) governed the sport. Football was violent (President Theodore Roosevelt threatened to ban it if it did not reform). The forward pass was legalized in 1906.
- **Boxing** : Boxing was popular among working-class men. John L. Sullivan (heavyweight champion, 1882-1892) was a national hero. "Gentleman Jim" Corbett defeated Sullivan in 1892. Boxing was illegal in many states (but matches were held in "friendly jurisdiction" – the 50th state, etc.).
- **Vaudeville** : Vaudeville (variety theater) featured comedians, singers, dancers, acrobats, jugglers, magicians, and animal acts. The "Keith-Albee" and "Orpheum" circuits (groups of theaters) booked vaudeville acts. Vaudeville was popular with immigrants and the working class.

## LO 6.E: Explain the socioeconomic continuities and changes associated with the growth of industrial capitalism from 1865 to 1898.

**Contextualization:** Industrial capitalism created enormous wealth for a few (Carnegie, Rockefeller, Morgan, Vanderbilt) and raised the standard of living for many. Real wages increased (by 50% between 1865 and 1900), the cost of goods fell (thanks to mass production), and new consumer products (electric lights, telephones, phonographs, bicycles, ready-made clothing) became available. But the gap between rich and poor grew dramatically (the richest 1% owned 51% of the nation's wealth; the poorest 50% owned 1%). Workers faced low wages (often below subsistence), long hours (10-12 hour days, 6 days a week), dangerous conditions (35,000-50,000 deaths per year from industrial accidents), and the constant threat of unemployment (financial panics in 1873, 1884, 1893, 1907). Child labor was widespread (1.7 million children under 16 worked for wages). Women earned 50-60% of what men earned.

**Thesis:** The growth of industrial capitalism from 1865 to 1898 brought both continuity and change to American society: **change** (real wages increased, the cost of goods fell, consumer products became widely available, the middle class grew, leisure time increased, public education expanded, cities grew, and the standard of living improved for many Americans); **continuity** (a vast gap between rich and poor persisted, workers faced low wages and dangerous conditions, child labor remained common, women earned less than men, African Americans and immigrants faced discrimination, and labor unions struggled to gain recognition and collective bargaining rights).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Change – Rising Real Wages (KC-6.1.I.C):**
- Real wages (adjusted for inflation) increased by 50% between 1865 and 1900. The average annual wage for manufacturing workers was $300-400 in 1865, $400-500 in 1880, $500-600 in 1900.
- **Purchasing power** : A worker could buy more with a day's wage in 1900 than in 1865 because the cost of goods fell (due to mass production, lower transportation costs, and a stable currency). For example, a pair of shoes cost $5 in 1865 (2 days' wages) and $2 in 1900 (1 day's wage). A suit cost $25 (10 days' wages) → $15 (5 days). A bicycle cost $150 (50 days) → $50 (20 days). A sewing machine cost $50 (20 days) → $20 (7 days).
- **Standard of living** : More workers could afford meat (pork, chicken), white bread, coffee, sugar, and canned goods. They could buy ready-made clothing (instead of homemade), factory-made shoes, and furniture.

**Change – The Rise of a Middle Class (KC-6.2.I.E):**
- A new "middle class" emerged: managers, clerical workers (office workers), salesmen, engineers, architects, accountants, teachers, doctors, lawyers, and small business owners. This middle class was distinct from the "working class" (manual laborers) and the "upper class" (the wealthy capitalists).
- **Middle-class values** : Hard work, thrift, education, self-improvement, "respectability." Middle-class families lived in "streetcar suburbs" (single-family homes with lawns). The "Victorian" home (named after Queen Victoria of England) was the middle-class ideal: large, ornate, with a parlor (for entertaining), a dining room, a kitchen, and bedrooms upstairs. Middle-class women were expected to be "angels of the hearth" (wives and mothers, not wage earners).
- **Consumer culture** : Middle-class families bought pianos, phonographs, books (best-sellers), magazines (the *Saturday Evening Post*, *Harper's Weekly*, *McClure's*), and newspapers (the "penny press" – *New York World, New York Journal, San Francisco Examiner*). Department stores (Macy's, Wanamaker's, Marshall Field's, Filene's) catered to middle-class women. Catalog sales (Montgomery Ward, Sears, Roebuck) brought consumer goods to rural families.

**Change – Increased Leisure Time (KC-6.2.I.E):**
- The 8-hour day movement (the slogan "Eight hours for work, eight hours for rest, eight hours for what we will") was a demand of labor unions. By 1890, some workers had 10-hour days (down from 12-14). Sunday was a day of rest (the "Christian Sabbath"). Labor Day (first celebrated 1882, made a federal holiday 1894) was a day for parades and picnics.
- **Leisure activities** : Amusement parks (Coney Island in New York – opened 1895; the "Steeplechase Park," "Luna Park," "Dreamland"), vaudeville theaters, nickelodeons (motion picture theaters), baseball games, boxing matches, bicycling, picnics, camping, fishing, hunting, and (for the middle class) vacationing at the beach (Atlantic City, New Jersey – Atlantic City boardwalk opened 1870) or the mountains (the Catskills, the Adirondacks, the White Mountains).

**Change – The Expansion of Public Education (KC-6.2.I.E):**
- By 1900, every state had compulsory education laws (requiring children to attend school for a certain number of years). The average school year was 150 days (up from 60 days in 1850). The literacy rate was 90% (for whites; 85% for Blacks – up from 95% illiteracy in 1865).
- **High schools** : The number of public high schools grew from 100 (1865) to 6,000 (1900). High schools prepared students for college (the classics) and for white-collar jobs (business, clerical).
- **Colleges and universities** : The Morrill Land-Grant Act (1862) gave federal land to states to establish agricultural and mechanical colleges (A&M). Examples: Iowa State (1858), Michigan State (1855), Penn State (1855), Kansas State (1863), Ohio State (1870), University of California (1868), Cornell University (1865, private but received land grant), MIT (1861, private). These colleges taught practical subjects (agriculture, engineering, mining, manufacturing). The Johns Hopkins University (1876) was the first American university to focus on graduate education (modeled on German universities). The University of Chicago (1890, founded by John D. Rockefeller) became a leading research university. Stanford University (1885, founded by Leland Stanford, railroad magnate) opened in 1891.
- **High school for girls** : The "Boston Latin School" (1877) admitted girls. The "Normal schools" (teacher training colleges) were often coeducational. Women's colleges (the "Seven Sisters"): Mount Holyoke (1837), Vassar (1861), Wellesley (1870), Smith (1871), Radcliffe (1879), Bryn Mawr (1885), Barnard (1889). The first woman to earn a PhD in the US was Helen Magill (Boston University, 1877). The first woman to earn a PhD in science (physics) was Margaret Maltby (MIT, 1895, but she had to study in Germany because US graduate schools did not admit women).

**Continuity – The Gap Between Rich and Poor (KC-6.1.I.C, KC-6.1.I.C):**
- **Income inequality** : The richest 1% of Americans owned 51% of the nation's wealth; the richest 10% owned 71%; the poorest 50% owned 1%. The Gilded Age (Mark Twain's term for the era) was an age of "conspicuous consumption" (the wealthy built mansions, bought yachts, threw lavish parties, and gave their daughters "coming out" balls). The "Cottage" of the Vanderbilts in Newport, Rhode Island (The Breakers) cost $7 million (1895). The "Biltmore Estate" of George Vanderbilt in Asheville, North Carolina (the largest private home in America) cost $5 million (1895).
- **Working-class poverty** : The average manufacturing wage was $500/year (in 1900). A "living wage" for a family of four was $600-800/year. Most families needed two or three wage earners (husband, wife, and children) to survive. Many families lived in tenements (one or two rooms, with no running water, no toilet, no bath). The death rate in tenements was 2-3 times higher than in middle-class neighborhoods.
- **Financial panics** : The Panic of 1873 (caused by railroad over-speculation) led to a 6-year depression (unemployment reached 14%). The Panic of 1893 (caused by the collapse of railroad stocks) led to a 4-year depression (unemployment reached 20%). The Panic of 1907 (caused by a run on banks) was a severe recession (unemployment reached 8%). There was no unemployment insurance, no welfare, and no federal relief. Unemployed workers relied on charity (the Salvation Army, the YMCA, the Volunteers of America) or their families.

**Continuity – Low Wages and Dangerous Conditions (KC-6.1.II.C):**
- **Working conditions** : 10-12 hour days, 6 days a week. No sick leave, no vacation, no workers' compensation. Factories were dangerous: unguarded machinery (pullies, belts, gears, saws, presses), slippery floors, poor lighting, poor ventilation, toxic chemicals, and extreme temperatures (steel mills, glass furnaces). The annual death rate from industrial accidents was 35,000-50,000 (in the 1880s-1890s). The injury rate was 500,000-1,000,000 per year. The "Pittsburgh Survey" (1907-1908, by the Russell Sage Foundation) documented the conditions: "The great majority of workmen... die before they are fifty years old."
- **Child labor** : 1.7 million children under 16 worked for wages. They worked in textile mills (the "lint-heads"), coal mines (the "breaker boys" – they picked slate out of coal, hunched over conveyor belts), glass factories (the "glory hole"), canneries, and sweatshops. They worked 12-14 hour days, 6 days a week. The National Child Labor Committee (founded 1904) hired photographer Lewis Hine to document child labor. His photographs (1908-1924) helped pass state child labor laws (and later the federal Fair Labor Standards Act, 1938).
- **The "company town"** : The Pullman Palace Car Company (George Pullman) built a company town for its workers (Pullman, Illinois, 1880). The town had housing, stores, a hotel, a church, a school, a library, a park, a theater, a bank, and a factory. But the rent was high (higher than in Chicago), and the company controlled every aspect of workers' lives (they could not buy from other stores, they could not drink alcohol, they could not have a newspaper). The Pullman strike (1894) was a revolt against the company town.
- **The "sweatshop"** : see LO 6.D

**Continuity – Wage Discrimination (KC-6.1.I.B.ii, KC-6.1.I.B.ii, KC-6.1.I.B.ii, KC-6.1.I.B.ii):**
- **Women** : Women earned 50-60% of what men earned for comparable work (the "gender gap"). The average female wage was $200-300 per year. Women worked as domestic servants (maids, cooks, nannies), textile workers, garment workers (sweatshops), clerical workers (typewriters, telephone operators), teachers, and nurses. The "marriage bar" (firing women when they married) was common. The "living wage" for a single woman (living alone) was $600-800 per year – unattainable for most.
- **African Americans** : Black workers earned 30-50% of what white workers earned. They were excluded from most skilled trades (by labor unions, by employers). They worked as farm laborers (sharecroppers), tenant farmers, domestic servants, and unskilled laborers (railroad track layers, levee builders, miners, stevedores). The "Great Migration" (1910-1920) began when Black workers moved north to factory jobs (but they still faced discrimination).
- **Immigrants** : Immigrants (especially Chinese, Japanese, Mexicans, and Italians) were paid less than white workers (often 50-75% of the white wage). They worked as railroad laborers (the Central Pacific hired 10,000 Chinese workers), farm laborers (Mexicans in the Southwest, Japanese in California, Italians in the Northeast), and factory workers (Jewish and Italian immigrants in the garment industry). Chinese immigrants were excluded from the US (the Chinese Exclusion Act, 1882). Japanese immigrants were excluded from citizenship (the Naturalization Act of 1790 restricted naturalization to "free white persons"). The "Gentlemen's Agreement" (1907) limited Japanese immigration.

**Continuity – The Struggle of Labor Unions (KC-6.1.II.C, KC-6.1.II.C, KC-6.1.II.C):**
- **Employer resistance** : Employers used "yellow-dog contracts" (workers had to agree not to join a union), "blacklists" (lists of union members circulated to employers, who would not hire them), "lockouts" (closing the factory to starve the workers into submission), and "strikebreakers" (hiring replacement workers, often immigrants or African Americans, to break strikes). Employers also used private police (the Pinkerton National Detective Agency) and local police to attack strikers.
- **Court injunctions** : Courts issued injunctions (court orders) prohibiting strikes, boycotts, and picketing. Workers who violated injunctions were arrested and fined (or jailed). The Sherman Antitrust Act (1890) was used against unions (the courts ruled that unions were "combinations in restraint of trade" – even though the act was intended for monopolies).
- **Violence** : The Homestead strike (1892), the Pullman strike (1894), the Coeur d'Alene mining strike (1892), the Cripple Creek mining strike (1894) – all involved violence, deaths, and the use of state militia or federal troops (to protect strikebreakers, not strikers).
- **Union membership** : Despite the obstacles, union membership grew: 300,000 (1880), 1 million (1890), 2 million (1900). The American Federation of Labor (AFL) was the most successful union (focusing on skilled workers, "bread and butter" issues, and collective bargaining). The Industrial Workers of the World (IWW, or "Wobblies," founded 1905) was a radical union (open to all workers, regardless of skill, race, or gender; advocated socialism, syndicalism, and direct action). The Socialist Party (Eugene Debs, founded 1901) won 900,000 votes for president (1912).

## LO 6.F: Explain how cultural and economic factors affected migration patterns over time.

**Contextualization:** Between 1870 and 1900, the US experienced massive internal and international migration. Millions of Americans moved from farms to cities (rural-to-urban migration). Millions of Europeans moved to the US (transatlantic migration). African Americans began moving from the South to the North (the "Great Migration," though it peaked during World War I). Chinese and Japanese immigrants moved to the West Coast (to work on railroads, farms, and in canneries). Mexicans moved north to work on railroads and farms (the "Mexican North").

**Thesis:** Migration patterns from 1870 to 1900 were shaped by **economic factors** (industrialization created jobs in cities; the Homestead Act offered free land in the West; the destruction of the bison and the Indian Wars opened Native lands to white settlement; the decline of cotton prices and the crop lien system pushed Black farmers off the land) and **cultural factors** (immigrants came to the US to escape poverty, religious persecution, and political oppression; they also came to join family members (chain migration) and to preserve their ethnic identity in ethnic neighborhoods).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Economic Factors – Industrialization and Urbanization (KC-6.2.I.A):**
- Cities offered jobs in factories, mills, mines, slaughterhouses, canneries, railroads, and construction. Wages were higher in cities than in rural areas (a factory worker in Chicago earned $500/year; a farm laborer earned $200-300/year plus room and board).
- Rural-to-urban migration: Between 1870 and 1900, the proportion of Americans living in cities (2,500+ population) grew from 25% to 40%. New York, Chicago, Philadelphia, St. Louis, Boston, and Baltimore grew rapidly.
- "Push" factors in rural areas: Falling farm prices (caused by overproduction, foreign competition, and deflation), the crop lien system (trapping farmers in debt), soil exhaustion (tobacco and cotton depleted the soil), and (for Black farmers) racial violence and discrimination.

**Economic Factors – The Homestead Act and Western Expansion (KC-6.2.II.A):**
- The Homestead Act (1862) offered 160 acres of free land to anyone who would live on it for 5 years and improve it (build a house, plant crops). This attracted immigrants (especially from Scandinavia, Germany, and Ireland) and Americans from the East.
- Railroad land grants: The railroads sold land (2-5 dollars per acre) to settlers. The railroads also advertised in Europe (in German, Swedish, Norwegian, Danish, French, and Italian) to attract immigrants who would buy their land and ship their crops on their railroads.
- The destruction of the bison and the Indian Wars (see LO 6.3) opened the Great Plains to white settlement. The "Indian Territory" (Oklahoma) was opened to white settlement in the Land Run of 1889.

**Economic Factors – The "Great Migration" of African Americans (KC-6.2.II.C):**
- The "Great Migration" (1910-1920, 1920-1930, 1940-1960) was the movement of 6 million African Americans from the rural South to the urban North, Midwest, and West. It began slowly in the 1890s.
- Push factors: Falling cotton prices (the "boll weevil" destroyed cotton crops in the 1890s-1900s), the crop lien system (trapping Black farmers in debt), Jim Crow segregation (separate and unequal), disenfranchisement (loss of the vote), racial violence (lynching, the KKK), and the failure of Reconstruction (the Compromise of 1877).
- Pull factors: Jobs in Northern factories (steel mills, auto plants, meatpacking plants, railroads, shipyards), higher wages (Northern factory workers earned $2-3 per day; Southern farm laborers earned $0.50-1.00 per day), and (relative) freedom (the North had no Jim Crow laws, though it did have de facto segregation in housing and employment).

**Economic Factors – Asian Immigration (KC-6.2.I.A):**
- Chinese immigrants: During the California Gold Rush (1849-1855), 20,000 Chinese immigrants arrived. In the 1860s, the Central Pacific Railroad hired 10,000 Chinese workers (they were paid $30/month vs. $45/month for white workers; they lived in camps and were given the most dangerous jobs – blasting tunnels through the Sierra Nevada mountains). After the railroad was completed (1869), Chinese immigrants worked as farmers, laundry workers, restaurant workers, fishermen, and cannery workers.
- Japanese immigrants: The Japanese government allowed emigration to Hawaii (1885) and the US mainland (1890). Japanese immigrants worked as farm laborers (in California, Oregon, Washington, and Hawaii), railroad workers, and cannery workers (Alaska). They were subject to racial discrimination (the San Francisco School Board ordered Japanese children to attend segregated "Chinese" schools in 1906; President Theodore Roosevelt intervened, but the "Gentlemen's Agreement" of 1907 limited Japanese immigration).
- Filipino immigration: The Philippines was a US colony (1898-1946). Filipinos were "nationals" (not citizens) and could travel freely to the US. They worked as farm laborers (Hawaii, California, Washington), domestic servants, and cannery workers (Alaska). The *Tydings-McDuffie Act* (1934) limited Filipino immigration to 50 persons per year (after this period).

**Economic Factors – Mexican Immigration (KC-6.2.II.D):**
- Mexican immigrants were "pulled" by jobs in the US: railroads (the Santa Fe, Southern Pacific, Union Pacific, and other railroads hired Mexicans to lay tracks and maintain roads), agriculture (the "Mexican North" – Texas, New Mexico, Arizona, California – was a center of cotton, citrus, and vegetable farming), mining (copper in Arizona, coal in New Mexico), and (after 1910) the Mexican Revolution (Refugees fled the violence).
- Mexican immigrants were "pushed" by poverty, landlessness, and political instability in Mexico. The Porfiriato (the dictatorship of Porfirio Díaz, 1876-1911) dispossessed Mexican peasants (the *campesinos*) and sold their land to wealthy landowners (the *hacendados*). The Mexican Revolution (1910-1920) caused 1 million deaths and drove 500,000-1 million Mexicans to the US.
- Mexican immigrants faced discrimination, low wages, and racial violence (the "Zoot Suit Riots" in Los Angeles, 1943; the "sleepy lagoon" incident in Texas, 1948). They were often deported (the "Mexican Repatriation" of 1929-1936 deported 1 million Mexicans and Mexican Americans – some of whom were US citizens – during the Great Depression).

**Cultural Factors – Chain Migration (KC-6.2.I.B):**
- Immigrants did not move blindly; they followed family members, friends, and neighbors who had already emigrated. A "chain" of migration was established: one family member immigrated, saved money, sent for the next, who sent for the next, etc. This created ethnic neighborhoods (Little Italy, Chinatown, Little Germany, Greektown, Polonia). These neighborhoods preserved the language, religion, customs, food, music, and social networks of the home country.

**Cultural Factors – Religious and Political Persecution (KC-6.2.I.A):**
- Jews: Russian Jews fled pogroms (state-sponsored massacres) in the Russian Empire (1881-1882, 1903-1906, 1919-1921). They settled in New York City (the Lower East Side), Chicago, Philadelphia, and Boston. Many worked in the garment industry (the "sweatshop") and were active in labor unions (the International Ladies' Garment Workers' Union, the Amalgamated Clothing Workers of America).
- Poles: Poles (Polish Catholics) fled economic hardship (the Russian Empire and German Empire controlled Poland; they imposed high taxes and conscription). They settled in industrial cities (Chicago, Buffalo, Detroit, Milwaukee, Pittsburgh) and worked in steel mills, slaughterhouses, and coal mines.
- Irish: The Irish had fled the Great Famine (1845-1852). By 1870, Irish immigration had slowed, but the Irish continued to emigrate for economic reasons (the Irish economy was still depressed). They settled in cities (Boston, New York, Chicago, San Francisco) and worked as laborers, domestic servants, police officers, firemen, and politicians.
- Germans: German immigration peaked in the 1880s. Germans fled economic hardship (crop failures, rising taxes) and political unrest (the failed revolutions of 1848). They settled in the Midwest (Wisconsin, Minnesota, Ohio, Illinois, Missouri, Texas) and in cities (Cincinnati, Milwaukee, St. Louis, Chicago). They built German-language churches (Lutheran, Catholic), schools, newspapers, and social clubs (Turnvereine, Liederkranz, Männerchor).

## LO 6.G: Explain the various responses to immigration in the period over time.

**Contextualization:** The influx of millions of immigrants (mostly from Southern and Eastern Europe – Italy, Poland, Russia, Austria-Hungary, Greece, Turkey – after 1890) produced a nativist backlash. Nativists (native-born Protestants) feared that immigrants (Catholic, Jewish, Orthodox) would corrupt American democracy, take jobs from native-born workers, and bring "dangerous" ideas (socialism, anarchism, communism). The response to immigration included: the Chinese Exclusion Act (1882), the Immigration Restriction League (1894), the passage of literacy tests (1896, vetoed by President Cleveland; passed over Wilson's veto in 1917), the "Gentlemen's Agreement" with Japan (1907), and the Johnson-Reed Immigration Act (1924, after this period).

**Thesis:** Responses to immigration from 1865 to 1900 included **assimilation** (the "melting pot" ideal – immigrants would adopt American customs, language, and values; public schools taught "Americanization"; the "settlement house" movement (Jane Addams, Hull House) helped immigrants adapt), **nativism** (the Know-Nothing Party revived as the American Party (1886); the Immigration Restriction League (1894) demanded a literacy test; the Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) banned Chinese immigration; the "Gentlemen's Agreement" (1907) limited Japanese immigration), and **Social Darwinism** (scientific racism – the theory that Anglo-Saxons were the "fittest" race; immigrants from Southern and Eastern Europe were "inferior").

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Response 1 – Assimilation and "Americanization" (KC-6.2.I.C, KC-6.3.I.A):**
- The "melting pot" (the phrase was coined by Israel Zangwill, 1908) was the ideal: immigrants would shed their old cultures and become "Americans." Public schools (the "common school") taught English, American history, and "American values." Teachers punished children for speaking their native languages (German, Polish, Italian, Yiddish) in school.
- **The settlement house movement** : Settlement houses (Hull House in Chicago, 1889, founded by Jane Addams and Ellen Gates Starr) were social service centers in immigrant neighborhoods. They offered English classes, childcare, kindergarten, cooking classes, sewing classes, art classes, music classes, theater, lectures, and social events. They also provided legal aid, medical care, and employment assistance. Settlement workers helped immigrants adapt to American life while respecting their cultures. Jane Addams was a leader of the Progressive movement and a peace activist (won the Nobel Peace Prize, 1931).
- **"Americanization" programs** : Corporations (Ford Motor Company) offered English classes, citizenship classes, and "Americanization" classes (how to act like an American – how to use a fork, how to shake hands, how to behave in public). Ford's "Sociological Department" (1914-1921) investigated workers' home lives and tried to "Americanize" them.

**Response 2 – Nativism and Restriction (KC-6.2.I.C):**
- **The Chinese Exclusion Act (1882)** : The first federal law to restrict immigration based on race. It prohibited the immigration of Chinese laborers (skilled and unskilled) for 10 years. It also denied citizenship to Chinese immigrants already in the US (the Naturalization Act of 1790 restricted naturalization to "free white persons"). The act was renewed in 1892 and made permanent in 1902. It was not repealed until 1943 (during World War II, China was an ally).
- **The Immigration Restriction League (1894)** : Founded by three Harvard graduates (Prescott F. Hall, Charles Warren, Robert DeCourcy Ward). The League demanded a literacy test (immigrants must be able to read and write in their own language). The literacy test passed Congress in 1896 (President Grover Cleveland vetoed it). It passed again in 1913 (President Taft vetoed it). It passed again in 1915 (President Wilson vetoed it). It finally passed over Wilson's veto in 1917 (as part of the Immigration Act of 1917, which also created an "Asiatic Barred Zone").
- **The "Gentlemen's Agreement" (1907)** : President Theodore Roosevelt agreed to restrict Japanese immigration (to California) in exchange for Japan's agreement to stop issuing passports to laborers. The agreement also provided for the desegregation of San Francisco schools (which had ordered Japanese children to attend "Chinese" schools). The "Gentlemen's Agreement" was in effect until 1924 (when the Johnson-Reed Act banned all Asian immigration).
- **The Johnson-Reed Immigration Act (1924)** : After this period (but a continuation of the restriction movement). The law established quotas (2% of each nationality's population in the US in 1890) – favoring Northern and Western Europeans (Britain, Ireland, Germany, Scandinavia) and severely limiting Southern and Eastern Europeans (Italians, Poles, Russians, Greeks, Turks). It also banned Asian immigration entirely.

**Response 3 – Social Darwinism and Scientific Racism (KC-6.3.I.A):**
- **Herbert Spencer (1820-1903)** : British philosopher who coined the phrase "survival of the fittest" (after reading Darwin's *Origin of Species*). Spencer argued that human society evolved through competition; the "fittest" (richest, most successful) had earned their place; the "unfit" (poor, sick, disabled) deserved their poverty. Critics called this "Social Darwinism." Spencer's ideas were popular among American industrialists (Carnegie, Rockefeller, Morgan) and intellectuals.
- **William Graham Sumner (1840-1910)** : Yale sociologist, the leading American Social Darwinist. He argued that government should not interfere with the "natural" process of competition (laissez-faire). He opposed social reform (welfare, regulation, labor laws) as misdirected charity that would reward the "unfit."
- **Eugenics** : The "science" of improving the human race by selective breeding (encouraging the "fit" to reproduce, preventing the "unfit" from reproducing). Eugenics was popular among Progressives (including Theodore Roosevelt, Margaret Sanger, and many academics). It led to forced sterilization laws (Indiana 1907, California 1909, 30 states by 1930). It also influenced immigration restriction (the "feebleminded," "criminal," "insane," "epileptic," "alcoholic," "homeless," "dependent" were to be excluded).
- **"Race science"** : "Scientists" measured skulls (phrenology), facial features, and body shapes to prove that "Nordics" (Anglo-Saxons, Germans, Scandinavians) were superior to "Alpines" (French, Swiss, Czechs) and "Mediterraneans" (Italians, Greeks, Spaniards). They also "proved" the inferiority of "Negroes," "Mongoloids" (Chinese, Japanese), and "Jews." These "scientific" theories justified discrimination and segregation.

**Response 4 – Labor Opposition to Immigration (KC-6.2.I.C):**
- **Labor unions** opposed immigration because immigrants were willing to work for lower wages (undercutting union wages) and were often used as strikebreakers (the Chinese were used to break the 1877 railroad strike; the Japanese were used to break the 1894 Pullman strike; the Mexicans were used to break the 1919 steel strike).
- The **American Federation of Labor (AFL)** supported immigration restriction (the AFL endorsed the literacy test). The **Knights of Labor** opposed Chinese immigration (the Knights supported the Chinese Exclusion Act).
- The **Workingmen's Party of California** (led by Denis Kearney) was a racist, anti-Chinese labor party. Its slogan: "The Chinese must go!" It helped pass the Chinese Exclusion Act.

**Response 5 – Immigrant Self-Defense and Ethnic Nationalism (KC-6.2.I.C):**
- Immigrants did not passively accept nativist attacks. They formed ethnic self-defense societies (the Irish "Ancient Order of Hibernians," the German "Turnvereine," the Polish "Polish National Alliance," the Italian "Order of the Sons of Italy"). These societies provided mutual aid (sickness insurance, death benefits), preserved ethnic culture, and lobbied for immigrant rights.
- **Ethnic newspapers** : Germans published the *New Yorker Staats-Zeitung*, the *Cincinnati Volksblatt*, the *Milwaukee Herold*. Jews published the *Forverts* (Jewish Daily Forward), the *Yiddishes Tageblatt*. Poles published the *Dziennik Związkowy* (Polish Daily News). These newspapers kept immigrants informed about events in the old country and in the US. They also helped immigrants find jobs, housing, and legal aid.
- **Ethnic churches** : Immigrants built their own churches (St. Patrick's Cathedral in New York, Irish; St. Stanislaus Kostka in Chicago, Polish; St. Anthony's in Brooklyn, Italian). The Catholic Church (the largest denomination in the US by 1900) was divided into ethnic parishes (each ethnic group had its own parish, priest, and school). The "Americanist" controversy (1890s) debated whether the Church should assimilate to American culture or preserve ethnic identities.

## LO 6.H: Explain the causes of increased economic opportunity and its effects on society.

**Contextualization:** The Gilded Age saw increased economic opportunity for some Americans: the middle class expanded, consumer goods became more affordable, and social mobility (at least for white men) seemed possible (the "American Dream"). But these opportunities were unevenly distributed: women, African Americans, immigrants, and the poor had fewer opportunities. The "rags to riches" story (Horatio Alger) was more myth than reality (most millionaires inherited their wealth or had wealthy relatives).

**Thesis:** Increased economic opportunity from 1865 to 1898 was caused by **the rise of industrial capitalism** (which created new jobs in factories, offices, and management), **the expansion of public education** (which allowed poor children to acquire skills and credentials), **the growth of cities** (which offered more diverse job opportunities than rural areas), and **philanthropy** (the "Gospel of Wealth" – Carnegie, Rockefeller, and other industrialists donated money to build libraries, universities, hospitals, and museums); its effects included **the growth of a middle class** (managers, clerical workers, professionals), **increased social mobility** (some workers rose from poverty to wealth – though most did not), **the expansion of consumer culture** (people could buy goods that had once been luxuries), **the rise of "conspicuous consumption"** (Thorstein Veblen, 1899 – the wealthy spent money to display their status), and **the persistence of poverty** (the "other half" – Jacob Riis – lived in tenements).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Cause 1 – The Rise of Industrial Capitalism (KC-6.1.I.B.ii):**
- Industrial capitalism created new jobs in:
  - **Management** : factory superintendents, plant managers, department heads, foremen
  - **Clerical work** : bookkeepers, accountants, stenographers, typists, telegraph operators, telephone operators, cashiers, clerks, secretaries
  - **Sales** : traveling salesmen (the "drummer"), store clerks, door-to-door salesmen, advertising agents
  - **Professional services** : engineers (mechanical, civil, electrical, chemical), architects, lawyers (corporate law, patent law, immigration law), doctors (public health, surgery, obstetrics), dentists, pharmacists
  - **Skilled trades** : machinists, electricians, plumbers, carpenters, bricklayers, stonemasons, printers, bookbinders, tailors, bakers, butchers, brewers
- These jobs paid better than unskilled labor (factory work, farm labor, domestic service). A manager earned $1,000-2,000 per year; a clerk earned $500-1,000 per year; a skilled tradesman earned $700-1,500 per year.

**Cause 2 – The Expansion of Public Education (KC-6.2.I.E):**
- By 1900, every state had compulsory education laws (requiring children to attend school until age 14-16). The average school year was 150 days. The literacy rate (for whites) was 90%.
- **High schools** : The number of public high schools grew from 100 (1865) to 6,000 (1900). High school graduates could get white-collar jobs (clerks, salesmen, teachers, bookkeepers). A high school diploma was a ticket to the middle class.
- **Colleges and universities** : The Morrill Land-Grant Act (1862) and the rise of research universities (Johns Hopkins, Chicago, Stanford) made higher education more accessible (to white men). College graduates earned more than high school graduates; they could become professionals (lawyers, doctors, engineers, scientists, college professors, high school teachers).
- **Vocational education** : The "manual training" movement (1870s-1890s) taught industrial skills (woodworking, metalworking, drafting, printing) to prepare students for skilled trades. The "Tuskegee Institute" (Booker T. Washington, 1881) taught industrial education to African Americans (carpentry, brick masonry, blacksmithing, printing, farming, domestic service).

**Cause 3 – The Growth of Cities (KC-6.2.I.A):**
- Cities offered more job opportunities than rural areas. A rural farm laborer earned $200-300/year (plus room and board). A city worker (factory, railroad, construction) earned $500-600/year. A city clerk earned $600-1,000/year.
- Cities also offered cultural opportunities (theaters, concerts, museums, libraries, parks, sports stadiums) and social opportunities (clubs, lodges, churches, saloons, dance halls). Cities were exciting and dangerous (crime, disease, fire).

**Cause 4 – Philanthropy (the "Gospel of Wealth") (KC-6.3.I.B):**
- Andrew Carnegie: "The Gospel of Wealth" (1889) – "The man who dies thus rich dies disgraced." Carnegie gave away $350 million (in today's dollars, $5 billion). He built 2,500 public libraries (the "Carnegie libraries"), Carnegie Hall (New York), Carnegie Mellon University (Pittsburgh), the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace, the Carnegie Institution for Science, and the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching.
- John D. Rockefeller: Gave away $500 million (in today's dollars, $10 billion). He founded the University of Chicago (1890), Rockefeller University (1901), the Rockefeller Foundation (1913), the General Education Board (1902), and the Laura Spelman Rockefeller Memorial (1918).
- Leland Stanford: Founded Stanford University (1885) in memory of his son (who died of typhoid fever at age 15). Stanford's motto: "The wind of freedom blows." Stanford became a leading research university (computer science, engineering, business, law, medicine).
- Johns Hopkins (1795-1873): Left $7 million to found Johns Hopkins University (1876) and Johns Hopkins Hospital (1889). Hopkins was the first American university to focus on graduate education and research (modeled on German universities). It also had the first medical school to admit women (1893) and to require a college degree for admission.

**Effect 1 – The Growth of a Middle Class (KC-6.2.I.E):**
- The middle class (managers, clerks, professionals, small business owners) grew from 15% of the workforce (1870) to 30% (1900). The "white collar" worker (paid by salary, not wages) was a new social category.
- Middle-class families lived in "streetcar suburbs" (single-family homes with lawns). They employed domestic servants (maids, cooks, nannies) – usually Irish or German immigrant women. The "Victorian" home was the middle-class ideal: large, ornate, with a parlor, dining room, kitchen, and bedrooms upstairs.
- Middle-class values: Hard work, thrift, education, self-improvement, "respectability." The "cult of domesticity" (separate spheres) – women ruled the home (the "angel of the hearth"); men ruled the public sphere (work, politics).

**Effect 2 – Increased Social Mobility (KC-6.2.I.E):**
- Some workers rose from poverty to wealth (the "rags to riches" story). Horatio Alger (1832-1899) wrote 100+ novels (the "Ragged Dick" series) about poor boys who became rich through hard work, honesty, and luck. Alger's books were popular (sales in the millions) and shaped the "American Dream" myth.
- In reality, social mobility was limited. Most millionaires inherited their wealth (or had wealthy relatives). The "self-made man" was an exception. The "American Dream" was easier to achieve for white men than for women, African Americans, immigrants, and the poor.
- However, social mobility did exist. Andrew Carnegie (born poor in Scotland, became the richest man in America) was the exception, but he was not alone (Rockefeller, Morgan, Stanford, Duke, Armour, Swift, Vanderbilt – all rose from modest origins).

**Effect 3 – The Expansion of Consumer Culture (KC-6.2.I.E):**
- Mass production (the "American System of Manufacturing") lowered the cost of goods. Consumer goods that had once been luxuries became affordable for the middle class (and some working-class families).
- **Department stores** : Macy's (New York), Wanamaker's (Philadelphia), Marshall Field's (Chicago), Filene's (Boston), The Emporium (San Francisco). Department stores offered a wide variety of goods (clothing, furniture, housewares, toys, books, jewelry, cosmetics). They had fixed prices (no bargaining), money-back guarantees, and sales (the "bargain counter"). They also employed women as salesclerks (a new job for women).
- **Catalog sales** : Montgomery Ward (1872) and Sears, Roebuck (1893) mailed catalogs to rural customers. Farmers could order anything (clothes, shoes, furniture, tools, seeds, medicines, books, musical instruments, sewing machines). The "wish book" (the Sears catalog) was popular.
- **Advertising** : Advertising agencies (N.W. Ayer & Son, 1869) created brand names (Coca-Cola, Quaker Oats, Ivory Soap, Pillsbury, Heinz, Campbell's, Kellogg's, Post). Advertising created demand for products (people bought things they did not need). The "slogan" (a catchy phrase) was invented (e.g., "Ivory Soap – 99 44/100% pure").

**Effect 4 – The Rise of "Conspicuous Consumption" (KC-6.2.I.E):**
- Thorstein Veblen (1857-1929), a Norwegian-American sociologist, wrote *The Theory of the Leisure Class* (1899). He coined the term "conspicuous consumption" – the practice of buying expensive goods to display one's wealth and social status.
- The "robber barons" (Carnegie, Rockefeller, Morgan, Vanderbilt, Astor) built mansions (Fifth Avenue in New York, Newport Rhode Island, the "Gold Coast" of Chicago). They threw lavish parties (the "Vanderbilt Ball" of 1883 cost $250,000). They bought yachts, private railroad cars, racing stables, and European art. Their wives wore expensive gowns, jewels, and furs. Their daughters had "coming out" balls (debutante balls) to enter high society.
- The middle class also engaged in conspicuous consumption (on a smaller scale). They bought pianos (to show they were cultured), parlor furniture (to show they were respectable), and fashionable clothing (to show they were "stylish").

**Effect 5 – The Persistence of Poverty (KC-6.2.I.E):**
- Jacob Riis (1849-1914), a Danish-American journalist, photographed the slums of New York City. His book *How the Other Half Lives* (1890) shocked middle-class readers. He showed tenements (crowded, dark, unsanitary, windowless rooms), child labor (newsboys, bootblacks, street vendors), and crime (gangs, prostitution, gambling).
- The "other half" lived on the edge of survival. They had little food, no heat, no running water, no toilet, no bath. They died young (average life expectancy in the tenements was 35-40 years; in the middle class, 50-60 years).
- Reformers (Jane Addams, Jacob Riis, Walter Rauschenbusch, Washington Gladden) tried to help the poor through settlement houses, charity, and the Social Gospel movement (the belief that Christians should work to improve society, not just save souls).

## LO 6.I: Explain how different reform movements responded to the rise of industrial capitalism in the Gilded Age.

**Contextualization:** The Gilded Age was an era of reform as well as corruption. Reformers responded to the excesses of industrial capitalism (monopolies, child labor, unsafe working conditions, slums, political corruption) by advocating for socialism, anarchism, the Social Gospel, the "single tax" (Henry George), and government regulation.

**Thesis:** Reform movements in the Gilded Age responded to the rise of industrial capitalism by proposing **alternative visions for the economy and society**: **socialism** (Eugene Debs, the Socialist Party – public ownership of the means of production), **anarchism** (Emma Goldman, Alexander Berkman – the abolition of government), **the Social Gospel** (Washington Gladden, Walter Rauschenbusch – applying Christian ethics to social problems), **the "single tax"** (Henry George – taxing land value to reduce inequality), and **Populism** (the People's Party – government ownership of railroads, free silver, a progressive income tax); these movements achieved some reforms (the Interstate Commerce Act 1887, the Sherman Antitrust Act 1890, state child labor laws, the 8-hour day for federal workers (1868), but most of their proposals were not enacted until the Progressive Era (1900-1920) or the New Deal (1933-1938).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Reform 1 – Socialism (KC-6.3.I.C):**
- **Socialism** : The belief that the means of production (factories, mines, railroads, banks) should be owned by the public (the workers, the community, the state). Socialism was a response to the exploitation of labor by capital (the "robber barons").
- **Karl Marx (1818-1883)** : German philosopher, author of *The Communist Manifesto* (1848, with Friedrich Engels) and *Das Kapital* (1867, 1885, 1894). Marx argued that capitalism was inherently exploitative (workers created value; owners (capitalists) appropriated that value as profit). Capitalism would eventually collapse, replaced by socialism and then communism (a classless, stateless society).
- **Eugene V. Debs (1855-1926)** : The most famous American socialist. He was the leader of the American Railway Union (ARU) and the Pullman Strike (1894). After his prison sentence, he converted to socialism. He ran for president 5 times as the Socialist Party candidate (1900, 1904, 1908, 1912, 1920). In 1912, he won 6% of the popular vote (900,000 votes). In 1920, he ran from prison (he was sentenced to 10 years for speaking against World War I). President Warren G. Harding commuted his sentence (1921). Debs's campaign slogan: "I am for socialism because I am for humanity."
- **The Socialist Party (founded 1901)** : The Socialist Party had a million members (1912), 300 newspapers, 1,000 elected officials (mayors, city councilmen, state legislators, one congressman – Victor Berger of Wisconsin). Socialist mayors were elected in Milwaukee (1910-1960), Schenectady, New York (1912-1913), and other cities. The Socialist Party declined after World War I (the Red Scare of 1919-1920, the Palmer Raids, the deportation of foreign-born radicals).

**Reform 2 – Anarchism (KC-6.3.I.C):**
- **Anarchism** : The belief that government is unnecessary and harmful; society should be organized by voluntary cooperation, not coercion.
- **Emma Goldman (1869-1940)** : The most famous American anarchist. She was born in Lithuania (Russian Empire), emigrated to the US (1885). She worked as a seamstress, became a radical, and was active in the anarchist movement. She advocated for free speech, birth control, women's rights (feminism), sexual freedom, and labor unions. She was imprisoned (1893, 1901, 1917). She was deported to Russia (1919) during the "Palmer Raids." She lived in Russia (disappointed by the Bolshevik regime), then in England, Canada, and France. She wrote *Living My Life* (1931).
- **Alexander Berkman (1870-1936)** : Goldman's lover and comrade. He attempted to assassinate Henry Clay Frick (Carnegie's partner) during the Homestead strike (1892). He was sentenced to 22 years in prison (released 1906). He wrote *Prison Memoirs of an Anarchist* (1912). He was deported in 1919 (died in France).
- **The Haymarket Riot (1886)** : See LO 6.A. The Haymarket Riot discredited anarchism in the US (though the bomb thrower was never identified). Anarchism remained a small, fringe movement.

**Reform 3 – The Social Gospel (KC-6.3.I.C):**
- **The Social Gospel** : A Protestant Christian movement that applied Christian ethics to social problems (poverty, inequality, child labor, slums, alcoholism, prostitution). The Social Gospel rejected the "individualistic" gospel (saving souls one by one) in favor of a "social" gospel (transforming society to reflect the Kingdom of God).
- **Walter Rauschenbusch (1861-1918)** : The leading theologian of the Social Gospel. He was a Baptist pastor in New York City (Hell's Kitchen). He wrote *Christianity and the Social Crisis* (1907), *Prayers of the Social Awakening* (1910), and *A Theology for the Social Gospel* (1917). He advocated for labor unions, the 8-hour day, the abolition of child labor, and the "single tax" (Henry George). He argued that capitalism was incompatible with Christianity.
- **Washington Gladden (1836-1918)** : A Congregationalist minister in Columbus, Ohio. He was a leader of the Social Gospel. He supported labor unions, opposed the "robber barons," and advocated for public ownership of utilities (water, gas, electricity, streetcars). He wrote *Applied Christianity* (1886), *Who Wrote the Bible?* (1891), and *The New Idolatry* (1905).
- **The "Institutional Church"** : Social Gospel churches built gymnasiums, swimming pools, libraries, and social halls (not just sanctuaries). They offered kindergartens, daycare, English classes, cooking classes, sewing classes, and employment assistance. They also ran settlement houses (Jane Addams's Hull House was a Social Gospel institution).
- **The "Social Creed" of the Methodist Episcopal Church (1908)** : The first social creed of a major denomination. It called for the 8-hour day, a living wage, the abolition of child labor, workers' compensation, unemployment insurance, and health insurance.

**Reform 4 – The "Single Tax" (KC-6.3.I.C):**
- **Henry George (1839-1897)** : American political economist, author of *Progress and Poverty* (1879). George argued that poverty persists even as society progresses and wealth increases. The cause: land speculation (rich people held land out of use, waiting for its value to increase). The solution: a "single tax" on land value (not on buildings or improvements). The tax would capture the "unearned increment" (the increase in land value due to population growth and social progress). The tax would fund government, reduce inequality, and eliminate poverty.
- George's ideas were popular (3 million copies of *Progress and Poverty* sold). Single tax clubs ("Henry George Clubs") formed across the US and Europe. George ran for mayor of New York City (1886, 1897). In 1886, he came in second (behind Democrat Abram Hewitt, ahead of Republican Theodore Roosevelt). He died in 1897 (he was running for mayor again when he died). His ideas influenced the Progressive movement (the "income tax" and the "inheritance tax" were substitutes for the single tax).

**Reform 5 – Populism (the People's Party) (KC-6.1.III.C, KC-6.3.II.A):**
- The **People's Party (the Populists)** was a political party (1891-1908) that represented farmers (especially in the South and West), laborers, and small business owners. The Populists were a response to the power of railroads, banks, and monopolies.
- **The Omaha Platform (1892)** : The Populist Party's platform. It called for:
  - **Unlimited coinage of silver** (free silver) – to inflate the currency, raise farm prices, and ease debts
  - **A progressive income tax** (tax the rich, not the poor)
  - **Government ownership of railroads, telegraphs, and telephones**
  - **Direct election of US senators** (17th Amendment, 1913)
  - **The secret ballot** (the "Australian ballot") – to prevent voter intimidation
  - **Initiative and referendum** – citizens could propose laws (initiative) and vote on them (referendum)
  - **Recall** – citizens could remove elected officials from office
  - **The 8-hour day** for federal workers
  - **Restriction of immigration** (to protect American jobs)
- The **Election of 1896** : The Populist Party endorsed William Jennings Bryan (Democrat) for president. Bryan ran on a free silver platform (the "Cross of Gold" speech). He lost to William McKinley (Republican, gold standard). The Populist Party collapsed after 1896.
- **Legacy of Populism** : The Populists' ideas (progressive income tax, direct election of senators, secret ballot, initiative, referendum, recall) were adopted by the Progressive movement and became law (16th, 17th, 19th Amendments).

**Reform 6 – Government Regulation (KC-6.1.III.C, KC-6.3.II.A):**
- **The Interstate Commerce Act (1887)** : The first federal law to regulate railroads. It banned rebates (secret discounts to large shippers), pools (secret agreements to fix rates), and price discrimination (charging different rates to different customers). It created the **Interstate Commerce Commission (ICC)** , the first federal regulatory agency. The ICC could investigate railroads, hold hearings, and issue "cease and desist" orders. The ICC was weak (it had no power to set rates; railroads could ignore its orders and appeal to the courts).
- **The Sherman Antitrust Act (1890)** : The first federal law to prohibit monopolies. It declared illegal "every contract, combination in the form of trust or otherwise, or conspiracy, in restraint of trade or commerce among the several states." It also authorized the federal government to sue monopolies and dissolve them. The law was weak (it was used more often against labor unions than against monopolies). The Supreme Court weakened it further (U.S. v. E.C. Knight Co., 1895 – the Court ruled that manufacturing was not "interstate commerce," so the Sherman Act did not apply).
- **State child labor laws** : By 1900, every state had a law prohibiting child labor (but the laws were weak, poorly enforced, and often exempted agriculture, domestic service, and street trades). The National Child Labor Committee (1904) campaigned for a federal child labor law. The Keating-Owen Act (1916) banned the interstate shipment of goods made by child labor (the Supreme Court struck it down in 1918). The Fair Labor Standards Act (1938) established a federal minimum wage and banned child labor.

## LO 6.J: Explain continuities and changes in the role of the government in the U.S. economy.

**Contextualization:** Before the Civil War, the federal government's role in the economy was limited: it imposed tariffs, distributed public land, operated the postal service, and (after 1816) chartered a national bank. After the Civil War, the government's role expanded: it subsidized railroads (land grants, loans), imposed protective tariffs (to shield American industry from foreign competition), created a national banking system (1863, 1864), and (with the Interstate Commerce Act 1887, the Sherman Antitrust Act 1890) began to regulate business. But this expansion was contested. Many Americans (Liberals, Social Darwinists) believed in laissez-faire ("let do" – the government should not interfere with the economy). The Supreme Court often struck down state and federal regulations as unconstitutional (e.g., U.S. v. E.C. Knight Co., 1895).

**Thesis:** The role of the federal government in the US economy between 1865 and 1898 was characterized by both continuity and change: **continuity** (the government continued to promote economic development through tariffs, land grants, and subsidies; the government continued to follow laissez-faire principles, especially in labor disputes, where it often sided with employers); **change** (the government created the first regulatory agencies – the Interstate Commerce Commission (1887) to regulate railroads, the Sherman Antitrust Act (1890) to prosecute monopolies; the government also created a national banking system (1863, 1864), adopted the gold standard (1873, affirmed 1900), and (in the case of the US vs. the railroads) began to assert its authority to regulate interstate commerce).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Continuity – Laissez-Faire Ideology (KC-6.1.II.A):**
- Laissez-faire (French for "let do" – leave alone) was the dominant economic ideology of the Gilded Age. It held that the government should not interfere with the economy (no price controls, no wage controls, no regulation of working conditions, no safety standards, no antitrust laws, no labor laws). The economy should be left to the "natural" forces of supply and demand.
- **Herbert Spencer** (Social Darwinism) provided a "scientific" justification for laissez-faire. Spencer argued that government intervention would interfere with "survival of the fittest" and weaken the human race.
- **William Graham Sumner** (Yale sociologist) argued that government should do nothing to help the poor (charity, welfare, regulation). He wrote: "The millionaires are a product of natural selection... They get high wages and live in luxury, but the bargain is a good one for society." He opposed any "absurd attempt" to "make the world a world of equal comfort for all."
- **Supreme Court** : The Supreme Court struck down state and federal regulations as violations of the 14th Amendment (due process clause) or the Commerce Clause. Examples:
  - *Munn v. Illinois* (1877) : The Court upheld state regulation of grain elevators (a "public interest" exception to laissez-faire).
  - *Wabash, St. Louis & Pacific Railway Co. v. Illinois* (1886) : The Court struck down an Illinois law regulating railroad rates (only Congress could regulate interstate commerce).
  - *U.S. v. E.C. Knight Co.* (1895) : The Court struck down the Sherman Antitrust Act's application to the American Sugar Refining Company (manufacturing was not "interstate commerce").
  - *Pollock v. Farmers' Loan & Trust Co.* (1895) : The Court struck down the income tax (the 16th Amendment, 1913, reversed this decision).

**Continuity – Government Support for Business (KC-6.1.I.A, KC-6.1.I.A, KC-6.1.I.A, KC-6.1.I.A, KC-6.1.I.A, KC-6.1.I.A):**
- **Protective tariffs** : The Morrill Tariff (1861) and subsequent tariffs (1862, 1864, 1870, 1875, 1883, 1890, 1897) protected American manufacturers from foreign competition. Tariff rates averaged 40-50% on manufactured goods. The "infant industry" argument: infant industries needed protection until they could compete with established British industries. The McKinley Tariff (1890) raised rates to 50%. The Dingley Tariff (1897) raised rates to 57% – the highest in American history.
- **Land grants to railroads** : The Pacific Railway Acts (1862, 1864) gave railroad companies 175 million acres of public land (10-20 miles on each side of the track). The railroads also received federal loans (in bonds) to build the transcontinental railroad. The Union Pacific and Central Pacific were required to repay the loans (with interest) – most did not.
- **National banking system** : The National Banking Acts (1863, 1864) created a system of nationally chartered banks (subject to federal regulation) and a national currency (greenbacks). The national banking system stabilized the banking industry (reducing bank failures) and provided credit for industrial expansion.
- **The gold standard** : The Coinage Act of 1873 ("the Crime of '73") demonetized silver (ended the free coinage of silver). The US was on the gold standard (gold was the only monetary standard). The Gold Standard Act (1900) formally placed the US on the gold standard (18 years later). The gold standard kept the currency stable (low inflation, low deflation). It favored creditors (bankers, bondholders) over debtors (farmers, small business owners). Populists demanded "free silver" (bimetallism) to inflate the currency and ease debts.
- **Subsidies to shipping** : The US government subsidized steamship lines (the Pacific Mail Steamship Company, the American Line). The "ocean mail" contracts were worth millions of dollars.

These contracts were often awarded to political allies (corruption – the "Grant administration" was notorious for the "Credit Mobilier" scandal, the "Whiskey Ring," the "Indian Ring," the "Salary Grab").

## LO 6.K: Explain the similarities and differences between the political parties during the Gilded Age.

**Contextualization:** The Gilded Age (1870-1900) was a period of intense political competition between the Democratic and Republican parties. Presidential elections were extremely close (1876, 1880, 1884, 1888, 1892 – all decided by less than 2% of the popular vote). Voter turnout was extraordinarily high (80%+ of eligible voters). But despite the fierce competition, the two parties had few policy differences. Both parties supported the gold standard (until 1896, when the Democrats adopted free silver), protective tariffs (Republicans strongly supported; Democrats were divided), and laissez-faire (limited government regulation of the economy). Both parties were corrupt (the "spoils system" – rewarding political supporters with government jobs). Both parties ignored the demands of farmers (the Populist Party) and workers (labor unions).

**Thesis:** During the Gilded Age, the Democratic and Republican parties were **similar** in their support for laissez-faire capitalism, the gold standard (until 1896), high tariffs (Republicans strongly; Democrats weakly), and the spoils system (patronage); they were **different** in their regional bases of support (Republicans dominated the North and Midwest; Democrats dominated the South and West), their **ethnic and religious coalitions** (Republicans were WASPs – White Anglo-Saxon Protestants, and supported prohibition; Democrats were Catholics, Lutherans, and immigrants, and opposed prohibition), and their **tariff policies** (Republicans strongly supported protective tariffs; Democrats supported lower tariffs – "tariff for revenue only").

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Similarity 1 – Support for Laissez-Faire Capitalism (KC-6.3.II.A):**
- Both parties believed that the government should not interfere with the economy (no price controls, no wage controls, no regulation of working conditions, no safety standards, no antitrust enforcement). The "Robber Barons" (Carnegie, Rockefeller, Morgan, Vanderbilt, Duke, Swift, Armour) gave generously to both parties. They did not care which party won; they knew that neither party would regulate them.
- The **Interstate Commerce Act (1887)** and the **Sherman Antitrust Act (1890)** were passed by a Republican-controlled Congress and signed by a Democratic president (Cleveland). But both parties supported these laws as "moderate" reforms (to appease the public). The laws were weak and poorly enforced.

**Similarity 2 – Support for the Gold Standard (Until 1896) (KC-6.3.II.A):**
- The **Coinage Act of 1873** (the "Crime of '73") demonetized silver (ended the free coinage of silver). The US was on the gold standard (gold was the only monetary standard). Both parties supported the gold standard (until 1896).
- **The "Crime of '73"** : Silver miners (in Nevada, Colorado, Montana) and farmers (who wanted inflation) called the Act a "crime" because it favored bankers (creditors) over debtors.
- **The Bland-Allison Act (1878)** and the **Sherman Silver Purchase Act (1890)** required the Treasury to purchase silver (but not to coin it freely). Silver was purchased, but silver certificates (paper money) could be redeemed for gold or silver. The gold standard remained.
- **The Panic of 1893** : The depression caused a run on the gold reserves. President Grover Cleveland (Democrat) repealed the Sherman Silver Purchase Act (1893) to protect the gold standard. He borrowed $50 million from J.P. Morgan (a private banker) to shore up the gold reserves.
- **The Election of 1896** : The Democrats (and Populists) nominated William Jennings Bryan (Nebraska) on a free silver platform ("You shall not crucify mankind upon a cross of gold"). The Republicans nominated William McKinley (Ohio) on a gold standard platform (with a promise of high tariffs). McKinley won. The Gold Standard Act (1900) formally placed the US on the gold standard.

**Similarity 3 – The Spoils System (Patronage) (KC-6.3.II.A):**
- The "spoils system" (the practice of rewarding political supporters with government jobs) was used by both parties. A newly elected president would fire thousands of postmasters, customs officers, tax collectors, and other federal employees and replace them with his political supporters.
- **Corruption** : The spoils system led to corruption. Officeholders were expected to kick back a percentage of their salary to the party. They also sold government contracts to the highest bidder. The **Credit Mobilier scandal** (1872) involved railroad construction and bribes to Congress. The **Whiskey Ring scandal** (1875) involved distillers bribing federal tax agents. The **Indian Ring scandal** (1875) involved Army officers and Indian agents stealing supplies meant for Native Americans (the "Indian Bureau").
- **Civil Service Reform** : The **Pendleton Act (1883)** established a Civil Service Commission and required that some federal jobs be filled by competitive examination (merit, not patronage). The Act was passed after President James Garfield was assassinated by a disappointed office-seeker (Charles Guiteau). The Act initially covered 10% of federal jobs (the "classified service"). It was expanded by later presidents (Theodore Roosevelt, Woodrow Wilson, Franklin D. Roosevelt).
- **The "Stalwarts" vs. the "Half-Breeds"** : The Republican Party was divided into two factions: the Stalwarts (led by Roscoe Conkling of New York) supported the spoils system; the Half-Breeds (led by James G. Blaine of Maine) supported civil service reform. The "Mugwumps" (Republicans who supported Democrat Grover Cleveland in 1884) were reformers.

**Similarity 4 – High Voter Turnout and Political Machines (KC-6.3.II.A):**
- Voter turnout was extraordinarily high (80%+ of eligible voters in presidential elections). The parties mobilized voters through parades, rallies, barbecues, and free whiskey.
- **Political machines** : Urban political machines (Tammany Hall in New York City, the "Bosses" – William M. Tweed, "Honest John" Kelly, Richard Croker, Charles Murphy) controlled elections through patronage, bribes, and fraud. They provided jobs, housing, food, coal, and legal assistance to immigrants (in exchange for votes). They also engaged in graft (kickbacks, bribes, and fraudulent contracts). Tweed stole $200 million from New York City (1865-1871). He was exposed by the *New York Times* and cartoonist Thomas Nast (Harper's Weekly). Tweed was convicted and died in prison (1878).

**Difference 1 – Regional Bases of Support (KC-6.3.II.A):**
- **Republicans** : The "Party of Lincoln." They dominated the North, the Midwest, and the West (except for the "solid South"). They were the party of business, industry, and finance. They supported protective tariffs, the gold standard, and internal improvements (federal funding for roads, canals, harbors, and railroads). Their base: Yankees (New Englanders who moved west), African Americans (in the North), and immigrants (German Lutherans, Scandinavians).
- **Democrats** : The "Party of Jackson." They dominated the South (the "solid South") and the West (the "Silver Democrats" – Nevada, Colorado, Montana). They were the party of farmers, laborers, and immigrants. They supported lower tariffs ("tariff for revenue only"), free silver (after 1896), and states' rights. Their base: white Southerners (who voted Democratic to prevent civil rights for African Americans), Irish Catholics, German Catholics, Polish Catholics, Italian Catholics, and other immigrants.

**Difference 2 – Ethnic and Religious Coalitions (KC-6.3.II.A):**
- **Republicans** : The Republican Party was the party of "Pietistic" Protestants (Methodists, Baptists, Congregationalists, Presbyterians, and other evangelicals). These groups supported **prohibition** (banning alcohol) and **moral reform** (Sunday blue laws, anti-gambling laws, anti-prostitution laws). They also supported **public schools** (the "common school") and **Americanization** (immigrants should assimilate to Anglo-Protestant culture).
- **Democrats** : The Democratic Party was the party of "Liturgical" Protestants (Episcopalians, Lutherans) and Catholics (Irish, German, Polish, Italian, French Canadian). These groups opposed prohibition (they drank alcohol). They supported **parochial schools** (private Catholic schools) and **immigrant rights** (they opposed "Americanization" programs). They also supported **religious freedom** (they did not want public schools to teach Protestantism).

**Difference 3 – Tariff Policies (KC-6.3.II.A):**
- **Republicans** : The Republican Party strongly supported protective tariffs. The "American System" (Henry Clay) called for high tariffs to protect American industry from foreign competition. The "infant industry" argument: American industries needed protection until they could compete with established British industries. The **McKinley Tariff (1890)** raised rates to 50%. The **Dingley Tariff (1897)** raised rates to 57% – the highest in American history. Republicans argued that high tariffs protected American jobs and wages.
- **Democrats** : The Democratic Party supported "tariff for revenue only" (low tariffs). Democrats argued that high tariffs were a "tax on the poor" (because they raised the price of imported goods). They also argued that high tariffs hurt farmers (who could not sell their crops to Europe if Europe retaliated with tariffs on American goods). The **Wilson-Gorman Tariff (1894)** reduced rates slightly (but not enough to satisfy the Democrats). The **Underwood Tariff (1913)** (after this period) reduced rates significantly.

**Difference 4 – Monetary Policy (After 1896) (KC-6.3.II.A):**
- **Republicans** : The Republican Party supported the gold standard (gold as the only monetary standard). The **Gold Standard Act (1900)** placed the US on the gold standard (18 years after the "Crime of '73"). The gold standard kept the currency stable (low inflation, low deflation). It favored creditors (bankers, bondholders) over debtors (farmers, small business owners).
- **Democrats** : The Democratic Party (after 1896) supported "free silver" (bimetallism – gold and silver). Free silver would inflate the currency, raise farm prices, and ease debts. The "Cross of Gold" speech (William Jennings Bryan, 1896) was the high point of the free silver movement. Bryan lost the election. The free silver movement died after 1896 (when gold was discovered in Alaska, the Klondike, and South Africa, increasing the money supply and raising prices).

## LO 6.L: Explain the extent to which industrialization brought change from 1865 to 1898.

**Contextualization:** Industrialization transformed the United States between 1865 and 1898. It created enormous wealth, raised the standard of living for many Americans, and made the US the world's largest industrial power. But it also created enormous inequality, exploited workers (including children), damaged the environment (air and water pollution, deforestation, soil erosion), and destroyed traditional ways of life (farming, artisan crafts).

**Thesis:** Industrialization brought **significant change** to the United States from 1865 to 1898 in **economic** terms (the US became the world's largest industrial power; the middle class expanded; consumer culture emerged), **social** terms (cities grew; immigration increased; labor unions formed; the "New South" industrialized), **political** terms (the Populist Party challenged the two-party system; the Grange and Farmers' Alliance lobbied for government regulation; the Sherman Antitrust Act and Interstate Commerce Act were passed), and **geographic** terms (the West was settled; the "frontier" closed; Native Americans were dispossessed); but there were also **continuities** (the gap between rich and poor remained vast; workers continued to face low wages, long hours, and dangerous conditions; women and African Americans continued to face discrimination; the federal government continued to support business and oppose labor unions).

**Body Evidence (Detailed Bullet Points):**

**Economic Change (KC-6.1):**
- The US became the world's largest industrial power by 1890 (surpassing Britain). Steel production: 20,000 tons (1865) → 10 million tons (1900). Oil production: 3 million barrels (1865) → 60 million barrels (1900). Coal production: 10 million tons (1865) → 250 million tons (1900). Railroad mileage: 35,000 miles (1865) → 199,000 miles (1900). Gross National Product (GNP): $9 billion (1870) → $37 billion (1900) → $91 billion (1916).
- Real wages increased by 50% between 1865 and 1900. The cost of goods fell (due to mass production, lower transportation costs, and a stable currency). The average work week declined (from 60-70 hours to 50-60 hours).
- The middle class grew (from 15% of the workforce in 1870 to 30% in 1900). Managers, clerical workers, professionals, and small business owners enjoyed higher incomes, more leisure time, and better living conditions.
- Consumer culture emerged. Department stores, catalog sales, and advertising created demand for consumer goods (clothing, furniture, pianos, phonographs, bicycles, sewing machines, watches, jewelry, books, magazines).
- The "New South" (Henry Grady) industrialized (textile mills, tobacco factories, steel mills, iron furnaces, railroad expansion, the growth of cities – Atlanta, Birmingham, Nashville, Richmond, Charlotte).

**Social Change (KC-6.2):**
- Urbanization: The proportion of Americans living in cities (2,500+ population) grew from 25% (1870) to 40% (1900). New York, Chicago, Philadelphia, St. Louis, Boston, and Baltimore became major metropolises.
- Immigration: 10 million immigrants arrived (1870-1900). They came from Germany, Ireland, Britain, Scandinavia, Canada, and later (after 1890) from Italy, Poland, Russia, Austria-Hungary, Greece, Turkey, and Japan.
- Labor unions: The Knights of Labor (1869-1890s), the American Federation of Labor (1886-1955), and the Industrial Workers of the World (1905-1920) organized workers to demand higher wages, shorter hours, and better working conditions.
- The "New Woman": Women entered the workforce in large numbers (as clerical workers, telephone operators, teachers, nurses, social workers, librarians, and factory workers). The "Gibson Girl" (artist Charles Dana Gibson) was the ideal of the "New Woman": independent, athletic, college-educated, and fashionable.
- The "National Pastime": Baseball became America's favorite sport. The National League (1876), the American League (1901), and the first World Series (1903) were established.
- The "Harlem Renaissance" (after 1918) began in the 1890s with the migration of African Americans to Harlem (New York City). The NAACP (1909) and the Urban League (1910) were founded.

**Political Change (KC-6.3):**
- The Populist Party (People's Party) challenged the two-party system (1892-1908). The Omaha Platform (1892) called for free silver, a progressive income tax, government ownership of railroads, direct election of senators, the secret ballot, initiative, referendum, recall, and the 8-hour day.
- The Grange (Patrons of Husbandry, 1867) and the Farmers' Alliance (1877) lobbied for government regulation of railroads (the Interstate Commerce Act, 1887) and antitrust laws (the Sherman Antitrust Act, 1890).
- The "Progressive Era" (1900-1920) began in the 1890s with the "muckrakers" (journalists who exposed corruption: Jacob Riis, *How the Other Half Lives* (1890); Ida Tarbell, *The History of the Standard Oil Company* (1904); Upton Sinclair, *The Jungle* (1906); Lincoln Steffens, *The Shame of the Cities* (1904)).
- The "Wisconsin Idea" (Robert La Follette, governor 1901-1906, senator 1906-1925) was a model of progressive reform: direct primaries (to limit the power of party bosses), railroad regulation (the Wisconsin Railroad Commission), workers' compensation, and the first state income tax.

**Geographic Change (KC-6.2.II):**
- The West was settled: the Homestead Act (1862) brought 1.6 million homesteaders to the Great Plains. The "bonanza farms" (Red River Valley) and cattle ranches (Texas to Montana) transformed the economy.
- The "frontier" closed: the Census Bureau declared the frontier "closed" in 1890 (no more continuous line of settlement). Frederick Jackson Turner delivered his "Frontier Thesis" (1893): the frontier had shaped American character (individualism, democracy, ingenuity); its closing would lead to social unrest and class conflict.
- The transcontinental railroad (1869) connected the East and West. The Northern Pacific (1883), the Southern Pacific (1883), the Great Northern (1893), and the Santa Fe (1887) followed. Railroads opened the West to settlement, trade, and tourism.
- National parks were established: Yellowstone (1872), Yosemite (1890), Sequoia (1890), Mount Rainier (1899), Crater Lake (1902), Mesa Verde (1906). The national parks preserved the "American wilderness" for future generations.
- Native Americans were dispossessed: The Dawes Act (1887) broke up reservations into individual allotments (160 acres per head of household). The "surplus" land (90 million acres) was sold to white settlers. The Wounded Knee massacre (1890) ended Native resistance.

**Continuities (KC-6.1, KC-6.2, KC-6.3):**
- Economic inequality: The richest 1% of Americans owned 51% of the nation's wealth; the poorest 50% owned 1%. The "Gilded Age" (Mark Twain) was an age of "conspicuous consumption" (Thorstein Veblen) and abject poverty.
- Workers' conditions: 12-hour days, 6 days a week. No sick leave, no vacation, no workers' compensation. 35,000-50,000 workers died each year in industrial accidents. 1.7 million children under 16 worked for wages.
- Discrimination: Women earned 50-60% of what men earned. African Americans earned 30-50% of what whites earned. African Americans were disenfranchised (poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses) and segregated (Jim Crow). Asians were excluded (Chinese Exclusion Act 1882, "Gentlemen's Agreement" 1907).
- Laissez-faire ideology: The Supreme Court struck down state and federal regulations as unconstitutional (U.S. v. E.C. Knight Co., 1895). Federal troops were used to break strikes (Homestead 1892, Pullman 1894). The Sherman Antitrust Act was used more often against unions than against monopolies.
- Political corruption: The spoils system (patronage) was used by both parties. Political machines (Tammany Hall) controlled elections through bribes, fraud, and graft. The Credit Mobilier, Whiskey Ring, and Indian Ring scandals revealed widespread corruption.
